🫘 Acute Kidney Injury

AKI Definition (KDIGO)

Any ONE of the following:
  • • ↑Cr ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours
  • • ↑Cr ≥1.5x baseline within 7 days
  • • Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr for 6 hours
Stage 1
Cr 1.5-1.9x OR ↑≥0.3
UO <0.5 for 6-12h
Stage 2
Cr 2.0-2.9x baseline
UO <0.5 for ≥12h
Stage 3
Cr ≥3x OR ≥4.0 OR RRT
UO <0.3 for ≥24h or anuria ≥12h

The Three Categories: Where's the Problem?

Pre-Renal (55-60%)

Problem BEFORE the kidney
Mechanism
↓Renal perfusion → ↓GFR
Kidney is RESPONDING appropriately to low flow
Causes:
• Hypovolemia (hemorrhage, dehydration, burns)
• ↓Cardiac output (CHF, MI)
• ↓Effective circulating volume (cirrhosis, sepsis)
• Renal vasoconstriction (NSAIDs, ACEi/ARB, contrast)
Key Feature:
Reversible with volume/perfusion restoration

Intrinsic (35-40%)

Problem IN the kidney
Mechanism
Structural damage to kidney tissue
Kidney itself is INJURED
By Location:
Tubular (ATN): Ischemia, toxins (90%)
Glomerular: GN, vasculitis
Interstitial: AIN (drugs, infection)
Vascular: TTP/HUS, renal artery
Key Feature:
Does NOT improve with fluids alone

Post-Renal (5%)

Problem AFTER the kidney
Mechanism
Obstruction → Back-pressure → ↓GFR
Must be BILATERAL (or single kidney)
Causes:
• BPH (most common in men)
• Bilateral stones
• Pelvic malignancy
• Neurogenic bladder
• Foley obstruction
Key Feature:
Reversible with relief of obstruction

Lab Differentiation: Pre-Renal vs ATN

Parameter Pre-Renal ATN (Intrinsic) Why?
FENa <1% >2% Pre-renal: Kidney holds onto Na⁺. ATN: Tubules can't reabsorb
FEUrea <35% >50% Better if on diuretics (Na unreliable)
Urine Na <20 >40 Pre-renal: Avid Na⁺ retention
Urine Osm >500 <350 Pre-renal: Concentrating urine. ATN: Can't concentrate
BUN:Cr >20:1 <15:1 Pre-renal: ↑Urea reabsorption (slow flow)
Urine Sediment Bland/Hyaline Muddy brown casts ATN: Sloughed tubular epithelial cells
FENa Formula
FENa = (UNa × PCr) / (PNa × UCr) × 100
⚠️ FENa unreliable with: Diuretics, contrast, CKD, GN, myoglobinuria. Use FEUrea instead.

ATN: The Most Common Intrinsic AKI

Ischemic ATN

Mechanism: Prolonged pre-renal state → Tubular necrosis
Vulnerable areas: S3 segment of PCT, thick ascending limb (high metabolic demand, low O₂)
Causes: Prolonged hypotension, sepsis, major surgery, hemorrhage

Nephrotoxic ATN

Mechanism: Direct tubular toxicity
Exogenous: Contrast, aminoglycosides, amphotericin, cisplatin
Endogenous: Myoglobin (rhabdo), hemoglobin (hemolysis), uric acid (TLS), light chains (myeloma)
ATN Course: Oliguric phase (days-weeks) → Diuretic/Recovery phase (polyuria, watch electrolytes!) → Recovery (may take months)

AIN: Acute Interstitial Nephritis

Classic Triad (only 10-30%)

  • • Fever
  • • Rash
  • • Eosinophilia
Most patients don't have the full triad!

Common Culprits

  • • Antibiotics: Penicillins, cephalosporins, sulfonamides, fluoroquinolones, rifampin
  • • NSAIDs
  • • PPIs (increasingly recognized)
  • • Allopurinol
Diagnosis: UA shows WBC casts, eosinophiluria (Hansel stain). Definitive = Renal biopsy. Treatment: Stop offending drug. Consider steroids if no improvement in 5-7 days.

Contrast-Induced AKI (CI-AKI)

Risk Factors

  • • Pre-existing CKD (biggest risk)
  • • Diabetes
  • • Volume depletion
  • • High contrast volume
  • • Concurrent nephrotoxins

Timeline

• Cr rises 24-48h post-contrast
• Peaks at 3-5 days
• Usually recovers by 7-10 days
Prevention: IV fluids (NS or bicarb) before and after. Minimize contrast volume. Hold metformin (lactic acidosis risk if AKI develops). N-acetylcysteine controversial.
