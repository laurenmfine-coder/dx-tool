🧠 Meningitis & Encephalitis

🚨 Bacterial Meningitis = Medical Emergency
Don't delay antibiotics for LP! Give empiric treatment if LP will be delayed.

Clinical Presentation

Classic Triad (only 44% have all three)
🤒
Fever
🤕
Headache
😵
Nuchal Rigidity
95% have at least 2 of: fever, headache, neck stiffness, altered mental status

Physical Exam Signs

Kernig's sign: Pain with knee extension when hip flexed 90°
Brudzinski's sign: Involuntary hip/knee flexion with passive neck flexion
Jolt accentuation: Headache worsens with horizontal head rotation (sensitive)

Red Flags

• Petechial/purpuric rash → Think N. meningitidis
• Seizures
• Focal neurological deficits
• Papilledema (↑ICP)
• Altered consciousness

Bacterial Pattern Summary
↑↑WBC (PMNs), ↓↓Glucose, ↑↑Protein
"Bacteria eat sugar and make protein mess"
Viral Pattern Summary
↑WBC (Lymphs), Normal glucose, Mild ↑Protein
"Viruses are nicer to your sugar"
Early Viral Exception: First 24-48h of viral meningitis may show PMN predominance (will shift to lymphocytes on repeat LP)

Bacterial Meningitis: Bugs by Age & Risk Factor

Neonates (<1 month)
GBS, E. coli, Listeria
Empiric: Ampicillin + Cefotaxime (or Gent)
Infants/Children (1mo-18y)
S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, (H. flu if unvaccinated)
Empiric: Vanc + Ceftriaxone
Adults (18-50y)
S. pneumoniae (#1), N. meningitidis
Empiric: Vanc + Ceftriaxone
Elderly (>50y) & Immunocompromised
S. pneumoniae, Listeria, Gram-negatives
Empiric: Vanc + Ceftriaxone + Ampicillin
Post-Neurosurgery/Trauma/Shunt
S. aureus, Gram-negatives (including Pseudomonas), Coag-neg Staph
Empiric: Vanc + Cefepime (or Meropenem)
CSF Leak/Basilar Skull Fracture
S. pneumoniae (most common)
🔑 Listeria Risk Factors: Age >50, pregnancy, immunocompromised, alcoholism. ADD AMPICILLIN (cephalosporins don't cover Listeria!)

💊 Empiric Treatment Algorithm

Standard Adult Empiric Regimen
Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone + Dexamethasone
Add Ampicillin if Listeria risk | Add Acyclovir if encephalitis suspected
Vancomycin
Covers drug-resistant S. pneumoniae (DRSP)
15-20 mg/kg q8-12h
Ceftriaxone
S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis, H. flu, Gram-negatives
2g IV q12h (meningitis dosing)
Dexamethasone
↓Inflammation, ↓Mortality in pneumococcal meningitis
0.15 mg/kg q6h × 4 days. Give BEFORE or WITH first abx dose
⚠️ Timing: Give antibiotics within 60 minutes of arrival. Don't wait for CT/LP if it will delay treatment!

CT Before LP? (IDSA Guidelines)

LP WITHOUT CT First

If NONE of these present:
  • ✓ No immunocompromise
  • ✓ No history of CNS disease
  • ✓ No seizure within 1 week
  • ✓ Normal level of consciousness
  • ✓ No papilledema
  • ✓ No focal neuro deficits

GET CT FIRST if ANY of:

  • • Immunocompromised (HIV, transplant)
  • • History of CNS lesion/disease
  • • New onset seizure
  • • Altered consciousness (GCS <10)
  • • Papilledema
  • • Focal neurological deficit
BUT: Give antibiotics BEFORE CT! Don't delay treatment.

Viral Meningitis

Common Causes

  • • Enteroviruses (most common - summer/fall)
  • • HSV-2 (recurrent, Mollaret's)
  • • Arboviruses (West Nile, EEE)
  • • HIV (acute seroconversion)
  • • Mumps (if unvaccinated)

Management

Usually supportive care only
• Self-limited (7-10 days)
• Symptomatic treatment (analgesics, antiemetics)
• May not need admission if immunocompetent with clear viral picture

🔥 HSV Encephalitis - Don't Miss It!

Encephalitis = Brain parenchyma involvement → Altered mental status, focal findings, seizures

Clinical Features

  • • Fever + altered mental status
  • • Personality/behavioral changes
  • • Seizures (often temporal lobe)
  • • Focal deficits (aphasia common)
  • • May have preceding viral prodrome

Diagnosis

  • • CSF PCR for HSV (gold standard)
  • • CSF: Lymphocytic pleocytosis, RBCs common, ↑protein
  • • MRI: Temporal lobe involvement (bilateral or unilateral)
  • • EEG: Periodic lateralized epileptiform discharges (PLEDs)
Treatment: IV Acyclovir 10 mg/kg q8h × 14-21 days. START EMPIRICALLY if any suspicion - don't wait for PCR! Mortality 70% untreated → 20% with treatment.

Special Situations

TB Meningitis

• Subacute onset (weeks)
• CSF: Lymphocytes, very low glucose, very high protein
• Basilar meningitis → CN palsies
• AFB smear often negative; need culture/PCR
Tx: RIPE + Steroids

Cryptococcal Meningitis

• HIV/AIDS (CD4 <100) or immunocompromised
• Subacute; headache prominent, minimal nuchal rigidity
• CSF: Lymphocytes, ↓glucose, ↑opening pressure
• India ink, Cryptococcal Ag (serum + CSF)
Tx: Ampho B + Flucytosine induction, then Fluconazole
