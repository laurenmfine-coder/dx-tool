Why Potassium is Tricky in DKA

On Presentation: Serum K+ Often Normal/High

Despite massive total body K+ depletion:

  • Acidosis → H+ enters cells → K+ exits cells
  • Insulin deficiency → K+ can't enter cells
  • Hyperosmolarity → Solvent drag pulls K+ out

After Treatment: K+ Drops Rapidly

As you correct DKA:

  • Insulin → K+ shifts INTO cells
  • Correcting acidosis → More K+ into cells
  • Ongoing urinary K+ losses
⚠️ Critical: Replace K+ before insulin if K+ < 3.3. Hold insulin until K+ > 3.3 to prevent fatal hypokalemia.

Treatment: Targeting Each Mechanism

Fluids
NS 15-20 mL/kg/hr initially
Target: Restore intravascular volume, improve renal perfusion
Mechanism: Addresses osmotic diuresis losses
Insulin
0.1 U/kg/hr IV infusion (after K+ check)
Target: ↓ Glucose 50-70 mg/dL/hr, close anion gap
Mechanism: Stops lipolysis → stops ketogenesis
K+
20-40 mEq/L in fluids (if K+ 3.3-5.3)
Target: Maintain K+ 4-5 mEq/L during treatment
Mechanism: Replaces total body deficit as insulin shifts K+ into cells
D5
Add when glucose < 200-250 mg/dL
Target: Prevent hypoglycemia while continuing insulin
Mechanism: Glucose drops faster than ketones clear; need ongoing insulin for ketosis

Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)

Extreme dehydration from profound hyperglycemia without significant ketosis

The Key Mechanistic Difference from DKA

In HHS, there is enough insulin to suppress lipolysis (and therefore ketogenesis) but not enough to prevent hyperglycemia.

↓↓↓ Insulin
Absolute deficiency
vs
HHS
↓ Insulin
Relative deficiency

The HHS Dehydration Spiral

Hyperglycemia (often > 600 mg/dL)
↓
Massive osmotic diuresis
↓
Profound dehydration (8-12L deficit)
↓
↓ Renal glucose excretion (can't keep up)
↓
Worsening hyperglycemia → More diuresis
This is a positive feedback loop that spirals out of control

Why Minimal Ketosis in HHS?

Insulin's Anti-Lipolytic Effect

Even small amounts of insulin potently suppress hormone-sensitive lipase in adipose tissue. This prevents FFA release for ketogenesis.

Hyperosmolarity Effect

Extreme hyperosmolarity may itself inhibit lipolysis and ketogenesis, though this mechanism is less well understood.

HHS Clinical Features (Mechanism-Based)

Altered Mental Status

Serum osmolality > 320 mOsm/kg → Neuronal dehydration → Confusion to coma

More common in HHS than DKA due to higher osmolarity

Seizures

Cerebral dehydration + electrolyte abnormalities → Lowered seizure threshold

Thrombotic Events

Hyperviscosity from dehydration → ↑ Risk of stroke, DVT, PE

Consider DVT prophylaxis

Slower Evolution

Develops over days to weeks (vs hours in DKA) → More time for dehydration

🎯 Clinical Decision Point

Some patients present with overlap features (ketosis AND hyperosmolarity). This is called "ketotic hyperosmolar state" and requires treating both:

  • Aggressive fluid resuscitation (like HHS)
  • Insulin to close the anion gap (like DKA)
  • Close K+ monitoring
  • Watch for cerebral edema with rapid osmolarity correction
