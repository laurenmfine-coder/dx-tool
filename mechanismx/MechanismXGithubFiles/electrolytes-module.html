Sodium Disorders

Sodium determines serum osmolality and water distribution

Serum Osm ≈ 2×[Na⁺] + [Glucose]/18 + [BUN]/2.8
Sodium is THE major determinant of osmolality

🔑 Key Concept: Sodium = Water Problem

Hyponatremia usually means too much water relative to sodium.
Hypernatremia usually means too little water relative to sodium.

Hyponatremia (Na⁺ < 135)

Step 1: Check Serum Osmolality

Hypotonic (<280)
TRUE hyponatremia
Proceed to Step 2
Isotonic (280-295)
Pseudohyponatremia
↑ Lipids or proteins displacing water
Hypertonic (>295)
Translocational
Hyperglycemia, mannitol pulling water

Step 2: Assess Volume Status (for Hypotonic)

Hypovolemic
Low total body Na AND water
  • • GI losses (vomiting, diarrhea)
  • • Diuretics
  • • Cerebral salt wasting
  • • Adrenal insufficiency
Tx: NS (give salt AND water)
Euvolemic
Normal Na, excess free water
  • • SIADH (most common)
  • • Hypothyroidism
  • • Psychogenic polydipsia
  • • Beer potomania
Tx: Fluid restriction
Hypervolemic
↑ Total body Na, even more ↑ water
  • • Heart failure
  • • Cirrhosis
  • • Nephrotic syndrome
  • • Kidney failure
Tx: Diuresis + fluid restrict

SIADH Mechanism

Inappropriate ADH → ↑ Water reabsorption → Dilutional ↓Na⁺ → Volume expansion → ↓ Aldosterone → Na⁺ wasting
Causes: CNS disorders, lung disease, malignancy, drugs (SSRIs, carbamazepine), pain, nausea
Diagnosis: Euvolemic + Uosm > 100 + UNa > 40 + low uric acid

⚠️ Correction Speed Matters!

Chronic hyponatremia (>48h): Correct ≤8-10 mEq/L per 24h
Risk of overcorrection: Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome (ODS)
Brain adapts to low osmolality by extruding osmoles. Rapid correction → brain cell shrinkage → pontine myelinolysis

Hypernatremia (Na⁺ > 145)

Almost always a water deficit problem (not salt excess)

↓ Water Intake
  • • Altered mental status
  • • No access to water
  • • Impaired thirst (elderly)
↑ Water Loss
  • • Diabetes insipidus
  • • Osmotic diuresis
  • • Diarrhea, sweating
↑ Sodium Gain (rare)
  • • Hypertonic saline
  • • NaHCO₃ infusion
  • • Salt tablets

Diabetes Insipidus Mechanism

Central DI:
↓ ADH production (pituitary)
Causes: surgery, trauma, tumor, infiltration
Nephrogenic DI:
Kidney resistant to ADH
Causes: lithium, hypercalcemia, hypokalemia

Potassium Disorders

K⁺ is critical for membrane potential and cardiac conduction

⚠️ Potassium Can Kill

Both hypo- and hyperkalemia cause fatal arrhythmias. Always check K⁺ and get an ECG!

Normal K⁺ Distribution

98%
Intracellular
|
2%
Extracellular
Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase maintains this gradient. Small shifts cause big serum changes!

Hypokalemia (K⁺ < 3.5)

Mechanisms

Transcellular Shift
  • • Insulin (drives K⁺ into cells)
  • • β-agonists (albuterol)
  • • Alkalosis (H⁺/K⁺ exchange)
GI Losses
  • • Diarrhea (direct K⁺ loss)
  • • Vomiting (metabolic alkalosis → renal K⁺ wasting)
Renal Losses
  • • Diuretics (loop, thiazide)
  • • Hyperaldosteronism
  • • RTA types 1 and 2
  • • Hypomagnesemia

ECG Changes

Mild: T wave flattening
Moderate: ST depression, U waves
Severe: Prolonged QT, arrhythmias
Why U waves?

Hypokalemia → hyperpolarization → delayed repolarization of Purkinje fibers

🔑 Check Magnesium!

Hypokalemia is often refractory until hypomagnesemia is corrected. Mg²⁺ is needed to close ROMK channels.

Hyperkalemia (K⁺ > 5.0)

Mechanisms

↓ Excretion
  • • Kidney failure (most common)
  • • Hypoaldosteronism
  • • K⁺-sparing diuretics
  • • ACEi/ARB, NSAIDs
Transcellular Shift
  • • Acidosis (H⁺/K⁺ exchange)
  • • Insulin deficiency (DKA)
  • • β-blockers
  • • Cell lysis (rhabdo, TLS)
Pseudohyperkalemia
  • • Hemolyzed sample
  • • Fist clenching during draw
  • • Extreme leukocytosis/thrombocytosis

ECG Progression

5.5-6.5: Peaked T waves
6.5-7.5: PR prolongation, P wave flattening
7.5-8.0: QRS widening
>8.0: Sine wave → VF → Asystole

Emergency Treatment (Mechanism-Based)

1
Calcium gluconate
Stabilizes cardiac membrane (doesn't lower K⁺)
2
Insulin + Glucose / β-agonists / Bicarb
Shift K⁺ INTO cells (temporary)
3
Kayexalate / Lokelma / Diuretics / Dialysis
Remove K⁺ from body (definitive)

Calcium Disorders

Calcium is essential for muscle contraction, nerve function, and bone health

🔑 Always Correct for Albumin!

Corrected Ca = Measured Ca + 0.8 × (4 - Albumin)

~40% of calcium is albumin-bound. Low albumin = falsely low total Ca

Calcium Regulation

PTH
↑ Bone resorption
↑ Renal reabsorption
↑ 1,25-D activation
Vitamin D
↑ GI absorption
↑ Bone resorption
Calcitonin
↓ Bone resorption
(minor role)

Hypercalcemia (Ca > 10.5)

Symptoms: "Stones, Bones, Groans, Moans"

Stones
Kidney stones
Bones
Bone pain
Groans
Constipation
Moans
Psych sx

PTH-Dependent (PTH ↑)

  • • Primary hyperparathyroidism (adenoma)
  • • Familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia
  • • Lithium-induced

PTH-Independent (PTH ↓)

  • • Malignancy (PTHrP, mets, myeloma)
  • • Granulomatous disease (↑ 1,25-D)
  • • Vitamin D toxicity
  • • Thiazides, immobilization

Treatment Principles

  1. Volume: NS to restore intravascular volume and ↑ Ca excretion
  2. Loop diuretic: After volume replete (blocks Ca reabsorption)
  3. Bisphosphonates: ↓ Bone resorption (takes 2-4 days)
  4. Calcitonin: Rapid but transient effect
  5. Dialysis: If severe/refractory

Hypocalcemia (Ca < 8.5)

Symptoms: Neuromuscular Irritability

Chvostek's Sign

Facial twitching when tapping facial nerve

Trousseau's Sign

Carpopedal spasm with BP cuff inflation

Causes

  • • Hypoparathyroidism (post-surgical)
  • • Vitamin D deficiency
  • • CKD (↓ 1,25-D production)
  • • Hypomagnesemia (blocks PTH)
  • • Acute pancreatitis (saponification)
  • • Hungry bone syndrome

ECG: Prolonged QT

Low Ca → delayed phase 2 repolarization → ↑ QT → risk of Torsades de Pointes

Magnesium Disorders

The forgotten electrolyte - critical for enzymatic reactions and electrolyte homeostasis

🔑 Mg Affects Everything

Hypomagnesemia causes refractory hypokalemia and refractory hypocalcemia. Always check and replace Mg when treating K⁺ or Ca²⁺ disorders!

Hypomagnesemia

GI Losses

Diarrhea, malabsorption, PPI use

Renal Losses

Diuretics, alcohol, aminoglycosides

Symptoms

Tremor, seizures, arrhythmias, Torsades

Hypermagnesemia

Causes

Renal failure + Mg intake (antacids, laxatives)

Symptoms (by level)
  • 4-6: ↓ DTRs
  • 6-10: Hypotension, bradycardia
  • >10: Respiratory paralysis, cardiac arrest
Treatment

IV calcium (antagonist), dialysis

Phosphate Disorders

Essential for ATP, bone, and acid-base buffering

Hypophosphatemia

Causes
  • • Refeeding syndrome
  • • Respiratory alkalosis
  • • DKA treatment (insulin shifts)
  • • Vitamin D deficiency
Refeeding Syndrome

Carb intake → Insulin → PO₄ shifts into cells for ATP synthesis → Severe hypophosphatemia → Cardiac/respiratory failure

Hyperphosphatemia

Causes
  • • Renal failure (most common)
  • • Tumor lysis syndrome
  • • Rhabdomyolysis
  • • Hypoparathyroidism
Consequences

High PO₄ binds Ca → ↓ Ca → Secondary hyperparathyroidism → Metastatic calcification
