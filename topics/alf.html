About Alf

ALF (Acute Liver Failure) is a rare but life-threatening condition characterized by rapid deterioration of liver function in patients without pre-existing liver disease, typically developing within 26 weeks of symptom onset. The condition leads to severe hepatocellular dysfunction, coagulopathy, and hepatic encephalopathy, requiring immediate medical intervention and often liver transplantation.

Pathophysiology

ALF results from massive hepatocyte necrosis that overwhelms the liver's regenerative capacity, leading to failure of synthetic, metabolic, and detoxification functions. Common causes include acetaminophen toxicity, viral hepatitis, drug-induced liver injury, and autoimmune hepatitis, with the underlying mechanism involving direct hepatotoxicity or immune-mediated destruction of liver cells.

Clinical Reasoning

Diagnosis requires evidence of acute liver injury with coagulopathy (INR ≥1.5) and altered mental status in the absence of chronic liver disease. Management focuses on identifying and treating the underlying cause, supporting organ function, managing complications like cerebral edema and bleeding, and determining candidacy for emergency liver transplantation based on prognostic criteria such as the King's College Criteria.
