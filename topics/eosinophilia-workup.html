About Eosinophilia Workup

Eosinophilia is defined as an absolute eosinophil count >500 cells/μL and can be classified as mild (500-1,500), moderate (1,500-5,000), or severe (>5,000 cells/μL). The workup requires systematic evaluation to identify underlying causes including allergic diseases, parasitic infections, medications, hematologic malignancies, and autoimmune conditions.

Pathophysiology

Eosinophils are granulocytes that normally comprise 1-4% of circulating white blood cells and play key roles in allergic responses and parasitic defense. Increased production occurs through IL-5, IL-3, and GM-CSF stimulation, while tissue migration is mediated by eotaxins and other chemokines. Eosinophils can cause tissue damage through release of cytotoxic granule proteins including major basic protein and eosinophil cationic protein.

Clinical Reasoning

The workup should begin with a thorough history focusing on travel, medications, allergies, and systemic symptoms, followed by physical examination for organomegaly or skin changes. Initial laboratory testing includes complete blood count with differential, comprehensive metabolic panel, and consideration of stool ova and parasites, vitamin B12/folate, and tryptase levels. Advanced testing may include bone marrow biopsy, flow cytometry for clonal markers, and specific parasitic serologies based on clinical suspicion.
