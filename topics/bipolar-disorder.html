About Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder is a chronic mental health condition characterized by extreme mood swings between manic or hypomanic episodes and major depressive episodes. The disorder affects approximately 1-3% of the population and typically emerges in late adolescence or early adulthood, requiring lifelong management with mood stabilizers and psychotherapy.

Pathophysiology

Bipolar disorder involves dysregulation of neurotransmitter systems, particularly dopamine, serotonin, and norepinephrine, along with abnormalities in brain circuits controlling mood, reward, and executive function. Genetic factors contribute significantly to risk, with heritability estimates of 60-85%, while environmental stressors can trigger mood episodes in susceptible individuals.

Clinical Reasoning

Diagnosis requires careful assessment of mood episode history, as patients often present during depressive phases and may not report past manic episodes without direct questioning. Distinguishing between Bipolar I (full manic episodes) and Bipolar II (hypomanic episodes with major depression) is crucial for appropriate treatment selection, as antidepressants alone can precipitate manic switching in bipolar patients.
