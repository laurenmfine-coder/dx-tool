About Dka

Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) is a life-threatening acute complication of diabetes mellitus characterized by severe hyperglycemia, ketosis, and metabolic acidosis. It most commonly occurs in patients with type 1 diabetes but can also affect those with type 2 diabetes under severe stress conditions. DKA requires immediate medical intervention and typically presents with nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and altered mental status.

Pathophysiology

DKA develops when insulin deficiency leads to uncontrolled lipolysis and subsequent ketone body production by the liver. The absence or inadequacy of insulin prevents glucose uptake by cells, causing severe hyperglycemia and osmotic diuresis, while simultaneously promoting fat breakdown into free fatty acids that are converted to ketones (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate). This excessive ketone production overwhelms the body's buffering capacity, resulting in metabolic acidosis with an increased anion gap.

Clinical Reasoning

Diagnosis of DKA requires the triad of hyperglycemia (>250 mg/dL), ketonemia or ketonuria, and metabolic acidosis (pH <7.3, bicarbonate <15 mEq/L). Treatment involves aggressive fluid resuscitation, insulin therapy to halt ketogenesis, and electrolyte replacement, particularly potassium and phosphate. Close monitoring is essential to prevent complications such as cerebral edema, hypokalemia, and hypoglycemia during treatment.
