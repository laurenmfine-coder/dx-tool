About Spinal Cord Compression

Spinal cord compression is a serious neurological emergency caused by external pressure on the spinal cord from tumors, herniated discs, infections, or trauma. It presents with progressive neurological deficits including weakness, sensory loss, and bowel/bladder dysfunction below the level of compression. Early recognition and treatment are critical to prevent permanent neurological damage.

Pathophysiology

Compression of the spinal cord disrupts normal neural transmission by physically deforming neural tissue and compromising local blood supply. The resulting ischemia and mechanical damage lead to axonal injury, demyelination, and neuronal death if pressure persists. The degree of dysfunction correlates with the severity and duration of compression, with early intervention offering the best chance for neurological recovery.

Clinical Reasoning

Diagnosis requires high clinical suspicion based on progressive neurological symptoms, particularly in patients with known malignancy or risk factors for spinal pathology. MRI is the gold standard imaging modality to identify the location and cause of compression. Treatment timing is crucial - surgical decompression within 24-48 hours of symptom onset provides the best outcomes, while delayed intervention may result in irreversible paralysis and loss of function.
