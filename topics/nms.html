About Nms

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS) is a rare but potentially fatal adverse reaction to antipsychotic medications characterized by hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, altered mental status, and autonomic instability. This medical emergency occurs in approximately 0.1-3% of patients exposed to neuroleptic drugs and requires immediate recognition and treatment to prevent serious complications or death.

Pathophysiology

NMS results from dopamine receptor blockade in the basal ganglia, hypothalamus, and spinal cord, leading to thermoregulatory dysfunction and extrapyramidal symptoms. The condition involves disruption of central dopaminergic pathways that control temperature regulation, muscle tone, and autonomic functions, resulting in the characteristic tetrad of clinical manifestations.

Clinical Reasoning

Early recognition of NMS is crucial as symptoms can rapidly progress from mild confusion and stiffness to life-threatening hyperthermia and rhabdomyolysis. The diagnosis relies on clinical presentation in the setting of recent neuroleptic exposure, supported by elevated creatine kinase levels and exclusion of other causes such as infection or heat stroke. Immediate discontinuation of the offending agent and supportive care with cooling measures, fluid resuscitation, and medications like dantrolene or bromocriptine can significantly improve outcomes.
