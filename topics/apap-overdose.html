About Apap Overdose

Acetaminophen (APAP) overdose is a leading cause of acute liver failure and drug-related poisoning deaths. Toxicity occurs when APAP intake exceeds the liver's ability to safely metabolize the drug through normal pathways, typically at doses >150 mg/kg in children or >7.5-10g total in adults.

Pathophysiology

Normal APAP metabolism occurs via glucuronidation and sulfation, but in overdose, these pathways become saturated. Excess APAP is metabolized by CYP2E1 to the toxic metabolite NAPQI (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine), which depletes glutathione stores and causes hepatocellular necrosis through oxidative stress and protein binding.

Clinical Reasoning

Early recognition within 8-10 hours allows for N-acetylcysteine antidote administration, which replenishes glutathione and prevents hepatotoxicity. The Rumack-Matthew nomogram guides treatment decisions for acute ingestions, while delayed presentations require assessment of hepatic function and consideration of transplant candidacy based on King's College criteria.
