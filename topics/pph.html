About Pph

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is defined as excessive bleeding following childbirth, typically blood loss greater than 500mL after vaginal delivery or 1000mL after cesarean section. It remains one of the leading causes of maternal morbidity and mortality worldwide, requiring immediate recognition and intervention to prevent life-threatening complications.

Pathophysiology

PPH occurs due to four main mechanisms remembered by the '4 Ts': Tone (uterine atony - most common cause), Tissue (retained placental fragments), Trauma (cervical/vaginal lacerations), and Thrombin (coagulopathy). Uterine atony results from failure of myometrial contraction after delivery, preventing compression of spiral arteries and leading to continued bleeding from the placental bed.

Clinical Reasoning

Early recognition involves monitoring vital signs and quantifying blood loss, as visual estimation often underestimates actual volume. Management follows a stepwise approach starting with uterine massage and uterotonics (oxytocin, methylergonovine), progressing to surgical interventions like balloon tamponade or hysterectomy if conservative measures fail. Risk factors include prolonged labor, multiple gestation, placental abnormalities, and history of previous PPH, which should prompt heightened surveillance during delivery.
