About Sickle Cell Disease

Sickle cell disease is an inherited hemoglobinopathy caused by a point mutation in the β-globin gene, resulting in the production of abnormal hemoglobin S (HbS). This autosomal recessive disorder primarily affects individuals of African, Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, and Indian descent, causing red blood cells to assume a characteristic sickle shape under deoxygenated conditions.

Pathophysiology

The glutamic acid to valine substitution at position 6 of the β-globin chain causes HbS to polymerize when deoxygenated, distorting red blood cells into rigid, crescent-shaped forms. These sickled cells have reduced deformability, leading to vaso-occlusive crises, hemolytic anemia, and chronic organ damage due to impaired microcirculation and shortened red cell lifespan.

Clinical Reasoning

Patients present with episodic painful vaso-occlusive crises, chronic hemolytic anemia, and progressive organ dysfunction affecting the spleen, kidneys, lungs, and central nervous system. Management focuses on pain control during acute crises, prophylactic measures including hydroxyurea therapy to increase fetal hemoglobin production, and comprehensive care to prevent complications such as stroke, acute chest syndrome, and infections.
