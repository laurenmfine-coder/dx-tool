About Diabetic Foot Infection

Diabetic foot infections are serious complications that occur in patients with diabetes mellitus, ranging from superficial skin infections to deep tissue infections involving bone. These infections result from the combination of peripheral neuropathy, vascular insufficiency, and impaired immune response characteristic of diabetes. Early recognition and aggressive treatment are crucial to prevent limb-threatening complications and potential amputation.

Pathophysiology

The pathophysiology involves three key diabetic complications: peripheral neuropathy leading to loss of protective sensation and increased trauma risk, peripheral arterial disease causing poor tissue perfusion and delayed healing, and hyperglycemia-induced immune dysfunction impairing bacterial clearance. The loss of sensation allows minor injuries to go unnoticed, while poor circulation and compromised wound healing create an environment conducive to bacterial invasion and proliferation.

Clinical Reasoning

Clinical assessment focuses on identifying signs of infection including erythema, warmth, purulent discharge, and systemic symptoms, while evaluating the depth and extent of tissue involvement. Imaging studies help determine bone involvement, and cultures guide antibiotic selection, with particular attention to polymicrobial infections common in diabetic patients. Treatment decisions must consider infection severity, vascular status, and glycemic control, often requiring multidisciplinary care including infectious disease specialists, vascular surgeons, and podiatrists.
