About Myasthenic Crisis

Myasthenic crisis is a life-threatening exacerbation of myasthenia gravis characterized by severe weakness of respiratory and bulbar muscles, requiring immediate mechanical ventilation. It represents the most serious complication of myasthenia gravis, with respiratory failure being the primary cause of morbidity and mortality. Crisis can be triggered by infections, medications, stress, or inadequate immunosuppressive therapy.

Pathophysiology

The crisis results from severe impairment of neuromuscular transmission due to increased autoimmune destruction of acetylcholine receptors at the motor endplate. Inflammatory mediators and complement activation lead to accelerated receptor degradation and functional blockade, causing profound muscle weakness. The respiratory muscles, including the diaphragm and intercostal muscles, become severely compromised, leading to hypoventilation and respiratory failure.

Clinical Reasoning

Patients present with rapidly progressive generalized weakness, dysphagia, dysarthria, and most critically, respiratory distress with shallow breathing and reduced vital capacity. The inability to handle secretions, weak cough, and declining oxygen saturation indicate impending respiratory failure requiring urgent intubation. Distinguishing myasthenic crisis from cholinergic crisis is crucial, as the former requires increased immunosuppression while the latter requires anticholinesterase withdrawal.
