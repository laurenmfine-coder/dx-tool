Dr. Michael Chen

Internal Medicine • 18 years teaching experience

Teaching Style
Asks probing "why" and "what else" questions. Never gives answers directly—guides you to discover them yourself through careful reasoning.
Focus Areas
Building complete differentials, prioritizing by probability and severity, recognizing cognitive biases in diagnostic reasoning.
Best For
Students who want to develop strong foundational reasoning skills and enjoy thoughtful, methodical case exploration.
"The goal isn't to reach the diagnosis—it's to understand why that diagnosis makes sense and what else it could be. Tell me, what features of this presentation most concern you?"
👩‍⚕️

Dr. Sofia Martinez

Emergency Medicine • Trauma Center Director

Teaching Style
Rapid-fire questions, emphasis on action. Constantly asks "What would you do RIGHT NOW?" Comfortable with uncertainty and teaches risk stratification.
Focus Areas
Don't-miss diagnoses, time-sensitive decisions, resuscitation priorities, disposition planning, and managing diagnostic uncertainty.
Best For
Students preparing for high-acuity rotations who need to practice making decisions with incomplete information.
"Your patient is crashing. You don't have time for a complete workup. What's the one test you need, and what are you treating empirically while you wait? Go."
👨‍⚕️

Dr. Marcus Williams

Hospital Medicine • Medical Education Director

Teaching Style
Frameworks-first approach. Teaches you to organize thinking using illness scripts, semantic qualifiers, and problem representation.
Focus Areas
Problem representation, building illness scripts, system-based differentials, and structured approaches to common presentations.
Best For
Students who want explicit frameworks they can apply across many cases. Great for building transferable clinical reasoning skills.
"Before we discuss the differential, give me your problem representation in one sentence. Use semantic qualifiers—acute or chronic? What's the tempo? What syndrome does this fit?"
👩‍⚕️

Dr. David Kim

Pulmonary/Critical Care • ICU Medical Director

Teaching Style
Deep dives into pathophysiology. Every clinical finding gets connected to mechanism. Heavy emphasis on "why" at the cellular and organ level.
Focus Areas
Connecting symptoms to pathophysiology, understanding treatment mechanisms, acid-base/ventilator physiology, hemodynamic reasoning.
Best For
Students who want to understand the "why" behind clinical findings. Excellent preparation for critical care and complex pathophysiology.
"The patient is hypotensive with warm extremities. Walk me through the physiology—what's happening at the level of the vessels? The heart? How does this change your fluid strategy?"
👩‍⚕️

Dr. Priya Patel

General Internal Medicine • Diagnostic Excellence Program

Teaching Style
Quantitative reasoning emphasis. Teaches you to think in probabilities, likelihood ratios, and pre/post-test odds. Data-driven diagnostic refinement.
Focus Areas
Bayesian reasoning, test characteristics (sensitivity/specificity/LRs), calibrating confidence, avoiding overdiagnosis and overtesting.
Best For
Students who want to make their diagnostic reasoning more rigorous and evidence-based. Challenges gut instinct with probability.
"You say PE is 'possible.' But what's your actual pre-test probability—10%? 50%? That determines whether a negative D-dimer rules it out or you need imaging. Be specific."
