🅰️ Hepatitis A (HAV)

Key Features

  • Transmission: Fecal-oral (contaminated food/water, shellfish, travel)
  • Course: Acute self-limited illness. NEVER chronic!
  • Symptoms: Jaundice, fatigue, nausea. Often asymptomatic in children.
  • Diagnosis: Anti-HAV IgM (acute), Anti-HAV IgG (immunity)
  • Treatment: Supportive only
  • Prevention: Vaccine (all children at 1 year, travelers, MSM, chronic liver disease)

Classic vignette: Traveler returns from developing country with jaundice + elevated transaminases. Or outbreak linked to contaminated food handler.

🅱️ Hepatitis B (HBV)

Key Features

  • Genome: Partially dsDNA with reverse transcriptase (only DNA hepatitis virus!)
  • Transmission: Blood, sexual, vertical (perinatal)
  • Chronicity: Age-dependent! 90% if infected at birth, 5% if infected as adult
  • Complications: Cirrhosis, Hepatocellular carcinoma (even without cirrhosis!)
  • Extrahepatic: Polyarteritis nodosa (PAN), membranous nephropathy

Treatment

  • Acute: Usually supportive (95% adults clear spontaneously)
  • Chronic: Tenofovir or entecavir (nucleos(t)ide analogs). Pegylated interferon-α alternative.
  • Goal: Suppress viral replication, prevent cirrhosis/HCC

©️ Hepatitis C (HCV)

Key Features

  • Transmission: Blood (IVDU #1, transfusions pre-1992, needle sticks)
  • Chronicity: ~80% become chronic! (opposite of HBV)
  • Course: Usually asymptomatic acute infection → silent progression to cirrhosis over decades
  • Complications: Cirrhosis, HCC (only with cirrhosis, unlike HBV)
  • Extrahepatic: Cryoglobulinemia, membranoproliferative GN, porphyria cutanea tarda

Diagnosis

  • Anti-HCV: Screening test. Positive = ever exposed (current OR past infection)
  • HCV RNA: Confirms active infection. Also used to monitor treatment.
  • HCV genotype: Guides treatment duration (less important with new DAAs)

Treatment - The DAA Revolution!

Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) have transformed HCV treatment - now >95% cure rates with 8-12 weeks of oral therapy!

  • Sofosbuvir: NS5B polymerase inhibitor (backbone of most regimens)
  • Ledipasvir, Velpatasvir: NS5A inhibitors
  • Common regimens: Sofosbuvir/velpatasvir, Glecaprevir/pibrentasvir
Screen for HCV: All adults 18-79 (one-time), IVDU, HIV+, pre-1992 transfusions, chronic liver disease, incarcerated. HCV is now CURABLE - find and treat!

🔺 Hepatitis D (HDV) - The "Defective" Virus

Key Features

  • Unique: REQUIRES HBV to replicate! Uses HBsAg for its envelope.
  • Transmission: Same as HBV (blood, sexual)
  • Two patterns:
    • Coinfection: HBV + HDV acquired together → usually self-limited
    • Superinfection: HDV infects chronic HBV carrier → accelerated liver damage, fulminant hepatitis
  • Prevention: HBV vaccine protects against HDV (no HBV = no HDV)
HDV superinfection (chronic HBV + new HDV) is WORSE than coinfection. Think of it: in superinfection, there's already established HBV providing endless HBsAg for HDV to use!

🅴 Hepatitis E (HEV)

Key Features

  • Transmission: Fecal-oral (like HAV). Contaminated water, undercooked pork/game.
  • Course: Usually self-limited. No chronic disease (except immunocompromised).
  • DANGER: HIGH mortality in PREGNANT women (~20% in 3rd trimester!)
  • Endemic: Asia, Africa, Mexico, India
  • Vaccine: Available in China, not US
HEV + Pregnancy = DANGER! If a pregnant woman from an endemic area presents with acute hepatitis, think HEV. 20% mortality in third trimester.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • Fecal-oral (vowels): HAV, HEV - acute, self-limited, no chronicity
  • Blood-borne (consonants): HBV, HCV, HDV - can become chronic, HCC risk
  • HBV: Only DNA virus. Serology is complex - know the patterns! Vertical transmission = 90% chronic.
  • HCV: 80% chronic, IVDU transmission, NOW CURABLE with DAAs (>95%)
  • HDV: Requires HBV. Superinfection worse than coinfection.
  • HEV: High mortality in PREGNANCY (20% 3rd trimester)
  • Window period: Only Anti-HBc IgM positive (HBsAg gone, Anti-HBs not yet)
  • Vaccines: HAV (inactivated), HBV (recombinant HBsAg), no HCV vaccine
