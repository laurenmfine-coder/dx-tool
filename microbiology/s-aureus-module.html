🔬 Organism Overview

🍇

Staphylococcus aureus is one of the most clinically significant pathogens, causing a spectrum from minor skin infections to life-threatening invasive disease. The name comes from Greek staphyle (bunch of grapes) and Latin aureus (golden) - describing its cluster morphology and yellow colonies.

S. aureus colonizes approximately 30% of the population asymptomatically (anterior nares, skin), but can cause severe disease when it breaches normal barriers.

Key Distinguishing Feature: Coagulase-positive differentiates S. aureus from coagulase-negative staphylococci (like S. epidermidis). Coagulase converts fibrinogen to fibrin, helping the organism evade phagocytosis.

⚡ Virulence Factors

S. aureus possesses an impressive arsenal of virulence factors that contribute to its pathogenicity:

Surface Proteins & Enzymes

Protein A

Binds Fc region of IgG, preventing opsonization and phagocytosis. "Wrong-way" antibody binding.

Coagulase

Converts fibrinogen → fibrin, creating protective clot around bacteria. Key diagnostic marker.

Catalase

Breaks down H₂O₂ from neutrophil oxidative burst. Distinguishes staph from strep.

MSCRAMMs

Microbial Surface Components Recognizing Adhesive Matrix Molecules - allow binding to host tissues (collagen, fibronectin).

Toxins

α-Toxin (Alpha-hemolysin)

Pore-forming toxin causing cell lysis. Contributes to tissue destruction in skin/soft tissue infections.

Panton-Valentine Leukocidin (PVL)

Associated with CA-MRSA. Causes leukocyte destruction, necrotizing pneumonia, severe skin infections.

TSST-1 (Toxic Shock Syndrome Toxin)

Superantigen causing massive T-cell activation → cytokine storm → shock. Causes menstrual and non-menstrual TSS.

Exfoliative Toxins (A & B)

Serine proteases targeting desmoglein-1. Cause Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS) in children.

Enterotoxins (A-E)

Heat-stable toxins causing food poisoning. Rapid onset (1-6 hours) vomiting and diarrhea. Preformed toxin in food.

Clinical Pearl: PVL-positive strains (often CA-MRSA, like USA300) are associated with more severe skin infections, recurrent abscesses, and necrotizing pneumonia. Think of PVL when you see recurrent furuncles or rapidly progressive pneumonia in young, healthy patients.

🛡️ Resistance Patterns

⚠️ MRSA - Methicillin-Resistant S. aureus

  • Mechanism: mecA gene encodes altered PBP2a with low affinity for beta-lactams
  • Phenotype: Resistant to ALL beta-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems)
  • HA-MRSA: Hospital-associated, often multi-drug resistant
  • CA-MRSA: Community-associated, often PVL+, typically susceptible to TMP-SMX, doxycycline, clindamycin

Treatment Approach

Empiric → Targeted Therapy

1
Empiric Coverage (Before Susceptibilities)

Vancomycin if MRSA risk factors (healthcare exposure, prior MRSA, high local prevalence). Add beta-lactam for MSSA coverage until sensitivities return.

2
MSSA - De-escalate to Beta-Lactam

Nafcillin/Oxacillin (anti-staph penicillins) or Cefazolin are SUPERIOR to vancomycin for MSSA. Lower mortality, better tissue penetration.

3
MRSA - Continue Vancomycin or Alternatives

Vancomycin (target trough AUC/MIC), Daptomycin (not for pneumonia - inactivated by surfactant), Linezolid (good for pneumonia, osteo), Ceftaroline (MRSA-active cephalosporin).

Key Point: For MSSA bacteremia, nafcillin/oxacillin or cefazolin are preferred over vancomycin. Multiple studies show higher mortality with vancomycin for MSSA. "Don't vanco when you can nafcillin."

🧪 Laboratory Diagnosis

Identification

  • Gram stain: Gram-positive cocci in clusters
  • Culture: Golden/yellow colonies on blood agar (beta-hemolytic), mannitol salt agar (yellow = mannitol fermenter)
  • Coagulase test: Positive (tube or slide) - distinguishes from CoNS
  • Catalase: Positive - distinguishes staph from strep
  • Rapid tests: Latex agglutination (Protein A, clumping factor), MALDI-TOF

Susceptibility Testing

  • Cefoxitin disk: Screening for MRSA (mecA detection)
  • D-test: Clindamycin inducible resistance (erm genes). If positive, avoid clindamycin.
  • Vancomycin MIC: VISA (MIC 4-8), VRSA (MIC ≥16) - rare but concerning

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Gram-positive cocci in clusters, coagulase-positive, catalase-positive
  • Virulence: Protein A (anti-opsonin), TSST-1 (superantigen → TSS), PVL (necrotizing infections)
  • MRSA: mecA gene → altered PBP2a → resistant to ALL beta-lactams
  • MSSA treatment: Nafcillin/oxacillin or cefazolin (NOT vancomycin)
  • MRSA treatment: Vancomycin, daptomycin, linezolid, ceftaroline
  • S. aureus bacteremia: NEVER a contaminant - always evaluate for metastatic infection
  • Food poisoning: Preformed enterotoxin, rapid onset (1-6h), no fever
