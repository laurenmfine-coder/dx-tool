🔬 Organism Overview

Legionella pneumophila is a Gram-negative bacillus that lives in freshwater environments and causes two clinical syndromes: Legionnaires' disease (pneumonia) and Pontiac fever (self-limited flu-like illness). It's a facultative intracellular pathogen that replicates within alveolar macrophages.

🔬 Lab Characteristics

Gram-negative rod but stains poorly (use silver stain). Requires special media: BCYE agar with L-cysteine and iron. Won't grow on standard media — this is high-yield!

🦠 Intracellular Pathogen

Survives inside alveolar macrophages by preventing phagosome-lysosome fusion. Cell-mediated immunity (T cells) essential for clearance — immunocompromised at high risk.

📊 Serogroups

Serogroup 1 causes ~80% of disease (detected by urine antigen). Other serogroups and species (L. longbeachae, L. micdadei) cause remainder.

💧 Water Source — Essential for Diagnosis

Legionella thrives in warm water (25-45°C / 77-113°F). Common sources: cooling towers, hot tubs/spas, hospital water systems, decorative fountains, potable water systems (showerheads, faucets). NO person-to-person transmission. Acquired by inhaling contaminated aerosols.

Board Pearl: Legionella requires BCYE agar with cysteine and iron — won't grow on regular media. If you suspect Legionella, must specifically request culture on this media.

Legionnaires' Disease — Clinical Features

📋 Pulmonary

Rapidly progressive pneumonia. Cough (initially dry → productive), dyspnea, chest pain. CXR: patchy infiltrates, often multilobar, can progress rapidly despite antibiotics initially.

🧠 Extrapulmonary Clues

High fever (often >39°C), relative bradycardia, GI symptoms (diarrhea, nausea, abdominal pain), neurologic (confusion, headache), hyponatremia.

🔬 Lab Abnormalities

Hyponatremia (SIADH), elevated LFTs, elevated CK, hematuria, hypophosphatemia. These extrapulmonary findings should raise suspicion!

Classic Board Vignette: Severe pneumonia + GI symptoms (diarrhea) + neurologic changes (confusion) + hyponatremia + recent hotel stay/cruise/hospital = Think Legionella! Order urine antigen.

Risk Factors

  • Smoking — most important risk factor
  • Age >50 years
  • Chronic lung disease (COPD)
  • Immunosuppression (steroids, transplant, malignancy)
  • Recent travel (hotels, cruises)
  • Healthcare exposure (hospital water systems)

🧪 Diagnosis

Test Sensitivity Notes
Urine antigen 70-80% Rapid, specific. Only detects L. pneumophila serogroup 1. Remains positive for weeks even after treatment.
Culture (BCYE) Variable Gold standard. Requires special media. Takes 3-5 days. Detects all species/serogroups.
PCR High Respiratory specimens. Faster than culture. Detects all species. Increasingly available.
DFA 25-75% Direct fluorescent antibody on respiratory specimens. Less sensitive than culture/PCR.
Serology Variable Requires paired sera (acute + convalescent). Retrospective diagnosis only.
Diagnostic Approach: Urine antigen is first-line (fast, specific). If negative but clinical suspicion remains high, send respiratory culture on BCYE and/or PCR. Remember: urine antigen only detects serogroup 1.
⚠️ Public Health Reporting
  • Legionellosis is a reportable disease in most jurisdictions
  • Report to public health for investigation of potential outbreak
  • Identify water source to prevent additional cases
  • Healthcare-associated cases trigger mandatory investigation

💊 Treatment

First-Line Options
  • Levofloxacin 750mg IV/PO daily — Excellent intracellular penetration
  • Azithromycin 500mg IV/PO daily — Good alternative; preferred in pregnancy
  • Moxifloxacin 400mg daily — Alternative fluoroquinolone
Treatment Considerations
  • Duration: 7-10 days for mild-moderate (azithromycin may be 5 days); 14-21 days for severe or immunocompromised
  • IV to PO: Switch when clinically improving and able to tolerate oral
  • Immunocompromised: Longer courses (21 days), fluoroquinolones may be preferred
⚠️ What Doesn't Work
  • Beta-lactams (penicillins, cephalosporins) — Legionella is intracellular; beta-lactams don't penetrate
  • Aminoglycosides — Poor intracellular activity
  • Empiric CAP regimens MUST cover atypicals (including Legionella) with macrolide or fluoroquinolone
Why Beta-Lactams Fail: Legionella lives INSIDE macrophages. Beta-lactams don't penetrate cells well. Fluoroquinolones and macrolides have excellent intracellular penetration — that's why they work.
📝 High-Yield Summary
  • ID: Gram-negative rod, facultative intracellular, requires BCYE agar (cysteine + iron)
  • Source: Contaminated water (cooling towers, hot tubs, hospital water). NO person-to-person.
  • Legionnaires': Severe pneumonia + GI symptoms + confusion + hyponatremia
  • Pontiac fever: Self-limited flu-like illness, no pneumonia
  • Risk factors: Smoking, age >50, COPD, immunosuppression, travel/hotel
  • Diagnosis: Urine antigen (serogroup 1 only), culture on BCYE, PCR
  • Treatment: Fluoroquinolones or azithromycin (NOT beta-lactams — intracellular!)
  • Report: Notifiable disease — public health investigation required
