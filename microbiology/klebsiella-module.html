🔬 Organism Overview

Klebsiella pneumoniae is a Gram-negative, encapsulated bacillus and member of the Enterobacteriaceae family. It's a major cause of hospital-acquired infections and is notorious for its thick polysaccharide capsule and antibiotic resistance.

Lab identification: Lactose fermenter (pink colonies on MacConkey), large mucoid "glistening" colonies due to capsule, non-motile (unlike most other Enterobacteriaceae), IMViC pattern --++ (opposite of E. coli ++--)

Klebsiella is one of the "SHiNE SKiS" encapsulated organisms that cause severe disease in asplenic patients: Strep pneumo, H. flu, Neisseria, E. coli (K1), Salmonella, Klebsiella, in Splenic patients.

⚡ Virulence Factors

Polysaccharide Capsule (K antigen)

MOST IMPORTANT. Antiphagocytic. Creates mucoid colonies and "currant jelly" sputum. >77 K serotypes.

Lipopolysaccharide (LPS)

Endotoxin triggers sepsis cascade. Lipid A activates TLR4 → cytokine storm.

Siderophores

Enterobactin and aerobactin steal iron from host. Essential for growth and virulence.

Fimbriae/Pili

Type 1 and Type 3 fimbriae mediate adhesion to epithelial cells and biofilm formation.

🏥 Clinical Syndromes

SyndromeRisk FactorsKey Features
Pneumonia Alcoholics, diabetics, COPD, elderly, hospitalized "Currant jelly" sputum (bloody, mucoid). Upper lobe cavitary lesions. "Bulging fissure" sign on CXR. High mortality!
UTI Catheterization, instrumentation, hospital #3 cause of UTI (after E. coli, Staph saprophyticus). Often complicated/nosocomial.
Bacteremia/Sepsis ICU, immunocompromised, lines/devices Often from UTI or pneumonia source. High mortality with resistant strains.
Liver Abscess Diabetics (especially in Asia), K1/K2 strains Hypervirulent Klebsiella (hvKp). Can metastasize to eye (endophthalmitis), CNS. "Invasive syndrome."
Wound Infections Surgical wounds, trauma, burns Nosocomial, often polymicrobial
Classic vignette: Alcoholic or diabetic patient with severe pneumonia, "currant jelly" sputum (blood + mucus from capsule), upper lobe involvement with cavitation, "bulging fissure" sign = Think Klebsiella!

Hypervirulent Klebsiella (hvKp)

Community-acquired strains (especially K1, K2 serotypes) cause an "invasive syndrome" primarily in Asia but increasingly worldwide. Features include:

  • Pyogenic liver abscess (often in diabetics)
  • Metastatic spread to eyes (endophthalmitis → blindness), CNS, lungs
  • Hypermucoviscous phenotype ("string test" positive - stretches >5mm)

🛡️ Antibiotic Resistance - A Major Problem!

Klebsiella is one of the ESKAPE pathogens (Enterococcus, Staph aureus, Klebsiella, Acinetobacter, Pseudomonas, Enterobacter) - organisms that "escape" antibiotic killing through multiple resistance mechanisms.

⚠️ Key Resistance Patterns

  • ESBL (Extended-Spectrum β-Lactamase): Hydrolyzes 3rd gen cephalosporins (ceftriaxone, ceftazidime). Treat with carbapenems.
  • KPC (Klebsiella pneumoniae Carbapenemase): Hydrolyzes ALL β-lactams including carbapenems! Major crisis. Treat with ceftazidime-avibactam, meropenem-vaborbactam, or newer agents.
  • Intrinsic AmpC: Chromosomal β-lactamase present in all strains. Resistant to ampicillin, 1st gen cephalosporins.
Klebsiella is INTRINSICALLY resistant to ampicillin (has chromosomal β-lactamase). For empiric gram-negative coverage, need at least a 3rd gen cephalosporin, fluoroquinolone, or broader agent.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Gram-negative rod, lactose fermenter, oxidase-negative, NON-MOTILE, large mucoid colonies (thick capsule)
  • Capsule: Most important virulence factor. Creates "currant jelly" sputum, antiphagocytic
  • Classic presentation: Alcoholic/diabetic with cavitary upper lobe pneumonia + bloody mucoid sputum
  • Other infections: UTI (#3 cause), liver abscess (hvKp), bacteremia, wound infections
  • Resistance: Intrinsically ampicillin-resistant. ESBL and KPC (carbapenemase) are major concerns.
  • ESBL: Treat with carbapenems
  • CRE/KPC: Ceftazidime-avibactam or newer β-lactam/β-lactamase inhibitor combos
  • IMViC: --++ (opposite of E. coli ++--)
