🔬 Organism Overview

Pseudomonas aeruginosa is the quintessential opportunistic pathogen - it rarely causes disease in healthy individuals but is a nightmare in hospitalized and immunocompromised patients. It's notorious for its intrinsic antibiotic resistance and ability to form biofilms.

Key identifying features: oxidase-positive (distinguishes from Enterobacteriaceae), produces blue-green pigments, fruity grape-like odor, and grows at 42°C (helps distinguish from other Pseudomonas species).

Pseudomonas is OXIDASE POSITIVE - this is a key distinguishing feature! Most Enterobacteriaceae (E. coli, Klebsiella, etc.) are oxidase-negative. Think: "Pseudo-positive for oxidase."

🎨 Characteristic Pigments

P. aeruginosa produces several pigments that give its colonies and infected tissues a distinctive blue-green color:

Pyocyanin (Blue)

Generates reactive oxygen species → tissue damage. Also inhibits ciliary function and causes oxidative stress.

Pyoverdine (Green-Yellow)

Siderophore - steals iron from host. Essential for virulence. Fluoresces under UV light!

Together, these pigments produce the classic "blue-green pus" seen in Pseudomonas infections. The combination also creates the distinctive grape-like odor.

⚡ Virulence Factors

Exotoxin A

Inactivates EF-2 (like diphtheria toxin) → inhibits protein synthesis → cell death. Most important toxin for systemic disease.

Phospholipase C

Degrades phospholipids → tissue destruction. Contributes to lung damage in CF patients.

Elastase (LasA, LasB)

Degrades elastin and collagen → tissue invasion. Damages blood vessel walls → hemorrhage.

Alginate (Mucoid Capsule)

Biofilm formation in CF lungs. Protects against phagocytosis and antibiotics. "Mucoid" phenotype.

Endotoxin (LPS)

Lipid A triggers septic shock pathway. Fever, hypotension, DIC in bacteremia.

Type III Secretion System

"Molecular syringe" - injects toxins directly into host cells. ExoS, ExoT, ExoU, ExoY.

Exotoxin A mechanism is HIGH YIELD: It ADP-ribosylates EF-2, inhibiting protein synthesis - SAME mechanism as diphtheria toxin! (Both target EF-2, just different cell entry.)

🏥 Clinical Syndromes

Syndrome Risk Factors Key Features
Pneumonia (VAP) Mechanical ventilation, ICU, prior antibiotics #1 cause of ventilator-associated pneumonia. High mortality (30-40%). Often necrotizing.
CF Lung Infection Cystic fibrosis Chronic colonization, mucoid phenotype (alginate). Leading cause of death in CF.
Burn Wound Infection Burns, wounds Blue-green discharge, rapid tissue invasion. Can lead to sepsis.
Otitis Externa Swimming ("swimmer's ear"), diabetes Ear pain, discharge. Malignant (necrotizing) otitis externa in diabetics - EMERGENCY!
UTI Catheters, instrumentation Catheter-associated UTI. Often hospital-acquired.
Keratitis Contact lens wear Rapidly progressive corneal ulcer. Can perforate in 24-48 hours! Ophthalmologic emergency.
Ecthyma Gangrenosum Neutropenia, sepsis Pathognomonic skin lesion: necrotic ulcer with black eschar. Indicates bacteremia.
Osteomyelitis Diabetic foot, puncture wounds Foot osteomyelitis from puncture through sneaker (nail through rubber sole).
Endocarditis IV drug use Right-sided endocarditis in IVDU. Tricuspid valve involvement.
Hot Tub Folliculitis Hot tubs, whirlpools Pruritic papulopustular rash in swimsuit distribution. Self-limited.

🧠 Pseudomonas Infections Mnemonic

BE PSEUDO

Burns • Ear (otitis externa) • Pneumonia (VAP, CF) • Skin (ecthyma) • Endocarditis (IVDU) • UTI (catheter) • Diabetic foot • Ocular (keratitis)

Malignant otitis externa in diabetics is a life-threatening emergency! It starts as otitis externa but invades skull base → osteomyelitis → cranial nerve palsies (CN VII first). Requires weeks of IV anti-pseudomonal antibiotics. "Malignant" refers to aggressive behavior, not cancer.

💊 Treatment

Anti-Pseudomonal Antibiotics

β-Lactams

Piperacillin-tazobactam, Ceftazidime, Cefepime, Aztreonam, Meropenem (not ertapenem!)

Fluoroquinolones

Ciprofloxacin (best), Levofloxacin. Good lung penetration.

Aminoglycosides

Tobramycin (inhaled for CF), Amikacin. Often used in combination.

Polymyxins

Colistin (polymyxin E). Last resort for MDR strains.

⚠️ Intrinsic & Acquired Resistance

  • Intrinsic resistance: Outer membrane barrier (reduced permeability), AmpC β-lactamase, efflux pumps (MexAB-OprM)
  • NOT active: 1st/2nd gen cephalosporins, ertapenem, most penicillins, TMP-SMX
  • Acquired: ESBLs, metallo-β-lactamases (MBL), porin mutations, upregulated efflux
  • Biofilms: 100-1000x more resistant than planktonic cells!
For serious Pseudomonas infections, combination therapy (β-lactam + aminoglycoside or fluoroquinolone) is often used empirically to prevent resistance emergence. Once susceptibilities return, can often de-escalate to monotherapy.

👥 Special Populations

Cystic Fibrosis

P. aeruginosa is the most important pathogen in CF lung disease. Over time, strains convert to a mucoid phenotype (alginate overproduction) that forms biofilms resistant to antibiotics and immune clearance. This chronic infection drives progressive lung destruction.

  • Inhaled tobramycin (TOBI) for chronic suppression
  • Azithromycin has anti-inflammatory effects on biofilms
  • Aggressive IV therapy for exacerbations

Neutropenic Patients

Pseudomonas bacteremia in neutropenic patients has 30-50% mortality. Look for ecthyma gangrenosum - necrotic skin lesions pathognomonic for Pseudomonas sepsis. Requires immediate broad-spectrum anti-pseudomonal coverage.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Gram-negative rod, OXIDASE POSITIVE, non-lactose fermenter, obligate aerobe, grows at 42°C
  • Pigments: Pyocyanin (blue) + pyoverdine (green) = blue-green pus, grape-like odor
  • Exotoxin A: ADP-ribosylates EF-2 (same as diphtheria toxin) → inhibits protein synthesis
  • Key infections: VAP (#1), CF lungs (mucoid), burns, malignant otitis externa (diabetics), contact lens keratitis, ecthyma gangrenosum (neutropenia), puncture wound osteomyelitis
  • Treatment: Anti-pseudomonal β-lactams (pip-tazo, ceftazidime, cefepime, meropenem), ciprofloxacin, aminoglycosides
  • NOT effective: 1st/2nd gen cephalosporins, ertapenem, ampicillin, TMP-SMX
  • Resistance: Intrinsic (AmpC, efflux, permeability) + acquired. Biofilms are huge problem.
