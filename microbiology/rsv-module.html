RSV (Respiratory Syncytial Virus)

#1 Cause of Bronchiolitis and Viral Pneumonia in Infants

RNA Virus Paramyxovirus Bronchiolitis Infants
Family
Paramyxoviridae
Genome
(-) ssRNA, non-segmented
Peak Age
2-6 months
Treatment
Supportive care

🔬 Virology

🧬 Structure

Enveloped virus with non-segmented negative-sense ssRNA. Fusion (F) protein and attachment (G) protein on surface. F protein causes cell fusion → syncytia (hence the name).

📅 Seasonality

Temperate climates: November-April (peaks January-February). Tropical: year-round or rainy season. Highly predictable annual epidemics.

🔄 Reinfection

Does NOT provide lasting immunity. Reinfection common throughout life. Subsequent infections usually milder. By age 2, nearly all children infected.

Board Pearl: RSV = Paramyxovirus family (along with parainfluenza, measles, mumps). Causes syncytia (multinucleated giant cells) from F protein fusion — this is why it's called "syncytial" virus.

🏥 Clinical Syndromes

Pediatric Disease

👶 Bronchiolitis

#1 cause in infants. Peak 2-6 months. URI prodrome → wheezing, tachypnea, retractions, hypoxia. Small airway inflammation and mucus plugging.

🫁 Viral Pneumonia

More common in very young infants and immunocompromised. Can overlap with bronchiolitis. CXR: hyperinflation, perihilar infiltrates, atelectasis.

🤧 URI / Otitis Media

Older children: usually just cold symptoms. RSV is common cause of acute otitis media with effusion. Less severe than infant disease.

Risk Factors for Severe Pediatric Disease

Risk Factor Why It Matters
Prematurity (<35 weeks) Immature lungs, less maternal antibody transfer
Age <6 months Small airways, immature immune system
Congenital heart disease Especially hemodynamically significant CHD
Chronic lung disease (BPD) Pre-existing respiratory compromise
Immunodeficiency Prolonged viral shedding, severe disease
Neuromuscular disease Impaired cough, secretion clearance

Adult Disease

👴 Elderly Adults

Increasingly recognized as serious pathogen in ≥65 years. Causes pneumonia, COPD/asthma exacerbations. Hospitalization and mortality similar to influenza in this age group.

🏥 Immunocompromised

HSCT recipients at highest risk. Can progress to severe LRTI, ARDS, death. Upper tract infection → lower tract in ~50%. Requires aggressive early treatment.

Clinical Pearl: Don't forget RSV in adults! In elderly and immunocompromised, RSV causes significant morbidity/mortality comparable to flu. Test respiratory panels, not just flu.

🧪 Diagnosis

Test Turnaround Notes
RT-PCR (molecular) Hours Gold standard. High sensitivity/specificity. Part of respiratory pathogen panels.
Rapid antigen detection 15-30 min Good in infants (high viral load). Lower sensitivity in adults/immunocompromised. Negative doesn't rule out.
DFA (direct fluorescent antibody) Hours Moderate sensitivity. Requires adequate specimen and skilled technician.
Viral culture Days Not used for clinical care. Research and surveillance.
When to Test: Testing generally changes management only in hospitalized patients, immunocompromised (where antivirals may be used), or for infection control purposes. In typical outpatient bronchiolitis, clinical diagnosis is usually sufficient.

💊 Treatment

Bronchiolitis in Infants — Supportive Care

  • Supplemental oxygen: Maintain SpO₂ ≥90% (some guidelines say ≥92%)
  • Hydration: IV or NG if not tolerating oral; avoid overhydration (SIADH risk)
  • Nasal suctioning: Gentle bulb suction PRN for nasal congestion
  • Monitoring: Apnea monitoring in high-risk infants (preterm, young age)

⚠️ What NOT to Do in Bronchiolitis (AAP Guidelines)

  • Bronchodilators: Not routinely recommended (no proven benefit)
  • Corticosteroids: Not recommended (don't reduce hospitalizations)
  • Antibiotics: Not indicated unless bacterial superinfection
  • Chest physiotherapy: Not recommended
  • Nebulized hypertonic saline: May consider in hospitalized, not for ED/outpatient

Ribavirin — Limited Indications

  • Mechanism: Nucleoside analog; inhibits viral RNA synthesis
  • Formulations: Inhaled (aerosol) or oral
  • Indications: Consider in HSCT recipients with RSV LRTI; not routine for bronchiolitis
  • Concerns: Teratogenic, expensive, difficult to administer (aerosol), limited efficacy data
Clinical Pearl: For HSCT patients with RSV, early treatment before progression to LRTI improves outcomes. Once lower tract involved, mortality is high despite treatment.

🛡️ Prevention

RSV Immunization — Recent Advances (2023-2024)

  • Nirsevimab (Beyfortus): Long-acting monoclonal antibody for infants; single dose provides season-long protection; recommended for all infants <8 months entering first RSV season
  • Palivizumab (Synagis): Monthly injections during RSV season; now largely replaced by nirsevimab except in specific high-risk groups
  • Maternal RSV vaccine (Abrysvo): Given during pregnancy (32-36 weeks); passive antibody transfer protects newborn
  • Adult RSV vaccines: Abrysvo and Arexvy approved for adults ≥60 years

Infection Control

  • Transmission: Contact and large droplet (not truly airborne)
  • Precautions: Contact + droplet precautions for hospitalized patients
  • Hand hygiene: Most important intervention; virus survives on hands ~25 min
  • Environmental: Survives on surfaces for hours; cleaning important
2024 Update: Nirsevimab has transformed RSV prevention — one dose protects infants for entire RSV season. Maternal vaccination is alternative if nirsevimab unavailable. Both options NOT both needed.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Paramyxovirus, (-) ssRNA, non-segmented, F protein causes syncytia
  • Epidemiology: #1 cause of bronchiolitis and infant pneumonia; peak 2-6 months; November-April
  • Bronchiolitis: URI → wheezing, tachypnea, retractions, hypoxia; supportive care only
  • High-risk: Premature, <6 months, CHD, CLD, immunocompromised, elderly
  • DON'T use: Bronchodilators, steroids, antibiotics in typical bronchiolitis
  • Adults: Significant cause of pneumonia in elderly and immunocompromised
  • Diagnosis: RT-PCR best; rapid antigen OK in infants
  • Prevention: Nirsevimab (infants), maternal vaccine, adult vaccines (≥60)
