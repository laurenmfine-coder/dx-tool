Clostridioides difficile

The Antibiotic-Associated Diarrhea Culprit

Gram-Positive Rod Spore-Forming Obligate Anaerobe Toxin-Mediated

🔬 Organism Characteristics

Clostridioides difficile (formerly Clostridium difficile) is a Gram-positive, spore-forming, obligate anaerobe that causes antibiotic-associated diarrhea and pseudomembranous colitis. It's the #1 cause of healthcare-associated infectious diarrhea.

  • Morphology: Gram-positive rod, spore-forming
  • Oxygen: Obligate anaerobe
  • Spores: Highly resistant to heat, alcohol, drying - persist in environment!
  • Transmission: Fecal-oral, healthcare settings, contaminated surfaces
C. diff spores are RESISTANT to alcohol-based hand sanitizers! This is why soap and water handwashing is required after caring for C. diff patients. Alcohol doesn't kill spores.

⚡ Pathogenesis & Toxins

C. diff causes disease through toxin-mediated damage, not tissue invasion. The key toxins are:

ToxinMechanismEffect
Toxin A (TcdA) Glucosyltransferase - inactivates Rho GTPases "Enterotoxin" - causes fluid secretion, inflammation, neutrophil recruitment
Toxin B (TcdB) Same mechanism, more potent "Cytotoxin" - causes cytoskeletal disruption, cell death, more tissue damage
Binary Toxin (CDT) ADP-ribosylates actin Present in hypervirulent strains (NAP1/B1/027). Increases severity.

How Antibiotics Cause C. diff

  1. Antibiotics disrupt normal gut flora ("dysbiosis")
  2. C. diff spores (ingested or already colonizing) germinate
  3. C. diff proliferates in the absence of competition
  4. Toxins A and B damage colonic epithelium → inflammation, pseudomembranes
Both toxins inactivate Rho GTPases by glucosylation → disrupts actin cytoskeleton → cell rounding, tight junction disruption, apoptosis. This causes the watery diarrhea and pseudomembrane formation.

⚠️ Risk Factors

  • Antibiotics: Clindamycin (classic), fluoroquinolones (NAP1 strain!), cephalosporins, carbapenems, penicillins - virtually ANY antibiotic
  • Healthcare exposure: Hospitalization, nursing homes, long-term care
  • Age: >65 years
  • PPI/H2 blockers: Controversial but associated
  • Immunosuppression: Chemotherapy, transplant
  • GI surgery/procedures: NG tubes, enemas
  • Chronic illness: Renal disease, IBD
The NAP1/BI/027 hypervirulent strain emerged in early 2000s associated with fluoroquinolone use. It produces more toxin and binary toxin → more severe disease, higher mortality, more recurrences.

🏥 Clinical Presentation

Spectrum of Disease

SeverityFeaturesLabs
Non-severe Watery diarrhea (≥3 stools/day), cramping, low-grade fever WBC <15,000, Cr <1.5x baseline
Severe Profuse diarrhea, abdominal pain, fever, dehydration WBC ≥15,000 OR Cr ≥1.5x baseline
Fulminant Hypotension, ileus, megacolon, mental status changes Lactate >2.2, WBC >20,000, organ failure

🚨 Toxic Megacolon - Surgical Emergency!

  • Colonic dilation >6 cm on imaging
  • Systemic toxicity (fever, tachycardia, hypotension)
  • Paradoxically, diarrhea may STOP (ileus)
  • Risk of perforation → emergent colectomy

Pseudomembranous Colitis

Classic finding on colonoscopy: yellow-white plaques ("pseudomembranes") on inflamed, friable colonic mucosa. Composed of fibrin, mucus, inflammatory cells, and necrotic epithelium.

🧪 Diagnosis

Only test symptomatic patients! Don't test formed stools or asymptomatic patients (colonization is common).

TestDetectsPros/Cons
NAAT (PCR) Toxin genes Very sensitive, fast. May detect colonization (not active disease).
Toxin EIA Toxins A and B Specific for active disease. Less sensitive.
GDH (glutamate dehydrogenase) C. diff enzyme Sensitive screening. Not specific (doesn't distinguish toxigenic).
Two-step algorithm GDH/NAAT → Toxin EIA Screen with sensitive test, confirm with specific test. Preferred approach.
Many labs use a two-step algorithm: Screen with GDH or NAAT (sensitive), then confirm with toxin EIA (specific). Discordant results (PCR+/Toxin-) may represent colonization vs early/resolving infection.

💊 Treatment

Step 1: STOP the Offending Antibiotic (if possible!)

This alone may resolve mild cases. Always discontinue unnecessary antibiotics.

Oral vancomycin for C. diff (NOT IV!) - it's not absorbed, so it stays in the GI tract where C. diff lives. IV vancomycin doesn't reach the colon. Fidaxomicin is now preferred (lower recurrence).

Recurrent CDI

  • 2nd recurrence or beyond: Consider fecal microbiota transplant (FMT) - ~90% cure rate!
  • Bezlotoxumab: Monoclonal antibody against toxin B, reduces recurrence
  • Vancomycin taper: 125mg QID x 10-14d → BID x 7d → daily x 7d → every 2-3 days x 2-8 weeks

🛡️ Prevention

  • Hand hygiene: SOAP AND WATER (not just alcohol) - removes spores
  • Contact precautions: Gown and gloves, private room
  • Environmental cleaning: Bleach (sporicidal)
  • Antibiotic stewardship: Limit unnecessary antibiotics, narrow spectrum when possible
  • Avoid PPIs: Unless clearly indicated

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • Organism: Gram-positive, spore-forming, obligate anaerobe
  • Toxins: Toxin A (enterotoxin) + Toxin B (cytotoxin) → inactivate Rho GTPases → cytoskeletal damage
  • Risk factors: Antibiotics (#1!), hospitalization, age >65, PPIs
  • High-risk antibiotics: Clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins
  • Diagnosis: Two-step algorithm (GDH/PCR → toxin EIA). Only test liquid stools!
  • Treatment: Fidaxomicin (preferred) or ORAL vancomycin. Fulminant = high-dose PO vanco + IV metronidazole.
  • Recurrence: FMT highly effective for multiple recurrences
  • Prevention: SOAP AND WATER (alcohol doesn't kill spores), contact precautions, antibiotic stewardship
