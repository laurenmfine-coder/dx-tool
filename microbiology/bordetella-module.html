🔬 Organism Overview

Bordetella pertussis causes whooping cough. Highly contagious (R0 ~15-17). Humans are only reservoir. Immunity wanes 5-10 years after vaccination.

🔬 Lab

Bordet-Gengou or Regan-Lowe media. PCR now preferred for diagnosis.

☠️ Toxins

Pertussis toxin: ADP-ribosylates Gi → lymphocytosis. Tracheal cytotoxin damages cilia.

Board Pearl: Pertussis toxin causes marked lymphocytosis (WBC 20,000-50,000+) — classic finding in infants!

📊 Clinical Stages

Stage 1: Catarrhal (1-2 weeks)

Most contagious! Looks like common cold. Antibiotics most effective here.

Stage 2: Paroxysmal (2-8 weeks)

Classic whoop: Paroxysms of coughing + inspiratory whoop. Post-tussive vomiting. Infants may have apnea instead.

Stage 3: Convalescent (weeks-months)

Gradual improvement. Total duration 6-10 weeks ("100-day cough").

Age-Specific Presentations

AgePresentation
InfantsApnea, cyanosis, may lack whoop. Lymphocytosis. Highest mortality.
ChildrenClassic paroxysmal cough with whoop, post-tussive vomiting.
AdultsProlonged cough (>2 weeks), atypical. Source for infant infection.
Classic Vignette: Infant with paroxysmal cough, post-tussive vomiting, apnea, WBC 45,000 with 70% lymphocytes. Mom had prolonged cough. = Pertussis!

💊 Treatment

First-Line: Macrolides

  • Azithromycin: 10 mg/kg day 1, then 5 mg/kg days 2-5 (preferred in infants)
  • Clarithromycin: 15 mg/kg/day divided BID × 7 days
  • Alternative: TMP-SMX if macrolide allergy
Timing: Antibiotics shorten illness only if given in catarrhal stage. Once paroxysmal, antibiotics reduce transmission but don't affect clinical course.

💉 Prevention

Vaccines

  • DTaP: Children. 5-dose series (2, 4, 6, 15-18 months, 4-6 years)
  • Tdap: Adolescents/adults. Single dose at 11-12, then during EACH pregnancy (27-36 weeks)
  • Cocooning: Vaccinate all close contacts of newborns
Why adults get pertussis: Immunity wanes 5-10 years after vaccination. Adults become reservoir for infant infection.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • Stages: Catarrhal (contagious) → Paroxysmal (whoop) → Convalescent
  • Infants: Apnea, lymphocytosis (WBC 20,000-50,000+), highest risk
  • Adults: Prolonged cough, atypical, source for infants
  • Treatment: Azithromycin (preferred)
  • Prevention: DTaP (kids), Tdap (adults, each pregnancy)
