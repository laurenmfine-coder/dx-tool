🔬 Organism Overview

🫁

Key Characteristics

  • No cell wall → Cannot Gram stain, resistant to beta-lactams
  • Smallest free-living organism capable of self-replication
  • Requires sterols for membrane stability (unique among bacteria)
  • Facultative anaerobe, grows slowly on special media
  • "Fried egg" colonies on Eaton agar
Board Pearl: M. pneumoniae lacks a cell wall → does NOT Gram stain → beta-lactams are INEFFECTIVE. Must use macrolides, doxycycline, or fluoroquinolones.

🏥 Clinical Syndromes

Primary: Atypical "Walking" Pneumonia

📋 Classic Presentation

Gradual onset over 3-5 days. Nonproductive cough (later may become productive). Low-grade fever, malaise, headache. Patient looks "better than expected" for CXR findings.

🫁 CXR Pattern

Patchy interstitial/reticulonodular infiltrates. Often worse than clinical exam suggests. May have small pleural effusions. Lower lobe predominance common.

👥 Epidemiology

Peak ages 5-20 years. Spreads in closed populations (schools, military barracks, camps). Incubation 2-3 weeks. Outbreaks every 4-7 years.

Extrapulmonary Manifestations

System Manifestation Notes
Dermatologic Erythema multiforme, Stevens-Johnson syndrome Most common infectious cause of SJS in children
Hematologic Cold agglutinin hemolytic anemia IgM autoantibodies to RBC I antigen
Neurologic Encephalitis, Guillain-Barré, transverse myelitis Rare but serious; may occur after respiratory illness
Cardiac Myocarditis, pericarditis Usually mild and self-limited
MSK Arthralgias, myalgias Common during acute illness
Clinical Pearl: Think Mycoplasma when you see pneumonia + cold agglutinin hemolytic anemia OR pneumonia + erythema multiforme/SJS in a young patient.

🧪 Diagnosis

🔬 PCR (Preferred)

Respiratory PCR is most sensitive and specific. Results in hours. Can detect in nasopharyngeal swab or sputum. Gold standard for diagnosis.

🩸 Serology

IgM appears week 1, peaks week 3-4. Requires paired sera (acute + convalescent) for confirmation. Less useful acutely.

❄️ Cold Agglutinins

Present in ~50% of cases. Bedside test: blood clumps when cooled. NOT specific (also in EBV, CMV). Supportive but not diagnostic.

Practical Tip: In most CAP cases, empiric treatment covers atypicals without specific testing. Reserve Mycoplasma PCR for: treatment failures, severe/complicated cases, outbreak investigation, or extrapulmonary manifestations.

💊 Treatment

First-Line Options

  • Azithromycin 500mg day 1, then 250mg days 2-5 (Z-pack) — Most commonly used
  • Doxycycline 100mg BID × 7-14 days — Avoid in children <8 years
  • Levofloxacin 750mg daily × 5 days — Reserve for treatment failures

⚠️ Macrolide Resistance

  • Increasing globally (>90% in some Asian countries, ~10-15% in US)
  • Point mutations in 23S rRNA
  • If no improvement in 48-72 hours on azithromycin → switch to doxycycline or fluoroquinolone
  • Resistance does NOT affect doxycycline or fluoroquinolones
Why beta-lactams fail: M. pneumoniae has NO CELL WALL → no peptidoglycan target → penicillins, cephalosporins, and carbapenems are completely ineffective.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Smallest free-living organism, NO cell wall, cannot Gram stain
  • Disease: "Walking pneumonia" — gradual onset, dry cough, looks better than CXR
  • Demographics: Ages 5-20, outbreaks in closed populations
  • CXR: Interstitial/patchy infiltrates, worse than clinical appearance suggests
  • Extras: Cold agglutinin hemolytic anemia, erythema multiforme, SJS
  • Diagnosis: PCR preferred; cold agglutinins supportive but not specific
  • Treatment: Macrolides (azithromycin), doxycycline, or fluoroquinolones
  • Key point: Beta-lactams DON'T work (no cell wall)
