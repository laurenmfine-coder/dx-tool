Herpes Viruses: HSV & VZV

dsDNA Viruses That Establish Lifelong Latency

🧬 Herpesviridae Family Overview

All human herpes viruses share key features: dsDNA genome, enveloped, icosahedral nucleocapsid, and the ability to establish latent infection with periodic reactivation.

All herpes viruses are ENVELOPED dsDNA viruses. Envelope = acquired from host nuclear membrane. They ALL establish LATENCY and can reactivate. "Herpes is forever!"

🔥 HSV-1 (Herpes Simplex Virus Type 1)

Key Features

  • Transmission: Direct contact with infected secretions (saliva, lesions)
  • Primary infection: Often childhood, may be asymptomatic
  • Latency: Trigeminal ganglia → reactivates as "cold sores"
  • Reactivation triggers: Stress, sunlight, fever, immunosuppression

Clinical Syndromes

SyndromeFeaturesTreatment
Gingivostomatitis Primary infection. Painful vesicles/ulcers on oral mucosa, gums, lips. Fever, lymphadenopathy. Acyclovir (severe cases)
Herpes labialis "Cold sores" - recurrent vesicles at lip vermilion border. Prodrome of tingling. Topical/oral acyclovir, valacyclovir
Herpetic whitlow Painful vesicles on finger. Healthcare workers, thumb-sucking children. Oral acyclovir. Do NOT incise (spreads!)
Herpes keratitis Dendritic corneal ulcers on fluorescein exam. Vision-threatening! Topical trifluridine or ganciclovir. NO steroids alone!
HSV encephalitis Most common sporadic viral encephalitis. Temporal lobe necrosis → fever, altered mental status, seizures, personality changes. IV acyclovir IMMEDIATELY. Don't wait for confirmation!

🚨 HSV Encephalitis Emergency!

  • Most common cause of SPORADIC viral encephalitis in US
  • Affects TEMPORAL lobes (hemorrhagic necrosis)
  • Presents: Fever, headache, altered mental status, seizures, personality changes
  • MRI: Temporal lobe enhancement
  • CSF: Lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein, RBCs (hemorrhage)
  • TREAT EMPIRICALLY with IV acyclovir - don't wait for PCR!

💜 HSV-2 (Herpes Simplex Virus Type 2)

Key Features

  • Transmission: Sexual contact (STI)
  • Primary infection: Painful genital vesicles/ulcers, dysuria, inguinal lymphadenopathy
  • Latency: Sacral ganglia → recurrent genital herpes
  • Asymptomatic shedding: Can transmit even without visible lesions!

Clinical Syndromes

  • Primary genital herpes: Painful grouped vesicles on erythematous base → ulcerate → crust. Severe systemic symptoms. Lasts 2-3 weeks.
  • Recurrent genital herpes: Milder, shorter duration (5-10 days). Prodrome of tingling/burning. Less frequent over time.
  • Neonatal herpes: Acquired during delivery through infected birth canal. HIGH morbidity/mortality!

🚨 Neonatal Herpes - Life-Threatening!

  • Highest risk: Mother with PRIMARY infection at delivery (30-50% transmission)
  • Three forms: Skin-eye-mouth (SEM), CNS disease, disseminated
  • Presents at 1-3 weeks of life
  • Treatment: IV acyclovir × 21 days
  • Prevention: C-section if active genital lesions at delivery
HSV-1 is increasingly causing genital herpes (via oral sex), while HSV-2 rarely causes oral disease. Either can cause either location! However, HSV-2 genital recurs more frequently than HSV-1 genital.

🐔 VZV (Varicella-Zoster Virus / HHV-3)

Key Features

  • Transmission: Respiratory droplets (highly contagious!) and contact with lesions
  • Primary infection: Varicella (chickenpox)
  • Latency: Dorsal root ganglia (all along spinal cord)
  • Reactivation: Herpes zoster (shingles)

Varicella (Chickenpox)

  • Prodrome: Fever, malaise 1-2 days before rash
  • Rash: Starts on trunk → spreads centrifugally to face/extremities
  • Classic feature: Lesions in ALL STAGES simultaneously (macules, papules, vesicles, pustules, crusts)
  • Description: "Dewdrops on rose petals" - clear vesicles on erythematous base
  • Contagious: 1-2 days before rash until all lesions crusted
Chickenpox vs Smallpox: Chickenpox has lesions in DIFFERENT stages (pleomorphic) starting on trunk. Smallpox had lesions in the SAME stage starting on face/extremities. (Smallpox is eradicated!)

Herpes Zoster (Shingles)

  • Pathophysiology: VZV reactivates from dorsal root ganglion → travels down sensory nerve
  • Presentation: Painful, unilateral, dermatomal vesicular rash. DOES NOT cross midline.
  • Common dermatomes: Thoracic (T3-L2), trigeminal (V1 - ophthalmic zoster!)
  • Prodrome: Pain/tingling/burning 2-3 days before rash appears

Zoster Complications

ComplicationFeatures
Postherpetic neuralgia (PHN)Most common. Pain persists >90 days after rash. More common in elderly. Treat: gabapentin, pregabalin, TCAs, lidocaine patch.
Herpes zoster ophthalmicusV1 (ophthalmic branch) involvement. Vesicles on nose tip (Hutchinson sign) = cornea at risk! Ophthalmology emergency.
Ramsay Hunt syndromeVZV reactivation in geniculate ganglion. Facial palsy + ear vesicles + hearing loss/vertigo. Treat: acyclovir + steroids.
Disseminated zosterImmunocompromised. >20 lesions outside primary dermatome. Can involve visceral organs.
Hutchinson sign: Vesicles on the TIP of the nose indicates nasociliary nerve involvement (V1) → HIGH risk of eye involvement in zoster ophthalmicus. Urgent ophthalmology consult!

💊 Treatment & Prevention

Antiviral Therapy

DrugMechanismUses
AcyclovirGuanosine analog → inhibits viral DNA polymerase. Requires viral thymidine kinase for activation (selective toxicity!)HSV (all forms), VZV, prophylaxis
ValacyclovirProdrug of acyclovir. Better oral bioavailability.Same as acyclovir. More convenient dosing.
FamciclovirProdrug of penciclovirHSV, VZV (alternative)
Acyclovir requires viral thymidine kinase (TK) for initial phosphorylation → then cellular kinases complete activation → inhibits viral DNA pol. This is why it's selectively toxic to infected cells! Resistance = TK mutations.

Vaccines

  • Varicella vaccine (Varivax): Live attenuated. 2 doses (12-15 months, 4-6 years). ~95% effective.
  • Zoster vaccine (Shingrix): Recombinant subunit (NOT live!). 2 doses for adults ≥50. >90% effective at preventing shingles and PHN. Preferred over old Zostavax.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • All herpes viruses: Enveloped dsDNA, establish LATENCY, reactivate
  • HSV-1: Oral herpes, keratitis, encephalitis (temporal lobe!). Latent in trigeminal ganglia.
  • HSV-2: Genital herpes, neonatal herpes. Latent in sacral ganglia.
  • VZV: Chickenpox (lesions in ALL stages), shingles (dermatomal, unilateral). Latent in dorsal root ganglia.
  • HSV encephalitis: Temporal lobe involvement. Treat IMMEDIATELY with IV acyclovir!
  • Neonatal herpes: Highest risk with maternal PRIMARY infection at delivery. C-section if active lesions.
  • Hutchinson sign: Nose tip vesicles in zoster = V1 = eye at risk!
  • PHN: Pain >90 days after zoster rash. Prevent with Shingrix vaccine ≥50yo.
  • Acyclovir MOA: Requires viral TK for activation → selective for infected cells
