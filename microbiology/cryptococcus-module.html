🔬 Organism Overview

Cryptococcus neoformans is an encapsulated yeast found worldwide in soil contaminated with pigeon droppings. It is the most common cause of fungal meningitis globally and a major AIDS-defining opportunistic infection. The polysaccharide capsule is its primary virulence factor.

🔬 Morphology

Round/oval yeast, 5-10 μm with narrow-based budding. Surrounded by large polysaccharide capsule (can be 2-3× cell diameter). NOT dimorphic — always yeast form.

🐦 Environmental Source

Pigeon/bird droppings, soil, eucalyptus trees. Inhalation of desiccated yeast cells or spores → primary pulmonary infection → dissemination.

🛡️ Capsule = Key Virulence

Polysaccharide capsule (glucuronoxylomannan) inhibits phagocytosis, complement, and antibody function. Basis of antigen detection tests.

💊

The "Only Encapsulated Yeast"

Cryptococcus is the only encapsulated fungus of clinical importance. The capsule is visualized with India ink (negative staining — capsule appears as clear halo) or mucicarmine stain (red capsule).

Species Comparison

Species Host Geography Source
C. neoformans Immunocompromised (HIV CD4 <100) Worldwide Pigeon droppings, soil
C. gattii Immunocompetent (can affect normal hosts) Pacific Northwest, Australia, tropics Eucalyptus trees, soil
Board Pearl: C. neoformans = immunocompromised (AIDS). C. gattii = can infect immunocompetent (think Pacific Northwest outbreak). Both cause meningitis and pulmonary disease.

🏥 Clinical Syndromes

Cryptococcal Meningitis (Most Important)

📋 Presentation

Subacute onset over days to weeks. Headache (most common), fever, malaise, altered mental status. Classic meningeal signs often ABSENT (minimal inflammation due to capsule).

👁️ Elevated ICP

Very common — headache, nausea, vomiting, papilledema, CN VI palsy. Opening pressure often >25 cm H₂O. Major cause of morbidity/mortality. Requires aggressive management.

🧠 CSF Findings

Lymphocytic pleocytosis (may be minimal in AIDS), elevated protein, LOW glucose. India ink positive in 50-75% (higher in AIDS due to high fungal burden).

Other Manifestations

🫁 Pulmonary

Primary site of infection. Often asymptomatic or mild. Nodules, infiltrates, cavities, pleural effusions. May be incidental finding on imaging.

🧴 Cutaneous

Disseminated disease marker. Papules, pustules, nodules that may ulcerate. Molluscum-like lesions (umbilicated) in AIDS patients. Always indicates dissemination — look for CNS involvement!

🦴 Other Sites

Bone (osteomyelitis), prostate (reservoir for relapse — poor drug penetration), eye (chorioretinitis), can affect virtually any organ in disseminated disease.

Clinical Pearl: In AIDS patients with skin lesions that look like molluscum contagiosum — think Cryptococcus! These patients need LP to rule out meningitis even if asymptomatic.

🧪 Diagnosis

Test Specimen Notes
Cryptococcal Antigen (CrAg) CSF, serum Detects capsular polysaccharide. Sensitivity/specificity >95%. Titers correlate with burden and can monitor response. BEST TEST.
India Ink CSF Negative staining shows clear halo (capsule). 50-75% sensitive. Higher yield in AIDS (high organism burden). Positive = diagnostic.
Culture CSF, blood, sputum Grows on Sabouraud agar. Definitive diagnosis. Also useful for species ID and susceptibility.
Histopathology Tissue Mucicarmine stain: red capsule. PAS, GMS also useful. Narrow-based budding yeast.
Serum CrAg Screening: In HIV patients with CD4 <100, screen with serum CrAg. If positive, perform LP to evaluate for meningitis before starting ART (to assess for asymptomatic meningitis and prevent IRIS).

⚠️ LP and Elevated ICP

  • Opening pressure >25 cm H₂O in >50% of AIDS patients with cryptococcal meningitis
  • Therapeutic LP: Remove enough CSF to reduce pressure by 50% or to <20 cm H₂O
  • May need daily LPs or lumbar drain for refractory elevated ICP
  • Do NOT avoid LP for fear of herniation — elevated ICP kills; drainage saves lives

💊 Treatment

Cryptococcal Meningitis (HIV/AIDS) — Three Phases

  • Induction (2 weeks): Liposomal Amphotericin B (3-4 mg/kg/day) + Flucytosine (100 mg/kg/day divided q6h)
  • Consolidation (8 weeks): Fluconazole 400-800 mg/day
  • Maintenance/Secondary Prophylaxis: Fluconazole 200 mg/day until CD4 >100 for ≥3 months on ART + undetectable viral load

ICP Management (Critical!)

  • Therapeutic LP: Daily LPs to maintain OP <20 cm H₂O or reduce by ≥50%
  • Lumbar drain: If daily LPs inadequate
  • VP shunt: Last resort for refractory cases
  • Avoid: Corticosteroids (worsen outcomes), mannitol/acetazolamide (ineffective)

Pulmonary Cryptococcosis

  • Mild-moderate, immunocompetent: Fluconazole 400 mg/day × 6-12 months
  • Severe or immunocompromised: Treat as disseminated (same as meningitis)
  • Always: LP to rule out CNS involvement, even if asymptomatic!
ART Timing: Delay ART for 4-6 weeks after starting antifungal therapy to reduce risk of IRIS (immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome). IRIS can cause paradoxical worsening of meningitis.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Encapsulated yeast, narrow-based budding, pigeon droppings/soil exposure
  • Capsule: Main virulence factor; basis of CrAg test; visualized with India ink or mucicarmine
  • Risk: HIV/AIDS with CD4 <100 (C. neoformans); immunocompetent can get C. gattii
  • Meningitis: Subacute onset, headache, minimal meningeal signs, ELEVATED ICP
  • Diagnosis: CrAg (serum/CSF) best test; India ink, culture, LP with opening pressure
  • Treatment: Induction (AmB + flucytosine) → Consolidation (fluconazole) → Maintenance
  • ICP management: Therapeutic LPs critical — may need daily drainage
  • ART timing: Delay 4-6 weeks to prevent IRIS
