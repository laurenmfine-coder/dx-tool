🔬 Organism Overview

Coccidioides (C. immitis and C. posadasii) is a dimorphic fungus endemic to the desert Southwest US, Mexico, and Central/South America. Also known as "Valley Fever" (San Joaquin Valley, CA) or "Desert Rheumatism." Cases increasing due to climate change, construction, and population growth in endemic areas.

🌡️ Dimorphism — UNIQUE Form

Mold at 25°C: Septate hyphae with arthroconidia (barrel-shaped, highly infectious). Tissue at 37°C: Large SPHERULES (20-60 μm) filled with endospores — pathognomonic!

🏜️ Environmental Source

Desert soil (alkaline, sandy). Infection from inhaling arthroconidia, often after soil disturbance (construction, earthquakes, dust storms).

⚠️ Highly Infectious

Arthroconidia are extremely infectious — BSL-3 pathogen. Lab workers at high risk. Single spore can cause infection. NO person-to-person transmission.

🗺️ Geographic Distribution

Highly endemic: Arizona (especially Phoenix, Tucson), California (San Joaquin Valley), Southern Nevada, New Mexico, West Texas. Also Mexico, Central and South America. Incidence increasing — now ~20,000 cases/year in US. Think "Lower Sonoran Life Zone" — hot, dry, low elevation desert.

Board Pearl: Coccidioides has a UNIQUE tissue form: large spherules filled with endospores — NOT yeasts like other dimorphic fungi. This is the most distinctive feature on histopath!

📊 Clinical Syndromes

Primary Pulmonary Coccidioidomycosis

🫁 Asymptomatic (60%)

Majority of infections are subclinical. May develop positive skin test or serology. Sometimes discovered as incidental pulmonary nodule on imaging.

🤒 Acute Pneumonia (40%)

"Valley Fever" — cough, fever, chest pain, fatigue, night sweats. Onset 1-3 weeks after exposure. Usually self-limited in 2-6 weeks. CXR: infiltrates, hilar LAD, pleural effusion.

🦵 "Desert Rheumatism"

Arthralgias, erythema nodosum (painful shin nodules), erythema multiforme. Immune-mediated; actually indicates GOOD prognosis — intact cellular immunity!

Complicated/Disseminated Disease

Form Features Notes
Chronic Pulmonary Persistent symptoms >6 weeks, cavitary disease Thin-walled cavities may rupture → pneumothorax/empyema
Disseminated Spread beyond lungs to skin, bone, CNS, other organs Risk: immunosuppressed, Filipino/African ancestry, pregnancy (3rd trimester), diabetes
Meningitis Most serious complication; high morbidity/mortality Requires LIFELONG fluconazole — relapses if stopped
Skeletal Osteomyelitis, septic arthritis Vertebrae, ribs, skull common sites
⚠️ Risk Factors for Dissemination
  • Immunosuppression: HIV/AIDS, transplant, TNF inhibitors, high-dose steroids
  • Race/Ethnicity: Filipino and African ancestry (10× higher risk of dissemination)
  • Pregnancy: Especially 3rd trimester and immediate postpartum
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Age extremes: Very young or elderly
Classic Board Vignette: Recent travel to Arizona/California desert + flu-like illness + erythema nodosum (painful shin nodules) + hilar lymphadenopathy = Think Coccidioidomycosis!

🧪 Diagnosis

Test Use Notes
Serology (EIA, ID, CF) Primary diagnostic method IgM appears first (acute); IgG (CF titer) correlates with disease severity. Rising CF titer = dissemination.
Culture Definitive diagnosis Grows in 1-3 weeks. HIGHLY INFECTIOUS — notify lab! BSL-3 handling required.
Histopathology Tissue diagnosis Pathognomonic: large SPHERULES (20-60 μm) filled with endospores. GMS, H&E stains.
Antigen (urine, serum) Severe/disseminated Cross-reacts with Histoplasma. Useful for monitoring response.
CSF Analysis (meningitis) CNS disease Lymphocytic pleocytosis, low glucose, elevated protein. CSF complement-fixing antibody positive.
Serology Pearls: IgM = acute infection. IgG (CF titer) = disease burden. CF titer ≥1:16 suggests dissemination risk. Rising titer = worsening. Falling titer = response to therapy.
💊 Treatment
Uncomplicated Primary Pulmonary
  • Most cases: No treatment needed — self-limited in immunocompetent
  • Consider treatment if: Symptoms >2 months, severe disease, high CF titer (≥1:16), risk factors for dissemination, immunocompromised
  • If treating: Fluconazole 400mg daily × 3-6 months
Disseminated (Non-CNS)
  • Mild-moderate: Fluconazole 400-800mg daily or Itraconazole 200mg BID
  • Severe: Liposomal Amphotericin B induction → azole consolidation
  • Duration: 12+ months; often years; some need lifelong
Coccidioidal Meningitis — LIFELONG TREATMENT
  • First-line: Fluconazole 400-800mg daily — LIFELONG (relapses if stopped)
  • Alternative: Intrathecal Amphotericin B for refractory cases
  • Hydrocephalus: Common complication — may need VP shunt
  • Never stop therapy — relapse rate >90% if discontinued
⚠️ Why Fluconazole (Not Itraconazole) for Meningitis
  • Fluconazole has excellent CNS penetration
  • Itraconazole has poor CNS penetration — NOT effective for meningitis
  • Voriconazole and posaconazole are alternatives if fluconazole fails
📝 High-Yield Summary
  • ID: Dimorphic fungus; tissue form = SPHERULES with endospores (unique!)
  • Geography: Desert Southwest (Arizona, California San Joaquin Valley)
  • Source: Desert soil; infection from inhaling arthroconidia (dust storms, construction)
  • Primary: "Valley Fever" — flu-like + erythema nodosum + hilar LAD; usually self-limited
  • Dissemination risk: Filipino/African ancestry, immunosuppressed, pregnant, diabetic
  • Diagnosis: Serology (CF titer correlates with severity); histopath shows spherules
  • Treatment: Uncomplicated = observe or fluconazole; Severe = AmB → azole
  • Meningitis: LIFELONG fluconazole — will relapse if stopped
