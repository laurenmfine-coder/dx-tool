🔬 Organism Overview

Mycobacterium tuberculosis is the causative agent of tuberculosis (TB), one of the world's deadliest infectious diseases. It's characterized by its unique cell wall, slow growth, and ability to persist within macrophages.

Unique Cell Wall Structure

The mycobacterial cell wall is unlike any other bacteria - it contains:

  • Mycolic acids: Long-chain fatty acids (60-90 carbons) → hydrophobic, waxy surface
  • Arabinogalactan: Links mycolic acids to peptidoglycan
  • Cord factor (trehalose dimycolate): Virulence factor, causes "serpentine cording" in culture
  • LAM (lipoarabinomannan): Induces TNF-α, inhibits macrophage activation
The waxy mycolic acid cell wall explains TB's unique features: (1) Doesn't Gram stain - needs ACID-FAST stain (Ziehl-Neelsen); (2) SLOW growth (doubling time 15-20 hours); (3) Resistant to drying, many disinfectants; (4) Requires prolonged antibiotic courses.

🔄 Pathogenesis

Primary Infection

  1. Inhaled droplet nuclei reach alveoli
  2. Engulfed by alveolar macrophages
  3. TB survives inside macrophages (inhibits phagosome-lysosome fusion)
  4. Bacteria replicate intracellularly, spread to regional lymph nodes
  5. Cell-mediated immunity develops (2-8 weeks) → granuloma formation
  6. Ghon complex: Primary lung lesion + ipsilateral hilar lymph nodes
  7. Ranke complex: Calcified Ghon complex (healed primary TB)

Granuloma Structure

The hallmark of TB is the caseating granuloma:

  • Central caseous ("cheese-like") necrosis
  • Surrounded by epithelioid macrophages and Langhans giant cells
  • Outer rim of lymphocytes and fibroblasts
  • Contains dormant bacteria that can reactivate years later
Caseating granulomas are highly suggestive of TB! Non-caseating granulomas are seen in sarcoidosis, fungal infections, and foreign body reactions. The caseous center is due to type IV hypersensitivity (delayed-type, T-cell mediated).

🏥 Clinical Syndromes

SyndromeFeaturesKey Points
Latent TB (LTBI) Asymptomatic, positive TST/IGRA, normal CXR ~25% of world population. 5-10% lifetime risk of reactivation (higher with HIV)
Primary TB Usually children. Middle/lower lobe. Hilar adenopathy. Often asymptomatic or mild flu-like illness. Ghon complex.
Reactivation TB (Post-primary) UPPER LOBE cavitary lesions, fever, night sweats, weight loss, cough, hemoptysis Most common form in adults. High O₂ tension in apices favors growth.
Miliary TB Disseminated "millet seed" pattern on CXR. Multi-organ involvement. More common in immunocompromised. Can affect any organ.
TB Meningitis Subacute meningitis, basilar meningitis, CN palsies CSF: ↑ protein, ↓ glucose, lymphocytic pleocytosis. High mortality.
Pott Disease Vertebral TB, usually thoracic/lumbar. Paraspinal "cold" abscess. Can cause kyphosis ("gibbus deformity"), cord compression
Reactivation TB classically presents in the upper lobes (especially apices and posterior segments) because: (1) High oxygen tension favors this obligate aerobe; (2) Less lymphatic drainage → less immune surveillance.

🧪 Diagnosis

Screening for Latent TB

TestMechanismPros/Cons
TST (Tuberculin Skin Test / PPD) Intradermal injection of purified protein derivative. Read induration at 48-72 hours. Cheap, widely available. Cross-reacts with BCG and NTM (false positives). Requires return visit.
IGRA (Interferon-Gamma Release Assay) Blood test measuring IFN-γ release from T cells exposed to TB antigens (ESAT-6, CFP-10) More specific (no BCG cross-reaction). Single visit. More expensive.

Active TB Diagnosis

  • Sputum AFB smear: Rapid, insensitive. Need 3 samples (spot-morning-spot).
  • Sputum culture: Gold standard. Takes 2-6 weeks on Löwenstein-Jensen or liquid media.
  • NAAT (e.g., Xpert MTB/RIF): Rapid PCR. Also detects rifampin resistance. Highly sensitive/specific.
  • CXR: Upper lobe cavitary lesions (reactivation), miliary pattern, hilar adenopathy (primary)
TST interpretation thresholds:
≥5mm: HIV, close TB contacts, CXR findings, immunosuppressed
≥10mm: Recent immigrants, high-risk groups (healthcare workers, homeless, prisoners)
≥15mm: Low-risk individuals

💊 Treatment

Latent TB (LTBI)

  • Rifampin x 4 months (preferred for most)
  • Isoniazid x 9 months (traditional regimen)
  • Isoniazid + Rifapentine weekly x 12 weeks (3HP) - directly observed

Active TB - Standard Regimen (Drug-Susceptible)

RIPE therapy: 2 months intensive + 4 months continuation = 6 months total

Rifampin (R)

Inhibits DNA-dependent RNA polymerase. Orange body fluids! CYP inducer.

Isoniazid (I)

Inhibits mycolic acid synthesis. Requires pyridoxine (B6) to prevent neuropathy.

Pyrazinamide (P)

Unknown mechanism. Active in acidic environment (inside macrophages). Hepatotoxic, ↑ uric acid.

Ethambutol (E)

Inhibits arabinosyl transferase (cell wall). Optic neuritis (check vision).

⚠️ Key Drug Toxicities (High Yield!)

  • Rifampin: Orange body fluids, hepatotoxicity, drug interactions (CYP450 inducer!)
  • Isoniazid: Hepatotoxicity, peripheral neuropathy (give B6!), lupus-like syndrome, sideroblastic anemia
  • Pyrazinamide: Hepatotoxicity (most hepatotoxic!), hyperuricemia → gout
  • Ethambutol: Optic neuritis (red-green color blindness, visual acuity loss)
Always give pyridoxine (vitamin B6) with isoniazid to prevent peripheral neuropathy. INH inhibits pyridoxine metabolism. Risk factors for neuropathy: diabetes, HIV, malnutrition, alcoholism, pregnancy.

🛡️ Drug Resistance

TypeDefinitionTreatment
MDR-TB Resistant to Isoniazid AND Rifampin 18-24 months with second-line agents (fluoroquinolone, injectable, etc.)
XDR-TB MDR + resistant to fluoroquinolone AND ≥1 injectable Very difficult to treat. May need newer agents (bedaquiline, linezolid).

🔬 TB and HIV

TB is the leading cause of death in people with HIV. Key points:

  • HIV increases TB risk 20-30 fold
  • TB can present atypically in HIV (extrapulmonary, disseminated, lower lobe)
  • Start ART within 2-8 weeks of TB treatment (earlier if CD4 very low)
  • Watch for IRIS (immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome) when starting ART
  • Drug interactions between rifampin and many ARVs (use rifabutin instead)

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • Organism: Acid-fast bacillus (Ziehl-Neelsen stain), obligate aerobe, very slow growing, survives in macrophages
  • Cell wall: Mycolic acids → waxy, resistant to drying, doesn't Gram stain
  • Pathology: Caseating granulomas, Langhans giant cells, type IV hypersensitivity
  • Primary TB: Middle/lower lobes, Ghon complex, usually asymptomatic
  • Reactivation: UPPER LOBE cavitary lesions (high O₂), night sweats, weight loss, hemoptysis
  • Diagnosis: TST or IGRA (screening), AFB smear/culture, NAAT (rapid PCR)
  • Treatment: RIPE x 6 months (2 mo intensive + 4 mo continuation)
  • Toxicities: Rifampin = orange fluids + CYP inducer; INH = neuropathy (give B6!); PZA = hyperuricemia; EMB = optic neuritis
  • MDR-TB: Resistant to INH + Rifampin. Requires prolonged second-line therapy.
