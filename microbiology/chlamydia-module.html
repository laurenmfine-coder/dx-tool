🔬 Organism Overview

Chlamydiae are obligate intracellular bacteria with a unique biphasic life cycle. They cannot make their own ATP ("energy parasites") and must live inside host cells. Three species cause human disease:

Species Primary Diseases Key Points
C. trachomatis STIs (urethritis, cervicitis, PID), trachoma, neonatal conjunctivitis/pneumonia, LGV #1 bacterial STI in US; serovars determine disease
C. pneumoniae Atypical pneumonia, bronchitis, pharyngitis Common cause of CAP; associated with atherosclerosis
C. psittaci Psittacosis (atypical pneumonia) Bird exposure (parrots, parakeets); occupational risk

🔄 Biphasic Life Cycle (High-Yield!)

1

Elementary Body (EB)
Infectious form, metabolically inactive, survives outside cells

2

Attachment
EB attaches to columnar epithelium, enters via endocytosis

3

Reticulate Body (RB)
EB → RB inside cell, metabolically active, replicates

4

Release
RB → EB, cell lysis or exocytosis releases new EBs

Board Pearl: EB = "Enfectious" (survives outside), RB = "Replicates" (inside cell). Chlamydia cannot make ATP → "energy parasites" → cannot grow on standard culture media.

🔴 Chlamydia trachomatis

Serovars and Disease

Serovars Disease Key Features
A, B, Ba, C Trachoma Leading infectious cause of blindness worldwide; repeated infection → scarring → trichiasis
D-K Urogenital infections, neonatal disease STI: urethritis, cervicitis, PID, epididymitis; Neonatal: conjunctivitis, pneumonia
L1, L2, L3 Lymphogranuloma venereum (LGV) Painless ulcer → painful inguinal lymphadenopathy ("groove sign"); proctocolitis in MSM

Genital Chlamydia (Serovars D-K)

👨 Male Presentation

Urethritis with mucopurulent discharge (often less purulent than gonorrhea). Dysuria. May be asymptomatic in 25%. Can cause epididymitis.

👩 Female Presentation

Often asymptomatic (70-80%). Cervicitis, mucopurulent discharge. Untreated → PID → tubal scarring → infertility, ectopic pregnancy.

👶 Neonatal Disease

Acquired during vaginal delivery. Conjunctivitis (days 5-14) — most common cause of neonatal conjunctivitis. Pneumonia (weeks 4-12) — staccato cough, no fever.

Clinical Pearl: Chlamydia and gonorrhea coinfection is common (~20-40%). Always treat for BOTH if one is suspected or confirmed.

⚠️ Reactive Arthritis (Reiter Syndrome)

  • Classic triad: "Can't see, can't pee, can't climb a tree" (conjunctivitis, urethritis, arthritis)
  • Triggered by C. trachomatis (urogenital) or enteric pathogens
  • Associated with HLA-B27
  • Asymmetric oligoarthritis, typically lower extremity

🔵 Chlamydia pneumoniae

📋 Clinical Presentation

Atypical pneumonia with gradual onset. Often biphasic: pharyngitis/laryngitis first, then lower respiratory symptoms. Persistent cough, often prolonged.

👥 Epidemiology

Very common; ~50% seropositive by age 20. Spread person-to-person via respiratory droplets. Re-infection common throughout life.

🫁 CXR Findings

Often normal or minimal findings despite symptoms. When abnormal: subsegmental infiltrates, usually single lobe. Less impressive than symptoms suggest.

Distinguishing Features: C. pneumoniae often presents with prominent pharyngitis/hoarseness (laryngitis) preceding pneumonia — more so than Mycoplasma. Both are "atypical" CAP pathogens treated the same way.

🦜 Chlamydia psittaci (Psittacosis)

🐦 Exposure History

Contact with birds — especially parrots, parakeets, cockatiels, turkeys, ducks. Occupational: pet store workers, poultry farmers, veterinarians.

📋 Clinical Features

Abrupt onset fever, severe headache, dry cough. May have relative bradycardia (pulse-temperature dissociation). Hepatosplenomegaly common.

⚠️ Complications

Can be severe: respiratory failure, endocarditis, hepatitis, encephalitis. Mortality higher than other atypical pneumonias if untreated.

Board Trigger: Atypical pneumonia + bird exposure (pet store, poultry farm) + severe headache + hepatosplenomegaly = Think Psittacosis!

🧪 Diagnosis

Test Use Notes
NAAT (PCR) C. trachomatis STI screening Gold standard; urine, cervical/urethral swab, self-collected vaginal swab all acceptable
Serology C. pneumoniae, C. psittaci Requires paired sera; mainly for epidemiologic purposes
Culture Research, legal cases Requires cell culture; not routine; needed for antimicrobial susceptibility
Screening Recommendations (USPSTF/CDC): Screen all sexually active women <25 years annually. Screen older women with risk factors. Screen MSM at all anatomic sites of exposure.

💊 Treatment

Genital C. trachomatis

  • Preferred: Doxycycline 100mg BID × 7 days (higher cure rate than azithromycin)
  • Alternative: Azithromycin 1g single dose (convenient but slightly lower efficacy)
  • Pregnancy: Azithromycin 1g single dose (doxycycline contraindicated)
  • LGV: Doxycycline 100mg BID × 21 days

C. pneumoniae / C. psittaci

  • First-line: Doxycycline 100mg BID × 10-14 days
  • Alternative: Azithromycin, fluoroquinolones
  • Psittacosis: Doxycycline preferred; treat 10-21 days depending on severity

⚠️ Partner Management (STI)

  • Treat ALL sexual partners from past 60 days
  • Expedited Partner Therapy (EPT) legal in most states
  • Abstain from sex until both patient and partner(s) complete treatment
  • Test of cure NOT recommended routinely (only in pregnancy or if symptoms persist)
  • Retest in 3 months (high reinfection rate)

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Obligate intracellular, cannot make ATP, EB/RB life cycle
  • C. trachomatis: #1 bacterial STI; serovars D-K = STI, A-C = trachoma, L1-3 = LGV
  • C. pneumoniae: Atypical CAP, often with pharyngitis/hoarseness first
  • C. psittaci: Bird exposure + severe atypical pneumonia + hepatosplenomegaly
  • Diagnosis: NAAT/PCR for STI; serology for respiratory
  • Treatment: Doxycycline preferred; azithromycin alternative (or in pregnancy)
  • Complications: PID → infertility/ectopic; Reactive arthritis; Neonatal disease
