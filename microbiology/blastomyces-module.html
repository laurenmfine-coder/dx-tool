🔬 Organism Overview

Blastomyces dermatitidis is a dimorphic fungus endemic to the Ohio and Mississippi River valleys, Great Lakes region, and southeastern US. Known for causing both pulmonary disease AND prominent skin lesions (hence "dermatitidis"). Often misdiagnosed initially.

🌡️ Dimorphism

Mold at 25°C: Septate hyphae with small conidia. Yeast at 37°C: Large (8-15 μm) thick-walled yeast with BROAD-BASED BUDDING — the key diagnostic feature!

🌲 Environmental Source

Moist soil, especially near waterways (rivers, lakes), decaying wood/vegetation. Occupational/recreational exposure: hunting, fishing, camping, forestry work.

📊 Epidemiology

Less common than histoplasmosis. Often affects otherwise healthy, immunocompetent men (outdoor occupations). Can occur in immunocompromised but not as classic as Histo/Crypto.

🗺️ Geographic Distribution

Primary endemic areas: Great Lakes states (Wisconsin, Michigan, Minnesota), Ohio/Mississippi River valleys, Southeastern US (Arkansas, Kentucky, Tennessee, Carolinas). Also found in parts of Canada and Africa. Overlaps significantly with Histoplasma territory.

Board Pearl: Blastomyces yeast has BROAD-BASED BUDDING (bud attached by a wide base). This distinguishes it from Histoplasma (small, narrow-based) and Cryptococcus (narrow-based). Remember: "Blasto = Broad-Based."
📊 Clinical Syndromes
🫁 Pulmonary Disease

Most common presentation (60-90%). Ranges from asymptomatic to ARDS. Acute: flu-like, fever, cough. Chronic: mimics TB or lung cancer — mass lesions, infiltrates. Often initially misdiagnosed!

🧴 Skin Disease

Second most common (40-80%). Verrucous (warty) lesions with irregular borders and central healing. Also pustules, ulcers. Can be mistaken for skin cancer, pyoderma gangrenosum.

🦴 Bone Disease

25-50% of disseminated cases. Osteomyelitis: vertebrae, ribs, long bones, skull. May present as "cold abscess." Often overlooked — get bone scan/imaging if suspected.

Disseminated Disease

Site Frequency Features
Lung Primary Infiltrates, masses, cavities, miliary pattern
Skin 40-80% Verrucous lesions, pustules, ulcers — often on face/extremities
Bone 25-50% Osteomyelitis, especially vertebrae and ribs
GU 10-30% Prostate, epididymis — can mimic TB or cancer
CNS 5-10% Meningitis, brain abscess — more common in AIDS
Classic Board Vignette: Middle-aged man from Wisconsin + chronic cough not responding to antibiotics + verrucous skin lesions + CXR with mass lesion → Think Blastomycosis! Often initially misdiagnosed as lung cancer or TB.
⚠️ ARDS Presentation
  • Can present as rapidly progressive ARDS with >50% mortality
  • Diffuse pulmonary infiltrates, respiratory failure
  • Requires immediate Amphotericin B + ICU care
  • More common in immunocompromised but can occur in healthy hosts
🧪 Diagnosis
Test Utility Notes
Histopathology / Direct Exam BEST rapid diagnosis Large thick-walled yeast with BROAD-BASED BUDDING. KOH prep, GMS/PAS stain. Can diagnose same day!
Culture Definitive Grows on standard fungal media. Takes 1-4 weeks. BSL-3 handling (mold form infectious).
Urine/Serum Antigen Moderate sensitivity ~90% in disseminated, ~75% in pulmonary. Cross-reacts with Histoplasma! Useful for monitoring.
Serology (Antibody) Poor Low sensitivity/specificity — NOT recommended for diagnosis.
Diagnostic Approach: Histopathology showing broad-based budding yeast is often the fastest and most reliable diagnosis. Culture confirms but takes weeks. Antigen tests helpful but cross-react with Histoplasma.
💊 Treatment
Mild-Moderate Pulmonary or Disseminated (Non-CNS, Non-ARDS)
  • Itraconazole 200mg TID × 3 days, then 200mg BID × 6-12 months
  • Excellent response rates (>90%) in immunocompetent
  • Monitor itraconazole levels (goal trough >1 μg/mL)
Severe Pulmonary (ARDS), CNS, or Immunocompromised
  • Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg/day × 1-2 weeks (induction)
  • Then Itraconazole 200mg BID × 12 months (consolidation)
  • CNS disease: Longer AmB course (4-6 weeks); some use high-dose fluconazole or voriconazole
Special Populations
  • Pregnancy: Amphotericin B (azoles are teratogenic)
  • AIDS: Lifelong suppressive itraconazole if severe disease at presentation
  • Itraconazole failure: Consider voriconazole or posaconazole
Why Itraconazole (Not Fluconazole)? Blastomyces responds poorly to fluconazole — itraconazole is significantly more active. This is different from Coccidioides where fluconazole is first-line.
📝 High-Yield Summary
  • ID: Dimorphic fungus; tissue form = large yeast with BROAD-BASED BUDDING
  • Geography: Great Lakes, Ohio/Mississippi valleys, Southeast US
  • Source: Moist soil near waterways; outdoor activities (hunting, fishing)
  • Clinical: Pulmonary (primary) + skin (verrucous lesions) ± bone, GU, CNS
  • Misdiagnosis: Often mistaken for lung cancer, TB, or skin cancer initially
  • Diagnosis: Histopath (broad-based budding) is best rapid test; culture definitive
  • Treatment: Itraconazole for mild-moderate; AmB for severe/CNS/ARDS
