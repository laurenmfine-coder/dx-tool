🔬 Organism Overview

💎

Streptococcus pneumoniae (the "pneumococcus") is the most common cause of bacterial pneumonia, meningitis, and otitis media. Its name comes from the lancet (pointed) shape of the diplococci - often described as "flame-shaped" or like two teardrops facing each other.

Unlike S. aureus (clusters), pneumococcus appears in pairs (diplococci) or short chains. The polysaccharide capsule is its major virulence factor and the target of vaccines.

S. pneumoniae is α-hemolytic (partial hemolysis = green zone on blood agar), optochin-SENSITIVE, and bile-SOLUBLE. This distinguishes it from Viridans streptococci which are α-hemolytic but optochin-RESISTANT. Remember: "Pneumo is OOPS - Optochin Positive, Soluble in bile."

🧠 High-Yield Mnemonic: Encapsulated Organisms

SHiNE SKiS
Strep pneumo • Haemophilus influenzae • Neisseria meningitidis • E. coli K1 • Salmonella • Klebsiella • Strep agalactiae (GBS)

Asplenic patients are at HIGH RISK for these organisms - the spleen is critical for clearing encapsulated bacteria!

⚡ Virulence Factors

🛡️ Polysaccharide Capsule (MOST IMPORTANT)

Prevents phagocytosis by inhibiting C3b opsonization. Over 90 serotypes based on capsular antigens. Capsule is the target of pneumococcal vaccines (PCV13, PPSV23).

IgA Protease

Cleaves secretory IgA at mucosal surfaces, allowing colonization of respiratory tract. Shared with H. influenzae and N. meningitidis.

Pneumolysin

Cholesterol-dependent cytolysin (similar to streptolysin O). Causes tissue damage, activates complement, and contributes to inflammatory response.

Autolysin (LytA)

Causes autolysis releasing cell wall components and pneumolysin. Contributes to intense inflammatory response. Also responsible for bile solubility.

Teichoic Acid (C-polysaccharide)

Activates complement via alternative pathway. Binds C-reactive protein (CRP) - hence the name "C-reactive"!

Adhesins

PspA, PspC, and other surface proteins facilitate binding to respiratory epithelium and translocation across blood-brain barrier.

Why Capsule Matters: The capsule is antiphagocytic - it prevents complement C3b from binding, so opsonization fails. Without a functional spleen (which filters encapsulated organisms) or specific antibodies (from vaccines or prior infection), the immune system cannot clear pneumococci effectively.

🏥 Clinical Syndromes

Syndrome Key Features High-Yield Points
Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP) Productive cough, rusty sputum, fever, pleuritic chest pain, lobar consolidation #1 cause of typical bacterial CAP. "Rusty" sputum from RBC breakdown. Lobar pattern on CXR.
Meningitis Fever, headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, altered mental status #1 cause of bacterial meningitis in adults. CSF: ↑WBC (PMNs), ↑protein, ↓glucose, gram+ diplococci.
Otitis Media Ear pain, fever, bulging tympanic membrane (children) #1 cause in children (along with H. flu, M. catarrhalis). Can lead to mastoiditis.
Sinusitis Facial pain/pressure, purulent nasal discharge, fever Common cause of acute bacterial sinusitis after viral URI.
Bacteremia/Sepsis Fever, rigors, hypotension; can seed other sites Can occur with or without focus. High mortality in asplenic patients (overwhelming post-splenectomy infection - OPSI).
In a patient with pneumonia who rapidly develops meningitis, think pneumococcus! It can spread hematogenously from lungs to meninges. Always consider LP in pneumonia patients with altered mental status.

Quellung Reaction Capsule swells with type-specific antisera Gold standard for serotyping; "quellung" = German for swelling
Urinary Antigen Detects C-polysaccharide Rapid diagnosis of pneumococcal pneumonia; stays positive even after antibiotics
Remember the "OOPS" mnemonic: Pneumococcus is Optochin-sensitive and bile-Soluble. Viridans streptococci are the opposite (optochin-resistant, bile-insoluble). Both are α-hemolytic!

💊 Treatment

Antibiotic Therapy by Syndrome

CAP (Outpatient)

Amoxicillin (first-line) or respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin, moxifloxacin) if penicillin-allergic or resistant

CAP (Inpatient)

β-lactam (ceftriaxone, ampicillin-sulbactam) + macrolide OR respiratory fluoroquinolone alone

Meningitis

Ceftriaxone + Vancomycin (empiric, until susceptibilities known) + Dexamethasone (before or with first antibiotic dose)

Otitis Media

Amoxicillin (high-dose: 80-90 mg/kg/day) or amoxicillin-clavulanate if resistant

⚠️ Resistance Patterns

  • Penicillin resistance: Due to altered PBPs (not β-lactamase!). Rates vary by region; check local susceptibility data.
  • Macrolide resistance: Increasing; efflux pumps (mef) or ribosomal modification (erm)
  • Fluoroquinolone resistance: Less common but emerging; topoisomerase mutations
  • Multi-drug resistant (MDR): Defined as resistance to ≥3 antibiotic classes; more common in children, daycare attendees
For pneumococcal meningitis, dexamethasone should be given BEFORE or WITH the first dose of antibiotics (not after). It reduces inflammation and improves outcomes. Vancomycin is added empirically because of increasing penicillin/cephalosporin resistance.

💉 Prevention: Pneumococcal Vaccines

Vaccine Types

PCV13 (Prevnar 13)

Conjugate vaccine - polysaccharide conjugated to protein carrier (diphtheria toxoid). Induces T-cell dependent response → better memory, works in infants. Covers 13 serotypes.

PCV15 (Vaxneuvance)

Conjugate vaccine - covers 15 serotypes. Newer option for adults.

PCV20 (Prevnar 20)

Conjugate vaccine - covers 20 serotypes. Newest option, broader coverage.

PPSV23 (Pneumovax)

Polysaccharide vaccine - T-cell independent, no memory response. Doesn't work well in children <2 years. Covers 23 serotypes.

Conjugate vs Polysaccharide vaccines: Conjugate vaccines link the polysaccharide antigen to a protein carrier, converting a T-independent response to a T-dependent response. This creates immunologic memory and works in infants (whose immune systems don't respond well to polysaccharides alone). This is why PCV is used in children, while PPSV23 is only for adults/older children.

Who Should Be Vaccinated?

  • All children: PCV13/15 series starting at 2 months
  • Adults ≥65: PCV15 or PCV20 (± PPSV23 if PCV15 given)
  • High-risk adults 19-64: Immunocompromised, asplenic, CSF leak, cochlear implant, chronic heart/lung/liver disease, diabetes, alcoholism, smokers

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Gram-positive lancet-shaped diplococci, α-hemolytic, optochin-SENSITIVE, bile-SOLUBLE
  • Virulence: Polysaccharide CAPSULE is #1 (antiphagocytic). Also IgA protease, pneumolysin, autolysin.
  • Diseases: #1 cause of CAP, bacterial meningitis (adults), otitis media (children)
  • Rusty sputum: Classic for pneumococcal pneumonia (RBC breakdown)
  • Asplenic patients: HIGH RISK for fulminant pneumococcal sepsis - vaccinate!
  • Treatment: β-lactams (amoxicillin, ceftriaxone). Add vancomycin for meningitis empirically.
  • Vaccines: Conjugate (PCV13/15/20) for children & high-risk; Polysaccharide (PPSV23) for adults
  • Resistance: Altered PBPs (not β-lactamase) → check susceptibilities
