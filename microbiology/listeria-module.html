🦠 Listeria monocytogenes

The Cold-Loving Intracellular Pathogen - Meningitis in the Vulnerable

🔬 Microbiology Basics

Gram StainGram-positive RODS (not cocci!) - small, sometimes coccobacillary
Hemolysisβ-hemolytic (narrow zone, enhanced by CAMP test with S. aureus)
CatalasePOSITIVE (distinguishes from streptococci)
MotilityTUMBLING motility at room temp (22-25°C), not at 37°C
GrowthGrows at REFRIGERATOR temperatures (4°C) - "cold enrichment"
FacultativeFacultative INTRACELLULAR pathogen
Catalase-positive Gram-positive rod: Listeria stands out from Corynebacterium (irregular/club-shaped), Bacillus (large, spore-forming), and others. The tumbling motility at room temp is pathognomonic!

🍴 Transmission & Sources

Foodborne transmission is the primary route:

SourceRisk
🧀 Unpasteurized dairySoft cheeses (brie, feta, queso fresco), raw milk
🥬 Ready-to-eat producePrecut melons, sprouts, prepared salads
🥩 Deli meats/hot dogsUnless reheated to steaming (165°F)
🐟 Smoked seafoodCold-smoked salmon, refrigerated pâté

⚠️ Pregnancy Warning

Pregnant women should AVOID: unpasteurized dairy, soft cheeses, deli meats (unless heated), refrigerated smoked seafood. Listeriosis in pregnancy can cause spontaneous abortion, stillbirth, or neonatal meningitis.

⚔️ Pathogenesis - The Intracellular Life Cycle

Listeria is a facultative intracellular pathogen with a unique mechanism of spread:

  1. Entry: Internalin A/B bind E-cadherin → phagocytosis by non-professional phagocytes
  2. Phagosome escape: Listeriolysin O (LLO) + phospholipases lyse phagosome membrane
  3. Cytoplasmic multiplication: Replicates in host cell cytoplasm
  4. Actin rocket: ActA polymerizes host actin → propels bacteria to cell membrane
  5. Cell-to-cell spread: Forms protrusion → engulfed by adjacent cell → escapes double membrane
Listeriolysin O (LLO) is a pore-forming toxin (cholesterol-dependent cytolysin, similar to streptolysin O and pneumolysin). It's essential for phagosome escape. ActA hijacks host actin for "rocket" propulsion - similar to Shigella!
Why cell-mediated immunity matters: Because Listeria lives INSIDE cells, antibodies can't reach it. T-cells (CD8+ cytotoxic and CD4+ Th1) are essential for killing infected cells. This is why immunocompromised patients (especially those with defective CMI) are at high risk.

🏥 Clinical Syndromes

At-Risk Populations (The "4 Ps")

  • Pregnant women
  • Pediatric (neonates)
  • People >65 years old
  • Patients who are immunocompromised (especially CMI defects: steroids, transplant, HIV with low CD4)
SyndromePopulationKey Features
Febrile gastroenteritis Healthy adults Self-limited diarrhea, fever, myalgias 24h after ingestion. Usually resolves spontaneously.
Bacteremia Immunocompromised, elderly Fever, may lack localizing symptoms. Can seed meninges, CNS.
Meningitis Neonates, elderly, immunocompromised #3 cause of bacterial meningitis (after S. pneumoniae, N. meningitidis). CSF may show only MILD pleocytosis with mononuclear predominance!
Rhombencephalitis Healthy adults possible Brainstem/cerebellar involvement. Cranial nerve palsies, ataxia, altered consciousness.
Pregnancy-associated Pregnant women Flu-like illness → amnionitis, spontaneous abortion, preterm labor, stillbirth. Fetus infected via transplacental spread.
Neonatal Newborns Early-onset (sepsis, granulomatosis infantiseptica) or late-onset (meningitis at 1-4 weeks).

🧪 Diagnosis

  • Blood cultures: Standard method. May take 24-48h.
  • CSF analysis:
    • WBC usually elevated but may be modest (100-1000)
    • Often MONONUCLEAR predominance (unusual for bacterial meningitis!)
    • Protein elevated, glucose low or normal
    • Gram stain often NEGATIVE (low organism load)
  • CSF culture: Gold standard but takes time
  • Stool culture: Not routinely helpful
CSF Gram stain pitfall: Listeria can look like diphtheroids (contaminants) or may not be seen at all. Don't dismiss a positive Gram stain for "GPR" in appropriate clinical context!

💊 Treatment

⚠️ Cephalosporins DON'T WORK!

Listeria has intrinsic resistance to all cephalosporins. Empiric meningitis therapy (ceftriaxone + vancomycin) does NOT cover Listeria. Must ADD AMPICILLIN in at-risk patients!

InfectionTreatmentDuration
Bacteremia Ampicillin ± gentamicin 2 weeks
Meningitis Ampicillin + gentamicin ≥3 weeks
Brain abscess/rhombencephalitis Ampicillin + gentamicin 6 weeks
Pregnancy Ampicillin (high-dose) 2 weeks minimum
PCN allergy TMP-SMX Same durations
Why add gentamicin? The combination is synergistic and bactericidal. Ampicillin alone is bacteriostatic against Listeria. For serious infections (meningitis, endocarditis), synergy improves outcomes.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Gram-positive rod, β-hemolytic, catalase(+), tumbling motility at room temp, grows at 4°C
  • Transmission: Foodborne - unpasteurized dairy, deli meats, soft cheeses
  • Pathogenesis: Facultative intracellular. LLO for phagosome escape, ActA for actin rocket.
  • At-risk: Pregnant women, neonates, elderly, immunocompromised (especially CMI defects)
  • Clinical: Meningitis (#3 cause), bacteremia, pregnancy complications, rhombencephalitis
  • CSF: May have MONONUCLEAR predominance (atypical for bacterial meningitis)
  • Treatment: AMPICILLIN ± gentamicin. CEPHALOSPORINS DON'T WORK!
  • Empiric meningitis: Add ampicillin to ceftriaxone + vancomycin in neonates, elderly, immunocompromised
