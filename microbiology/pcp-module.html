PCP: The AIDS-Defining Pneumonia Treated with an Antibiotic

🔬 Organism Overview

Pneumocystis jirovecii (formerly P. carinii — renamed; "PCP" now stands for Pneumocystis Pneumonia) is an atypical fungus that causes pneumonia almost exclusively in immunocompromised hosts. Despite being a fungus, it does NOT respond to standard antifungals — TMP-SMX is first-line.

🧬 Unique Classification

Classified as fungus by rRNA analysis, but has unique features: lacks ergosterol (so azoles/amphotericin don't work), cannot culture on artificial media, responds to antifolate drugs (like bacteria).

🔬 Life Cycle

Two forms: Trophozoites (small, thin-walled) and Cysts (thick-walled, contain 8 intracystic bodies). Cysts seen with GMS/silver stain.

📍 Reservoir

Humans are only host for P. jirovecii. Likely person-to-person respiratory transmission. Most people exposed by age 2-4 (latent colonization possible).

Board Pearl: PCP is a fungus but treated with TMP-SMX (an antibacterial antifolate) because Pneumocystis lacks ergosterol, making azoles and amphotericin ineffective. This is very high-yield!

🎯 Risk Factors

Population When to Worry Prophylaxis Indicated
HIV/AIDS CD4 <200 cells/μL (or CD4% <14%) Yes — until CD4 >200 × 3 months on ART
HSCT recipients All patients Yes — for ≥6 months post-transplant
Solid organ transplant All patients, especially lung Yes — typically 6-12 months
Hematologic malignancy ALL, prolonged lymphopenia Yes — during chemotherapy
Prolonged steroids ≥20 mg prednisone daily × ≥4 weeks Consider prophylaxis
Other immunosuppression Biologics (anti-TNF), rituximab, temozolomide, etc. Case-by-case
Clinical Pearl: PCP in a patient not on prophylaxis with no known immunocompromise = test for HIV. PCP is an AIDS-defining illness and may be the first presentation of undiagnosed HIV.

🏥 Clinical Presentation

📋 HIV-Associated PCP

Indolent onset over weeks. Progressive dyspnea, dry cough, fever, fatigue. Hypoxia often worse than exam suggests. Exam may show only tachypnea; rales often absent early.

📋 Non-HIV PCP

More acute/fulminant onset over days. Higher mortality. More likely to need ICU. Lower organism burden (harder to diagnose). Often occurs when steroids tapered.

⚠️ Hypoxemia

Hallmark: profound hypoxemia disproportionate to clinical exam. A-a gradient markedly elevated. Room air SpO₂ and exercise desaturation key findings.

Imaging

🩻 CXR

Bilateral interstitial infiltrates, "bat wing" or perihilar haze. May be normal early! Classic: diffuse bilateral ground-glass. Cysts/pneumatoceles can develop.

📷 CT Chest

More sensitive. Bilateral ground-glass opacities (GGO) with perihilar predominance and peripheral sparing. Cysts (thin-walled) in 10-30%.

⚠️ Pneumothorax Risk

  • PCP-associated cysts can rupture → spontaneous pneumothorax
  • Higher risk with aerosolized pentamidine prophylaxis (upper lobe disease)
  • May be bilateral, recurrent, and difficult to manage

🧪 Diagnosis

Test Specimen Notes
Induced sputum with DFA/GMS Sputum (induced) Sensitivity 50-90% (higher in HIV due to high burden). Non-invasive first step.
BAL with DFA/GMS/PCR Bronchoalveolar lavage Gold standard. Sensitivity >95%. Required if sputum negative but high suspicion.
PCR Sputum, BAL Very sensitive — may detect colonization. Quantitative PCR helps distinguish infection from colonization.
β-D-glucan Serum Very high in PCP (often >500 pg/mL). Good sensitivity but not specific. NPV useful.
LDH Serum Elevated (often >500 IU/L) — correlates with disease severity. Non-specific but supportive.
Diagnostic Approach: Elevated LDH + bilateral GGO + risk factors = high suspicion for PCP. β-D-glucan often very elevated. Confirm with induced sputum or BAL. Start empiric therapy while awaiting results — don't delay!

💊 Treatment

First-Line: TMP-SMX (Bactrim)

  • Dose: TMP 15-20 mg/kg/day + SMX 75-100 mg/kg/day IV divided q6-8h
  • Duration: 21 days for HIV; 14 days may suffice for non-HIV
  • Transition: IV → PO when clinically improved (same total daily dose)
  • Monitor: Renal function, potassium, rash, cytopenias

Alternatives (for sulfa allergy or intolerance)

  • Pentamidine IV 4 mg/kg/day — toxic; hypotension, nephrotoxicity, hypoglycemia, pancreatitis
  • Primaquine + Clindamycin — check G6PD first! (primaquine causes hemolysis)
  • Atovaquone 750 mg PO BID — mild disease only; take with fatty food
  • Dapsone + TMP — for mild-moderate; check G6PD

⚠️ Adjunctive Corticosteroids (CRITICAL)

  • Indication: PaO₂ <70 mmHg or A-a gradient ≥35 mmHg on room air
  • Dose: Prednisone 40 mg BID × 5 days → 40 mg daily × 5 days → 20 mg daily × 11 days
  • Timing: Start within 72 hours of PCP treatment (ideally with or before)
  • Benefit: Reduces mortality and respiratory failure — proven in RCTs
  • Mechanism: Blunts inflammatory response to dying organisms
Clinical Pearl: Patients often get WORSE in first 3-5 days of treatment due to inflammatory response to dying organisms. This is expected — not treatment failure. Steroids help mitigate this.

🛡️ Prophylaxis

Primary Prophylaxis Regimens

  • First-line: TMP-SMX 1 DS tablet daily (or 1 SS daily, or 1 DS 3×/week)
  • Alternative: Dapsone 100 mg daily (check G6PD first)
  • Alternative: Atovaquone 1500 mg daily with food
  • Alternative: Aerosolized pentamidine 300 mg monthly (least effective; doesn't prevent extrapulmonary or upper lobe disease)

When to Stop Prophylaxis (HIV)

  • CD4 >200 cells/μL for ≥3 months on ART
  • Restart if CD4 falls <200 or if PCP occurs despite CD4 >200

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Atypical fungus, cannot culture, lacks ergosterol → standard antifungals don't work
  • Risk: HIV with CD4 <200, transplant, hematologic malignancy, prolonged steroids
  • Presentation: Subacute dyspnea, dry cough, fever, profound hypoxemia > clinical exam
  • Imaging: Bilateral ground-glass opacities (GGO), perihilar, ± cysts
  • Diagnosis: Induced sputum or BAL with DFA/GMS/PCR; elevated LDH and β-D-glucan supportive
  • Treatment: TMP-SMX × 21 days; ADD STEROIDS if PaO₂ <70 or A-a gradient ≥35
  • Prophylaxis: TMP-SMX first-line; for HIV when CD4 <200
