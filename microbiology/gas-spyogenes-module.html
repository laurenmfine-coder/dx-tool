🦠 Group A Streptococcus (S. pyogenes)

The "Flesh-Eating" β-Hemolytic Streptococcus - Pharyngitis to Necrotizing Fasciitis

🔬 Microbiology Basics

Gram StainGram-positive cocci in CHAINS
Hemolysisβ-hemolytic (complete hemolysis, clear zone)
CatalaseNEGATIVE (like all streptococci)
Lancefield GroupGroup A (carbohydrate antigen in cell wall)
BacitracinSENSITIVE (differentiates from other β-hemolytic strep)
PYRPOSITIVE (differentiates from Group B strep)
β-hemolytic Strep ID:
• Bacitracin Sensitive + PYR(+) = Group A (S. pyogenes)
• Bacitracin Resistant + PYR(-) + CAMP(+) = Group B (S. agalactiae)
• Bacitracin Resistant + PYR(-) + CAMP(-) = Groups C, F, G

⚔️ Virulence Factors

FactorFunctionClinical Relevance
M protein Antiphagocytic; inhibits complement Major virulence factor. >200 types. Antibodies to M protein are protective. Molecular mimicry → rheumatic fever.
Hyaluronic acid capsule Antiphagocytic; "molecular camouflage" Chemically identical to human hyaluronic acid - not immunogenic
Streptolysin O Pore-forming toxin (oxygen-labile) Lyses RBCs, WBCs, platelets. Induces ASO antibody response.
Streptolysin S Pore-forming toxin (oxygen-stable) Responsible for β-hemolysis on agar. Not immunogenic.
Pyrogenic exotoxins (SpeA, B, C) Superantigens Scarlet fever rash. Streptococcal toxic shock syndrome (STSS).
Streptokinase Activates plasminogen → plasmin Dissolves clots; promotes spread. Induced antibody (ASK) indicates recent infection.
DNases (A, B, C, D) Degrades DNA in pus Anti-DNase B antibody useful for diagnosing prior skin infection.
C5a peptidase Cleaves complement C5a Inhibits neutrophil chemotaxis

🏥 Suppurative (Direct) Infections

InfectionKey Features
Pharyngitis ("Strep throat") Peak ages 5-15. Sudden onset fever, sore throat, tonsillar exudates, tender anterior cervical lymph nodes. NO cough (cough suggests viral).
Scarlet fever Pharyngitis + diffuse erythematous "sandpaper" rash + strawberry tongue + Pastia lines (dark lines in skin folds). From pyrogenic exotoxins.
Impetigo Superficial skin infection with "honey-crusted" lesions. Often mixed with S. aureus.
Erysipelas Well-demarcated, raised, painful red skin lesion with sharp borders. Often on face. Dermis involvement.
Cellulitis Spreading skin infection, deeper than erysipelas. Borders less distinct.
Necrotizing fasciitis "Flesh-eating bacteria." Rapidly progressive, severe pain out of proportion. Requires emergent surgical debridement!
Streptococcal TSS Superantigen-mediated shock, often with necrotizing fasciitis. High mortality.
Puerperal sepsis Postpartum endometritis/sepsis. Historical killer ("childbed fever").

🚨 Necrotizing Fasciitis Emergency Signs

  • Pain out of proportion to exam findings
  • Rapid progression
  • Systemic toxicity (fever, tachycardia, hypotension)
  • Skin changes: bullae, crepitus, dusky color
  • Treatment: Emergent surgical debridement + penicillin + clindamycin (inhibits toxin production)

🔥 Non-Suppurative (Post-Infectious) Complications

These occur AFTER the acute infection (latent period) and are due to immune-mediated damage, NOT direct bacterial invasion.

Major: Joints (migratory polyarthritis), ♡ Carditis, Nodules (subcutaneous), Erythema marginatum, Sydenham chorea
Minor: Fever, arthralgia, ↑ESR/CRP, prolonged PR interval 		YES! Treating strep pharyngitis prevents ARF.

🧪 Diagnosis

Pharyngitis

  • Centor criteria: Fever, Tonsillar exudates, No cough, Tender anterior cervical nodes. Age modifier: +1 if 3-14yo, 0 if 15-44, -1 if ≥45.
  • Rapid antigen detection test (RADT): High specificity (~95%), moderate sensitivity (~70-90%). If negative in child, confirm with culture.
  • Throat culture: Gold standard. More sensitive but takes 24-48h.

Serologic Tests (for post-infectious complications)

  • ASO titer: Rises 1-3 weeks post-infection, peaks 3-5 weeks. Better after pharyngitis.
  • Anti-DNase B: Better after skin infections (ASO often negative post-impetigo).
  • Streptozyme: Detects multiple antibodies. Good screening test.

💊 Treatment

InfectionTreatmentNotes
Pharyngitis Penicillin V x 10 days
OR Amoxicillin x 10 days
OR Benzathine PCN IM x 1		 NO resistance to penicillin ever documented! PCN allergy: azithromycin or cephalexin.
Skin infections Penicillin or amoxicillin
(add MRSA coverage if suspected mixed infection)		 Impetigo often mixed with S. aureus - may need anti-staph coverage.
Necrotizing fasciitis / STSS Penicillin + Clindamycin + emergent surgery Clindamycin inhibits toxin production (ribosomal inhibitor). Surgery is CRITICAL!
Secondary prophylaxis (post-ARF) Benzathine PCN IM every 3-4 weeks Duration: 10 years or until age 40 (if no carditis). Lifelong if carditis with residual heart disease.
Why clindamycin for necrotizing fasciitis? It's a protein synthesis inhibitor that reduces toxin production. β-lactams (cell wall inhibitors) work well when bacteria are dividing, but in high-inoculum infections, many bacteria are in stationary phase. Clindamycin works regardless of growth phase.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Gram-positive cocci in chains, β-hemolytic, catalase(-), bacitracin-sensitive, PYR(+)
  • Key virulence: M protein (antiphagocytic, molecular mimicry), pyrogenic exotoxins (superantigens), streptolysin O/S
  • Suppurative: Pharyngitis, scarlet fever, impetigo, erysipelas, cellulitis, necrotizing fasciitis, STSS
  • Non-suppurative: ARF (Jones criteria, post-pharyngitis only, prevented by Abx), PSGN (nephritic, post-pharyngitis or skin, NOT prevented by Abx)
  • Diagnosis: RADT + culture for pharyngitis; ASO for post-pharyngeal; anti-DNase B for post-skin
  • Treatment: Penicillin (NO resistance!). Add clindamycin for necrotizing fasciitis/STSS. Surgery for nec fasc!
  • Prophylaxis: Long-term PCN after ARF to prevent recurrence
