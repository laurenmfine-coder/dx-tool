Enterococcus

The Tough Gram-Positive That Tolerates Everything

🔬 Microbiology Basics

MorphologyGram-positive cocci in PAIRS and SHORT CHAINS
HemolysisUsually γ-hemolytic (non-hemolytic) or α-hemolytic
CatalaseNEGATIVE (like all streptococci)
GrowthGrows in 6.5% NaCl, bile, wide pH and temperature range
PYR testPOSITIVE (hydrolyzes L-pyrrolidonyl-β-naphthylamide)
HabitatNormal GI flora, also found in GU tract
Key ID features: Gram-positive cocci, catalase-negative (like strep), but grows in 6.5% NaCl and bile (strep can't). PYR positive helps differentiate from Group D strep.

🔷 Key Species

E. faecalis (~80-90% of infections)

  • More common but generally more susceptible
  • Usually susceptible to ampicillin and vancomycin
  • Intrinsic resistance to cephalosporins, TMP-SMX, clindamycin

E. faecium (~10-20% of infections)

  • Less common but MORE resistant
  • Often ampicillin-resistant
  • Higher rates of vancomycin resistance (VRE)
  • May require linezolid or daptomycin
Remember: E. faeCalis = more Common, more Curable. E. faeCium = more Challenging (resistant).

🏥 Clinical Syndromes

SyndromeKey FeaturesNotes
UTI Catheter-associated, complicated UTI #2 cause of nosocomial UTI. Usually in instrumented/catheterized patients.
Bacteremia Line infections, GI/GU source Often polymicrobial. Look for intra-abdominal source.
Endocarditis Subacute, older patients, GI/GU procedures #3 cause of native valve endocarditis. Requires synergistic therapy.
Intra-abdominal infections Often part of polymicrobial infection Pathogenic role debated - may not always need coverage.
Wound infections Surgical site infections Usually polymicrobial with GI flora
For enterococcal endocarditis, think: older patient + recent GI/GU procedure + subacute presentation. Colonoscopy and prostate procedures are classic risk factors.

💊 Treatment Challenges

Intrinsic Resistance (All Enterococci)

  • Cephalosporins: ALL generations - can't use ceftriaxone!
  • TMP-SMX: Can use exogenous folate
  • Clindamycin: Intrinsically resistant
  • Aminoglycosides (low-level): Monotherapy ineffective, but synergy still works

Acquired Resistance

ResistanceMechanismTreatment Options
Ampicillin resistance Altered PBPs (E. faecium) Vancomycin (if susceptible)
VRE (vanA) D-ala-D-ala → D-ala-D-LAC Linezolid, daptomycin, tigecycline
VRE (vanB) Same mechanism, inducible Same as vanA (teicoplanin may work)
High-level aminoglycoside resistance (HLAR) Aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes No synergy! Use ampicillin + ceftriaxone for endocarditis
⚠️ VRE Alert: Vancomycin-resistant enterococci (especially E. faecium) are a serious threat. Contact precautions required. Treatment options: linezolid (watch for thrombocytopenia), daptomycin (NOT for pneumonia), tigecycline.

💉 Endocarditis Treatment

Enterococci are only INHIBITED (bacteriostatic) by cell wall agents alone. Need synergy for BACTERICIDAL activity:

Ampicillin-Susceptible, No HLAR

Ampicillin + Gentamicin x 4-6 weeks (synergy!)

Ampicillin-Susceptible WITH HLAR

Ampicillin + Ceftriaxone x 6 weeks (double β-lactam synergy - saturates different PBPs)

Ampicillin-Resistant (VRE)

Daptomycin (high dose, 8-10 mg/kg) or Linezolid x 6 weeks. ID consult essential.

The ampicillin + ceftriaxone combo for HLAR enterococcal endocarditis is a key clinical pearl. It works because the two drugs saturate different PBPs, achieving synergistic killing without needing aminoglycosides.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Gram-positive cocci in pairs/chains, catalase-negative, PYR-positive, grows in 6.5% NaCl and bile
  • E. faecalis: More common, usually susceptible to ampicillin
  • E. faecium: Less common, more resistant (often VRE)
  • Intrinsic resistance: ALL cephalosporins, TMP-SMX, clindamycin, low-level aminoglycosides
  • VRE: vanA/vanB genes → D-ala-D-lactate. Treat with linezolid or daptomycin.
  • Endocarditis: Need synergy! Amp + gent (if no HLAR), or amp + ceftriaxone (if HLAR)
  • Key infections: UTI (#2 nosocomial), endocarditis (#3 native valve), bacteremia, intra-abdominal
