Histoplasma capsulatum

Ohio/Mississippi River Valleys — The "Cave Disease"

Dimorphic Fungus Endemic Mycosis Intracellular Bat/Bird Droppings
Classification
Dimorphic Fungus
Endemic Region
Ohio/Mississippi Valleys
Source
Bat/bird droppings
Treatment

🔬 Organism Overview

Histoplasma capsulatum is a dimorphic fungus endemic to the Ohio and Mississippi River valleys. It's the most common endemic mycosis in the US. Despite its name, it does NOT have a capsule — the name is a misnomer from early microscopy.

🌡️ Dimorphism

Mold at 25°C (environment): septate hyphae with tuberculate macroconidia. Yeast at 37°C (body): small oval budding yeast INSIDE macrophages.

🦇 Environmental Source

Soil enriched with bat or bird droppings (nitrogen source). Caves, chicken coops, old buildings, under bridges. Infection from inhaling microconidia.

🦠 Intracellular Pathogen

Like TB, lives inside macrophages. Cell-mediated immunity (T cells) essential for control. Immunocompromised at risk for disseminated disease.

🗺️ Geographic Distribution

Highly endemic: Ohio River Valley, Mississippi River Valley, Central America, parts of South America, Africa, Southeast Asia. In US, extends from Texas to New York along river valleys. ~80% of people in endemic areas have been exposed (positive skin test). Most infections asymptomatic or mild.

Board Pearl: "Mold in the cold, yeast in the beast" — Dimorphic fungi (Histoplasma, Blastomyces, Coccidioides) are mold in environment (cold), yeast in body (warm). This is high-yield!

📊 Clinical Syndromes

Syndrome Host Features
Asymptomatic/Subclinical Most (>90%) No symptoms; may have calcified granulomas on CXR later
Acute Pulmonary Immunocompetent, heavy exposure Flu-like: fever, cough, chest pain, myalgias. Self-limited 2-4 weeks. CXR: infiltrates ± hilar LAD
Chronic Pulmonary COPD, emphysema Mimics TB: upper lobe cavitary disease, weight loss, cough. Progressive if untreated.
Disseminated AIDS (CD4 <150), immunosuppressed Fever, weight loss, hepatosplenomegaly, pancytopenia, mucocutaneous lesions. Can be fatal.
Mediastinal Any Granulomatous: enlarged nodes, can cause compression. Fibrosing: rare, progressive fibrosis.

Disseminated Histoplasmosis (High-Yield for AIDS)

🎯 Risk: CD4 <150

AIDS-defining illness. Often presents with fever of unknown origin, weight loss, fatigue. Can be rapidly fatal without treatment.

📋 Clinical Features

Hepatosplenomegaly (90%), pancytopenia, elevated LFTs, skin/mucosal ulcers, GI involvement, adrenal insufficiency.

🩺 Physical Findings

Oral/skin ulcers, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly. Can mimic disseminated TB or lymphoma.

Classic Board Vignette: Patient from Ohio River Valley + spelunking (cave exploration) or cleaning chicken coop + flu-like illness ± hilar lymphadenopathy = Think Histoplasmosis!

🧪 Diagnosis

Test Best For Notes
Urine/Serum Antigen Disseminated, acute pulmonary (severe) Sensitivity 90%+ in disseminated. Cross-reacts with Blastomyces. Best rapid test.
Serology (antibody) Subacute/chronic, immunocompetent Complement fixation, immunodiffusion. Takes 4-8 weeks to develop. May be negative in immunocompromised.
Culture Definitive diagnosis Gold standard but slow (2-4 weeks). Requires special handling (BSL-3). Mold form is hazardous!
Histopathology Tissue diagnosis Small (2-4 μm) oval yeasts INSIDE macrophages. GMS or PAS stain. Looks like "dots" in cells.
Bone Marrow Biopsy Disseminated with pancytopenia High yield in disseminated disease. Shows intracellular yeasts.
Diagnostic Approach: For disseminated or severe acute → urine/serum antigen (fast, sensitive). For chronic/subacute in immunocompetent → serology. For definitive → culture or histopath showing intracellular yeasts.

💊 Treatment

Mild-Moderate Acute Pulmonary (<4 weeks symptoms)

  • Usually no treatment needed — self-limited in immunocompetent
  • If symptomatic >4 weeks or immunocompromised: Itraconazole 200mg TID × 3 days, then 200mg BID × 6-12 weeks

Moderate-Severe Acute or Chronic Pulmonary

  • Liposomal Amphotericin B 3-5 mg/kg/day × 1-2 weeks (induction)
  • Then Itraconazole 200mg BID × 12 months (consolidation)

Disseminated Histoplasmosis

  • Induction: Liposomal Amphotericin B 3 mg/kg/day × 1-2 weeks (until clinical improvement)
  • Consolidation: Itraconazole 200mg BID × ≥12 months
  • AIDS patients: Continue itraconazole as secondary prophylaxis until CD4 >150 × 6 months on ART
  • Monitor: Urine antigen to assess response

⚠️ Itraconazole Considerations

  • Requires acidic environment for absorption — take with food, avoid PPIs/H2 blockers
  • Use solution formulation if capsules not absorbing (better bioavailability)
  • Check itraconazole levels (goal trough >1 μg/mL)
  • Drug interactions: CYP3A4 inhibitor — check all medications!

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Dimorphic fungus — mold in environment, yeast in body (inside macrophages)
  • Geography: Ohio/Mississippi River Valleys; bat/bird droppings (caves, chicken coops)
  • Acute: Flu-like illness, usually self-limited; may see hilar lymphadenopathy
  • Chronic: Mimics TB — upper lobe cavitary disease in COPD patients
  • Disseminated: AIDS (CD4 <150) — fever, hepatosplenomegaly, pancytopenia, skin lesions
  • Diagnosis: Urine antigen (disseminated), serology (chronic), histopath shows intracellular yeasts
  • Treatment: Mild = observation or itraconazole; Severe/disseminated = AmB → itraconazole
