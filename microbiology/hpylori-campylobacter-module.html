🦠 H. pylori & Campylobacter

Curved/Spiral Gram-Negative Rods - GI Pathogens

🔵 Helicobacter pylori

Microbiology

MorphologyCurved/spiral Gram-negative rod with multiple flagella
CatalasePOSITIVE
OxidasePOSITIVE
UreaseSTRONGLY POSITIVE - KEY diagnostic feature!
GrowthMicroaerophilic, requires special media
HabitatGastric mucosa (antrum and body)
Urease is critical for H. pylori survival! Urease converts urea → ammonia + CO₂. Ammonia neutralizes gastric acid, creating a protective alkaline microenvironment. This is why urease testing (breath test, rapid urease test) is used for diagnosis!

🏥 H. pylori - Clinical Manifestations

Associated Diseases

ConditionAssociationKey Points
Chronic gastritis Most common manifestation Type B (antral) gastritis. May be asymptomatic.
Peptic ulcer disease ~80% of duodenal ulcers, ~60% of gastric ulcers Duodenal > gastric. Eradication reduces recurrence dramatically.
Gastric adenocarcinoma 6x increased risk WHO Group I carcinogen. Intestinal metaplasia → dysplasia → cancer.
MALT lymphoma Strong association May regress with H. pylori eradication alone (early stage)!
Nobel Prize 2005: Barry Marshall and Robin Warren won for discovering H. pylori causes peptic ulcers. Marshall famously drank H. pylori to prove Koch's postulates!

🧪 H. pylori Diagnosis

Non-Invasive Tests (No Endoscopy)

  • Urea breath test (UBT): Patient ingests ¹³C or ¹⁴C-labeled urea. If H. pylori present, urease cleaves urea → labeled CO₂ detected in breath. BEST for confirming eradication.
  • Stool antigen test: Detects H. pylori antigens in stool. Good for diagnosis and confirming eradication.
  • Serology (IgG): Can't distinguish active vs past infection. NOT useful for confirming eradication.

Invasive Tests (Endoscopy Required)

  • Rapid urease test (CLO test): Biopsy placed in urea-containing gel. Urease → ammonia → pH change → color change. Results in hours.
  • Histology: Silver stain or H&E. Can assess for atrophy, metaplasia.
  • Culture: Difficult, mainly for susceptibility testing.
Stop PPIs and antibiotics 2 weeks before testing! They suppress H. pylori and cause false negatives (except serology).

💊 H. pylori Treatment

Standard Triple Therapy (14 days)

PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin (or metronidazole if penicillin allergy)

Bismuth Quadruple Therapy (14 days)

PPI + Bismuth + Metronidazole + Tetracycline

Preferred if prior macrolide exposure or high clarithromycin resistance area.

Newer Regimens

  • Concomitant therapy: PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin + metronidazole (all together)
  • Sequential therapy: PPI + amoxicillin (5 days) → PPI + clarithromycin + metronidazole (5 days)
  • Levofloxacin-based: For clarithromycin-resistant or treatment failures
Confirm eradication 4+ weeks after treatment with urea breath test or stool antigen. Serology stays positive for years - don't use it to confirm cure!

🔴 Campylobacter jejuni

Microbiology

MorphologyCurved/S-shaped ("seagull wing") Gram-negative rod
MotilityDarting motility (single polar flagellum)
Catalase/OxidaseBoth POSITIVE
GrowthMicroaerophilic, 42°C (thermophilic!), Campy-BAP media
ReservoirPoultry, cattle, other animals
Campylobacter is the #1 cause of bacterial gastroenteritis in the US! Often from undercooked poultry. Grows at 42°C (chicken body temp = 41-42°C).

🏥 Campylobacter - Clinical Disease

Acute Gastroenteritis

  • Incubation: 2-5 days (longer than Salmonella)
  • Prodrome: Fever, headache, myalgias for 1 day before diarrhea
  • Diarrhea: Can be watery OR bloody. Often crampy abdominal pain.
  • Duration: Self-limited, 5-7 days
  • Source: Undercooked poultry (#1), unpasteurized milk, contaminated water

Complications - HIGH YIELD!

ComplicationTimingFeatures
Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS) 1-3 weeks post-infection #1 identified cause of GBS! Molecular mimicry (LOS mimics GM1 ganglioside). Ascending paralysis.
Reactive arthritis 1-4 weeks post-infection Asymmetric oligoarthritis, especially knees/ankles. HLA-B27 associated.
Bacteremia During acute illness Rare, mainly in immunocompromised or elderly.
Board classic: Young patient with ascending weakness 2 weeks after a diarrheal illness = think Campylobacter-associated GBS!

💊 Campylobacter Treatment

  • Most cases: Supportive care only (self-limited)
  • Antibiotics indicated for: Severe disease, bloody diarrhea, high fever, immunocompromised, prolonged symptoms (>1 week)
  • First-line: Azithromycin 500mg x 3 days
  • Alternative: Fluoroquinolones (but resistance is increasing - ~25%+)
Unlike EHEC (where antibiotics may worsen HUS), treating Campylobacter with antibiotics does NOT increase GBS risk - but antibiotics also don't prevent GBS.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • H. pylori: Curved GNR, urease(+), lives in gastric mucosa. Causes PUD, gastric cancer, MALT lymphoma.
  • H. pylori diagnosis: Urea breath test or stool antigen (best). Stop PPIs 2 weeks before!
  • H. pylori treatment: Triple therapy (PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin) or bismuth quadruple x 14 days.
  • Campylobacter: Curved "seagull wing" GNR, grows at 42°C, #1 bacterial gastroenteritis in US.
  • Campylobacter source: Undercooked poultry! Also unpasteurized milk.
  • Campylobacter complications: GBS (#1 cause! - molecular mimicry), reactive arthritis.
  • Campylobacter treatment: Usually supportive. If needed: azithromycin (FQ resistance increasing).
