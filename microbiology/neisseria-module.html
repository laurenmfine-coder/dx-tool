Neisseria Species

Gram-Negative Diplococci: N. gonorrhoeae & N. meningitidis

Gram-Negative Diplococci Oxidase Positive Intracellular Fastidious

🔬 Genus Overview

Neisseria are Gram-negative diplococci (kidney bean-shaped pairs) that are oxidase-positive and fastidious (require special media). The two pathogenic species are N. gonorrhoeae (gonorrhea) and N. meningitidis (meningitis, sepsis).

Key shared features:

  • Gram-negative diplococci (appear as pairs of kidney beans)
  • Oxidase POSITIVE (distinguishes from Enterobacteriaceae)
  • Intracellular pathogens (seen within PMNs)
  • Fastidious - require Thayer-Martin (chocolate agar + antibiotics) or modified Thayer-Martin media
  • Pili mediate attachment and undergo antigenic variation
  • IgA protease - cleaves secretory IgA on mucosal surfaces
Both Neisseria species are OXIDASE POSITIVE diplococci. They're the only Gram-negative diplococci you need to know! Distinguish them by: maltose fermentation (meningitidis +, gonorrhoeae -) and capsule (meningitidis has one, gonorrhoeae doesn't).

🔶 N. gonorrhoeae (Gonococcus)

The STI pathogen - "The Clap"

Clinical Syndromes:

  • Urethritis (men): Purulent urethral discharge, dysuria. Symptoms 2-5 days after exposure.
  • Cervicitis (women): Often asymptomatic! Mucopurulent discharge, friable cervix.
  • PID: Ascending infection → endometritis, salpingitis. Risk of infertility, ectopic pregnancy.
  • Disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI): Bacteremia → septic arthritis (usually monoarticular), tenosynovitis, pustular skin lesions.
  • Ophthalmia neonatorum: Neonatal conjunctivitis from birth canal. Prevented with erythromycin eye drops.
  • Pharyngitis, proctitis: From oral/anal sex

Treatment (2023 CDC):

Ceftriaxone 500mg IM x1 (1g if >150kg). Due to rising resistance, FQs no longer recommended. Always treat presumptively for chlamydia too (doxycycline 100mg BID x7d).

🔷 N. meningitidis (Meningococcus)

The meningitis/sepsis pathogen

Clinical Syndromes:

  • Meningitis: #2 cause of bacterial meningitis (after S. pneumoniae). Fever, headache, neck stiffness, photophobia.
  • Meningococcemia: Fulminant sepsis with petechial/purpuric rash. Can progress to DIC and shock within hours!
  • Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome: Bilateral adrenal hemorrhage from DIC → acute adrenal insufficiency. Often fatal.
  • Chronic meningococcemia: Rare. Recurrent fever, rash, arthralgias over weeks.

Treatment:

Ceftriaxone or penicillin G IV. For empiric meningitis treatment (before pathogen known), use ceftriaxone + vancomycin.

Prophylaxis (close contacts):

Rifampin, ciprofloxacin, or ceftriaxone for household/close contacts to prevent secondary cases.

Antigenic variation of pili and Opa proteins explains why there's no vaccine for gonorrhea - the surface antigens keep changing! Meningococcus can be vaccinated against because the CAPSULE (not surface proteins) is the vaccine target.

🧪 Laboratory Diagnosis

Culture

  • Thayer-Martin agar: Chocolate agar + VCN antibiotics (vancomycin, colistin, nystatin) to suppress normal flora
  • Requires: CO₂ enriched atmosphere, 35-37°C
  • Oxidase positive, Gram-negative diplococci

Sugar Fermentation

  • N. gonorrhoeae: Glucose only (no maltose)
  • N. meningitidis: Glucose AND maltose
  • Mnemonic: "MeninGococci ferMent Maltose" (both have M's)

Modern Diagnostics

  • NAAT (nucleic acid amplification): Now standard for gonorrhea screening. Highly sensitive, can use urine.
  • CSF for meningitis: Gram stain (diplococci in PMNs), culture, latex agglutination, PCR

Serogroup B meningococcus has a capsule that resembles human neural tissue (sialic acid) → poor immunogenicity → needed protein-based vaccine instead of polysaccharide conjugate.

🛡️ Complement Deficiency Connection

Patients with terminal complement deficiency (C5-C9, MAC) have dramatically increased risk of Neisseria infections (especially meningococcus). The membrane attack complex is crucial for killing Gram-negative bacteria with thin peptidoglycan layers.

⚠️ When to suspect complement deficiency:

  • Recurrent Neisseria infections (meningitis, DGI)
  • Neisseria infection with unusual serogroups (Y, W)
  • First episode at older age (>5 years)
  • Family history of Neisseria infections

Screen with CH50 (total complement). These patients need meningococcal vaccination AND prophylactic antibiotics.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • Both: Gram-negative diplococci, oxidase+, intracellular (in PMNs), IgA protease, pili with antigenic variation
  • N. gonorrhoeae: NO capsule, ferments glucose ONLY, STI (urethritis, cervicitis, PID, DGI), no vaccine. Treat: Ceftriaxone IM.
  • N. meningitidis: HAS capsule (vaccine target), ferments glucose + MALTOSE, respiratory spread, meningitis/meningococcemia. Vaccines: MenACWY, MenB.
  • Waterhouse-Friderichsen: Bilateral adrenal hemorrhage from meningococcal DIC
  • Complement deficiency (C5-C9): High risk for recurrent Neisseria infections
  • Prophylaxis: Rifampin or cipro for close contacts of meningitis cases
  • Lab: Thayer-Martin agar, sugar fermentation (MeninGococcus ferMents Maltose)
