🔬 Overview

Mucormycosis is caused by fungi in the order Mucorales (Rhizopus, Mucor, Rhizomucor). Rapidly progressive, angioinvasive infection with high mortality. Requires BOTH antifungal therapy AND surgical debridement.

🔬 Morphology

Broad, ribbon-like, pauciseptate (few septa) hyphae with irregular 90° branching. Contrast with Aspergillus: narrow, septate, 45° branching.

🩸 Angioinvasion

Invades blood vessels → thrombosis → tissue necrosis (black eschar). Spreads rapidly along vessels.

🔍 Mucor vs Aspergillus — HIGH YIELD!

Board Pearl: 90° = Mucor. 45° = Aspergillus. This distinction on histopathology is extremely high-yield!

🎯 Risk Factors

🍬 DKA

Classic association. Acidosis + hyperglycemia + elevated free iron = perfect environment. Ketoacidosis impairs neutrophil function.

⚪ Neutropenia

Hematologic malignancy, HSCT, chemotherapy.

🩸 Iron Overload

Deferoxamine therapy (provides iron to fungus!), transfusion-related hemosiderosis.

Other risks: Solid organ transplant, high-dose steroids, trauma/burns, IV drug use (contaminated needles).

🏥 Clinical Syndromes

Rhino-Orbital-Cerebral (Most Common)

👃 Rhinocerebral

Starts in sinuses → orbits → brain. Facial pain, nasal congestion, bloody discharge. Black necrotic eschar on palate or turbinates = classic finding.

👁️ Orbital Signs

Periorbital edema, proptosis, vision loss, ophthalmoplegia. Cavernous sinus thrombosis possible.

Other Forms

  • Pulmonary: Neutropenic patients; similar to invasive aspergillosis but more hemorrhagic
  • Cutaneous: Trauma, burns, contaminated dressings
  • GI: Premature neonates, malnourished patients
  • Disseminated: Very high mortality
Classic Vignette: Patient in DKA + facial pain + black necrotic eschar on palate + proptosis = Rhinocerebral mucormycosis! Start amphotericin B and call ENT/surgery STAT.
🧪 Diagnosis
  • Histopathology: Gold standard — broad, pauciseptate, 90° branching hyphae invading tissue/vessels
  • Culture: Can grow on standard media; cottony white-gray colonies; confirm species
  • CT/MRI: Sinus disease, orbital involvement, intracranial extension
  • Note: No useful serologic or antigen tests (unlike Aspergillus galactomannan)
💊 Treatment
⚠️ BOTH Medical AND Surgical Treatment Required
  • Antifungals alone are insufficient — surgery is essential
  • Mortality remains 40-80% even with optimal therapy
  • Control underlying condition (correct DKA, stop deferoxamine, reduce immunosuppression)
Antifungal Therapy
  • Liposomal Amphotericin B 5-10 mg/kg/day — first-line
  • Isavuconazole or Posaconazole — step-down or salvage
  • Voriconazole has NO activity against Mucorales!
  • Duration: Weeks to months; until clinical resolution + immune recovery
Surgical Debridement
  • Aggressive surgical removal of necrotic tissue — often disfiguring
  • May require repeated debridements
  • Orbital exenteration sometimes necessary to save life
Voriconazole Warning: Unlike Aspergillus, Mucorales are RESISTANT to voriconazole. If a patient on voriconazole prophylaxis develops breakthrough mold infection, think Mucor!
📝 High-Yield Summary
  • Morphology: Broad, ribbon-like, pauciseptate, 90° branching (vs Aspergillus 45°)
  • Risk factors: DKA (#1), neutropenia, iron overload, deferoxamine
  • Rhinocerebral: Black eschar on palate/turbinates, orbital involvement, rapid progression
  • Angioinvasive: Vessel invasion → thrombosis → necrosis
  • Treatment: Liposomal amphotericin B + aggressive surgical debridement
  • Voriconazole: NO activity — Mucor is resistant
