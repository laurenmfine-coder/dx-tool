🦠 Group B Streptococcus (S. agalactiae)

The Neonatal Pathogen - #1 Cause of Neonatal Sepsis & Meningitis

🔬 Microbiology Basics

Gram StainGram-positive cocci in CHAINS
Hemolysisβ-hemolytic (narrow zone of hemolysis)
CatalaseNEGATIVE
Lancefield GroupGroup B
BacitracinRESISTANT (vs GAS which is sensitive)

CapsulePolysaccharide capsule - 10 serotypes (Ia, Ib, II-IX). Type III = most neonatal meningitis.
CAMP Test: GBS produces CAMP factor, which enhances hemolysis by S. aureus β-toxin (sphingomyelinase). Results in an arrowhead-shaped zone of complete hemolysis.

🏠 Colonization & Transmission

  • Colonization: ~25% of pregnant women are colonized (vaginal/rectal)
  • Transmission to neonate: Ascending infection in utero or during passage through birth canal
  • Risk of neonatal disease: 1-2% of colonized mothers' babies (without prophylaxis)
  • Adult colonization: GI tract, vagina; usually asymptomatic

🚨 Clinical Presentation of Neonatal GBS

  • Respiratory distress (tachypnea, grunting, retractions)
  • Temperature instability (fever OR hypothermia)
  • Lethargy, poor feeding
  • Apnea, bradycardia
  • Hypotension, signs of septic shock
  • Seizures (if meningitis)

🛡️ Prevention - Intrapartum Antibiotic Prophylaxis (IAP)

Universal Screening Approach (CDC/ACOG Recommended)

  • Screen ALL pregnant women at 36 0/7 - 37 6/7 weeks gestation
  • Vaginal-rectal swab for GBS culture
  • Results valid for 5 weeks

Indications for IAP

Give IAP if:
✓ Positive GBS culture in current pregnancy
✓ GBS bacteriuria in current pregnancy (any amount)
✓ Previous infant with invasive GBS disease
✓ Unknown GBS status AND: preterm labor (<37 weeks), PROM ≥18 hours, OR intrapartum fever ≥100.4°F (38°C)

IAP Regimen

AgentDoseNotes
Penicillin G5 million units IV load, then 2.5-3 million q4hPREFERRED - narrow spectrum
Ampicillin2g IV load, then 1g q4hAlternative first-line
Cefazolin2g IV load, then 1g q8hFor PCN allergy without anaphylaxis risk
Clindamycin900mg IV q8hOnly if susceptible AND high-risk PCN allergy
Vancomycin20 mg/kg IV q8h (max 2g/dose)If clindamycin-resistant AND high-risk PCN allergy
Adequate IAP: Penicillin or ampicillin ≥4 hours before delivery provides optimal protection. The goal is to achieve therapeutic levels in the fetus/amniotic fluid.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Gram-positive cocci in chains, β-hemolytic, catalase(-), bacitracin-resistant, CAMP test(+), hippurate(+)
  • Colonization: ~25% pregnant women; vaginal/rectal
  • #1 cause of neonatal sepsis and meningitis
  • Early-onset (0-6 days): Vertical transmission, sepsis/pneumonia. PREVENTED by IAP.
  • Late-onset (7-90 days): Often meningitis (type III). NOT prevented by IAP.
  • Screening: All pregnant women at 36-37 weeks (vaginal-rectal swab)
  • IAP indications: GBS(+) culture, GBS bacteriuria, prior GBS infant, unknown status with risk factors
  • IAP regimen: Penicillin G or ampicillin IV ≥4 hours before delivery
  • Adult disease: Increasing in diabetics, elderly (skin infections, osteo, bacteremia)
  • Treatment: Penicillin (no resistance). Clindamycin resistance increasing.
