🔬 Virology

🧬 Structure

Enveloped virus with 8 segments of negative-sense ssRNA. Segmented genome allows genetic reassortment. Surface studded with hemagglutinin (HA) and neuraminidase (NA) glycoproteins.

🔑 Hemagglutinin (HA)

Binds sialic acid receptors on respiratory epithelium → viral entry. Target of neutralizing antibodies. 18 subtypes (H1-H18). Key determinant of host range.

✂️ Neuraminidase (NA)

Cleaves sialic acid → releases new virions from infected cells. 11 subtypes (N1-N11). Target of oseltamivir and zanamivir.

Influenza Types

Type Epidemiology Antigenic Variation Pandemic Potential
Influenza A Humans, birds, pigs, horses, others Drift AND shift YES — all pandemics
Influenza B Humans only Drift only (no subtypes) No (no animal reservoir)
Influenza C Humans; mild disease Minimal No

🧬 Antigenic Variation (High-Yield!)

Antigenic DRIFT

Minor mutations in HA/NA genes (point mutations). Occurs in BOTH A and B. Causes seasonal epidemics. Requires annual vaccine updates.

Antigenic SHIFT

Major change — reassortment of genome segments (requires co-infection with 2 strains). ONLY Influenza A. Creates novel subtypes → PANDEMICS. Population has no immunity.

Board Pearl: Antigenic SHIFT = Segmented genome + Swine/bird + Serious (pandemic). Requires reassortment in animal host (usually pigs = "mixing vessel"). Only Influenza A can shift because only A has animal reservoirs.

🏥 Clinical Features

📋 Classic Presentation

Abrupt onset: high fever, myalgias, headache, malaise, nonproductive cough, sore throat. "Flu feels like you were hit by a truck." Incubation 1-4 days.

🆚 Flu vs Cold

Flu: Sudden onset, high fever, severe myalgias, prostration. Cold: Gradual onset, low/no fever, milder symptoms, prominent rhinorrhea.

📅 Seasonality

Northern hemisphere: October-May, peaks December-February. Transmission: respiratory droplets + contact. R₀ ~1.3-1.5 for seasonal flu.

Complications

🫁 Primary Viral Pneumonia

Direct viral damage to lungs. Rapid progression to ARDS. High mortality. Most common in pregnant women, immunocompromised.

🦠 Secondary Bacterial Pneumonia

Typically S. aureus (including MRSA), S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae. Biphasic illness: improving flu then sudden worsening. Most common cause of flu-related death.

⚠️ Other Complications

Myocarditis, encephalitis, Reye syndrome (aspirin + viral illness in children), myositis/rhabdomyolysis, COPD/asthma exacerbation.

⚠️ High-Risk Groups for Severe Disease

  • Age ≥65 years or <5 years (especially <2 years)
  • Pregnancy and up to 2 weeks postpartum
  • Chronic medical conditions (heart, lung, kidney, liver, diabetes, immunosuppression)
  • Morbid obesity (BMI ≥40)
  • Nursing home residents
  • American Indians/Alaska Natives

🧪 Diagnosis

Test Time to Result Sensitivity Notes
RT-PCR Hours High (>95%) Gold standard; detects and types A/B; subtypes A
Rapid Antigen (RIDT) 15-30 min Moderate (50-70%) Point-of-care; negative does NOT rule out flu
Rapid Molecular 15-30 min High (~95%) Combines speed of RIDT with accuracy of PCR
Viral Culture Days Variable For surveillance/characterization; not clinical care
Clinical Pearl: During flu season with known community activity, clinical diagnosis often sufficient to initiate treatment. Don't delay empiric oseltamivir waiting for test results in high-risk patients.

💊 Treatment

Neuraminidase Inhibitors

  • Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) 75mg PO BID × 5 days — First-line; oral
  • Zanamivir (Relenza) 10mg inhaled BID × 5 days — Avoid in asthma/COPD
  • Peramivir (Rapivab) 600mg IV once — Single dose; for patients who can't take oral

Cap-Dependent Endonuclease Inhibitor

  • Baloxavir (Xofluza) 40-80mg PO once — Single dose; approved for uncomplicated flu
  • Different mechanism: inhibits viral replication initiation
  • Resistance developing; not first-line for high-risk or hospitalized
Timing Matters: Antivirals most effective when started within 48 hours of symptom onset. However, treat hospitalized patients and high-risk individuals regardless of timing — still provides benefit even if started late.

Who to Treat

  • Always treat: Hospitalized patients, severe/progressive illness, high-risk groups
  • Consider treating: Outpatients with uncomplicated flu if within 48 hours
  • Don't need treatment: Low-risk outpatients presenting >48 hours with mild symptoms (supportive care)

⚠️ Resistance

  • Adamantanes (amantadine, rimantadine) NO LONGER RECOMMENDED — widespread resistance
  • Oseltamivir resistance: H275Y mutation in NA; rare but monitor
  • Baloxavir: I38X mutations developing; concern for rapid resistance

💉 Prevention

Annual Influenza Vaccination

  • Who: Everyone ≥6 months of age (universal recommendation)
  • When: Ideally by end of October; continue through flu season
  • Types: Inactivated (IIV), recombinant (RIV), live attenuated (LAIV — nasal spray)
  • High-dose/adjuvanted: Available for ≥65 years — enhanced immunogenicity
  • Composition: Updated annually (typically 2 A strains + 1-2 B strains)
  • Contraindications: Severe allergy to vaccine component; LAIV contraindicated in immunocompromised, pregnant, <2 years

Chemoprophylaxis

  • Post-exposure: Oseltamivir 75mg daily × 7-10 days
  • Indications: High-risk unvaccinated exposed contacts; outbreaks in long-term care
  • NOT a substitute for vaccination

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Orthomyxovirus, enveloped, segmented (−) ssRNA, HA and NA surface proteins
  • Types: A (pandemics, drift + shift), B (epidemics, drift only), C (mild)
  • Antigenic variation: DRIFT = minor mutations (seasonal); SHIFT = reassortment (pandemic)
  • Clinical: Abrupt onset fever, myalgias, cough, prostration
  • Complications: Viral pneumonia, secondary bacterial pneumonia (S. aureus!), myocarditis
  • Diagnosis: RT-PCR gold standard; rapid antigen lacks sensitivity
  • Treatment: Oseltamivir within 48h; always treat hospitalized/high-risk regardless of timing
  • Prevention: Annual vaccination for everyone ≥6 months
