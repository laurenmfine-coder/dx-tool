🔬 Organism Overview

Treponema pallidum is a thin, tightly coiled spirochete that causes syphilis. It cannot be cultured on artificial media and is too thin to visualize with standard Gram stain. Known as "The Great Imitator" because it can mimic many other diseases.

🔬 Morphology

Thin spirochete (0.1-0.2 μm wide), 6-20 μm long, tight regular coils. Corkscrew motility via endoflagella (axial filaments).

🧫 Cannot Culture

Cannot grow on artificial media — requires living cells. Diagnosis relies on serology and direct visualization.

🔍 Visualization

Darkfield microscopy or direct fluorescent antibody (DFA) from lesion exudate. Not seen on Gram stain.

Board Pearl: The 3 pathogenic spirochetes: Treponema (syphilis), Borrelia (Lyme, relapsing fever), Leptospira (leptospirosis). Remember: "TBL" or "The Big Lie" — syphilis is the great imitator!

📊 Stages of Syphilis

PRIMARY SYPHILIS (10-90 days after exposure)

Chancre: Single, painless, clean-based ulcer with indurated edges at site of inoculation (usually genital). Highly infectious. Heals spontaneously in 3-6 weeks even without treatment. Painless regional lymphadenopathy (bilateral, non-tender, rubbery).

SECONDARY SYPHILIS (4-10 weeks after chancre)

Systemic dissemination: Diffuse maculopapular rash including palms and soles (classic!), condyloma lata (moist, flat genital warts), mucous patches, generalized lymphadenopathy, constitutional symptoms (fever, malaise, arthralgias). May have hepatitis, nephritis, uveitis. Highly infectious. Resolves spontaneously.

LATENT SYPHILIS (asymptomatic)

Early latent: <1 year since infection; may still be infectious. Late latent: >1 year or unknown duration; non-infectious except vertically. Positive serology, no clinical manifestations. Without treatment, 1/3 progress to tertiary.

TERTIARY SYPHILIS (years to decades later)

Three forms: (1) Gummatous: Granulomatous lesions in skin, bone, liver. (2) Cardiovascular: Aortitis → ascending aortic aneurysm, aortic regurgitation. (3) Neurosyphilis: Tabes dorsalis, general paresis. Rare in antibiotic era.

Classic Board Question: Rash on palms and soles = Think syphilis (secondary), Rocky Mountain Spotted Fever, or hand-foot-mouth disease. Secondary syphilis rash is NOT itchy (unlike most other rashes).

🧠 Neurosyphilis

Can occur at ANY stage of syphilis, though classically associated with tertiary. Requires CSF examination for diagnosis.

Type Features Classic Findings
Asymptomatic CSF abnormalities only Positive CSF VDRL, pleocytosis
Meningitis Early neurosyphilis Headache, cranial nerve palsies (especially VII, VIII)
Meningovascular Stroke in young patient Endarteritis → ischemic stroke, especially MCA territory
Tabes dorsalis Late; posterior columns Ataxia, lightning pains, Argyll Robertson pupils, Charcot joints
General paresis Late; frontal cortex Personality changes, dementia, psychosis — "paresis of the insane"
Argyll Robertson Pupils: "Prostitute's pupils" — accommodate but do not react to light. Small, irregular, bilateral. Pathognomonic for neurosyphilis.

🧪 Diagnosis

Serologic Testing (Two-Step Algorithm)

Test Type Examples Characteristics
Nontreponemal (screening) RPR, VDRL Detect antibodies to cardiolipin. Quantitative (titers). False positives possible. Decline with treatment.
Treponemal (confirmatory) FTA-ABS, TP-PA, EIA/CIA Detect antibodies to T. pallidum. Usually remain positive for life. More specific.

🔄 Traditional Algorithm

1. Screen with RPR or VDRL
→ 2. If positive, confirm with treponemal test (FTA-ABS or TP-PA)
→ 3. Both positive = Active or past syphilis; treat based on history/staging

🔄 Reverse Algorithm (increasingly common)

1. Screen with treponemal EIA/CIA (automated, cheaper)
→ 2. If positive, confirm with RPR (quantitative)
→ 3. If discordant, do TP-PA as tiebreaker

⚠️ False Positive Nontreponemal Tests

  • Pregnancy
  • Autoimmune diseases (SLE, antiphospholipid syndrome)
  • IV drug use
  • Other infections (HIV, EBV, hepatitis, malaria)
  • Advanced age
Prozone Phenomenon: Very high antibody titers can cause false-negative RPR (antibody excess). If clinical suspicion high but RPR negative, request dilution. Classic scenario: secondary syphilis with negative RPR.

💊 Treatment

Primary, Secondary, Early Latent (<1 year)

  • Benzathine Penicillin G 2.4 million units IM × 1 dose
  • Penicillin allergy: Doxycycline 100mg BID × 14 days

Late Latent (>1 year), Latent Unknown Duration, Tertiary (non-neuro)

  • Benzathine Penicillin G 2.4 million units IM weekly × 3 doses
  • Penicillin allergy: Doxycycline 100mg BID × 28 days

Neurosyphilis (including ocular/otic)

  • Aqueous Penicillin G 18-24 million units/day IV (3-4 MU q4h) × 10-14 days
  • Alternative: Procaine PCN 2.4 MU IM daily + Probenecid 500mg PO QID × 10-14 days
  • Penicillin allergy: DESENSITIZE — there is no proven alternative for neurosyphilis

⚠️ Jarisch-Herxheimer Reaction

  • Acute febrile reaction within 24 hours of treatment
  • Fever, headache, myalgias, worsening of skin lesions
  • Due to release of cytokines from dying spirochetes
  • More common in early syphilis (high organism burden)
  • Self-limited; supportive care only. Does NOT mean penicillin allergy!
  • Warn pregnant patients — can precipitate preterm labor

🤰 Congenital Syphilis

📋 Early Manifestations (<2 years)

Hepatosplenomegaly, jaundice, rhinitis ("snuffles"), maculopapular rash, osteochondritis/periostitis, pseudoparalysis of Parrot.

📋 Late Manifestations (>2 years)

Hutchinson teeth (notched incisors), mulberry molars, interstitial keratitis, saddle nose, saber shins, Clutton joints.

🛡️ Prevention

Screen ALL pregnant women at first prenatal visit. Rescreen in 3rd trimester and at delivery if high risk. Treat with penicillin (desensitize if allergic).

Hutchinson Triad: Hutchinson teeth + interstitial keratitis + CN VIII deafness = classic stigmata of congenital syphilis.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Spirochete, too thin for Gram stain, darkfield microscopy, cannot culture
  • Primary: Painless chancre, heals spontaneously
  • Secondary: Rash on palms/soles, condyloma lata, mucous patches — highly infectious
  • Tertiary: Gummas, cardiovascular (aortitis), neurosyphilis
  • Neurosyphilis: Argyll Robertson pupils, tabes dorsalis, general paresis
  • Diagnosis: Nontreponemal (RPR/VDRL) + Treponemal (FTA-ABS/TP-PA)
  • Treatment: Penicillin G (IM for early, IV for neuro). No alternatives for neurosyphilis — must desensitize.
  • Jarisch-Herxheimer: Fever within 24h of treatment — NOT an allergy
