🔬 Organism Overview

Aspergillus is a ubiquitous saprophytic mold found in soil, decaying vegetation, and indoor environments. Humans inhale hundreds of conidia (spores) daily without consequence. Disease occurs when host defenses are impaired (neutropenia, steroids, CGD) or when there is abnormal airway architecture (cavities, bronchiectasis).

🔬 Microscopy

Septate hyphae with acute-angle branching (45°). Contrast with Mucor/Rhizopus: broad, ribbon-like, pauciseptate, 90° branching.

🧫 Culture

Grows rapidly on Sabouraud agar. Characteristic conidiophore ("aspergillum" = holy water sprinkler). Species ID by morphology or molecular methods.

📊 Key Species

A. fumigatus (most common, ~90% of invasive). A. flavus (sinusitis, cutaneous). A. niger (otomycosis). A. terreus (amphotericin resistant!).

Board Pearl: Septate hyphae with 45° branching = Aspergillus. Broad, ribbon-like, pauciseptate with 90° branching = Mucormycosis. This distinction is critical on histopathology!

📊 Spectrum of Aspergillus Disease

Syndrome Host Pathophysiology Key Features
Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA) Asthma, Cystic Fibrosis Type I + III hypersensitivity to Aspergillus antigens Wheezing, mucus plugs, central bronchiectasis, elevated IgE, eosinophilia
Aspergilloma Pre-existing cavity (TB, sarcoid, emphysema) Fungus ball in cavity; no tissue invasion "Crescent sign" on CT, hemoptysis (can be massive), positive precipitins
Chronic Pulmonary Aspergillosis Mild immunocompromise, structural lung disease Slowly progressive; semi-invasive Cavitation, weight loss, cough; months to years progression
Invasive Pulmonary Aspergillosis (IPA) Severe neutropenia, HSCT, solid organ transplant, high-dose steroids Angioinvasion → hemorrhagic infarcts Fever refractory to antibiotics, pleuritic pain, hemoptysis, "halo sign"
Invasive Sinusitis Neutropenia, diabetes (also consider Mucor) Direct tissue invasion from sinuses Facial pain, nasal eschar, can extend to orbit/brain

Invasive Pulmonary Aspergillosis (IPA) — High-Yield

🎯 Risk Factors

Prolonged neutropenia (>10 days), HSCT (especially GVHD), solid organ transplant, high-dose corticosteroids, CGD, advanced AIDS, ICU patients with severe influenza/COVID.

📷 Imaging

Halo sign (early): nodule with surrounding ground-glass (hemorrhage). Air crescent sign (late): recovering neutropenia, cavity forming around necrotic tissue.

⚠️ Prognosis

Mortality 30-95% depending on host. Early diagnosis and treatment critical. Immune reconstitution essential for cure.

Clinical Pearl: In neutropenic patients, fever + pleuritic chest pain + hemoptysis not responding to antibiotics = think invasive aspergillosis. Don't wait for culture — start empiric voriconazole and get CT chest.

🧪 Diagnosis

Test Use Notes
Galactomannan (GM) Serum or BAL; screening and diagnosis of IPA Cell wall component. Serum sensitivity ~70% in neutropenic; higher in BAL. False positives: piperacillin-tazobactam (historical), some foods.
β-D-glucan Pan-fungal marker Elevated in Aspergillus, Candida, PCP. Not specific. Useful as part of composite diagnosis.
Culture Respiratory specimens, tissue Positive culture from sterile site = definitive. From sputum: may be colonization vs. infection — interpret clinically.
Histopathology Tissue biopsy Gold standard: septate, 45° branching hyphae invading tissue. Use GMS or PAS stain.
CT Imaging Essential in suspected IPA Halo sign (early), air crescent (late), nodules, wedge-shaped infarcts.
Aspergillus PCR Blood, BAL Increasingly available; good NPV; helps in combination with GM.
EORTC/MSG Criteria: Diagnosis of "proven" IPA requires histopathology or positive culture from sterile site. "Probable" requires host factors + clinical features + mycological evidence (GM, culture, PCR). In practice, often treat empirically based on clinical suspicion.

💊 Treatment

Invasive Aspergillosis

  • First-line: Voriconazole — IV 6mg/kg q12h × 2 doses, then 4mg/kg q12h; transition to oral when stable
  • Alternative: Isavuconazole — better tolerated, fewer drug interactions
  • Salvage: Liposomal amphotericin B — for voriconazole failure/intolerance
  • Combination therapy: Voriconazole + echinocandin considered in severe disease (HSCT)
  • Duration: Minimum 6-12 weeks; until immunosuppression resolved and lesions stable/improved

Other Aspergillus Syndromes

  • ABPA: Systemic corticosteroids (prednisone) ± itraconazole as steroid-sparing agent
  • Aspergilloma: Observation if asymptomatic; surgical resection for recurrent hemoptysis; antifungals often ineffective
  • Chronic pulmonary: Long-term oral azoles (itraconazole or voriconazole) for ≥6 months

⚠️ Treatment Considerations

  • A. terreus: Intrinsically resistant to amphotericin B — use voriconazole
  • Azole resistance: Increasing, especially in Europe; consider susceptibility testing
  • Voriconazole toxicity: Visual disturbances, hepatotoxicity, photosensitivity, periostitis (long-term)
  • Drug interactions: Voriconazole is CYP450 inhibitor — check interactions!
  • TDM: Voriconazole levels recommended (target trough 1-5 mg/L)
Source Control: Surgical resection may be needed for localized disease, especially before further immunosuppression (e.g., next chemo cycle) or for hemoptysis from aspergilloma.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Septate hyphae with 45° acute-angle branching (vs. Mucor: 90°, pauciseptate)
  • Species: A. fumigatus most common; A. terreus is amphotericin-resistant
  • Spectrum: ABPA (allergic) → Aspergilloma (fungus ball) → Chronic → Invasive
  • IPA risk: Neutropenia, HSCT, transplant, high-dose steroids, CGD
  • Imaging: Halo sign (early), air crescent sign (late)
  • Diagnosis: Galactomannan (serum/BAL), CT, histopath; culture may be colonization
  • Treatment: Voriconazole first-line for invasive; steroids for ABPA; surgery for aspergilloma with hemoptysis
