🔬 Organism Overview

🦠 Morphology

Large spirochete with loose, irregular coils. Motile via periplasmic flagella (endoflagella). Can be visualized with Giemsa or silver stain.

🪲 Vector

Ixodes scapularis (deer tick/black-legged tick) in Northeast/Midwest. Ixodes pacificus in West Coast. Nymphs most common source (small, hard to see).

🦌 Reservoir

White-footed mouse (primary), white-tailed deer (maintains tick population). Ticks acquire infection from mice, not deer.

🗺️ Geographic Distribution

Highly endemic: Northeast (Connecticut to Maine), Mid-Atlantic, Upper Midwest (Wisconsin, Minnesota). ~95% of US cases from these regions. Peak transmission: late spring to early fall (May-August). Requires tick attachment for 36-48 hours for transmission — early tick removal prevents infection.

Board Pearl: Ixodes ticks also transmit Anaplasma (anaplasmosis) and Babesia (babesiosis). Consider coinfection if patient with Lyme has unusually severe symptoms, high fever, or doesn't improve with doxycycline.

📊 Clinical Stages of Lyme Disease

🟢 STAGE 1: Early Localized (days to weeks)

  • Erythema migrans (EM): "Bull's eye" rash at bite site
  • Expanding red patch, often with central clearing
  • ≥5 cm diameter (average 15 cm)
  • Usually NOT itchy or painful
  • Present in 70-80% of cases
  • Flu-like symptoms: fatigue, fever, myalgias, arthralgias

🟡 STAGE 2: Early Disseminated (weeks to months)

  • Multiple EM lesions (hematogenous spread)
  • Cardiac: AV block (usually 1st or 2nd degree), myocarditis
  • Neurologic: CN VII palsy (bilateral!), meningitis, radiculopathy
  • Migratory arthralgias/myalgias
  • Lymphocytic meningitis with cranial neuropathy = classic

🔴 STAGE 3: Late Disseminated (months to years)

  • Lyme arthritis: Intermittent monoarticular or oligoarticular
  • Large joints, especially KNEE
  • Swelling > pain
  • Late neuroborreliosis: Encephalopathy, polyneuropathy
  • Acrodermatitis chronica atrophicans (Europe)
🎯

Erythema Migrans — The Pathognomonic Rash

Expanding erythematous patch with central clearing ("target" or "bull's eye"). Appears 7-14 days after tick bite. Diagnostic — if present, treat empirically WITHOUT waiting for serology. Serology often NEGATIVE in early localized disease!

Clinical Pearl: Bilateral facial nerve palsy in summer/endemic area = Lyme until proven otherwise. Also consider Lyme in any young patient with new heart block.

🧪 Diagnosis

Two-Tier Serologic Testing (CDC Recommended)

Step Test Interpretation
Step 1 EIA or IFA (sensitive screening) If negative → No Lyme (unless very early). If positive/equivocal → proceed to Step 2
Step 2 Western blot (specific confirmation) IgM (early): ≥2 of 3 bands. IgG (late): ≥5 of 10 bands

⚠️ Diagnostic Pitfalls

  • Erythema migrans = Clinical diagnosis — treat WITHOUT waiting for serology (often negative early)
  • Serology takes 2-4 weeks to become positive
  • IgM alone positive >4 weeks into illness = likely false positive
  • Antibodies persist for years even after treatment — cannot use to assess cure
  • Do NOT test asymptomatic patients or use as screening

When to Do Lumbar Puncture

Consider LP if neurologic symptoms suggest CNS involvement (meningitis, encephalopathy, myelitis). CSF shows: lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein, positive Lyme antibody index (CSF:serum ratio).

Modified Two-Tier Testing: Newer approach uses two different EIAs instead of Western blot — faster, equally accurate. Check local lab protocols.

💊 Treatment

Early Localized / Early Disseminated (without neuro/cardiac)

  • Doxycycline 100mg BID × 10-14 days — First-line for adults
  • Amoxicillin 500mg TID × 14 days — First-line for children <8, pregnancy
  • Cefuroxime axetil 500mg BID × 14 days — Alternative
  • Azithromycin only if above contraindicated (less effective)

Lyme Arthritis

  • Doxycycline 100mg BID × 28 days, OR
  • Amoxicillin 500mg TID × 28 days
  • If persistent after oral course → IV ceftriaxone × 2-4 weeks

Neurologic Lyme (meningitis, radiculopathy, CN palsy)

  • IV Ceftriaxone 2g daily × 14-21 days — for meningitis, encephalitis
  • Oral doxycycline × 14-21 days — acceptable for isolated facial palsy without meningitis

Lyme Carditis

  • High-degree AV block: IV ceftriaxone + cardiac monitoring, consider temporary pacer
  • 1st degree or mild: Oral doxycycline may be sufficient
  • Most resolve within 1-2 weeks with antibiotics
Post-Treatment Lyme Disease Syndrome: ~10-20% have persistent fatigue, pain, cognitive complaints after appropriate treatment. NOT due to persistent infection — prolonged/repeated antibiotics NOT recommended. Supportive care only.

🛡️ Prevention

🔍 Tick Checks

Daily tick checks after outdoor activity. Check warm, moist areas: groin, armpits, scalp, behind ears. Remove ticks promptly with fine-tipped tweezers.

👕 Protective Clothing

Long sleeves, pants tucked into socks in endemic areas. Light-colored clothing to spot ticks. Permethrin-treated clothing effective.

💊 Prophylaxis

Single dose doxycycline 200mg within 72 hours of tick removal IF: endemic area + attached ≥36 hours + doxy not contraindicated. Reduces risk by ~87%.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Spirochete, transmitted by Ixodes tick (deer tick), requires 36-48h attachment
  • Geography: Northeast, Mid-Atlantic, Upper Midwest US
  • Stage 1: Erythema migrans (bull's eye) — pathognomonic, treat clinically
  • Stage 2: Multiple EM, facial palsy (bilateral!), heart block, meningitis
  • Stage 3: Lyme arthritis (large joints, especially knee)
  • Diagnosis: Two-tier serology (EIA → Western blot), but EM is clinical diagnosis
  • Treatment: Doxycycline (oral) for most; IV ceftriaxone for neuro/severe cardiac
  • Prophylaxis: Single dose doxy 200mg within 72h if high-risk exposure
  • Coinfections: Consider Anaplasma, Babesia if atypical/severe
