🔬 Organism Overview

Candida species are oval budding yeasts that are part of normal human flora (GI tract, skin, genitourinary). They become pathogenic in immunocompromised hosts or when normal barriers are disrupted. Candida albicans is the most common species, but non-albicans species (especially C. glabrata, C. auris) are increasingly important due to antifungal resistance.

🔬 Lab Identification

Gram stain: Gram-positive budding yeast. Culture: grows on standard media. C. albicans forms germ tubes in serum (distinguishing test). CHROMagar differentiates species by colony color.

🌡️ Dimorphism

C. albicans is dimorphic: yeast form in cooler temps, pseudohyphae/hyphae at body temperature (37°C). Hyphae important for tissue invasion and biofilm formation.

🛡️ Virulence Factors

Adhesins, biofilm formation (especially on catheters), secreted proteases and lipases, phenotypic switching, hyphal formation for tissue invasion.

Board Pearl: C. albicans produces germ tubes within 2-3 hours in serum at 37°C — rapid presumptive ID. Other species require additional testing (CHROMagar, MALDI-TOF, sequencing).

📊 Candida Species & Resistance Patterns

Species Prevalence Fluconazole Echinocandins Key Points
C. albicans ~50% Susceptible Susceptible Most common; germ tube positive
C. glabrata ~25% Often resistant Susceptible Intrinsically reduced fluconazole susceptibility; increasing echinocandin resistance
C. parapsilosis ~15% Susceptible Higher MIC Associated with TPN, hand carriage by HCWs; biofilms on catheters
C. tropicalis ~5% Susceptible Susceptible More common in malignancy, neutropenia
C. krusei ~2% Intrinsically resistant Susceptible Intrinsic fluconazole resistance; associated with prior fluconazole use
C. auris Emerging Often resistant Variable MDR, nosocomial transmission, environmental persistence, infection control emergency

⚠️ Candida auris — Emerging Threat

  • Multi-drug resistant: often resistant to fluconazole, variably to echinocandins, some to amphotericin B
  • Nosocomial transmission (patient-to-patient, environmental contamination)
  • Survives on surfaces for weeks — requires enhanced cleaning
  • Often misidentified by conventional methods — requires MALDI-TOF or sequencing
  • CDC urgent threat; requires contact precautions + notification

🏥 Clinical Syndromes

Mucocutaneous Candidiasis

👄 Oropharyngeal (Thrush)

White, curd-like plaques on oral mucosa that scrape off revealing erythematous base. Risk: HIV/AIDS, inhaled steroids, dentures, antibiotics, infants.

🍽️ Esophageal

Odynophagia, dysphagia in immunocompromised (AIDS-defining illness if CD4 <200). Dx: EGD shows white plaques. Treat with fluconazole.

👩 Vulvovaginal

Thick white "cottage cheese" discharge, vulvar pruritus and erythema. Common (75% of women have ≥1 episode). Usually C. albicans.

Invasive Candidiasis

🩸 Candidemia

Blood culture positive for Candida. Mortality 30-40%. Always pathogenic — NEVER a contaminant. Requires treatment + source control + ophthalmology consult.

❤️ Endocarditis

Large vegetations, high embolic risk. Often prosthetic valves or IVDU. Requires surgery + prolonged antifungal (amphotericin B ± flucytosine initially).

🫘 Hepatosplenic

Chronic disseminated candidiasis. Neutropenic patients recovering from prolonged neutropenia. "Bull's eye" lesions on imaging. Prolonged therapy required.

🎯 Risk Factors for Invasive Candidiasis

🏥 ICU admission
💉 Central venous catheters
🍽️ Total parenteral nutrition
💊 Broad-spectrum antibiotics
🩺 Abdominal surgery
⚪ Neutropenia
🔄 Hemodialysis
🫀 Prosthetic devices
Clinical Pearl: Candida in blood = always significant. Unlike coagulase-negative staph, Candida is NEVER a contaminant. Start empiric echinocandin immediately, remove all central lines if possible, and get ophthalmology consult (endophthalmitis in 10-15%).

🧪 Diagnosis

Test Use Notes
Blood culture Gold standard for candidemia Sensitivity ~50-70%; may take 1-3 days to turn positive
β-D-glucan Screening for invasive fungal infection Sensitive but not specific (elevated in many fungi, some bacteria, gauze contamination)
T2Candida Rapid detection from blood PCR-based; results in 3-5 hours; species-level ID
Tissue biopsy Deep-seated infection Shows yeast/pseudohyphae invading tissue
Candidemia Workup: Repeat blood cultures q24-48h until negative. Dilated fundoscopic exam (ophthalmology). Echocardiogram if prolonged candidemia, prosthetic valve, or clinical concern for endocarditis. Remove all central lines if feasible.

💊 Treatment

Candidemia / Invasive Candidiasis

  • Initial therapy: Echinocandin (caspofungin, micafungin, or anidulafungin)
  • Step-down: Fluconazole if species susceptible, clinically stable, negative follow-up cultures
  • Duration: 14 days after first negative blood culture AND resolution of symptoms
  • Source control: Remove all central lines, drain abscesses

Mucocutaneous Disease

  • Oropharyngeal thrush: Clotrimazole troches or nystatin swish-and-swallow; fluconazole for refractory
  • Esophageal: Fluconazole 200-400mg daily × 14-21 days
  • Vulvovaginal: Topical azole (clotrimazole, miconazole) or single-dose fluconazole 150mg

Special Situations

  • C. glabrata: Echinocandin preferred (often fluconazole resistant)
  • C. krusei: Echinocandin or voriconazole (intrinsic fluconazole resistance)
  • C. parapsilosis: Fluconazole often adequate (echinocandin MICs higher, though still effective)
  • CNS/eye: Amphotericin B + flucytosine (echinocandins don't penetrate well)
Why Echinocandins First? Fungicidal against Candida, well-tolerated, active against most species including fluconazole-resistant C. glabrata. Step down to fluconazole once species/susceptibilities known and patient improving.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Budding yeast ± pseudohyphae; C. albicans forms germ tubes
  • Species: C. albicans most common; C. glabrata often fluconazole-resistant; C. krusei intrinsically resistant; C. auris MDR emergency
  • Mucocutaneous: Thrush, esophagitis (AIDS), vulvovaginitis — treat with azoles
  • Invasive: Candidemia (30-40% mortality), endocarditis, hepatosplenic
  • Risk factors: ICU, central lines, TPN, antibiotics, surgery, neutropenia
  • Candidemia = always treat: Echinocandin first-line, remove lines, ophthalmology consult
  • Duration: 14 days after first negative culture
