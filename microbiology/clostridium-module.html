🦠 Clostridium Species

Spore-Forming Anaerobic Gram-Positive Rods - Toxin-Mediated Diseases

🔬 Common Features of Clostridia

MorphologyLarge Gram-positive RODS
SporesYES - terminal or subterminal endospores. Highly resistant to heat, chemicals, desiccation.
OxygenObligate ANAEROBES (except C. perfringens can tolerate brief O₂)
HabitatSoil, GI tract of animals and humans
PathogenesisTOXIN-mediated diseases (toxins are the key!)
Only TWO genera form spores: Bacillus (aerobic) and Clostridium (anaerobic). "Canned Bacillus, Closed-tridium" - Clostridium needs anaerobic (closed) environment!

🔴 Clostridium tetani - TETANUS

Pathogenesis

  • Source: Spores in soil enter wound (classically puncture wound, "rusty nail")
  • Germination: Spores germinate in anaerobic wound environment
  • Toxin: Produces TETANOSPASMIN (second deadliest toxin after botulinum)
  • Toxin action: Travels retrograde up motor neurons → inhibits release of inhibitory neurotransmitters (GABA, glycine) from Renshaw cells
  • Result: UNCONTROLLED muscle contraction (spastic paralysis)
Tetanus = SPASTIC paralysis. Toxin blocks inhibitory signals → muscles fire unopposed. Contrast with botulism (FLACCID paralysis - blocks excitatory ACh).

Clinical Presentation

Incubation3-21 days (shorter incubation = worse prognosis)
Trismus"Lockjaw" - masseter spasm (often first sign)
Risus sardonicusSustained facial muscle contraction → "sardonic smile"
OpisthotonosSevere arching of back from paraspinal muscle spasm
Autonomic instabilityBP fluctuations, arrhythmias, sweating
TriggersSpasms triggered by minor stimuli (noise, touch, light)

Treatment & Prevention

  • Wound care: Debridement essential (remove anaerobic environment)
  • Human tetanus immunoglobulin (TIG): Neutralizes circulating toxin (bound toxin is irreversible)
  • Antibiotics: Metronidazole (kills vegetative bacteria, prevents more toxin production)
  • Supportive: ICU, benzodiazepines for spasms, possible intubation/paralysis
  • Vaccination: Tetanus toxoid (DTaP for children, Tdap booster). Still need full vaccination series after infection!
Tetanus Prophylaxis for Wounds:
• Clean, minor wound: Td/Tdap if >10 years since last booster
• Dirty/contaminated wound: Td/Tdap if >5 years since last booster, PLUS TIG if <3 doses or unknown

🟡 Clostridium botulinum - BOTULISM

Pathogenesis

  • Toxin: BOTULINUM TOXIN - most potent toxin known! (LD₅₀ = 1-2 μg/kg)
  • Mechanism: Blocks ACh release at neuromuscular junction (cleaves SNARE proteins)
  • Result: FLACCID paralysis (descending)
Botulism = FLACCID paralysis (descending). Toxin blocks ACh release → muscles can't contract. Starts with cranial nerves (diplopia, dysphagia) then descends. OPPOSITE of tetanus!

Forms of Botulism

TypeSourceKey Features
Foodborne Ingestion of preformed toxin in improperly canned foods Classic: home-canned vegetables, honey (adults). Incubation 12-36 hours.
Infant (intestinal) Ingestion of spores → colonize gut → produce toxin in vivo HONEY is the classic source! "Floppy baby" syndrome. Age <1 year.
Wound Spores contaminate wound → local toxin production Associated with IVDU (especially "black tar" heroin). No GI symptoms.
Iatrogenic Therapeutic Botox overdose Rare. Cosmetic or medical injections.

Clinical Presentation

  • Descending paralysis: Cranial nerves first → bulbar symptoms (diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia) → trunk → extremities
  • "4 Ds": Diplopia, Dysarthria, Dysphonia, Dysphagia
  • Anticholinergic symptoms: Dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, dilated pupils
  • NO sensory deficits, NO fever (unlike GBS)
  • Respiratory failure can occur (diaphragm paralysis)

Treatment

  • Supportive care: Respiratory support is critical (may need prolonged ventilation)
  • Antitoxin: Equine antitoxin (adults) or BabyBIG/BIG-IV (infants). Doesn't reverse existing paralysis but prevents progression.
  • Wound botulism: Debridement + antibiotics
  • NO antibiotics for infant botulism (lysis may release more toxin)
Never give honey to infants <1 year! Their gut flora can't compete with C. botulinum spores, allowing colonization and toxin production.

🟠 Clostridium perfringens - GAS GANGRENE & FOOD POISONING

Unique Features

  • Double-zone hemolysis on blood agar (α-toxin lecithinase effect)
  • Relatively aerotolerant compared to other Clostridia
  • Multiple toxins: α-toxin (lecithinase) is most important for gas gangrene

Gas Gangrene (Clostridial Myonecrosis)

Risk factorsTrauma, surgery, vascular disease, contaminated wounds
Pathogenesisα-toxin (lecithinase) destroys cell membranes → tissue necrosis. Gas production from fermentation.
PresentationSevere pain, rapid progression, skin discoloration (bronze → black), crepitus (gas in tissue), foul smell
SystemicShock, multi-organ failure, very high mortality if untreated
Gas gangrene is a surgical EMERGENCY! Treatment: aggressive surgical debridement (may need amputation) + high-dose penicillin + clindamycin (inhibits toxin synthesis). Hyperbaric oxygen is adjunctive.

Food Poisoning

  • Source: Reheated meat dishes (spores survive cooking, germinate during cooling)
  • Toxin: Enterotoxin produced in gut (heat-labile)
  • Presentation: Watery diarrhea + cramping 8-16 hours after eating. NO vomiting (unlike S. aureus). Resolves in 24 hours.
  • Treatment: Supportive only

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • All Clostridia: Gram-positive rods, obligate anaerobes (mostly), SPORE-forming, TOXIN-mediated diseases
  • C. tetani: Tetanospasmin blocks GABA/glycine release → SPASTIC paralysis (lockjaw, opisthotonus). Treat with TIG + metronidazole + wound care.
  • C. botulinum: Botulinum toxin blocks ACh release → FLACCID DESCENDING paralysis. Infant botulism from honey. Treat with antitoxin + supportive (respiratory!).
  • C. perfringens: Gas gangrene (α-toxin lecithinase) + food poisoning (watery diarrhea, reheated meats). Gas gangrene = surgical emergency!
  • C. difficile: See separate C. diff module - CDI from antibiotic disruption of gut flora.
  • Memory aid: TETanus = TEnse (spastic); BOTulism = BOT out of gas (flaccid)
