🔬 Organism Overview

Escherichia coli is the most studied organism in biology - the "lab rat" of the microbial world. It's a normal inhabitant of the human GI tract (commensal), but certain strains have acquired virulence factors that make them pathogenic.

E. coli is a facultative anaerobe in the family Enterobacteriaceae. It ferments lactose (appears PINK on MacConkey agar) and produces indole from tryptophan - key biochemical features.

E. coli is the #1 cause of: (1) UTIs (especially uncomplicated cystitis), (2) Gram-negative neonatal meningitis, (3) Traveler's diarrhea (ETEC strain). It's also the most common cause of Gram-negative bacteremia/sepsis.

🦠 Diarrheagenic E. coli Pathotypes

There are several pathotypes of E. coli that cause diarrhea, each with distinct mechanisms. These are HIGH YIELD for MCAT and Step 1!

EHEC Enterohemorrhagic E. coli (O157:H7)

Mechanism: Shiga-like toxin (Stx1, Stx2) - inactivates 60S ribosome by cleaving rRNA → inhibits protein synthesis → cell death. Toxin damages endothelium, especially in kidneys and colon.
Clinical: Bloody diarrhea (hemorrhagic colitis), NO FEVER (distinguishes from Shigella). Can progress to HUS (Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome) - microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, acute kidney injury. Common source: undercooked beef, unpasteurized juice.

ETEC Enterotoxigenic E. coli

Mechanism: Heat-Labile toxin (LT) - activates adenylate cyclase → ↑cAMP → Cl⁻ secretion (like cholera toxin). Heat-Stable toxin (ST) - activates guanylate cyclase → ↑cGMP → similar effect.
Clinical: Traveler's diarrhea - watery diarrhea, no blood, no fever. Self-limited. "Labile in the Air (cAMP), Stable on the Ground (cGMP)"

EIEC Enteroinvasive E. coli

Mechanism: Invades intestinal epithelium (similar to Shigella) - no toxin, just tissue invasion and destruction.
Clinical: Dysentery-like illness - bloody diarrhea WITH fever, inflammatory cells in stool. Clinically indistinguishable from Shigella.

EPEC Enteropathogenic E. coli

Mechanism: "Attaching and Effacing" lesions - bacteria attach intimately to enterocytes, efface (destroy) microvilli via Type III secretion system. No toxin production.
Clinical: Watery diarrhea in infants (developing countries). Major cause of infant diarrhea worldwide.

EAEC Enteroaggregative E. coli

Mechanism: "Stacked brick" adherence pattern - bacteria aggregate on intestinal surface forming biofilm. Produce enterotoxins and cytotoxins.
Clinical: Persistent watery diarrhea (>14 days), especially in children, HIV patients, and travelers.

⚠️ EHEC/O157:H7 - Critical Points

  • DO NOT give antibiotics! Antibiotic lysis releases more Shiga toxin → increases HUS risk
  • DO NOT give antimotility agents (loperamide) - prolongs toxin exposure
  • Supportive care only - fluids, monitor for HUS (starts 5-10 days after diarrhea onset)
  • HUS triad: Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes), thrombocytopenia, AKI
Memory trick for toxin mechanisms: "Labile like the Air, Stable on the Ground" - Heat-Labile toxin increases cAMP; Heat-Stable toxin increases cGMP. Also remember: LT = Like choLera Toxin (both ↑cAMP via Gs activation).

🏥 Extraintestinal E. coli (ExPEC)

Beyond diarrheal disease, E. coli causes several important extraintestinal infections:

Syndrome Pathogenesis High-Yield Points
UTI (Cystitis/Pyelonephritis) P pili (pyelonephritis-associated pili) bind uroepithelium; Type 1 fimbriae for bladder attachment #1 cause of UTI. Uropathogenic E. coli (UPEC). Women >> men due to short urethra.
Neonatal Meningitis K1 capsular antigen (sialic acid) prevents complement-mediated killing; crosses blood-brain barrier #1 cause of Gram-negative neonatal meningitis. Acquired during vaginal delivery.
Bacteremia/Sepsis LPS (endotoxin) triggers sepsis cascade; usually from UTI, biliary, or intra-abdominal source Most common cause of Gram-negative sepsis. LPS activates TLR4 → cytokine storm.
Intra-abdominal Infections Part of gut flora; causes peritonitis, abscess when bowel integrity compromised Common in appendicitis, diverticulitis, post-surgical. Often polymicrobial.
K1 Capsule: The K1 polysaccharide capsule contains sialic acid, which is also found on human cells. This "molecular mimicry" allows E. coli K1 to evade the immune system - the bacteria appear "self" to the complement system.

🧪 Laboratory Identification

Culture Characteristics

  • MacConkey agar: Pink colonies (lactose fermenter)
  • EMB agar: Green metallic sheen (classic for E. coli)
  • Sorbitol-MacConkey: O157:H7 does NOT ferment sorbitol (colorless colonies) - screening test for EHEC

Biochemical Tests

  • Indole: Positive (produces indole from tryptophan)
  • Methyl Red: Positive (mixed acid fermentation)
  • Voges-Proskauer: Negative
  • Citrate: Negative (cannot use citrate as sole carbon source)
IMViC reactions for E. coli: + + - - (Indole+, Methyl red+, VP-, Citrate-). Compare to Klebsiella/Enterobacter which are - - + +. This pattern helps differentiate Enterobacteriaceae.

🛡️ Antibiotic Resistance

⚠️ Extended-Spectrum β-Lactamases (ESBLs)

ESBL-producing E. coli are increasingly common and are resistant to:

  • All penicillins (including piperacillin-tazobactam in high inoculum)
  • All cephalosporins (including 3rd and 4th generation)
  • Aztreonam

Treatment: Carbapenems (meropenem, ertapenem) are drugs of choice for serious ESBL infections.

Other Resistance Concerns

  • Fluoroquinolone resistance: Common in UTIs - check susceptibilities before using empirically
  • TMP-SMX resistance: Variable by region (10-30%); may need to avoid for empiric UTI treatment
  • Carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae (CRE): Emerging threat - limited treatment options (ceftazidime-avibactam, meropenem-vaborbactam, or polymyxins)
For uncomplicated UTI, nitrofurantoin or fosfomycin are good first-line options because resistance rates remain low. Avoid fluoroquinolones for uncomplicated cystitis (FDA warning due to serious side effects for minor infections).

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • ID: Gram-negative rod, lactose fermenter (pink on MacConkey), indole+, oxidase-
  • EMB agar: Green metallic sheen (classic finding)
  • EHEC O157:H7: Shiga toxin → bloody diarrhea → HUS. NO antibiotics! Sorbitol non-fermenter.
  • ETEC: Traveler's diarrhea. LT (↑cAMP) + ST (↑cGMP) = watery diarrhea
  • EIEC: Invades like Shigella → bloody diarrhea WITH fever
  • EPEC: Attaching/effacing → infant diarrhea (developing countries)
  • UTI: #1 cause. P pili for upper tract, Type 1 fimbriae for bladder
  • Neonatal meningitis: K1 capsule (sialic acid = molecular mimicry)
  • ESBL: Resistant to all cephalosporins → treat with carbapenems
