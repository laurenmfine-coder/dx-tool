🔬 Overview: Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic

Bacteria are prokaryotes - cells without a true membrane-bound nucleus or organelles. This fundamental difference from eukaryotic cells (like human cells) provides targets for selective antibiotic therapy.

Remember the key prokaryotic features: no nucleus (nucleoid region instead), no membrane-bound organelles, 70S ribosomes (vs 80S in eukaryotes), circular chromosome, and unique cell wall structures.
Feature Bacteria (Prokaryote) Human Cells (Eukaryote) Clinical Relevance
Nucleus No true nucleus (nucleoid) Membrane-bound nucleus —
Ribosomes 70S (30S + 50S) 80S (40S + 60S) Target for aminoglycosides, macrolides, tetracyclines
Cell Wall Peptidoglycan None Target for β-lactams, vancomycin
Cell Membrane No sterols (except Mycoplasma) Cholesterol-containing Daptomycin, polymyxins target membrane
DNA Circular, single chromosome Linear, multiple chromosomes Fluoroquinolones target DNA gyrase

🧱 The Cell Wall: Gram-Positive vs Gram-Negative

The bacterial cell wall is the key structural feature that determines Gram staining, antibiotic susceptibility, and many pathogenic mechanisms.

Peptidoglycan (Murein)

The cell wall's essential component is peptidoglycan - a mesh-like polymer providing structural rigidity. It consists of:

  • NAG (N-acetylglucosamine) and NAM (N-acetylmuramic acid) - sugar backbone
  • Peptide cross-links - D-alanine-D-alanine bonds (target of vancomycin!)
  • Transpeptidases (PBPs) - enzymes that cross-link peptidoglycan (target of β-lactams!)
Key Concept: Peptidoglycan synthesis is the target of our most important antibiotics. β-lactams inhibit transpeptidases (PBPs), while vancomycin binds D-ala-D-ala, preventing cross-linking.

Gram-Positive Cell Wall

🟣 Gram-Positive Structure

Thick peptidoglycan layer (20-80nm) - retains crystal violet stain

Teichoic acids: Negatively charged polymers extending through peptidoglycan

  • Lipoteichoic acid (LTA): Anchored to cell membrane - triggers immune response (like LPS but weaker)
  • Wall teichoic acid (WTA): Anchored to peptidoglycan - important for cell division

🎯 Drug targets: β-lactams (PBPs in peptidoglycan), Vancomycin (D-ala-D-ala), Daptomycin (membrane)

Gram-Negative Cell Wall

🟢 Gram-Negative Structure

Thin peptidoglycan layer (2-7nm) - loses crystal violet, takes up safranin (pink)

Outer Membrane: Unique second membrane containing:

  • LPS (Lipopolysaccharide/Endotoxin): Lipid A (toxic) + Core + O-antigen
  • Porins: Channels allowing small molecules through outer membrane
  • Periplasmic space: Between inner and outer membranes - contains β-lactamases!

🎯 Drug targets: β-lactams (must penetrate outer membrane via porins), Polymyxins (disrupt outer membrane)

LPS = Endotoxin. Lipid A is the toxic component that triggers septic shock by activating TLR4 on macrophages → massive cytokine release. This is why Gram-negative sepsis can be so severe!
Feature Gram-Positive Gram-Negative
Peptidoglycan Thick (20-80nm) Thin (2-7nm)
Outer Membrane Absent Present (with LPS)
Teichoic Acids Present Absent
LPS (Endotoxin) Absent Present
Periplasmic Space Minimal Large (contains enzymes)
Gram Stain Color Purple/Blue Pink/Red
Examples Staph, Strep, Enterococcus, Listeria, Clostridium E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas, Neisseria, H. flu
The outer membrane of Gram-negatives is a permeability barrier. Many antibiotics must enter through porins. This is why Pseudomonas is intrinsically resistant to many drugs - it has very restrictive porins and active efflux pumps!

🧫 Other Important Bacterial Structures

🔵 Capsule

Polysaccharide layer outside cell wall

Provides protection against phagocytosis (anti-opsonin). Key virulence factor.

Encapsulated organisms (SHiNE SKiS): Strep pneumo, H. flu, Neisseria, E. coli K1, Salmonella, Klebsiella, Group B Strep

🎯 Clinical: Asplenic patients at high risk for encapsulated organisms. Vaccines target capsular polysaccharides (Prevnar, Hib, meningococcal).

🧬 Pili/Fimbriae

Hair-like appendages for attachment

Common pili: Adherence to host cells (E. coli P pili → uropathogenicity)

Sex pili: Conjugation - DNA transfer between bacteria

🎯 MCAT: Conjugation via F plasmid (sex pilus) is HIGH YIELD. Also understand transformation (naked DNA uptake) and transduction (phage-mediated).

🌀 Flagella

Motility - rotating protein filaments

Made of flagellin protein. Important for pathogenesis (reaching infection sites).

H antigen: Flagellar antigen used in bacterial serotyping (e.g., E. coli O157:H7)

💤 Spores (Endospores)

Dormant, highly resistant survival form

Only formed by Bacillus and Clostridium (Gram-positive rods)

Contains dipicolinic acid (heat resistance). Resistant to heat, desiccation, chemicals, radiation.

🎯 Clinical: Autoclave (121°C, 15 psi) required to kill spores. C. diff spores survive alcohol hand sanitizer - use soap and water!

🔬 Ribosomes (70S)

Protein synthesis machinery

30S subunit: Target of aminoglycosides (irreversible), tetracyclines (reversible)

50S subunit: Target of macrolides, clindamycin, chloramphenicol, linezolid

🎯 MCAT: 70S = 30S + 50S (prokaryotic), 80S = 40S + 60S (eukaryotic). This difference allows selective toxicity!

🧠 Check Your Understanding

A patient with Gram-negative sepsis develops hypotension and fever. Which bacterial component is most directly responsible for triggering the inflammatory response?

A. Peptidoglycan
B. Teichoic acid
C. Lipopolysaccharide (LPS)
D. Capsule
Answer: C. LPS (endotoxin), specifically the Lipid A component, activates TLR4 on macrophages, triggering massive cytokine release (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6) leading to septic shock. This is unique to Gram-negative bacteria.

Which antibiotic class inhibits bacterial protein synthesis by binding to the 30S ribosomal subunit?

A. Aminoglycosides
B. Macrolides
C. β-lactams
D. Fluoroquinolones
Answer: A. Aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin) bind irreversibly to the 30S subunit. Macrolides target 50S. β-lactams target cell wall synthesis (PBPs). Fluoroquinolones target DNA gyrase/topoisomerase IV.

An asplenic patient is at highest risk for infection with which type of bacteria?

A. Encapsulated organisms
B. Intracellular organisms
C. Anaerobic organisms
D. Spore-forming organisms
Answer: A. The spleen is critical for clearing encapsulated bacteria (opsonization and phagocytosis). Asplenic patients need vaccination against S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, and N. meningitidis, plus prophylactic antibiotics.
