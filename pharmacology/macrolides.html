Macrolides

50S Ribosome Inhibitors - The Atypical Pneumonia Fighters

50S Ribosome Inhibitor Bacteriostatic Excellent Intracellular CYP3A4 Inhibitor

📋 Drug Class Overview

Azithromycin (Zithromax)

Most commonly used. Once daily, 5-day "Z-pack." Longest half-life (68h). Fewest drug interactions. GI better tolerated.

Clarithromycin (Biaxin)

Used in H. pylori regimens and MAC prophylaxis. More drug interactions (CYP3A4). Metallic taste.

Erythromycin

Original macrolide. More GI side effects (prokinetic). Used for gastroparesis, chlamydia in pregnancy, acne.

Fidaxomicin

Narrow-spectrum macrolide for C. difficile only. Minimal systemic absorption. Preferred for CDI recurrence.

⚙️ Mechanism of Action

How Macrolides Work

  1. Bind to 23S rRNA of the 50S ribosomal subunit
  2. Block the translocation step (movement of tRNA from A-site to P-site)
  3. Prevents peptide chain elongation → inhibits protein synthesis
  4. Result: Bacteriostatic (but can be bactericidal at high concentrations or against certain organisms)
Macrolides bind the 50S ribosome and are bacterioSTATIC. They're in the same "club" as chloramphenicol, clindamycin, and linezolid - all 50S inhibitors. Remember: "Buy AT 50, CELL at 30" - 50S inhibitors are Bacteriostatic (except linezolid which has some cidal activity).

Excellent Intracellular Penetration

Macrolides concentrate inside cells (especially macrophages and PMNs) at 10-100x serum levels. This makes them ideal for:

  • Intracellular pathogens: Legionella, Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Chlamydophila
  • Organisms hiding in phagocytes

For community-acquired pneumonia (CAP), macrolides cover the "atypical" pathogens (Mycoplasma, Chlamydophila, Legionella) that β-lactams miss. This is why CAP treatment often combines a β-lactam + macrolide, or uses a respiratory fluoroquinolone alone.

Azithromycin's extremely long half-life (68 hours) explains why the "Z-pack" works: 5 days of dosing provides ~10 days of antibiotic effect due to tissue accumulation and slow release. It also means a single 1g dose can treat chlamydia.

⚠️ Adverse Effects & Drug Interactions

Key Toxicities

  • GI upset: Most common. Erythromycin stimulates motilin receptors (prokinetic) → nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, cramps
  • QT prolongation: Risk of torsades de pointes, especially erythromycin IV. Avoid with other QT-prolonging drugs.
  • Cholestatic hepatitis: Rare, primarily with erythromycin estolate
  • Ototoxicity: High-dose IV, especially in renal failure
  • Pyloric stenosis: Risk in infants <2 weeks given erythromycin (use azithromycin instead for pertussis prophylaxis)

Drug Interactions (CYP3A4)

Erythromycin and clarithromycin are CYP3A4 inhibitors → increase levels of many drugs:

  • Statins: ↑ risk of rhabdomyolysis (especially simvastatin, lovastatin)
  • Warfarin: ↑ INR, bleeding risk
  • Carbamazepine: ↑ levels, toxicity
  • Theophylline: ↑ levels
  • Digoxin: ↑ levels (also kills gut flora that metabolize digoxin)
  • Ergotamines: Contraindicated - risk of ergotism

Azithromycin has minimal CYP3A4 inhibition - preferred when drug interactions are a concern!

🛡️ Resistance Mechanisms

  • Target modification (methylation): erm genes methylate 23S rRNA → prevents macrolide binding. Most common mechanism. Can cause cross-resistance to lincosamides and streptogramins (MLSB phenotype).
  • Efflux pumps: mef genes pump macrolides out of cell (streptococci)
  • Drug inactivation: Esterases or phosphotransferases (rare)
S. pneumoniae macrolide resistance is a growing problem (~30% in US). For CAP, don't use macrolide monotherapy if local resistance is high. The combination with a β-lactam provides coverage even if macrolide resistance is present.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • Mechanism: Bind 50S ribosome (23S rRNA) → block translocation → bacterioSTATIC
  • Spectrum: Atypical pneumonia pathogens (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella), streptococci, H. flu, Bordetella, Campylobacter
  • Key advantage: Excellent INTRACELLULAR penetration (concentrates in macrophages)
  • Azithromycin: Long half-life (68h), once daily, minimal CYP interactions, best GI tolerance
  • Erythromycin: Prokinetic (gastroparesis), most GI side effects, strongest CYP3A4 inhibition
  • Clarithromycin: H. pylori, MAC, moderate CYP3A4 inhibition
  • Toxicities: GI upset, QT prolongation, cholestatic hepatitis, drug interactions
  • Resistance: Methylation of 23S rRNA (erm genes) - causes MLSB phenotype
