Aminoglycosides

Powerful Gram-Negative Killers with Concentration-Dependent Killing

30S Ribosome Inhibitor Bactericidal Concentration-Dependent Post-Antibiotic Effect Nephro/Ototoxic

📋 Drug Class Overview

Gentamicin

Workhorse aminoglycoside. Gram-negative coverage. Synergy with β-lactams for enterococcal endocarditis.

Tobramycin

Best vs Pseudomonas. Inhaled form (TOBI) for CF. Similar to gentamicin otherwise.

Amikacin

Most resistant to aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes. Reserve for resistant organisms.

Streptomycin

Oldest. Now mainly for TB (2nd line), plague, tularemia. Rarely used otherwise.

Neomycin

Too toxic for systemic use. Topical (ear drops, skin) or oral for hepatic encephalopathy (not absorbed).

🧠 Aminoglycosides End in "-mycin" or "-micin"

GNATS: Gent, Neo, Amik, Tobra, Strep

All are bactericidal protein synthesis inhibitors that bind 30S ribosome

⚙️ Mechanism of Action

How Aminoglycosides Kill Bacteria

  1. Uptake: Aminoglycosides enter bacteria via oxygen-dependent transport (requires electron transport chain → explains why anaerobes are resistant!)
  2. Binding: Irreversibly bind to 30S ribosomal subunit (16S rRNA)
  3. Misreading: Cause misreading of mRNA → abnormal proteins inserted into membrane
  4. Membrane damage: Abnormal proteins disrupt membrane → increased drug uptake (positive feedback!)
  5. Cell death: Bactericidal effect from both protein errors and membrane disruption
30S vs 50S ribosome inhibitors: Aminoglycosides bind 30S and are BACTERICIDAL. Most other ribosome inhibitors (macrolides, tetracyclines, clindamycin) bind 50S and are BACTERIOSTATIC. Remember: "A-G-gressively kills" (Aminoglycosides are bactericidal).

Why Are They Bactericidal?

Unlike other ribosome inhibitors, aminoglycosides cause irreversible binding and produce defective proteins that damage the bacterial membrane. This "double hit" makes them bactericidal rather than just bacteriostatic.

📈 Unique Pharmacokinetics

Concentration-Dependent Killing

Aminoglycosides show concentration-dependent killing: higher peak levels = greater bacterial killing. This is different from β-lactams and vancomycin (time-dependent).

  • Peak/MIC ratio ≥8-10 correlates with efficacy
  • Post-antibiotic effect (PAE): Bacterial killing continues even after drug levels fall below MIC (4-8 hours for Gram-negatives)
  • These properties allow once-daily (extended-interval) dosing
Parameter Aminoglycosides Clinical Implication
Absorption NOT absorbed orally (too polar) Must give IV/IM for systemic infections. Oral only for local GI effect.
Distribution Low Vd, poor intracellular penetration Don't work for intracellular pathogens (Legionella, Chlamydia, Rickettsia)
Elimination 100% renal (unchanged) Must adjust dose in renal impairment. Monitor levels!
Killing pattern Concentration-dependent + PAE Once-daily dosing is preferred (better efficacy, less toxicity)
Once-daily dosing (7 mg/kg gentamicin) maximizes peak/MIC ratio and allows drug-free interval that reduces nephrotoxicity. This is now preferred over traditional divided dosing (1.7 mg/kg q8h) for most indications.

🎯 Spectrum of Activity

Organism Activity Notes
Aerobic Gram-negatives ✓ Excellent E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas, Enterobacter, Serratia, Proteus
Pseudomonas ✓ Good Tobramycin > Gentamicin. Often used in combination therapy.
Staphylococci ⚠ Synergy only Not used as monotherapy. Synergistic with β-lactams or vancomycin.
Enterococci ⚠ Synergy only Must combine with cell wall agent (ampicillin/vancomycin) for bactericidal effect.
Streptococci ⚠ Weak Poor activity as monotherapy
Anaerobes ✗ No activity Require O2 for uptake! No activity against Bacteroides, Clostridium
Intracellular organisms ✗ No activity Poor cell penetration → no activity vs Legionella, Chlamydia, Rickettsia
Aminoglycosides are INACTIVE against ANAEROBES! They require oxygen-dependent transport into bacteria. Think: "A-G-needs Air" (Aminoglycosides need aerobic environment).

⚠️ Toxicities - The "Two Tox"

Major Dose-Limiting Toxicities

🔊 Ototoxicity

Vestibular: Vertigo, nystagmus, ataxia (more common with gentamicin, streptomycin)
Cochlear: Tinnitus, hearing loss - may be irreversible! (more common with amikacin, neomycin)
Risk factors: Duration >7 days, high cumulative dose, concurrent loop diuretics, pre-existing hearing loss

🔴 Nephrotoxicity

Accumulates in renal cortex → proximal tubule necrosis → non-oliguric ATN
Features: Rising creatinine, ↑K⁺, ↓Mg²⁺, ↓Ca²⁺, casts
Risk factors: Duration >5 days, trough levels >2, concurrent nephrotoxins (amphotericin, NSAIDs, vancomycin), volume depletion
Usually reversible if caught early!

Other Adverse Effects

  • Neuromuscular blockade: Rare but can be fatal. Blocks presynaptic ACh release. Risk with anesthetics, myasthenia gravis. Reversible with calcium, neostigmine.
  • Hypomagnesemia: Renal Mg wasting. Monitor levels.
To minimize toxicity: (1) Use once-daily dosing, (2) Keep duration <7 days when possible, (3) Monitor trough levels and renal function, (4) Avoid concurrent nephro/ototoxins, (5) Ensure adequate hydration.

🏥 Clinical Indications

Indication Drug Choice Notes
Serious Gram-negative infections Gentamicin, Tobramycin Usually combined with β-lactam empirically. Can de-escalate once susceptibilities known.
Pseudomonas (CF lungs) Tobramycin (inhaled TOBI) Inhaled delivery minimizes systemic toxicity. Chronic suppressive therapy.
Enterococcal endocarditis Gentamicin + ampicillin Synergy required for bactericidal effect. Check for high-level resistance (HLAR).
Streptococcal endocarditis Gentamicin + penicillin Short-course synergy allows 2-week therapy (vs 4 weeks for penicillin alone).
Tuberculosis Streptomycin, Amikacin Second-line agents for MDR-TB
Plague, Tularemia Streptomycin, Gentamicin Drug of choice for these bioterror agents
Hepatic encephalopathy Neomycin (oral) Reduces ammonia-producing gut bacteria. Not absorbed.

🛡️ Resistance Mechanisms

Mechanism Description Clinical Significance
Aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes (AMEs) Acetyltransferases, phosphotransferases, nucleotidyltransferases modify drug Most common. Amikacin is most resistant to modification - reserve for resistant organisms.
Decreased uptake Mutations in transport proteins or anaerobic conditions Explains intrinsic anaerobe resistance
Ribosomal mutations Mutations in 16S rRNA or ribosomal proteins Streptomycin resistance in TB (rpsL gene)
Efflux pumps Active export of drug from cell Contributes to Pseudomonas resistance
Aminoglycoside-Modifying Enzymes (AMEs) are the most common resistance mechanism. They add acetyl, phosphate, or adenylyl groups to the drug. Amikacin has fewest sites for modification → use when gentamicin/tobramycin fail.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • Mechanism: Bind 30S ribosome (irreversible) → misreading + membrane damage → bactericidal
  • Killing: CONCENTRATION-dependent (not time-dependent) + post-antibiotic effect → once-daily dosing
  • Spectrum: Aerobic Gram-negatives. NO anaerobes (need O2 for uptake). Synergy with cell wall agents.
  • PK: Not absorbed orally, 100% renal elimination, poor intracellular penetration
  • Toxicity: Nephrotoxicity (reversible ATN), Ototoxicity (may be permanent!), NM blockade (rare)
  • Resistance: Aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes (most common). Amikacin = most resistant to modification.
  • Key uses: Serious GNR infections, synergy for endocarditis, TB (streptomycin), inhaled for CF (tobramycin)
