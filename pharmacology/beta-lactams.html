Beta-Lactam Antibiotics

The Foundation of Antibacterial Therapy - Penicillins, Cephalosporins, Carbapenems & Monobactams

Cell Wall Synthesis Inhibitor Bactericidal Time-Dependent Killing PBP Target

  • Compare the spectrum of coverage across beta-lactam classes (Step 1, Clinical)

  • Apply knowledge to select appropriate beta-lactam therapy (Clinical)

⚙️ Mechanism of Action

Beta-lactam antibiotics contain a characteristic beta-lactam ring - a four-membered cyclic amide that is essential for activity. They work by inhibiting bacterial cell wall synthesis.

Step-by-Step Mechanism

1
Structural mimicry: The beta-lactam ring mimics the D-alanyl-D-alanine terminus of peptidoglycan precursors
2
PBP binding: Beta-lactams bind covalently to Penicillin-Binding Proteins (PBPs) - transpeptidases that cross-link peptidoglycan
3
Inhibition: PBP inhibition prevents cross-linking of peptidoglycan chains → weakened cell wall
4
Autolysis: Autolytic enzymes (murein hydrolases) continue degrading existing wall → osmotic lysis → cell death
Beta-lactams are bactericidal (kill bacteria) and exhibit time-dependent killing - efficacy depends on the TIME that drug concentration exceeds MIC, not peak concentration. This is why continuous or frequent dosing is important. Compare to aminoglycosides which show concentration-dependent killing.
Why bacteria, not humans? Human cells don't have cell walls - they have cell membranes with cholesterol instead. This is why beta-lactams have excellent selective toxicity (harm bacteria, not host cells).

📊 Beta-Lactam Classes

Penicillins

Natural Penicillins (Penicillin G, Penicillin V)

Spectrum: Streptococci (including S. pyogenes, S. pneumo - if susceptible), Neisseria, Treponema pallidum, Listeria, Actinomyces
Key Uses: Strep pharyngitis, syphilis (all stages), rheumatic fever prophylaxis
NOT Effective: Most Staph (β-lactamase), Gram-negatives (can't penetrate outer membrane)

Anti-Staphylococcal Penicillins (Nafcillin, Oxacillin, Dicloxacillin)

Spectrum: MSSA (methicillin-susceptible S. aureus), Streptococci
Key Feature: β-lactamase RESISTANT (bulky side chain protects β-lactam ring)
Key Uses: MSSA bacteremia, endocarditis, osteomyelitis - PREFERRED over vancomycin for MSSA!
NOT Effective: MRSA (altered PBP2a), Gram-negatives, Enterococci

Aminopenicillins (Ampicillin, Amoxicillin)

Spectrum: Same as natural penicillins PLUS: E. coli, H. influenzae, Salmonella, Shigella, Listeria, Enterococcus
Key Feature: Extended Gram-negative coverage (amino group allows penetration through porins)
Key Uses: Listeria meningitis, enterococcal infections, H. pylori (with other agents), otitis media
Rash Alert: Ampicillin causes rash in EBV mononucleosis (not true allergy - mechanism unknown)

Anti-Pseudomonal Penicillins (Piperacillin, Ticarcillin)

Spectrum: Broadest penicillin spectrum - includes Pseudomonas aeruginosa, many Enterobacteriaceae
Combined with: β-lactamase inhibitors (piperacillin-tazobactam = "Pip-Tazo" or Zosyn)
Key Uses: Hospital-acquired infections, neutropenic fever, intra-abdominal infections
For serious MSSA infections (bacteremia, endocarditis), nafcillin/oxacillin or cefazolin are PREFERRED over vancomycin. Studies show better outcomes with β-lactams for MSSA - "Don't vanco when you can nafcillin!"

🔬 Cephalosporins by Generation

1st Generation (Cefazolin, Cephalexin)

Spectrum: Excellent Gram-positive (MSSA, Strep), limited Gram-negative (E. coli, Klebsiella, Proteus)

Uses: Surgical prophylaxis (#1 choice), skin/soft tissue, UTI, MSSA infections

Remember: "First = Focused on Gram-positives"

2nd Generation (Cefuroxime, Cefoxitin, Cefotetan)

Spectrum: Less Gram-positive, more Gram-negative (H. flu, M. catarrhalis). Cefoxitin/cefotetan have anaerobe coverage.

Uses: CAP, sinusitis, intra-abdominal (cefoxitin), surgical prophylaxis (GI/GYN)

Remember: "Second = Shifts toward Gram-negatives + some anaerobes"

3rd Generation (Ceftriaxone, Cefotaxime, Ceftazidime)

Spectrum: Excellent Gram-negative (crosses BBB!), less Gram-positive. Ceftazidime covers Pseudomonas.

Uses: Meningitis, serious Gram-negative infections, gonorrhea (ceftriaxone), CAP (hospitalized)

Remember: "Third = Terrific for meningitis and serious GNRs"

4th Generation (Cefepime)

Spectrum: Broad - good Gram-positive AND Gram-negative including Pseudomonas

Uses: Neutropenic fever, nosocomial infections, Pseudomonal infections

Remember: "Fourth = Full spectrum (both sides)"

5th Generation (Ceftaroline, Ceftobiprole)

Spectrum: MRSA coverage! Binds altered PBP2a. Also covers usual Gram-negatives (not Pseudomonas)

Uses: MRSA skin infections, CAP

Remember: "Fifth = Finally covers MRSA"

General rule: As cephalosporin generation increases, Gram-NEGATIVE coverage increases and Gram-POSITIVE coverage decreases (except 5th gen which adds MRSA back). Think of it as a trade-off... until 5th generation breaks the pattern.

💪 Carbapenems & Monobactams

Carbapenems (Meropenem, Imipenem, Ertapenem, Doripenem)

Spectrum: BROADEST of all β-lactams! Gram-positive, Gram-negative, anaerobes. ESBL-stable.
Key Feature: Resistant to most β-lactamases (including ESBLs). "Big guns" of antibiotics.
Notable Gaps: MRSA, VRE, atypicals (Legionella, Mycoplasma), Stenotrophomonas
Ertapenem: Once-daily dosing BUT no Pseudomonas or Acinetobacter coverage
Imipenem: Combined with cilastatin (prevents renal degradation). Lowers seizure threshold!

Monobactams (Aztreonam)

Spectrum: ONLY Gram-negatives (including Pseudomonas). No Gram-positive or anaerobe activity.
Key Feature: Safe in penicillin-allergic patients! Different ring structure = minimal cross-reactivity.
Key Use: Gram-negative infections in patients with severe penicillin allergy

🛡️ β-Lactamase Inhibitors

These agents have minimal antibacterial activity alone but protect β-lactams from enzymatic degradation:

Inhibitor Combined With Trade Name Added Coverage
Clavulanic acid Amoxicillin Augmentin β-lactamase producing H. flu, M. catarrhalis, MSSA, some anaerobes
Sulbactam Ampicillin Unasyn Similar to above; also has intrinsic activity vs Acinetobacter
Tazobactam Piperacillin Zosyn Broad spectrum including many ESBL producers (but not all!)
Avibactam Ceftazidime Avycaz KPC carbapenemases, ESBLs, AmpC - for CRE!
Vaborbactam Meropenem Vabomere KPC carbapenemases - for CRE!
Important: β-lactamase inhibitors do NOT help against MRSA (which has altered PBPs, not β-lactamases) or against metallo-β-lactamases (like NDM-1). Know the difference between enzymatic resistance and target modification!

🦠 Mechanisms of Resistance

⚠️ How Bacteria Resist Beta-Lactams

1. β-Lactamase Production (Most Common)

Enzymes that hydrolyze the β-lactam ring. Types include: penicillinases, ESBLs (extended-spectrum β-lactamases), AmpC β-lactamases, carbapenemases (KPC, NDM, OXA). Genes often on plasmids = transferable!

2. Altered PBPs (Target Modification)

MRSA has mecA gene encoding PBP2a with low affinity for β-lactams. S. pneumoniae acquires altered PBPs through transformation. This is NOT overcome by β-lactamase inhibitors!

3. Decreased Permeability

Gram-negatives can lose or modify outer membrane porins (OprD in Pseudomonas). Prevents drug entry into periplasmic space.

4. Efflux Pumps

Active transport of drug out of cell. Important in Pseudomonas (MexAB-OprM system). Often contributes to multidrug resistance.

MRSA = altered PBP (mecA gene → PBP2a). NOT β-lactamase! This is why β-lactamase inhibitors don't work against MRSA, and why ALL β-lactams fail (penicillins, cephalosporins, AND carbapenems) - except ceftaroline which binds PBP2a.

⚠️ Beta-Lactam Allergy

Understanding Penicillin Allergy

  • ~10% of patients report penicillin allergy, but only ~1-2% have true IgE-mediated allergy when tested
  • Most "allergies" are not true allergies: childhood rashes (often viral), GI upset, family history
  • True IgE-mediated reactions: urticaria, angioedema, anaphylaxis within 1 hour of dose
  • Delayed reactions: maculopapular rash (days later) - generally not dangerous, may be able to continue

Cross-Reactivity

Drug Class Cross-Reactivity with Penicillin Clinical Guidance
Cephalosporins ~2% (mostly 1st gen, side-chain dependent) Generally safe to use 3rd/4th gen in penicillin allergy. Avoid if anaphylaxis to amoxicillin (similar side chain to cephalexin)
Carbapenems < 1% Safe in most penicillin-allergic patients. Consider in severe allergy.
Aztreonam ~0% (different ring structure) Safe to use in penicillin allergy. EXCEPT: cross-reacts with ceftazidime (same side chain)
Penicillin allergy delabeling is important! Many patients labeled "allergic" can safely receive penicillins, leading to better outcomes and less antibiotic resistance. Skin testing can identify true IgE-mediated allergy. Consider referral to allergist for high-risk patients.

📈 Pharmacokinetic Pearls

Property Clinical Implication
Time-dependent killing Efficacy depends on time above MIC. Frequent dosing or continuous infusion improves outcomes.
Renal excretion Most β-lactams require dose adjustment in renal impairment. Exception: nafcillin, ceftriaxone (hepatic).
CNS penetration Inflamed meninges allow penetration. 3rd gen cephalosporins (ceftriaxone) and carbapenems (meropenem) preferred for meningitis.
Ceftriaxone + calcium Do NOT mix with calcium-containing solutions in neonates - can form fatal precipitates!
Probenecid interaction Blocks renal tubular secretion → increased penicillin levels. Used therapeutically (e.g., with ceftriaxone for gonorrhea).

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • Mechanism: Inhibit PBPs (transpeptidases) → prevent peptidoglycan cross-linking → cell wall weakening → osmotic lysis
  • Time-dependent killing: Efficacy depends on time above MIC (not peak concentration)
  • Penicillins: Natural (strep, syphilis), anti-staph (MSSA), aminopenicillins (+ Listeria, enterococci), anti-pseudomonal
  • Cephalosporins: ↑ generation = ↑ Gram-negative, ↓ Gram-positive (except 5th gen = MRSA)
  • Carbapenems: Broadest spectrum, ESBL-stable. Use for serious resistant infections.
  • Aztreonam: Only Gram-negatives. Safe in penicillin allergy!
  • MRSA resistance: Altered PBP2a (mecA gene), NOT β-lactamase. All β-lactams fail except ceftaroline.
  • Cross-reactivity: Penicillin → cephalosporin ~2%. Aztreonam safe (except ceftazidime cross-reaction).
