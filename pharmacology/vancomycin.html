Vancomycin & Glycopeptides

The MRSA Fighter - Cell Wall Synthesis Inhibitor with Unique Mechanism

Cell Wall Inhibitor Bactericidal Gram-Positive Only Time-Dependent TDM Required

📋 Overview

Vancomycin is a glycopeptide antibiotic that inhibits cell wall synthesis by a different mechanism than β-lactams. It's the cornerstone of MRSA treatment and is used empirically for serious gram-positive infections pending culture results.

Despite being discovered in 1953, vancomycin remained a "drug of last resort" for decades due to toxicity concerns. With the MRSA epidemic, it became one of the most commonly used antibiotics in hospitals.

Key Distinguishing Feature: Vancomycin binds to the D-ala-D-ala terminus of peptidoglycan precursors (in the periplasm), while β-lactams bind to PBPs (in the inner membrane). Different binding site = different resistance mechanisms!

⚙️ Mechanism of Action

Step-by-Step Mechanism

1
Vancomycin binds to D-alanyl-D-alanine (D-ala-D-ala) terminus of peptidoglycan precursor (lipid II)
2
This binding prevents transglycosylation (polymerization of glycan chains)
3
Also blocks transpeptidation (cross-linking) by steric hindrance
4
Result: Weakened cell wall → osmotic lysis → cell death (bactericidal)
Key difference from β-lactams: Vancomycin binds the SUBSTRATE (D-ala-D-ala), while β-lactams bind the ENZYME (PBPs/transpeptidases). Both inhibit cell wall synthesis but through different targets!

Why Only Gram-Positives?

Vancomycin is a large, hydrophilic molecule (~1450 Da) that cannot penetrate the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria. It works by binding to peptidoglycan precursors in the periplasmic space - in Gram-negatives, it can't get there.

🎯 Spectrum of Activity

Organism Activity Notes
MRSA ✓ Active Drug of choice for serious MRSA infections (with newer alternatives emerging)
MSSA ✓ Active But inferior to nafcillin/cefazolin! Don't use vanco for MSSA if you can avoid it.
Coagulase-negative staph ✓ Active Most are methicillin-resistant. Common in line infections.
Streptococci ✓ Active Including penicillin-resistant S. pneumoniae
Enterococcus faecalis ✓ Active Usually susceptible (but bacteriostatic, not bactericidal)
Enterococcus faecium ⚠ Variable Often VRE - check susceptibilities! May need linezolid or daptomycin.
Clostridioides difficile ✓ Active (PO) Oral vancomycin for C. diff colitis (NOT absorbed - local effect)
Gram-negatives ✗ No activity Cannot penetrate outer membrane
For MSSA bacteremia/endocarditis, nafcillin or cefazolin are SUPERIOR to vancomycin (better killing, better outcomes). Always de-escalate from vancomycin when cultures show MSSA!

📈 Pharmacokinetics & Dosing

Key PK Parameters

Administration

IV for systemic infections. Oral for C. diff only (not absorbed!)

Distribution

Vd ~0.4-1 L/kg. Good tissue penetration except CSF (need inflammation).

Elimination

Renal (>90% unchanged). Must adjust for CrCl! Half-life 4-6 hrs (longer in renal failure).

Killing Pattern

Time-dependent (like β-lactams). AUC/MIC ratio best predictor of efficacy.

Therapeutic Drug Monitoring (TDM)

Vancomycin has a narrow therapeutic window requiring monitoring:

Parameter Target Rationale
AUC/MIC 400-600 (MRSA) Current preferred method. Correlates with efficacy and reduced nephrotoxicity.
Trough (older method) 15-20 mg/L (serious infections) Still used but AUC-guided dosing is now preferred per guidelines.
Loading dose 25-30 mg/kg For serious infections to rapidly achieve therapeutic levels.
Maintenance 15-20 mg/kg q8-12h Adjust based on renal function and TDM results.
Oral vancomycin is NOT absorbed - it stays in the GI tract. This is why it works for C. difficile colitis (local effect) but NOT for systemic infections. IV vancomycin does NOT work for C. diff (doesn't get into gut lumen)!

⚠️ Adverse Effects

Major Toxicities

Nephrotoxicity

Risk increases with high troughs (>20), prolonged therapy, concurrent nephrotoxins (aminoglycosides, piperacillin-tazobactam). AUC-guided dosing reduces risk.

Red Man Syndrome

Histamine release (NOT true allergy) → flushing, pruritus, hypotension. Prevent by infusing over ≥1 hour. Treat with antihistamines, slower infusion.

Ototoxicity

Rare, usually with concurrent aminoglycosides or very high levels. May be irreversible.

Thrombophlebitis

Irritating to veins. Use central line or dilute and infuse slowly for peripheral access.

Red Man Syndrome is NOT an allergy! It's due to direct mast cell degranulation from rapid infusion. Patients can continue vancomycin with slower infusion (≥2 hours) and pretreatment with diphenhydramine. Don't label as "vancomycin allergy"!

🛡️ Resistance Mechanisms

Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococci (VRE)

VRE have acquired genes that modify the peptidoglycan precursor terminus:

  • vanA: Changes D-ala-D-ala → D-ala-D-lactate. High-level resistance (MIC >64). Inducible by vancomycin. Most common.
  • vanB: Same modification but lower-level resistance. Inducible. NOT resistant to teicoplanin.
  • vanC: Intrinsic in E. gallinarum, E. casseliflavus. Low-level resistance. D-ala-D-serine.

Vancomycin-Intermediate S. aureus (VISA)

VISA strains have thickened cell walls that "trap" vancomycin before it reaches its target. MIC 4-8 μg/mL. Treat with alternative agents (daptomycin, linezolid, ceftaroline).

Vancomycin-Resistant S. aureus (VRSA)

Extremely rare (~15 cases in US). Acquired vanA from VRE. MIC ≥16 μg/mL. Public health emergency when identified!

VRE resistance mechanism: D-ala-D-ala → D-ala-D-LACTATE. Vancomycin can't bind lactate with same affinity → resistance. The vanA gene encodes enzymes that make this modification.

💊 Related Glycopeptides & Lipoglycopeptides

Drug Key Features Advantages
Teicoplanin Similar mechanism. Not available in US. Once daily dosing, less nephrotoxicity. Active against vanB VRE.
Dalbavancin Lipoglycopeptide. Very long half-life. Once weekly dosing! Good for OPAT, skin infections.
Oritavancin Lipoglycopeptide. Single-dose therapy. ONE dose for skin infections. Multiple mechanisms (also disrupts membrane).
Telavancin Lipoglycopeptide. Dual mechanism. Also depolarizes membrane. Active against some VISA. Nephrotoxic.

🏥 Clinical Indications

Indication Role of Vancomycin
MRSA bacteremia First-line. Check MIC - if ≥2, consider alternatives. 4-6 weeks for endocarditis.
MRSA pneumonia Option, but linezolid may be preferred (better lung penetration).
Bacterial meningitis Empiric (with ceftriaxone) for possible penicillin-resistant S. pneumoniae.
C. difficile infection Oral vancomycin (125mg QID) is first-line for most cases. Fidaxomicin preferred for recurrence.
Febrile neutropenia Added if MRSA risk, mucositis, catheter infection, skin infection, hypotension.
Surgical prophylaxis Alternative for β-lactam allergy. Use for MRSA-colonized patients.

📝 High-Yield Summary

  • Mechanism: Binds D-ala-D-ala → blocks transglycosylation AND transpeptidation
  • Spectrum: Gram-positives only (can't penetrate GNR outer membrane). MRSA, strep, most enterococci.
  • MSSA: Active but INFERIOR to nafcillin/cefazolin - always de-escalate!
  • Dosing: IV for systemic, PO for C. diff (not absorbed). AUC/MIC 400-600 target.
  • Red Man Syndrome: Histamine release, NOT allergy. Slow infusion rate.
  • Nephrotoxicity: Monitor levels, avoid concurrent nephrotoxins
  • VRE: vanA/vanB genes change D-ala-D-ala → D-ala-D-lactate
  • VISA/VRSA: Thickened cell wall (VISA) or acquired vanA (VRSA) - use alternatives
