Antiparasitic Drugs

Antimalarials, Antiprotozoals, Antihelminthics

🦟 Antimalarials

Artemisinin Derivatives (Artesunate, Artemether)

  • Mechanism: Generate free radicals → damage parasite membranes
  • Use: First-line for severe malaria (IV artesunate); ACTs for uncomplicated
  • Key point: Fast-acting, short half-life → combine with longer-acting drug
  • Toxicity: Generally well-tolerated; delayed hemolysis possible

Chloroquine

  • Mechanism: Concentrates in parasite food vacuole → inhibits heme polymerization
  • Use: P. vivax, P. ovale, P. malariae (chloroquine-sensitive areas only)
  • Resistance: Widespread P. falciparum resistance — NOT first-line for falciparum
  • Toxicity: Retinopathy (long-term), QT prolongation, pruritus

Primaquine

  • Mechanism: Generates reactive oxygen species; kills hypnozoites (liver stage)
  • Use: Radical cure of P. vivax/ovale (kills hypnozoites to prevent relapse)
  • CRITICAL: Causes hemolysis in G6PD deficiency — must test before use!
  • Contraindicated: G6PD deficiency, pregnancy

Atovaquone-Proguanil (Malarone)

  • Mechanism: Atovaquone inhibits mitochondrial electron transport; proguanil inhibits DHFR
  • Use: Treatment and prophylaxis of malaria
  • Advantage: Well-tolerated, short prophylaxis tail (7 days after travel)
  • Toxicity: GI upset, elevated LFTs
G6PD and Primaquine: ALWAYS check G6PD before primaquine. Hemolysis can be severe/fatal in deficient patients. This is extremely high-yield!

🦠 Antiprotozoals

Metronidazole

  • Mechanism: Reduced by anaerobic organisms → DNA damage
  • Protozoa: Giardia, Trichomonas, Entamoeba (amebic dysentery/abscess)
  • Also: Anaerobic bacteria, C. diff (second-line)
  • Toxicity: Disulfiram reaction with alcohol, metallic taste, peripheral neuropathy

Tinidazole

  • Mechanism: Same as metronidazole
  • Use: Giardia, Trichomonas, amebiasis — often single-dose
  • Advantage: Better tolerated than metronidazole, shorter course

Nitazoxanide

  • Mechanism: Inhibits pyruvate:ferredoxin oxidoreductase (PFOR)
  • Use: Giardia, Cryptosporidium (only FDA-approved drug for Crypto)
  • Advantage: Liquid formulation for children

Pyrimethamine + Sulfadiazine

  • Mechanism: Synergistic folate synthesis inhibition
  • Use: Toxoplasmosis (with leucovorin to prevent bone marrow suppression)
  • Toxicity: Bone marrow suppression — always give leucovorin (folinic acid)
🪱 Antihelminthics

Albendazole / Mebendazole

  • Mechanism: Inhibit microtubule synthesis (β-tubulin binding)
  • Use: Most intestinal nematodes — pinworm, roundworm, hookworm, whipworm
  • Also: Cysticercosis (neurocysticercosis), hydatid cyst (Echinococcus)
  • Toxicity: GI upset, elevated LFTs, bone marrow suppression (rare)

Ivermectin

  • Mechanism: Binds glutamate-gated Cl⁻ channels → paralysis
  • Use: Strongyloides, Onchocerciasis (river blindness), scabies, lice
  • Key point: Drug of choice for Strongyloides (can be fatal if missed before immunosuppression)
  • Toxicity: Mazzotti reaction in onchocerciasis (dying microfilariae)

Praziquantel

  • Mechanism: Increases Ca²⁺ permeability → paralysis and tegument damage
  • Use: All Schistosoma species, tapeworms (Taenia, Diphyllobothrium), flukes
  • Key point: Drug of choice for schistosomiasis and most flatworms
  • Toxicity: GI upset, headache, dizziness
⚠️ Strongyloides Before Immunosuppression
  • Screen patients from endemic areas before steroids/chemo/transplant
  • Hyperinfection syndrome can be fatal
  • Treat with ivermectin if positive

📝 High-Yield Summary
  • Primaquine: Kills hypnozoites (vivax/ovale relapse) — G6PD hemolysis risk!
  • Artesunate: IV for severe malaria — call CDC
  • Metronidazole: Giardia, Trichomonas, Entamoeba — disulfiram reaction
  • Ivermectin: Strongyloides (screen before immunosuppression!), onchocerciasis
  • Albendazole: Most intestinal nematodes, neurocysticercosis
  • Praziquantel: Schistosomiasis, tapeworms, flukes
