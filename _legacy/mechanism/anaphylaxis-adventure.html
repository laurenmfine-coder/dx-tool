Clinical Decision Point
Anaphylaxis: multisystem involvement (skin + respiratory + cardiovascular). Hypotensive and hypoxic. This is immediately life-threatening. What is the FIRST intervention?
DECISION 1
Immediate Action
✅
Correct!
Correct! IM epinephrine is ALWAYS the first treatment in anaphylaxis. For this ~25kg child: 0.15mg auto-injector dose (or 0.25mg of 1:1000 IM). Give in the anterolateral thigh. It reverses bronchospasm, increases BP, and reduces angioedema. Delay in epinephrine is the #1 cause of anaphylaxis death.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Diphenhydramine treats hives but does NOT reverse bronchospasm, hypotension, or airway edema. Albuterol helps wheezing but doesn't address cardiovascular collapse. These are adjuncts AFTER epinephrine. Giving antihistamines instead of epinephrine is the most common error in anaphylaxis management and increases mortality.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Steroids take 4-6 hours to work and do NOTHING for acute anaphylaxis. They may prevent biphasic reactions but are never first-line. This child is in anaphylactic shock — he needs epinephrine NOW, not a medication that takes hours to act.

Correct!
Correct! Epinephrine can be repeated every 5-15 minutes. 35% of anaphylaxis requires a second dose. NS bolus 20 mL/kg for distributive shock (may need 40-60 mL/kg total). If refractory after 2-3 IM doses, start IV epinephrine drip (0.1-1 mcg/kg/min). Also: place patient supine with legs elevated, high-flow O2, and prepare for intubation.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
While vasopressin can be used in refractory anaphylactic shock, it should not replace repeat epinephrine doses. Epinephrine has unique benefits in anaphylaxis: bronchodilation (β2), vasoconstriction (α1), and mast cell stabilization. Repeat IM epi first, then escalate to IV epi drip if needed. Vasopressin is third-line.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Airway management is critical, but repeat epinephrine + fluids may resolve the angioedema and avoid intubation. If the airway IS closing (stridor, inability to phonate), then early intubation is appropriate — but use a smaller tube (edema narrows the airway). The priority sequence is: epi → fluids → airway if needed.

Response
After second IM epi + 500mL NS bolus: BP 96/62, HR 118, SpO2 95% on high-flow O2. Wheezing resolved. Facial swelling stabilizing. The child is more alert and less anxious.
✅
Correct!
Correct! Now give adjuncts: diphenhydramine (H1), famotidine (H2), dexamethasone (prevents biphasic reaction in ~20% of cases). Observe minimum 4-8 hours (some guidelines say 24h for severe cases). Biphasic reaction occurs in 1-20% of cases, typically within 8-12 hours.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
NEVER discharge immediately after anaphylaxis treatment! Biphasic reactions occur in up to 20% of cases and can be fatal. Minimum observation is 4-6 hours, with 8-24 hours for severe cases. He needs monitoring, adjunct medications, and formal allergy follow-up planning.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
His vitals are improving — tachycardia and mild hypotension are expected as the epinephrine wears off and the reaction resolves. A third dose isn't indicated when symptoms are clearly trending better. Continue IV fluids and give adjunct medications. Reserve further epi for clinical worsening.
