MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

SLE is a chronic autoimmune disease with loss of tolerance to self-antigens, particularly nuclear antigens. Key mechanism: defective clearance of apoptotic cells → exposure of nuclear autoantigens → autoantibody production → immune complex deposition → complement activation → tissue damage.

🔑 Key Concept: SLE is the classic Type III hypersensitivity (immune complex-mediated). Immune complexes deposit in kidneys (glomerulonephritis), skin, joints, and serosal surfaces. Complement consumption → LOW C3 and C4 during flares (tracks disease activity).

⚠️ Lupus Nephritis Classification: Class I-II = mild (mesangial). Class III = focal proliferative. Class IV = diffuse proliferative (WORST prognosis, most common biopsy finding). Class V = membranous (nephrotic syndrome). Class VI = sclerotic (end-stage). Biopsy ALL patients with renal involvement.
TREATMENT
Management

Treatment by Severity

SeverityTreatment
All SLE patientsHydroxychloroquine (HCQ) — reduces flares, thrombosis, mortality. ONLY drug proven to improve survival. Annual eye exams (retinal toxicity)
Mild (skin, joints)HCQ + NSAIDs ± low-dose prednisone
Moderate (serositis, cytopenias)HCQ + moderate steroids + steroid-sparing agent (azathioprine, mycophenolate, methotrexate)
Severe (nephritis, cerebritis)Pulse IV methylprednisolone → mycophenolate or cyclophosphamide induction → mycophenolate or AZA maintenance. Belimumab (anti-BAFF) or voclosporin as add-on
🔑 Drug-Induced Lupus: Common causes: Hydralazine, procainamide, isoniazid, minocycline, TNF inhibitors. Features: serositis, arthralgias, rash. Anti-histone antibodies positive >95%. Does NOT cause nephritis or CNS disease. Resolves with drug discontinuation.

Monitoring

Anti-dsDNA titers and complement (C3/C4) track disease activity. Rising dsDNA + falling complement → impending flare. ESR rises in flares; CRP rises suggest infection (SLE flares usually have normal CRP unless serositis).
