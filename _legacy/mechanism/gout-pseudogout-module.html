MECHANISM
Crystal Arthropathies

Crystal arthropathies result from crystal deposition in joints and soft tissues, triggering acute inflammation via NLRP3 inflammasome activation → IL-1β release → intense neutrophilic inflammation.

FeatureGout (MSU)Pseudogout (CPPD)
CrystalMonosodium urate (MSU)Calcium pyrophosphate dihydrate (CPPD)
ShapeNeedle-shapedRhomboid (rectangular)
BirefringenceStrongly negatively birefringent (yellow parallel)Weakly positively birefringent (blue parallel)
Classic joint1st MTP (podagra) — 50% first attackKnee (most common), wrist
Age/SexMen 40-60; postmenopausal womenElderly (>65), associated with OA
RadiologyRat-bite erosions with overhanging margins; tophiChondrocalcinosis (linear calcification in cartilage)
TriggersAlcohol (beer), red meat, seafood, thiazides, surgery, dehydrationAcute illness, surgery, hypomagnesemia, hypothyroidism, hyperparathyroidism
🔑 Mnemonic for birefringence: "Gout is Negative and Needle-shaped" — both start with N. Under polarized microscopy: negatively birefringent = yellow when parallel to compensator axis.
CLINICAL
Gout Clinical Stages

Natural History of Gout

StageFeatures
Asymptomatic hyperuricemiaElevated uric acid (>6.8 mg/dL) without symptoms. Do NOT treat with urate-lowering therapy
Acute gouty arthritisSudden onset monoarthritis: exquisitely painful, red, warm, swollen. 1st MTP most common. Peaks 12-24 hours. Self-resolves in 7-14 days
Intercritical periodAsymptomatic between attacks. Duration shortens with recurrent attacks. Crystals persist in joints
Chronic tophaceous goutTophi (MSU crystal deposits) in joints, ears, olecranon, fingertips. Chronic destructive arthritis. Renal uric acid stones
⚠️ Diagnostic Gold Standard: Arthrocentesis with polarized light microscopy showing intracellular MSU crystals. Serum uric acid may be NORMAL during acute attacks (misleading!). Joint fluid shows inflammatory picture (WBC 20,000-100,000, neutrophil predominant). Always rule out septic arthritis (Gram stain, culture).
TREATMENT
Management

Acute Flare Treatment

AgentMechanismKey Points
NSAIDs (indomethacin)COX inhibition → ↓ prostaglandinsFirst-line for most patients. Start at maximum dose, taper as symptoms improve. Avoid in CKD, GI bleed risk
ColchicineDisrupts microtubule assembly → ↓ neutrophil migrationMost effective within 36 hours of onset. Low-dose regimen (1.2 mg then 0.6 mg 1 hour later). GI side effects at high doses
CorticosteroidsAnti-inflammatoryIf NSAIDs/colchicine contraindicated (CKD, GI bleed). Intra-articular preferred for monoarthritis. Oral burst for polyarticular
🔑 Critical Rule: Do NOT start or stop urate-lowering therapy (ULT) during acute attack — changing uric acid levels can prolong or trigger flares. Wait 2-4 weeks after resolution.

Urate-Lowering Therapy (Chronic)

AgentMechanismIndications
AllopurinolXanthine oxidase inhibitor (↓ uric acid production)First-line ULT. Start low (100 mg), titrate to target uric acid <6 mg/dL. Screen HLA-B*5801 in Southeast Asian/Black patients (risk of severe DRESS/SJS)
FebuxostatXanthine oxidase inhibitor (non-purine)Alternative to allopurinol. FDA warning: possible CV risk
ProbenecidUricosuric (↑ renal uric acid excretion)Underexcretors only. Contraindicated with uric acid stones or CKD
PegloticaseRecombinant uricase → converts uric acid to allantoinRefractory tophaceous gout. Risk of anaphylaxis, check G6PD first
