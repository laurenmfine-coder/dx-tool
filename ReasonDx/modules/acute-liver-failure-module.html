MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

Acute liver failure (ALF) is defined as severe hepatic dysfunction with coagulopathy (INR ≥ 1.5) and hepatic encephalopathy in a patient without pre-existing liver disease, occurring within 26 weeks of symptom onset. Classification by onset: hyperacute (<7 days), acute (7-21 days), subacute (21 days-26 weeks).

🔑 Key Concept: Paradoxically, hyperacute ALF (e.g., acetaminophen) has the best prognosis with spontaneous recovery rates of 40-70%, while subacute ALF has the worst prognosis (~15% spontaneous recovery). This is because hyperacute injury reflects a massive but potentially reversible insult, while subacute injury reflects progressive hepatocyte loss.

Indeterminate (14%)UnknownWorst prognosis. Must be referred for transplant evaluation early.
⚠️ Critical: Mushroom poisoning (Amanita phalloides) causes ALF with a characteristic delay of 6-12 hours between ingestion and GI symptoms, followed by apparent improvement, then fulminant hepatic failure at 48-96 hours. Mortality >50% without transplant.
CLINICAL
Diagnosis & Complications

Initial Workup

CBC, CMP, INR/PT, arterial lactate, arterial ammonia, acetaminophen level, toxicology screen, viral hepatitis panel (HAV IgM, HBsAg, HBV DNA, HCV RNA), autoimmune markers (ANA, ASMA, IgG), ceruloplasmin, pregnancy test, Doppler ultrasound of liver.

Complications

ComplicationMechanismManagement
Cerebral EdemaAmmonia → astrocyte swelling → increased ICPHOB 30°, hypertonic saline (goal Na 145-155), mannitol, avoid hyperthermia. ICP monitoring controversial.
CoagulopathyDecreased synthesis of factors AND anticoagulantsDo NOT correct INR unless actively bleeding or pre-procedure — INR is a prognostic marker. Vitamin K 10 mg IV × 1.
Infection/SepsisImpaired immune functionSurveillance cultures. Low threshold for empiric antibiotics. Fungal coverage if no improvement.
MetabolicHepatic gluconeogenesis failureContinuous glucose monitoring. D10 infusion to maintain glucose >150 mg/dL.
Renal FailureHepatorenal syndrome, ATN, APAP direct toxicityCRRT preferred over intermittent HD (less hemodynamic instability).
🔑 Board Pearl: In ALF, the INR reflects both pro- and anticoagulant factor loss. Patients may actually be in a rebalanced hemostatic state — viscoelastic testing (TEG/ROTEM) is more accurate than INR for bleeding risk assessment. Correcting INR with FFP obscures an important prognostic marker.
TREATMENT
Management & Transplant Criteria

Specific Treatments

EtiologyTreatmentNotes
AcetaminophenN-acetylcysteine (NAC)Oral: 140 mg/kg load, then 70 mg/kg q4h × 17 doses. IV: 150/50/100 mg/kg protocol. Effective even late. Also beneficial in non-APAP ALF.
AutoimmuneCorticosteroidsPrednisolone 40-60 mg/day. Response seen in ~50%. No response → transplant.
HSV/VZVAcyclovirStart empirically if immunosuppressed, pregnant, or skin lesions present. Don't wait for biopsy.
Wilson DiseasePlasmapheresis → transplantVirtually all Wilson ALF requires transplant. MELD often underestimates severity.
AmanitaPenicillin G + NAC + silibininNo proven antidote. Transplant often required.

King's College Criteria for Transplant

Acetaminophen ALF: Arterial pH < 7.30 after resuscitation, OR all three: Grade III/IV encephalopathy + INR > 6.5 + Creatinine > 3.4 mg/dL.

Non-Acetaminophen ALF: INR > 6.5 (irrespective of encephalopathy grade), OR any three of: Age <10 or >40, unfavorable etiology, jaundice >7 days before encephalopathy, INR > 3.5, bilirubin > 17.5 mg/dL.

⚠️ Transfer Early: All patients with ALF should be managed in or transferred to a transplant center. Deterioration can be rapid and unpredictable. MELD score underestimates urgency — ALF patients get UNOS Status 1A listing.
