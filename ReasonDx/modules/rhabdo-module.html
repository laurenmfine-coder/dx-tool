⚙️ Rhabdomyolysis

Pathophysiology & Clinical Correlations | Nephrology

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • CK >5x ULN (usually >10,000 for significant risk)
  • Aggressive IV fluids: target UOP >200-300 mL/hr
  • Monitor and treat hyperkalemia
  • Consider bicarbonate for urine alkalinization (controversial)

🔬 Core Pathophysiology

Myoglobin-Induced AKI

Muscle breakdown releases myoglobin which is toxic to kidneys.

Pathway

Muscle injury (crush, exertion, drugs)→Myocyte death→Myoglobin release into circulation→Renal tubular obstruction + toxicity→Acute kidney injury

🏥 Clinical Correlations

Tea/cola-colored urine

Mechanism: Myoglobin pigment in urine

Clinical Significance: Dipstick positive for blood but no RBCs on microscopy

Hyperkalemia

Mechanism: Release of intracellular potassium from damaged myocytes

Clinical Significance: Can be severe; cardiac monitoring required

CK >5x normal

Mechanism: Release of muscle enzymes

Clinical Significance: Diagnostic criterion; peak 24-72 hours post-injury
