MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

MS is a chronic autoimmune demyelinating disease of the CNS (brain and spinal cord). T-cell mediated destruction of oligodendrocytes → demyelination → plaques (white matter lesions) → neurologic deficits. Peripheral nerves are NOT affected (those are myelinated by Schwann cells).

Subtypes

Type%Course
Relapsing-remitting (RRMS)85%Discrete attacks (relapses) with full/partial recovery. Most common initial presentation
Secondary progressive (SPMS)~50% of RRMSInitially RRMS → gradual progression. Develops after 10-15 years
Primary progressive (PPMS)10-15%Steady decline from onset without distinct relapses. Older onset, equal sex ratio
🔑 Demographics: Young women (20-40), Northern latitudes (vitamin D hypothesis), HLA-DR2 association. Female:male ratio 3:1 for RRMS. Dissemination in SPACE (multiple CNS locations) and TIME (separate episodes) is required for diagnosis.
CLINICAL
Presentation & Diagnosis

Common Presentations

SyndromeFeatures
Optic neuritisMost common initial presentation. Unilateral vision loss, pain with eye movement, afferent pupillary defect (Marcus Gunn pupil). Often the first episode
Transverse myelitisSpinal cord inflammation → bilateral weakness, sensory level, bowel/bladder dysfunction. Lhermitte sign (electric shock down spine with neck flexion)
Internuclear ophthalmoplegia (INO)MLF lesion → impaired adduction of ipsilateral eye + nystagmus of contralateral abducting eye. Bilateral INO in young patient = highly suggestive of MS
CerebellarIntention tremor, ataxia, scanning speech (Charcot triad — NOT the same as Charcot triad of cholangitis)

Diagnosis

TestFindings
MRI (gold standard)Multiple periventricular white matter plaques ("Dawson fingers" — perpendicular to ventricles). Enhancing = acute, non-enhancing = chronic. Spinal cord lesions
CSFOligoclonal bands (present in >90%), elevated IgG index. NOT required if MRI diagnostic
Visual evoked potentialsDelayed P100 latency (subclinical optic nerve demyelination)
🔑 Uhthoff phenomenon: Worsening of MS symptoms with heat (hot bath, exercise, fever). Demyelinated nerves conduct worse at higher temperatures. Pathognomonic for MS. Symptoms improve when body cools.
TREATMENT
Management

Acute Relapse

IV methylprednisolone 1g/day × 3-5 days (shortens relapse duration but does NOT change long-term disability). Plasmapheresis for steroid-refractory relapses.

Disease-Modifying Therapy (DMT)

CategoryAgentsNotes
InjectableInterferon-beta, glatiramer acetateOlder, moderate efficacy. Flu-like symptoms (IFN-β)
OralFingolimod, dimethyl fumarate, teriflunomideFingolimod: first oral DMT. Risk of bradycardia (first dose). Monitor lymphocytes
High-efficacyNatalizumab, ocrelizumab, alemtuzumabNatalizumab: anti-α4 integrin. Risk of PML (JC virus). Check JCV antibody. Ocrelizumab: anti-CD20, also approved for PPMS
⚠️ PML (Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy): JC virus reactivation in immunosuppressed patients. Associated with natalizumab (especially if JCV antibody positive + >2 years use + prior immunosuppression). Rapidly progressive multifocal white matter disease. Often fatal. Must monitor JCV status.
