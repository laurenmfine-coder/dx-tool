🧴 Atopic Dermatitis

ReasonDx — Pathophysiology, Diagnosis & Modern Management

📚 Overview & Epidemiology

Atopic dermatitis (AD) is a chronic, relapsing inflammatory skin disease characterized by intense pruritus, xerosis, and eczematous lesions with age-specific distribution patterns.

Key Epidemiologic Facts

  • Prevalence: 15-20% of children, 7-10% of adults
  • Onset: 60% by age 1, 90% by age 5
  • Natural history: ~70% remit by adolescence; 30% persist to adulthood
  • Atopic march: AD → allergic rhinitis → asthma in ~30%

The Atopic March

AD is often the first manifestation of atopy, followed sequentially by food allergy, allergic rhinitis, and asthma. Early AD treatment may modify this progression.

🔑 Clinical Pearl: The "itch that rashes" - pruritus is the hallmark! AD is one of the most pruritic conditions in dermatology. No itch = question the diagnosis.

🔬 Pathophysiology: The Two-Hit Model

1. Skin Barrier Dysfunction

Filaggrin (FLG) Mutations

  • Present in 20-50% of AD patients (vs 10% general population)
  • Filaggrin aggregates keratin filaments → cornified envelope integrity
  • Breakdown products (NMF) → hydration, acidic pH
  • FLG deficiency → dry skin, increased allergen penetration, elevated pH

2. Type 2 Immune Dysregulation

The Th2 Cytokine Axis

CytokineSourceEffect
IL-4Th2 cells, ILC2sIgE class switch, barrier dysfunction
IL-13Th2 cells, ILC2sBarrier dysfunction, fibrosis, pruritus
IL-31Th2 cells"Itch cytokine" - major pruritogen

The Itch-Scratch Cycle

IL-31 activates sensory neurons → pruritus → scratching → barrier damage → more inflammation → more IL-31

🔑 Clinical Pearl: Understanding IL-4/IL-13 as central drivers led to dupilumab (anti-IL-4Rα). Understanding IL-31 led to nemolizumab (anti-IL-31RA). Pathophysiology drives therapeutics!

🩺 Clinical Features & Diagnosis

Age-Specific Distribution

👶 Infantile (0-2 years)

  • Face, scalp, cheeks
  • Extensor surfaces
  • Spares diaper area
  • Often weepy/crusted

🧒 Childhood (2-12 years)

  • Flexural involvement (antecubital, popliteal)
  • Wrists, ankles
  • Lichenification develops

🧑 Adolescent/Adult

  • Flexures persist
  • Hands, eyelids, face
  • Lichenification prominent
  • Prurigo nodularis variant

Hanifin & Rajka Diagnostic Criteria

Must have ≥3 Major + ≥3 Minor criteria:

Major (must have 3+):

  • Pruritus
  • Typical morphology and distribution
  • Chronic/relapsing course
  • Personal or family history of atopy

Minor (examples):

Xerosis, keratosis pilaris, palmar hyperlinearity, Dennie-Morgan lines, white dermatographism, elevated IgE, early age of onset, food/wool intolerance

💊 Treatment Approach

Step-Wise Management

Step 1: ALL Patients - Foundation

  • Emollients: Apply liberally, immediately after bathing ("soak and seal")
  • Trigger avoidance: Irritants (wool, fragrances), allergens if relevant
  • Bathing: Lukewarm, short duration, gentle cleansers

Step 2: Mild-Moderate - Topical Therapy

  • Topical corticosteroids: Mainstay of flare treatment
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors: Tacrolimus, pimecrolimus - steroid-sparing, safe for face
  • Crisaborole: PDE4 inhibitor - mild disease, safe for face
  • Ruxolitinib: Topical JAK inhibitor - newest option

Step 3: Moderate-Severe - Systemic Therapy

AgentMechanismKey Points
DupilumabAnti-IL-4RαFirst-line biologic; blocks IL-4 and IL-13; conjunctivitis side effect
TralokinumabAnti-IL-13Alternative to dupilumab
JAK inhibitorsAbrocitinib, upadacitinibOral; rapid onset; monitor labs (CBC, lipids)
NemolizumabAnti-IL-31RATargets itch pathway specifically
CyclosporineCalcineurin inhibitorOlder option; renal toxicity limits long-term use
🔑 Clinical Pearl: Dupilumab revolutionized AD treatment! It's injectable every 2 weeks, highly effective, and well-tolerated. Main side effect is conjunctivitis (10-20%) - manageable with eye drops.

📝 Knowledge Check

1. A 3-year-old has itchy rash in the antecubital and popliteal fossae. Mom has asthma. What genetic defect is most commonly associated?

A. Collagen VII mutation
B. Filaggrin (FLG) loss-of-function mutation
C. Keratin 5 mutation
D. Connexin 26 mutation
Filaggrin mutations are the strongest genetic risk factor for AD, present in 20-50% of patients. FLG is essential for skin barrier integrity and natural moisturizing factor production.

2. Which cytokine is primarily responsible for the intense pruritus in AD?

A. IL-4
B. IL-13
C. IL-31
D. IL-5
IL-31 is known as the "itch cytokine" because it directly stimulates sensory neurons expressing IL-31RA. This is why nemolizumab (anti-IL-31RA) specifically targets pruritus in AD.

3. What is the most common side effect of dupilumab?

A. Hepatotoxicity
B. Conjunctivitis
C. Neutropenia
D. Renal insufficiency
Conjunctivitis occurs in 10-20% of dupilumab-treated patients. It's usually manageable with lubricating eye drops or topical tacrolimus. The mechanism may involve altered ocular surface immunity.

4. Which area is typically SPARED in infantile AD?

A. Cheeks
B. Scalp
C. Diaper area
D. Extensor surfaces
The diaper area is characteristically SPARED in infantile AD - the moist, occluded environment is actually protective. Diaper dermatitis with AD involvement suggests irritant or candidal component.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • AD = barrier dysfunction (filaggrin) + Th2 immune dysregulation (IL-4, IL-13, IL-31)
  • Distribution changes with age: face/extensors (infant) → flexures (child/adult)
  • Foundation: emollients for ALL patients ("soak and seal")
  • Dupilumab (anti-IL-4Rα) is first-line biologic for moderate-severe AD
  • IL-31 drives pruritus → nemolizumab targets this specifically
