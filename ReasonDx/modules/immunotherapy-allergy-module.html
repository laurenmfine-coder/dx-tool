MECHANISM
Pathophysiology of Immune Tolerance

Allergen immunotherapy (AIT) is the only disease-modifying treatment for IgE-mediated allergic disease. It works by shifting the immune response from a Th2-dominant (allergic) to a Th1/Treg-dominant (tolerant) phenotype through repeated exposure to increasing doses of allergen.

Immunologic Mechanisms

PhaseTimelineImmunologic Change
Early DesensitizationHours-weeksMast cell/basophil desensitization → decreased mediator release. Rapid increase in allergen-specific IgG4 (blocking antibody).
T-cell ModulationWeeks-monthsShift from Th2 → Treg cells. Increased IL-10 and TGF-β production. Decreased IL-4, IL-5, IL-13. Immune deviation toward Th1.
Long-term ToleranceMonths-yearsDecreased allergen-specific IgE (late). Increased IgG4 blocking antibodies. Reduced end-organ responsiveness. Prevention of new sensitizations.
🔑 Key Concept: IgG4 is the key blocking antibody induced by immunotherapy. It competes with IgE for allergen binding, preventing mast cell crosslinking and degranulation. IgG4 levels correlate with clinical improvement and can serve as a biomarker of treatment response.
CLINICAL
Indications, Routes & Protocols

Indications

Allergic rhinitis, allergic asthma (controlled), Hymenoptera venom allergy, confirmed IgE-mediated sensitivity with clinical correlation, inadequate symptom control with avoidance + pharmacotherapy.

Routes of Administration

FeatureSCIT (Subcutaneous)SLIT (Sublingual)
RouteInjection in medical officeTablet or drops under tongue at home
BuildupWeekly injections × 6-8 months, then monthly maintenanceDaily dosing from start (some have brief escalation)
Duration3-5 years total3-5 years total
Efficacy30-40% symptom improvement20-35% symptom improvement
Anaphylaxis Risk~0.1% per injection (higher during buildup)Very rare (first dose in office required)
Observation30 minutes post-injection requiredFirst dose in office; home administration thereafter
FDA-approved SLITN/AGrastek (grass), Ragwitek (ragweed), Odactra (dust mite), Palforzia (peanut OIT — not SLIT)
⚠️ Contraindications: Uncontrolled asthma (FEV1 <70%), beta-blocker use (relative — increases risk/severity of anaphylaxis and limits treatment), severe immunodeficiency, inability to use epinephrine, pregnancy (do not initiate — may continue if established). SCIT should not be administered during active asthma exacerbation.
TREATMENT
Venom Immunotherapy & Special Considerations

Venom Immunotherapy (VIT)

VIT is the gold standard for Hymenoptera venom allergy with a history of systemic reaction. It reduces risk of future systemic reactions from 60% → 2-5%.

FeatureDetails
IndicationsHistory of systemic reaction (not just large local) + positive venom skin test or specific IgE
ProtocolBuildup to maintenance dose of 100 μg (conventional: weekly × 8-16 weeks; rush: 1-2 days; cluster: 2-3 visits)
DurationMinimum 5 years. Consider lifelong in: mastocytosis, elevated baseline tryptase, history of very severe reaction, honeybee allergy.
Efficacy95-98% protection from future systemic reactions
MonitoringSerum venom-specific IgE may decline. Negative skin test at 3-5 years supports discontinuation (except high-risk patients).
🔑 Board Pearl: All patients receiving VIT must carry epinephrine auto-injectors throughout treatment and for at least 1-2 years after stopping. Patients with mastocytosis or elevated baseline tryptase should continue VIT indefinitely — they have higher risk of fatal sting anaphylaxis.

Disease-Modifying Effects

AIT is the only allergic treatment that can: prevent new allergen sensitizations, prevent progression from allergic rhinitis to asthma (especially in children), provide sustained benefit after discontinuation, and fundamentally alter the natural history of allergic disease.
