🧪 Contact Dermatitis

ReasonDx — Allergic vs Irritant, Patch Testing & Management

📚 Overview: Two Types of Contact Dermatitis

Feature Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD) Allergic Contact Dermatitis (ACD)
Mechanism Direct cytotoxic damage (non-immune) Type IV delayed hypersensitivity (T-cell mediated)
Prevalence 80% of contact dermatitis 20% of contact dermatitis
Prior sensitization Not required REQUIRED (prior exposure)
Onset after exposure Minutes to hours 24-72 hours (delayed)
Dose-dependent Yes - concentration matters No - even tiny amounts trigger
Anyone can develop Yes - anyone with enough exposure No - only sensitized individuals
🔑 Clinical Pearl: The key distinction is MECHANISM. ICD is toxic damage to skin (like a chemical burn). ACD is an immune response requiring prior sensitization. Both can look identical clinically!

🔬 Allergic Contact Dermatitis: Pathophysiology

Phase 1: Sensitization (Afferent Phase)

  • Hapten (small chemical) penetrates skin
  • Binds to carrier protein → becomes immunogenic
  • Langerhans cells (dendritic cells) capture antigen
  • Migrate to lymph nodes → present to T cells
  • Generate allergen-specific memory T cells (Th1/Tc1)
  • Takes 10-14 days - patient is now SENSITIZED

Phase 2: Elicitation (Efferent Phase)

  • Re-exposure to same allergen
  • Memory T cells activated within hours
  • Cytokine release (IFN-γ, TNF-α)
  • Inflammation at contact site
  • Clinical reaction: 24-72 hours

Why "Delayed" Hypersensitivity?

Unlike IgE-mediated reactions (minutes), Type IV reactions require T-cell trafficking and cytokine cascades, taking 1-3 days to manifest clinically.

🔑 Clinical Pearl: First exposure NEVER causes ACD! Sensitization takes time. If a patient reacts to a product "immediately" on first use, it's likely ICD, not ACD.

🔍 Diagnosis & Treatment

Patch Testing - The Gold Standard for ACD

Procedure:

  • Day 0: Allergens applied to back under occlusion (Finn chambers or T.R.U.E. test)
  • Day 2 (48h): Patches removed, initial reading
  • Day 4-7: Delayed reading (ESSENTIAL - some reactions appear late)

Reading Scale:

  • Negative: No reaction
  • +: Erythema only
  • ++: Erythema, papules, vesicles
  • +++: Intense vesicles, bullae
  • IR: Irritant reaction (not allergy)

⚠️ Patch Test Pitfalls

  • False negatives: On systemic steroids, recent UV exposure, wrong allergen concentration
  • False positives: Irritant reactions (sharp borders, pustules), "angry back" syndrome
  • Must read at 96+ hours: Many positives appear on delayed reading

📝 Knowledge Check

1. A patient develops a rash within hours of using a new harsh cleaning product without gloves. This is most likely:

A. Irritant contact dermatitis
B. Allergic contact dermatitis
C. Contact urticaria
D. Atopic dermatitis flare
ICD occurs rapidly after exposure to irritating substances and does NOT require prior sensitization. ACD requires prior exposure and has a delayed onset of 24-72 hours.

2. A 25-year-old woman presents with bilateral eyelid dermatitis but no hand involvement. What should you ask about?

A. New eye drops
B. New mascara
C. New nail polish
D. New face wash
Nail polish dermatitis classically causes eyelid (and face/neck) dermatitis WITHOUT hand involvement! Wet polish transfers to the thin eyelid skin before drying. The thicker palmar skin is resistant.

3. Patch test readings are typically performed at which time points?

A. 24 hours only
B. 48 hours AND 96+ hours
C. 15 minutes and 48 hours
D. One week only
Standard patch testing requires readings at BOTH 48 hours (when patches are removed) AND a delayed reading at 96+ hours. Many true allergic reactions only appear on delayed reading!

4. Which is the most common contact allergen worldwide?

A. Nickel
B. Fragrance
C. Neomycin
D. Formaldehyde
Nickel is the #1 contact allergen worldwide. Sources include jewelry (especially costume), belt buckles, snaps, watches, and metal tools. Prevalence is higher in women due to ear piercing.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • ICD (80%) = toxic damage; ACD (20%) = Type IV hypersensitivity requiring prior sensitization
  • ACD has delayed onset (24-72h) because it's T-cell mediated
  • Patch testing is gold standard - read at 48h AND 96+h
  • Distribution pattern often suggests the allergen (eyelids→nail polish, ears→nickel)
  • Primary treatment is allergen avoidance; steroids are supportive
