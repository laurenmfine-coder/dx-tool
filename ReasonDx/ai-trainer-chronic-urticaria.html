💡 Key Teaching Point
Chronic urticaria is defined as hives occurring most days for ≥6 weeks. When no external trigger is identified (food, drug, infection), it's called Chronic Spontaneous Urticaria (CSU). Despite patient and physician instincts to "find the cause," extensive allergy testing is NOT recommended and rarely identifies a trigger.

🔬 The Limited Workup
Step 4 of 6
⚠️ Common Mistake: Overtesting in CSU
Many patients with CSU receive extensive (and expensive) allergy panels, autoimmune workups, and imaging. Guidelines recommend a LIMITED workup because extensive testing rarely changes management and can cause patient anxiety from false positives.
✅ Guideline-Recommended CSU Workup
Routine (all patients): CBC with differential, CRP or ESR

If history suggests: TSH (thyroid symptoms), consider IgE (if planning omalizumab)

NOT recommended routinely: Allergy skin testing, food-specific IgE panels, ANA, complement levels, hepatitis serologies, stool O&P, H. pylori testing

📋 CSU Treatment Algorithm (Guideline-Based)
1
Second-generation H1 antihistamine (standard dose)
Cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine, etc. She's already on this and it's not working.
2
INCREASE antihistamine up to 4× standard dose
Example: Cetirizine 10mg → 20mg → 40mg daily. This is the NEXT step for Sarah. Safe and effective in most patients.
3
Add omalizumab (Xolair) if antihistamines fail
Anti-IgE biologic, FDA-approved for CSU. Works regardless of IgE level. Given as injection q4 weeks.
4
Consider cyclosporine if omalizumab fails
Reserved for refractory cases. Requires monitoring. Avoid chronic systemic steroids!
