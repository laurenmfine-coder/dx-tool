D-dimer--
Your Prediction
--
Actual
4,850 ng/mL
Why elevated: D-dimer is a fibrin degradation product released when clots break down. In acute PE, both clot formation AND fibrinolysis are active. Key point: D-dimer is sensitive but NOT specific — also elevated in surgery, trauma, pregnancy, cancer, infection. Use: Only helpful to RULE OUT PE in LOW probability patients (high NPV).
Troponin--
Your Prediction
--
Actual
0.12 ng/mL
Why mildly elevated: PE causes acute RV strain from sudden increased pulmonary vascular resistance. RV wall stress → myocyte injury → troponin release. Elevated troponin in PE = worse prognosis (intermediate-high risk), may warrant closer monitoring or intervention. NOT coronary ischemia!

Why mildly elevated: BNP is released in response to ventricular wall stretch. Acute PE → RV dilation → BNP release. Like troponin, elevated BNP in PE indicates RV dysfunction and higher risk. Used for risk stratification, not diagnosis.

58 mmHg
Why low: V/Q mismatch is the primary mechanism. Clot blocks perfusion to ventilated lung → dead space. Blood shunts to other areas → relative shunt physiology. Also: atelectasis from surfactant loss, and possible R-to-L shunting via PFO. Pearl: ~20% of PE patients have normal PaO2!

28 mmHg
Why low (hypocapnia): Hypoxemia triggers hyperventilation via peripheral chemoreceptors. Patient "blows off" CO2, causing respiratory alkalosis. This is the CLASSIC ABG pattern in PE: hypoxemia + hypocapnia + respiratory alkalosis. A-a gradient is WIDENED (calculated: ~35, normal <15).

🎯 Key Takeaways
  • D-dimer rules OUT: Only useful in LOW probability patients
  • Troponin/BNP = RV strain: Risk stratification, not diagnosis
  • Classic ABG: Hypoxemia + hypocapnia + resp alkalosis
  • A-a gradient widened: V/Q mismatch increases gradient
  • Wells score guides workup: High prob → CT angio (skip D-dimer)
