Case: Upper GI Bleed
GI / Hepatology

Chief Complaint: 58-year-old man with melena and lightheadedness for 2 days

History: History of osteoarthritis (takes ibuprofen daily), hypertension, and GERD. Reports 3 episodes of black, tarry stools over past 2 days. Felt dizzy when standing this morning. Denies hematemesis, hematochezia, abdominal pain. No prior GI bleeds. Takes aspirin 81mg daily.

Vitals: BP 108/72 (lying), 88/60 (standing), HR 98 (lying), 118 (standing), RR 18, SpO2 98% on RA

Physical Exam: Pale, mild epigastric tenderness, melena on rectal exam, no hepatosplenomegaly or stigmata of liver disease.

Why moderate: The Hgb may underestimate acute blood loss because it measures concentration, not total volume. Hemodilution takes 24-72 hours. Orthostatic changes suggest significant loss even if Hgb is "only" 9.2.
BUN--
Your Prediction
--
Actual
52 mg/dL
Why high: Blood in the GI tract is digested like a high-protein meal. Amino acids are absorbed and converted to urea in the liver. Also, hypovolemia increases urea reabsorption.

Why very high: BUN:Cr >30:1 is classic for UGIB. The ratio helps distinguish upper from lower GI bleeds. In LGIB, the ratio is typically normal because blood isn't digested.

🎯 Key Takeaways
  • BUN:Cr >30:1: Suggests upper GI bleed (blood is digested)
  • Initial Hgb lies: Hemodilution takes 24-72h — use clinical signs
  • Orthostatic changes: Significant volume loss (~15-20%)
  • NSAID + ASA: High risk for peptic ulcer bleeding
  • Lactate tracks severity: Higher = more shock
