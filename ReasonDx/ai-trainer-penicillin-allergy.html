📋 Clinical Scenario
Patient Presentation

45-year-old woman admitted for cellulitis of the left lower extremity requiring IV antibiotics.

Her chart shows "Penicillin Allergy" documented in the medication allergy section.

Why This Matters

The ID team recommends cefazolin or ampicillin-sulbactam as first-line therapy for her cellulitis. Before selecting an alternative antibiotic, you need to evaluate this reported allergy.

🔍 Allergy History Taking

You ask the patient about her penicillin allergy. Select the most important questions to ask:

What key information should you gather about the reported reaction?
A
What symptoms occurred during the reaction?
B
How long ago did the reaction occur?
C
Was epinephrine or hospitalization required?
D
Has she tolerated penicillins or cephalosporins since?
💡 Clinical Pearl

All four questions are essential! A complete drug allergy history includes: (1) specific symptoms, (2) timing since reaction, (3) severity markers, and (4) subsequent tolerance. ~90% of patients labeled "penicillin allergic" can safely take penicillins after proper evaluation.

Patient's Response

She reports: "I was told I had a rash as a baby when I was given amoxicillin. I've avoided penicillin my whole life. I don't remember any details because I was too young."

She has never been hospitalized for the reaction, never received epinephrine, and has taken cephalexin (Keflex) without problems for a UTI 2 years ago.

🧪 Testing Strategy
PEN-FAST Interpretation

Score -2 points = Very Low Risk (<1% chance of positive penicillin skin test)

Key factors: Remote childhood rash (>10 years ago), no anaphylaxis features, tolerated cephalosporin since (-2 points)

What is the appropriate next step for this very low-risk patient?
A
Penicillin skin testing before any beta-lactam use
B
Direct oral amoxicillin challenge (no skin testing needed)
C
Avoid all beta-lactams and use alternative antibiotics
D
Only use cephalosporins, never penicillins
💡 Clinical Pearl: Cross-Reactivity

The 10% cross-reactivity myth is outdated! True cross-reactivity between penicillins and cephalosporins is ~2% (driven by similar R1 side chains, not the beta-lactam ring). Patients who tolerate cephalosporins have an extremely low risk of reacting to penicillin.

💊 Direct Oral Challenge
Challenge Protocol

You proceed with a direct oral amoxicillin challenge in a monitored setting:

  • Amoxicillin 250mg PO x1 dose
  • Observe for 60 minutes
  • Monitor for urticaria, angioedema, respiratory symptoms, hypotension
  • Emergency medications at bedside (epinephrine, antihistamines, steroids)
60 minutes after the challenge dose, the patient has no symptoms. What should you document?
A
"Penicillin allergy - tolerated challenge" (keep allergy label)
B
"Penicillin allergy REMOVED - negative oral challenge" (delabel)
C
"Use penicillins with caution" (ambiguous label)
D
Wait for further testing before making any documentation change
✅ Case Summary & Outcomes
Patient Outcome

The patient tolerated the amoxicillin challenge without any reaction. Her penicillin allergy label was removed from the medical record.

She was started on ampicillin-sulbactam IV for her cellulitis with excellent response.

🎯 Why Delabeling Matters

Penicillin allergy labels cause real harm:

  • Increased use of broad-spectrum antibiotics → antibiotic resistance
  • Higher rates of C. difficile infection
  • Increased MRSA and VRE infections
  • Longer hospital stays and higher costs
  • Suboptimal treatment for many infections
Key Takeaways
  • ~90% of patients labeled "penicillin allergic" are NOT truly allergic
  • PEN-FAST score helps stratify risk quickly
  • Very low-risk patients (score <3) can proceed to direct oral challenge
  • Prior cephalosporin tolerance is strongly reassuring
  • Delabeling improves patient safety and antibiotic stewardship
