Case: Decompensated Cirrhosis with Hepatic Encephalopathy
GI / Hepatology

Chief Complaint: 58-year-old man brought in by family for confusion and sleepiness for 2 days

History: Alcoholic cirrhosis diagnosed 3 years ago, complicated by ascites requiring periodic paracentesis. Stopped drinking 6 months ago. Family notes he has been increasingly confused, sleeping more, and talking to himself. Missed lactulose doses for 3 days due to diarrhea. Recent URI treated with antibiotics.

Vitals: BP 102/58, HR 88, RR 16, SpO2 98% on RA, Temp 37.3C

Physical Exam: Jaundiced sclera, temporal wasting. Asterixis present. Distended abdomen with shifting dullness, caput medusae. Spider angiomata on chest. 2+ pitting edema bilateral legs. Disoriented to time and place.

Ammonia--
Your Prediction
--
Actual
128 ug/dL
Why elevated: The liver converts ammonia (from gut bacteria/protein metabolism) to urea. In cirrhosis, both impaired hepatocyte function AND portosystemic shunting allow ammonia to bypass the liver and cross BBB causing encephalopathy. Caveat: Ammonia levels do not correlate well with HE severity. Diagnosis is clinical!
INR--
Your Prediction
--
Actual
2.1
Why elevated: The liver synthesizes most clotting factors (I, II, V, VII, IX, X). Factor VII has the shortest half-life (6 hours), so INR rises early in liver failure. Pearl: INR does not fully capture bleeding risk in cirrhosis because they also have reduced anticoagulant factors (protein C, S), creating rebalanced hemostasis.
Albumin--
Your Prediction
--
Actual
2.2 g/dL
Why very low: Albumin is synthesized exclusively by the liver. Low albumin indicates chronic synthetic dysfunction. Clinical impact: Low albumin = low oncotic pressure = ascites/edema. Also means reduced protein binding of drugs. Used in Child-Pugh and MELD-Na scoring.

Your Prediction
--
Actual
4.8 mg/dL
Why elevated: Impaired bilirubin conjugation and excretion. Clinical jaundice visible when bili >2.5 (scleral icterus first). In cirrhosis, both direct and indirect fractions rise. Higher bilirubin = worse prognosis (part of MELD score).

68 K/uL
Why low: Two main mechanisms: (1) Splenic sequestration from portal hypertension causing splenomegaly, (2) Decreased thrombopoietin production (TPO made by liver). Platelet count below 150K is often the FIRST lab clue to cirrhosis!

Key Takeaways
  • Synthetic function: Low albumin, elevated INR, low platelets
  • Excretory function: Elevated bilirubin
  • Detox function: Elevated ammonia
  • Thrombocytopenia: Splenic sequestration + decreased TPO
  • HE triggers: Infection, GI bleed, constipation, meds, dehydration
  • Ammonia caveat: Level does not correlate with severity
