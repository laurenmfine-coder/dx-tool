Clinical Reasoning Adventure

You're the senior resident in the ICU when the ED calls: "We have a 58-year-old man with progressive dyspnea over the past week. He had CABG 10 days ago. He looks really sick - blood pressure is tanking."

What is your primary differential diagnosis?

A. Cardiogenic shock from post-operative MI
B. Cardiac tamponade from post-operative pericardial effusion

Scene 2: Recognizing the Triad

You immediately think of Beck's Triad. Let's review what you're seeing:

💡 Beck's Triad for Tamponade

  1. Hypotension (BP 82/68) ✓
  2. Elevated JVP (JVP at angle of jaw) ✓
  3. Muffled heart sounds (distant sounds) ✓

You ask the nurse to get the blood pressure in both arms while you continue your exam. You notice something as the patient breathes...

What physical finding would MOST support your diagnosis?

A. S3 gallop
B. Pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg
C. Friction rub
D. Peripheral edema
Scene 3: Confirming the Diagnosis

The nurse measured the blood pressure with respiration:

  • Inspiration: BP 68/52
  • Expiration: BP 84/66
  • Pulsus paradoxus = 16 mmHg (>10 mmHg is significant)

You order an ECG and bedside echo:

ECG Shows:
  • Sinus tachycardia at 118 bpm
  • Low voltage QRS complexes in limb leads
  • Electrical alternans (beat-to-beat QRS amplitude variation)
Bedside Echo Shows:
  • Large circumferential pericardial effusion
  • Right atrial collapse during systole
  • Right ventricular diastolic collapse
  • "Swinging heart" motion

Which echo finding is the EARLIEST sign of tamponade physiology?

A. Right atrial collapse in late diastole/early systole
B. Right ventricular diastolic collapse
C. IVC plethora without respiratory variation
D. Left atrial collapse
Scene 4: Definitive Management

You have confirmed cardiac tamponade. The patient's blood pressure drops further to 76/58. He's becoming more confused.

⚠️ Tamponade is a CLINICAL DIAGNOSIS

The size of the effusion doesn't determine tamponade - it's the RATE of accumulation. A rapidly accumulating 200mL can cause tamponade, while a chronic 1L effusion may be tolerated.

What is the definitive treatment?

A. IV fluids and inotropes while preparing for surgery
B. Emergent pericardiocentesis
C. Diuretics to reduce preload
D. Intubation and mechanical ventilation

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • Beck's Triad: Hypotension + JVD + muffled heart sounds
  • Pulsus paradoxus: SBP drop >10 mmHg with inspiration
  • ECG clues: Low voltage + electrical alternans
  • Echo findings: RA collapse (earliest) → RV diastolic collapse → IVC plethora
  • Treatment: Pericardiocentesis (avoid diuretics/inotropes alone!)
  • Bridge: IV fluids maintain preload until drainage
