👶 Pediatric Consideration

Febrile seizures are common in children 6 months to 5 years. However, a seizure lasting >5 minutes or with focal features requires aggressive treatment as status epilepticus regardless of fever.

👶 Pediatric Routes

If IV access is delayed, IM midazolam or rectal diazepam are effective alternatives. Intranasal midazolam (0.2 mg/kg) is another option. Don't delay treatment waiting for IV access!

👶 Pediatric Agent Selection

Levetiracetam is often preferred in pediatrics due to its favorable safety profile. Avoid valproate in children <2 years (mitochondrial disease risk) and in suspected metabolic disorders. Fosphenytoin requires cardiac monitoring.

You choose levetiracetam. Despite loading, seizures continue at 25 minutes. This is now:

🏥 Refractory Status Epilepticus Scene 4 of 6
The patient is now in refractory status epilepticus (RSE) - seizures persisting despite adequate doses of benzodiazepine AND one second-line agent. This requires ICU admission, intubation, and continuous anesthetic infusion with EEG monitoring.

👶 Pediatric Anesthetic Considerations

Propofol infusion syndrome (PRIS) risk is higher in children - avoid prolonged high-dose infusions. Midazolam is generally preferred for pediatric RSE. Pentobarbital is reserved for super-refractory cases. Continuous EEG monitoring is essential.

🔬 Etiology Workup Scene 5 of 6
While managing the seizures, you must identify and treat the underlying cause. The mnemonic VITAMINS helps remember causes of status epilepticus.

📚 VITAMINS - Causes of Status Epilepticus

Vascular (stroke, hemorrhage, PRES, vasculitis)
Infection (meningitis, encephalitis, abscess)
Trauma (TBI, subdural)
Autoimmune (anti-NMDA, autoimmune encephalitis)
Metabolic (hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, uremia, hepatic)
Ingestion/Intoxication (drugs, toxins, withdrawal)
Neoplasm (primary CNS, metastases, paraneoplastic)
Seizure disorder (breakthrough, non-compliance)

👶 Pediatric-Specific Causes

Febrile SE: Most common in children 6mo-5yr. Genetic epilepsies: Dravet, SCN1A mutations. Metabolic: Higher index of suspicion for inborn errors. Infectious: HSV encephalitis, bacterial meningitis. Always check glucose!

🔑 Key Learning Points

  • Time is brain: Start benzodiazepines immediately - don't wait for IV access
  • Definition: Seizure ≥5 min OR ≥2 seizures without return to baseline = status epilepticus
  • Benzos first: Lorazepam IV or midazolam IM - up to 2 doses
  • Second-line agents: Fosphenytoin, levetiracetam, or valproate are equivalent
  • RSE definition: Seizures despite benzo + one second-line agent
  • EEG target: Burst suppression for 24-48 hours, then slow wean
  • Always check glucose: Hypoglycemia is rapidly reversible cause

👶 Pediatric Key Points

• Weight-based dosing is critical - have a reference or calculator ready
• Don't delay for IV - use IM/IN/PR benzodiazepines if needed
• Levetiracetam preferred over valproate in young children
• PRIS risk higher - use midazolam over propofol for RSE
• Febrile SE still requires full treatment but prognosis often good
