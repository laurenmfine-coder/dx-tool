📊 Initial Response Scene 3 of 6
You give IM epinephrine 0.22 mg. After 5 minutes: HR 140, BP 85/55, RR 28, SpO2 94% on O2. Wheezing improved but still present. Hives persisting. IV access established.

⏱️ Epinephrine Timing

Epinephrine can be repeated every 5-15 minutes if symptoms persist. Most patients need 1-2 doses. If >2 doses needed, consider epinephrine infusion.

What is your NEXT step?

⏱️ Biphasic Reaction Risk Scene 5 of 6
After epinephrine infusion and additional fluids, the child stabilizes. BP 98/62, HR 110, SpO2 99% RA. Parents ask if they can take him home now.

🔄 Biphasic Anaphylaxis

Incidence: 5-20% of cases, more common in children
Timing: Usually 1-72 hours after initial resolution
Risk factors: Severe initial reaction, delayed epinephrine, food triggers

How long should this child be observed?

🎓 Discharge Planning Scene 6 of 6
After 6 hours with no recurrence, the child is ready for discharge.

📋 Essential Discharge Components

1. Epinephrine Auto-injector × 2 (home + school)
2. Written Anaphylaxis Action Plan
3. Training on auto-injector use
4. School 504 plan
5. Allergist referral within 4-6 weeks
6. MedicAlert bracelet

🔑 Key Learning Points

  • Epinephrine FIRST: 0.01 mg/kg IM (max 0.5 mg)
  • Lateral thigh: Best absorption site
  • Repeat q5-15 min: If symptoms persist
  • Refractory = infusion: After 2 IM doses, start IV drip
  • Observe 4-6 hours: Biphasic risk in 5-20%
  • Prescribe 2 devices: Home + school
  • Allergist referral: All anaphylaxis needs follow-up
