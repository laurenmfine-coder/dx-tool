🔺 Virchow's Triad: The Foundation

Three Factors Predisposing to Thrombosis

Rudolf Virchow identified three categories of factors that promote venous thrombosis. Most VTE events involve at least one of these:

  • Stasis — Reduced blood flow allows accumulation of clotting factors
  • Endothelial injury — Damage exposes subendothelial collagen and tissue factor
  • Hypercoagulability — Inherited or acquired states favoring clot formation

🔄 How Each Factor Contributes

S
Stasis: Immobility, paralysis, long flights, venous obstruction, heart failure
E
Endothelial injury: Surgery, trauma, central lines, prior DVT, vasculitis
H
Hypercoagulability: Cancer, pregnancy, estrogen, Factor V Leiden, antiphospholipid syndrome

💡 Clinical Pearl

Always ask about recent surgery, travel, immobility, cancer history, pregnancy, and hormone use. A patient with no identifiable risk factors who develops unprovoked VTE should be evaluated for occult malignancy.

🫁 Pulmonary Embolism Pathophysiology

Hemodynamic Consequences

When clot embolizes to the pulmonary arteries, the effects depend on clot burden and underlying cardiopulmonary reserve:

  • Mechanical obstruction — Blocks blood flow through pulmonary vasculature
  • Increased RV afterload — RV pressure rises to overcome obstruction
  • RV dilation — RV dilates as it fails against increased afterload
  • Interventricular dependence — Dilated RV compresses LV, reducing LV filling
  • Decreased cardiac output — LV can't fill properly → systemic hypotension

🔄 The Hemodynamic Spiral

1
PE causes acute increase in pulmonary vascular resistance
2
RV dilates and wall tension increases (Laplace's law)
3
RV oxygen demand rises while coronary perfusion falls
4
RV ischemia leads to further dysfunction → cardiogenic shock

💡 Clinical Pearl

A normal oxygen saturation does not rule out PE. Young patients with good cardiopulmonary reserve can maintain saturation despite significant clot burden through compensatory hyperventilation and vasoconstriction.

📊 Clinical Probability Assessment

Wells Criteria for PE

The Wells score helps estimate pre-test probability of PE:

Criterion Points
Clinical signs/symptoms of DVT+3
PE is #1 diagnosis or equally likely+3
Heart rate >100 bpm+1.5
Immobilization ≥3 days or surgery in past 4 weeks+1.5
Previous PE or DVT+1.5
Hemoptysis+1
Malignancy (treatment within 6 months or palliative)+1

Interpretation: ≤4 points = PE unlikely; >4 points = PE likely

D-dimer: Understanding the Test

D-dimer is a fibrin degradation product released when cross-linked fibrin is broken down:

  • High sensitivity (~95%)}} — A negative result effectively rules out VTE in low-probability patients
  • Low specificity (~40%) — Elevated in infection, inflammation, cancer, pregnancy, recent surgery
  • Age-adjusted cutoff — Using (age × 10) μg/L for patients >50 improves specificity

⚠️ When NOT to Use D-dimer

D-dimer should not be used to rule out PE in high-probability patients. If clinical suspicion is high, proceed directly to imaging regardless of D-dimer result.

⚠️ PE Risk Stratification

Why Stratification Matters

PE mortality ranges from <1% to >50% depending on hemodynamic status. Risk stratification determines treatment intensity:

Category Features Mortality Management
Low Risk Stable, no RV dysfunction, normal biomarkers <1% Outpatient anticoagulation possible
Intermediate-Low Stable, RV dysfunction OR elevated troponin 3-5% Inpatient anticoagulation, monitoring
Intermediate-High Stable, RV dysfunction AND elevated troponin 5-15% ICU monitoring, rescue reperfusion ready
High Risk (Massive) Hypotension, shock, or cardiac arrest >15% Reperfusion therapy (thrombolysis, embolectomy)

Markers of RV Dysfunction

  • Echocardiography — RV dilation, hypokinesis, McConnell's sign
  • CT findings — RV/LV ratio >1.0, septal bowing into LV
  • ECG — S1Q3T3 pattern, right heart strain, new RBBB
  • BNP/NT-proBNP — Elevated due to RV wall stress
  • Troponin — Indicates RV myocardial injury

💡 Clinical Pearl

The classic S1Q3T3 pattern is specific but insensitive (seen in only ~20% of PE). Sinus tachycardia is the most common ECG finding. A normal ECG does not exclude PE.
