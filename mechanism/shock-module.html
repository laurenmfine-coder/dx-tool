⚙️ Shock Pathophysiology

Pathophysiology & Clinical Correlations | Critical Care

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Identify shock type to guide treatment (fluids vs vasopressors vs inotropes)
  • Distributive = vasodilation (warm), others = vasoconstriction (cold)
  • Lactate elevation indicates tissue hypoperfusion regardless of BP
  • MAP ≥65 is target but end-organ perfusion is the real goal

🔬 Core Pathophysiology

Distributive Shock (Sepsis)

Vasodilation and capillary leak cause relative hypovolemia despite normal or high cardiac output.

Pathway

Pathogen triggers immune response→ Cytokine release (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6)→ Vasodilation + capillary leak→ Decreased SVR→ Tissue hypoperfusion despite ↑CO

Cardiogenic Shock

Pump failure leads to inadequate cardiac output and tissue hypoperfusion.

Pathway

Myocardial injury (MI, myocarditis)→ Decreased contractility→ Reduced cardiac output→ Compensatory vasoconstriction→ Increased afterload → further ↓CO

Hypovolemic Shock

Volume loss leads to decreased preload and cardiac output.

Pathway

Volume loss (hemorrhage, dehydration)→ Decreased preload→ Decreased stroke volume→ Compensatory tachycardia + vasoconstriction→ Eventually inadequate perfusion

🏥 Clinical Correlations

Warm extremities in sepsis

Mechanism: Vasodilation causes increased blood flow to periphery

Clinical Significance: "Warm shock" - distinguishes early sepsis from cardiogenic/hypovolemic

JVD in cardiogenic shock

Mechanism: Pump failure causes backup into venous system

Clinical Significance: Indicates fluid overload - avoid aggressive fluid resuscitation

Narrowed pulse pressure in hypovolemia

Mechanism: Compensatory vasoconstriction maintains diastolic BP

Clinical Significance: Early sign before hypotension develops
