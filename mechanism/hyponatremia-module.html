Hyponatremia

Master the systematic approach to low sodium — from osmolality to volume status to urine studies. The most common electrolyte abnormality, and one of the most misunderstood.

📚 10 Concepts ❓ 8 MCQs ⏱ ~45 min

🧪 Sodium Physiology

What Does Sodium Really Measure?

Serum sodium is a measure of water balance, not sodium balance:

  • Hyponatremia = too much water relative to sodium
  • Hypernatremia = too little water relative to sodium
  • You can have hyponatremia with high, normal, or low total body sodium
  • The key regulator is ADH (vasopressin), which controls water retention

The Edelman Equation (Simplified)

Serum Na⁺ ≈ (Total body Na⁺ + Total body K⁺) / Total body water

This means sodium can be low because:

  • Total body water is increased (dilution)
  • Total body sodium is decreased (depletion)
  • Both (most common scenario)

💡 Clinical Pearl

Hyponatremia is almost always a problem of too much ADH (or ADH-like effect). Ask: "Why can't this patient excrete free water?" The answer usually points to the cause.

🔍 The Diagnostic Algorithm

Step-by-Step Approach

Step 1: Check Serum Osmolality

Low (<280) → True hypotonic hyponatremia (proceed to step 2)
Normal (280-295) → Pseudohyponatremia (hyperlipidemia, hyperproteinemia)
High (>295) → Hypertonic hyponatremia (hyperglycemia, mannitol)

Step 2: Assess Volume Status

Hypovolemic → Skin turgor ↓, HR ↑, orthostasis (renal vs extrarenal losses)
Euvolemic → No edema, no volume depletion (SIADH, hypothyroid, adrenal insufficiency)
Hypervolemic → Edema, JVD, ascites (CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome)

Step 3: Check Urine Studies

Urine Na <20 + Urine Osm <100 → Primary polydipsia, reset osmostat
Urine Na <20 + Urine Osm >100 → Extrarenal losses, low effective arterial volume
Urine Na >20 + Urine Osm >100 → Renal losses (diuretics) or SIADH

Key Urine Studies

TestWhat It Tells YouInterpretation
Urine Osm Is ADH active? <100 = maximally dilute (ADH suppressed); >100 = concentrated (ADH present)
Urine Na Kidney sodium handling <20 = avid retention (hypovolemia); >20 = wasting (diuretics, SIADH)
FENa Fractional excretion Useful in AKI; less helpful in hyponatremia workup

📊 Causes by Volume Status

Hypovolemic Hyponatremia

Lost sodium AND water, but relatively more sodium. ADH stimulated by hypovolemia.

Renal Losses (UNa >20)Extrarenal Losses (UNa <20)
Diuretics (especially thiazides) Vomiting, diarrhea
Salt-wasting nephropathy Burns, pancreatitis
Cerebral salt wasting Third-spacing
Mineralocorticoid deficiency Excessive sweating

Euvolemic Hyponatremia

Excess water retention without obvious volume overload or depletion. This is where SIADH lives.

  • SIADH — The classic cause (see next section)
  • Hypothyroidism — Decreased cardiac output triggers ADH
  • Adrenal insufficiency — Cortisol normally suppresses ADH
  • Primary polydipsia — Overwhelms diluting capacity (urine osm <100)
  • Beer potomania / Tea and toast — Low solute intake limits free water excretion

Hypervolemic Hyponatremia

Total body sodium AND water are increased, but relatively more water. Low effective arterial volume triggers ADH.

  • Heart failure — Poor forward flow → ADH activation
  • Cirrhosis — Splanchnic vasodilation → underfilling
  • Nephrotic syndrome — Oncotic pressure loss → underfilling
  • Advanced CKD — Impaired diluting capacity

💡 Clinical Pearl

Thiazides cause hyponatremia much more than loop diuretics. Thiazides impair the diluting segment (DCT) while preserving concentrating ability. Loops impair both, so urine stays relatively isotonic.

🎯 SIADH Deep Dive

SIADH Diagnostic Criteria

  • Serum osmolality <275 mOsm/kg
  • Urine osmolality >100 mOsm/kg (inappropriately concentrated)
  • Urine sodium >20-40 mEq/L (euvolemic → no sodium retention)
  • Clinical euvolemia
  • Normal thyroid and adrenal function
  • No diuretic use

Causes of SIADH

CategoryExamples
CNS disordersStroke, hemorrhage, infection, trauma
PulmonaryPneumonia, TB, lung cancer, positive pressure ventilation
MalignancySmall cell lung cancer (classic), head/neck cancers
MedicationsSSRIs, carbamazepine, cyclophosphamide, NSAIDs, opioids
OtherPain, nausea, post-operative state

💡 Clinical Pearl

SIADH is a diagnosis of exclusion. Always rule out hypothyroidism and adrenal insufficiency first — both can mimic SIADH exactly but have specific treatments. Check TSH and AM cortisol.

⚠️ Symptoms and Severity

Clinical Manifestations

Symptoms correlate with rate of development more than absolute sodium level:

SeverityNa LevelSymptoms
Mild130-135Often asymptomatic; subtle gait/attention issues
Moderate125-129Nausea, headache, confusion, fatigue
Severe<125Vomiting, seizures, obtundation, coma

Acute vs Chronic Hyponatremia

  • Acute (<48 hours) — Brain hasn't adapted; high risk of cerebral edema; can correct more rapidly
  • Chronic (>48 hours) — Brain has extruded osmolytes; adapted to low Na; rapid correction causes ODS
  • Unknown duration — Treat as chronic unless clearly acute

⚠️ Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome (ODS)

Formerly "central pontine myelinolysis." Caused by overly rapid correction of chronic hyponatremia.

  • Symptoms emerge 2-6 days after correction: dysarthria, dysphagia, quadriparesis, "locked-in" syndrome
  • Risk factors: alcoholism, malnutrition, hypokalemia, liver disease
  • Prevention: correct Na no more than 8-10 mEq/L per 24 hours

💊 Treatment

Treatment Principles

ScenarioTreatmentGoal
Severe symptoms (seizures, coma) Hypertonic saline (3%) 100-150 mL bolus Raise Na 4-6 mEq/L in first 1-2 hours
Symptomatic but stable Hypertonic saline infusion 8-10 mEq/L in first 24 hours
Chronic, asymptomatic Treat underlying cause; fluid restriction Gradual correction over days
Hypovolemic Normal saline Restore volume (may autocorrect)

SIADH-Specific Treatments

  • Fluid restriction — First-line; limit to 800-1000 mL/day
  • Salt tablets + loop diuretic — Increase solute excretion
  • Urea — Osmotic diuresis; unpleasant taste
  • Vaptans (tolvaptan) — ADH receptor antagonist; avoid in hypovolemia; liver toxicity risk
  • Demeclocycline — Induces nephrogenic DI; rarely used now

⚠️ Overcorrection Management

If Na corrects too fast (>10-12 mEq/24h), you can "re-lower" sodium:

  • Give D5W (free water)
  • Administer desmopressin (DDAVP) to prevent further water excretion
  • Goal: bring Na back down to target correction rate

💡 Clinical Pearl

Watch for "autocorrection" in hypovolemic hyponatremia. Once volume is restored, ADH shuts off and the kidneys dump free water rapidly. This can cause overcorrection and ODS. Monitor Na frequently (q2-4h) during treatment.

🔍 Special Populations

Exercise-Associated Hyponatremia

  • Common in marathon runners, triathletes
  • Mechanism: excessive hypotonic fluid intake + non-osmotic ADH release
  • Prevention: drink to thirst, not on a schedule
  • Treatment: fluid restriction if mild; hypertonic saline if symptomatic

Beer Potomania / Tea and Toast Syndrome

  • Very low solute intake → limited free water excretion capacity
  • Normal kidneys can only dilute urine to ~50 mOsm/kg
  • With low solute, can only excrete limited free water (solute ÷ 50 = max L/day)
  • Treatment: increase solute intake (protein, salt); urine osm will be low

Post-Operative Hyponatremia

  • Pain, nausea, opioids all stimulate ADH
  • Hypotonic fluid administration (D5W, ½NS) compounds problem
  • Young women at highest risk for complications
  • Prevention: use isotonic fluids; monitor Na postoperatively
