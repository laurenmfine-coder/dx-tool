MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

Sickle cell disease (SCD) results from a point mutation in the β-globin gene (chromosome 11): glutamic acid → valine at position 6 (HbS). Under deoxygenation, HbS polymerizes → RBC sickling → vaso-occlusion + chronic hemolysis.

🔑 Key Concept: SCD is autosomal recessive. HbSS = sickle cell disease. HbAS = sickle cell trait (generally asymptomatic, protective against falciparum malaria). HbSC disease and HbS-β thalassemia are compound heterozygous forms with variable severity.

Two Core Pathologies

Vaso-OcclusionChronic Hemolysis
Sickled cells obstruct microvasculature → ischemia/infarction → PAIN crises, acute chest syndrome, stroke, avascular necrosis, priapism, splenic infarctionChronic RBC destruction → anemia (Hb 6-9), elevated reticulocytes, elevated LDH, elevated indirect bilirubin, low haptoglobin → gallstones (pigmented), aplastic crisis (parvovirus B19)
CLINICAL
Acute Complications

Vaso-Occlusive Crisis (VOC)

Most common reason for ED visits. Triggered by dehydration, infection, cold, stress. Management: aggressive IV fluids + IV opioids (PCA) + NSAIDs + incentive spirometry. Do NOT undertreat pain.

Acute Chest Syndrome (ACS)

⚠️ Leading cause of death in SCD. New pulmonary infiltrate + respiratory symptoms (fever, cough, chest pain, hypoxia). Causes: fat embolism from bone marrow infarction, infection, in situ sickling. Treatment: antibiotics (ceftriaxone + azithromycin), supplemental O2, simple/exchange transfusion if severe, incentive spirometry (prevention key!).

Stroke

11% of SCD patients have stroke by age 20. Transcranial Doppler (TCD) screening annually ages 2-16 — elevated velocity (>200 cm/s) → chronic transfusion program reduces risk by 92%. Acute stroke → exchange transfusion to HbS <30%.

Splenic Sequestration

Acute trapping of blood in spleen → rapidly enlarging spleen + acute severe anemia (Hb drops ≥2 from baseline) + hypovolemic shock. Emergency: volume resuscitation + transfusion. Most common ages 6mo-5yr (before autosplenectomy).

Aplastic Crisis

Parvovirus B19 infects erythroid precursors → transient red cell aplasia → sudden severe anemia with LOW reticulocyte count (unlike other crises). Self-limited (7-10 days), may need transfusion.

TREATMENT
Chronic Management

Disease-Modifying Therapies

TherapyMechanismKey Points
HydroxyureaIncreases HbF production → prevents HbS polymerizationFirst-line for all SCD patients ≥9 months. Reduces VOC, ACS, transfusion needs, mortality by 40%
L-glutamine (Endari)Reduces oxidative stress in sickled RBCsFDA-approved add-on to hydroxyurea
Voxelotor (Oxbryta)Increases Hb-O2 affinity → prevents polymerizationImproves hemoglobin but withdrew from market 2023
Crizanlizumab (Adakveo)Anti-P-selectin antibody → reduces vaso-occlusionReduces VOC frequency. Also had regulatory concerns
Gene therapyExagamglogene (Casgevy) = CRISPR; Lovotibeglogene (Lyfgenia) = lentiviralFDA-approved 2023. Potentially curative. One-time treatment

Preventive Care

🔑 Critical preventives: Penicillin prophylaxis (birth to age 5 — functional asplenia makes encapsulated organisms lethal), pneumococcal + meningococcal vaccines, folate supplementation, TCD screening (ages 2-16), annual ophthalmology (proliferative retinopathy), iron chelation if chronically transfused.

Functional asplenia (autosplenectomy by age 5 from repeated infarction) → susceptible to encapsulated organisms: S. pneumoniae (#1 cause of fatal infection), H. influenzae, N. meningitidis, Salmonella (most common cause of osteomyelitis in SCD — NOT Staph).
