Clinical Decision Point
Hyperkalemia emergency: CKD patient with missed dialysis, on K+-sparing drugs (spironolactone + ACEi), peaked T waves on EKG, and bradycardia. This can progress to fatal arrhythmia within minutes.
DECISION 1
Immediate Treatment
✅
Correct!
Correct! Calcium gluconate 1-2g IV over 2-3 minutes is ALWAYS first in hyperkalemia with EKG changes. It stabilizes the cardiac membrane within 1-3 minutes but does NOT lower potassium. Think of it as buying time. Then proceed to: (1) shift K+ intracellularly (insulin + glucose, albuterol), (2) remove K+ from body (dialysis, kayexalate/patiromer). Continuous cardiac monitoring is mandatory.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Insulin + glucose is critical (shifts K+ 0.5-1.2 mEq/L in 15-30 min), but calcium gluconate must come first when EKG changes are present. Without membrane stabilization, the myocardium is vulnerable to lethal arrhythmias during the 15-30 minutes it takes for insulin to shift potassium. Always: stabilize → shift → remove.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Kayexalate takes 1-4 hours to work and is NOT appropriate as initial therapy in hyperkalemia with EKG changes. It's also poorly effective and carries risk of intestinal necrosis. This is a medical emergency requiring immediate cardiac stabilization (calcium) and rapid K+ shifting (insulin), followed by definitive removal (dialysis in this patient).

Correct! Layer all temporizing measures while arranging dialysis: (1) Regular insulin 10U IV + D50 50mL (shifts K+ ~1 mEq/L in 15-30 min — check glucose q1h to prevent hypoglycemia). (2) Nebulized albuterol 10-20mg (shifts K+ 0.5-1 mEq/L in 30 min). (3) Emergent hemodialysis is the only definitive treatment — removes K+ from the body. (4) Discontinue spironolactone and lisinopril permanently.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
IV bicarbonate is a weak potassium-shifting agent and takes hours to work. It is NOT effective as monotherapy for severe hyperkalemia. In CKD patients with volume overload, the sodium load can worsen fluid status. Insulin + albuterol are far more effective and rapid. Bicarbonate is only a minor adjunct, not a primary treatment.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Calcium can be repeated if EKG changes persist (effect lasts ~30-60 min), but it does NOT lower potassium. You must simultaneously shift K+ (insulin, albuterol) and arrange removal (dialysis). Calcium alone just delays the inevitable fatal arrhythmia without addressing the underlying hyperkalemia.

OUTCOME
🏆 Excellent Clinical Reasoning
Excellent hyperkalemia management! Discharge plan: (1) Permanently discontinue spironolactone (contraindicated in severe CKD). (2) Restart lisinopril at lower dose only if K+ stable and with close monitoring. (3) Low-potassium diet education (<2g/day). (4) Social work referral to address barriers to dialysis attendance. (5) Prescribe patiromer or sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (Lokelma) as outpatient K+ binder. (6) Ensure reliable dialysis transportation.
