Heart Failure

Understanding HFrEF vs HFpEF — from neurohormonal activation to guideline-directed medical therapy and the mechanisms behind each drug class.

📚 8 Concepts ❓ 8 MCQs ⏱ ~45 min

🫀 Defining Heart Failure

What Is Heart Failure?

Heart failure is a clinical syndrome where the heart cannot pump enough blood to meet metabolic demands, or can only do so at elevated filling pressures:

  • Forward failure — Inadequate cardiac output → fatigue, weakness, hypoperfusion
  • Backward failure — Elevated filling pressures → congestion (pulmonary edema, peripheral edema)
  • Low output vs high output — Most HF is low output; high output occurs in severe anemia, thyrotoxicosis, AV fistula

Classification by Ejection Fraction

TypeEFMechanismKey Features
HFrEF ≤40% Systolic dysfunction Dilated LV, weak contraction, responds well to GDMT
HFmrEF 41-49% Intermediate May behave like HFrEF; emerging evidence for GDMT benefit
HFpEF ≥50% Diastolic dysfunction Stiff LV, impaired relaxation, fewer proven therapies

💡 Clinical Pearl

EF alone doesn't determine symptoms. A patient with EF 25% may be well-compensated, while someone with EF 55% and severe diastolic dysfunction can be highly symptomatic. Always correlate EF with clinical status.

🔄 Neurohormonal Activation

The Compensatory Response Gone Wrong

When cardiac output falls, the body activates compensatory mechanisms that are initially helpful but become maladaptive over time:

🔄 The Vicious Cycle

1
↓ Cardiac output → Baroreceptors sense reduced pressure
2
↑ Sympathetic activation → Tachycardia, vasoconstriction, increased contractility
3
↑ RAAS activation → Na/H2O retention, vasoconstriction, aldosterone
4
Cardiac remodeling → Hypertrophy, fibrosis, dilation, worsening function
5
Further ↓ cardiac output → Cycle repeats and worsens

Key Neurohormonal Systems

SystemShort-term EffectLong-term Harm
Sympathetic ↑ HR, contractility, BP Myocyte toxicity, arrhythmias, remodeling
RAAS Maintains BP and perfusion Fibrosis, hypertrophy, sodium retention
ADH Water retention Hyponatremia, congestion
Natriuretic peptides Vasodilation, natriuresis (counterregulatory) Overwhelmed by RAAS; used as biomarker

💡 Clinical Pearl

GDMT works by interrupting this neurohormonal cycle. Beta-blockers block sympathetic activation. ACEi/ARBs/ARNIs block RAAS. MRAs block aldosterone. Each targets a different piece of the maladaptive response.

💊 Guideline-Directed Medical Therapy

The Four Pillars of HFrEF Treatment

All patients with HFrEF (EF ≤40%) should be on all four drug classes unless contraindicated:

Drug ClassMechanismMortality Benefit
ACEi/ARB/ARNI Block RAAS; ARNI also inhibits neprilysin ~20-25% reduction
Beta-blocker Block sympathetic activation ~30-35% reduction
MRA Block aldosterone (anti-fibrotic) ~30% reduction
SGLT2i Multiple mechanisms (natriuresis, metabolic) ~20% reduction

Key Medication Notes

  • ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan) — Preferred over ACEi/ARB; 36-hour washout from ACEi required
  • Beta-blockers — Carvedilol, metoprolol succinate, or bisoprolol only; titrate slowly
  • MRA — Spironolactone or eplerenone; watch K+ and creatinine
  • SGLT2i — Dapagliflozin or empagliflozin; benefit regardless of diabetes status

⚠️ Beta-Blocker Timing

Never start or uptitrate beta-blockers during acute decompensation. Wait until the patient is euvolemic and stable. Starting during congestion can worsen symptoms.

💡 Clinical Pearl

Modern HF management emphasizes rapid initiation of all four pillars at low doses, then uptitrating, rather than sequential introduction. Getting all four on board quickly provides additive benefit.

💧 Diuretics and Volume Management

Loop Diuretics: The Workhorses

Loop diuretics relieve congestion but don't improve mortality:

  • Mechanism — Block Na-K-2Cl cotransporter in thick ascending limb
  • Effect — Massive natriuresis and diuresis; also lose K+, Mg2+, Ca2+
  • Dosing — IV furosemide 2.5x more potent than oral; need adequate dose to reach threshold
  • Ceiling effect — Increasing dose beyond ceiling doesn't increase diuresis

Diuretic Resistance

When loop diuretics stop working, consider:

  • Inadequate dosing — May need higher doses with renal impairment
  • Poor absorption — Gut edema reduces oral bioavailability; switch to IV
  • Braking phenomenon — Post-diuretic sodium reabsorption; try continuous infusion
  • Nephron remodeling — Distal tubule hypertrophy; add thiazide for sequential nephron blockade

💡 Clinical Pearl

"Sequential nephron blockade" means adding a thiazide (like metolazone) to a loop diuretic. The thiazide blocks compensatory sodium reabsorption in the distal tubule, dramatically enhancing diuresis. Watch closely for electrolyte derangements.

📊 HFpEF: A Different Disease

Understanding Diastolic Dysfunction

In HFpEF, the LV contracts normally but cannot relax and fill properly:

  • Stiff ventricle — Often from hypertension, aging, diabetes, obesity
  • Impaired relaxation — Active process requiring ATP; fails early in disease
  • Elevated filling pressures — Needed to fill the stiff ventricle → pulmonary congestion
  • Exercise intolerance — Filling pressures rise dramatically with exercise

HFpEF Treatment: What Works?

InterventionEvidence
SGLT2 inhibitors✓ Proven benefit (EMPEROR-Preserved, DELIVER)
Diuretics✓ Symptom relief for congestion
Treat comorbidities✓ HTN, AFib, CAD, obesity, diabetes
ACEi/ARB? Modest or no benefit in trials
Beta-blockers? No proven mortality benefit in HFpEF
MRA? May reduce hospitalizations

💡 Clinical Pearl

SGLT2 inhibitors are now the only drug class with clear mortality/hospitalization benefit in HFpEF. Emphasize treating underlying conditions (hypertension, diabetes, obesity, atrial fibrillation) which drive the syndrome.

🚨 Acute Decompensated Heart Failure

The Four Hemodynamic Profiles

Classify patients by congestion (wet/dry) and perfusion (warm/cold):

Warm (adequate perfusion)Cold (low perfusion)
Dry (no congestion) Warm & Dry — Compensated; optimize GDMT Cold & Dry — Needs volume, then reassess
Wet (congested) Warm & Wet — Most common; diuretics Cold & Wet — Worst prognosis; inotropes + diuretics

Signs of Congestion vs Low Perfusion

  • Congestion (wet) — JVD, edema, rales, orthopnea, weight gain, hepatomegaly
  • Low perfusion (cold) — Cool extremities, narrow pulse pressure, altered mental status, declining renal function, low urine output

⚠️ Cardiogenic Shock

Cold & wet patients with persistent hypotension despite filling pressures may need inotropes (dobutamine, milrinone) or mechanical support (IABP, Impella). Early cardiology/critical care consultation is essential.

⚡ Device Therapy

ICD for Primary Prevention

Implantable cardioverter-defibrillator prevents sudden cardiac death:

  • Indication — EF ≤35% despite ≥3 months of optimal GDMT
  • Mechanism — Detects and terminates ventricular arrhythmias
  • Life expectancy — Expected survival >1 year required for benefit

CRT for Dyssynchrony

Cardiac resynchronization therapy improves coordination of contraction:

  • Indication — EF ≤35% + LBBB with QRS ≥150ms + NYHA II-IV symptoms
  • Mechanism — Biventricular pacing resynchronizes LV contraction
  • Benefit — Improves EF, symptoms, and survival in appropriate patients
  • CRT-D — Combines CRT with ICD function

💡 Clinical Pearl

CRT works best with true LBBB and QRS ≥150ms. The benefit is less clear with non-LBBB patterns or narrower QRS. Always optimize GDMT first — some patients improve EF enough to no longer qualify for devices.
