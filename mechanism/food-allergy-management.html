🍽️ Food Allergy Management

Avoidance strategies, action plans, and emerging therapies for food-allergic patients

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe the three pillars of food allergy management
  • Counsel patients on effective allergen avoidance including label reading
  • Create and explain components of an anaphylaxis action plan
  • Identify candidates for oral immunotherapy (OIT)
  • Discuss natural history and potential for outgrowing food allergies

🏛️ Three Pillars of Food Allergy Management

🚫
Strict Avoidance
Eliminate exposure to confirmed allergens through careful label reading and cross-contact prevention
💉
Emergency Preparedness
Carry epinephrine, have written action plan, train caregivers
📚
Education
Symptom recognition, when to use epinephrine, restaurant communication
💡 The Goal

Help patients live safely with food allergy while maintaining quality of life. Overly restrictive diets cause nutritional deficiencies and psychosocial burden. Confirm allergies with proper testing before recommending avoidance.

🚫 Allergen Avoidance

Label Reading: FALCPA Requirements

The Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act (FALCPA) requires clear labeling of the Top 9 allergens in plain language:

⚠️ Precautionary Advisory Labels (PAL)
"May contain..." Voluntary - NOT regulated by FDA. Risk varies by manufacturer.
"Made in facility that processes..." Cross-contact possible. Generally advise avoidance for high-risk patients.
"Made on shared equipment..." Higher risk than "facility" warnings. Avoid if severely allergic.
No warning label Not guaranteed safe - cross-contact may still occur. Read full ingredients.
💎 The "Read Every Time" Rule

Manufacturers change recipes without warning. Patients should read labels EVERY time they purchase a product, even if they've bought it safely before. Ingredients can change between production runs.

Cross-Contact Prevention

Cross-contact occurs when a safe food touches an allergen or surface contaminated with allergen. This is different from cross-contamination (bacteria).

  • At home: Separate cutting boards, utensils, cooking surfaces; wash hands thoroughly; consider allergen-free zones
  • At restaurants: Communicate clearly with staff; ask about shared fryers, grills, prep surfaces; request fresh pan/utensils
  • At school: Allergen-free table option; no food sharing; trained staff with access to epinephrine
  • Travel: Carry safe snacks; chef cards in local language; know how to access medical care

📋 Anaphylaxis Action Plan

Every patient with food allergy should have a written emergency action plan that specifies when and how to treat reactions.

🚨 Sample Anaphylaxis Action Plan Components
MILD SYMPTOMS (Skin only - no breathing/BP issues)
  • Localized hives, itching, flushing
  • Give antihistamine (diphenhydramine or cetirizine)
  • WATCH CLOSELY - may progress to anaphylaxis
  • If symptoms worsen → GIVE EPINEPHRINE
SEVERE SYMPTOMS → GIVE EPINEPHRINE IMMEDIATELY
  • Widespread hives, severe itching/flushing
  • Throat tightness, trouble swallowing, voice changes
  • Shortness of breath, wheezing, repetitive cough
  • Dizziness, fainting, pale/blue color
  • Vomiting, severe abdominal pain, diarrhea
  • Sense of doom, confusion
AFTER GIVING EPINEPHRINE
  • CALL 911 immediately - even if symptoms improve
  • Lay person flat (or sitting if breathing difficulty)
  • Give second epinephrine in 5-15 min if no improvement
  • Go to ER for observation (biphasic reaction risk)
  • Bring used autoinjector to show medical team
⚠️ "When in Doubt, Give Epinephrine"

Antihistamines cannot treat or prevent anaphylaxis. If a patient has ingested their allergen and develops ANY symptoms beyond isolated skin findings, epinephrine is indicated. Early administration improves outcomes. There are no absolute contraindications.

🎯 Oral Immunotherapy (OIT)

OIT involves giving gradually increasing doses of allergen to raise the reaction threshold. FDA-approved for peanut (Palforzia®) in ages 4-17.

OIT Candidacy Assessment

✓ Good Candidates

  • Age 4-17 (FDA indication)
  • Confirmed IgE-mediated peanut allergy
  • Motivated family with good adherence
  • Able to attend frequent clinic visits
  • Controlled asthma
  • Understand: desensitization, NOT cure

✗ Poor Candidates / Cautions

  • Uncontrolled asthma
  • Eosinophilic esophagitis (relative)
  • Unable to avoid cofactors during dosing
  • Poor adherence likelihood
  • Severe baseline reactions (relative)
  • Expecting "cure" - must continue maintenance

What Patients Need to Know About OIT

  • Goal: Raise threshold to protect against accidental exposures - NOT enable free eating
  • Timeline: ~6 months of updosing, then maintenance indefinitely
  • Reactions: Common during OIT - GI symptoms, oral itching; anaphylaxis possible
  • Cofactors: Must avoid exercise, hot showers, NSAIDs, alcohol around dosing
  • Maintenance: Must take dose daily FOREVER to maintain protection
  • Still carry epinephrine: OIT does not eliminate need for emergency preparedness
💎 Sustained Unresponsiveness vs Desensitization

Desensitization: Protection while actively taking maintenance doses. Lost within weeks if dosing stops.
Sustained unresponsiveness: Protection persists even after stopping OIT. Rare - only ~20-30% achieve this. Most patients need lifelong maintenance.

📈 Natural History: Will They Outgrow It?

AllergenLikelihood of ResolutionTiming
Milk~80% outgrowMost by age 6; some by teens
Egg~70% outgrowMost by age 6; some by teens
Wheat~65% outgrowUsually by school age
Soy~70% outgrowUsually by school age
Peanut~20% outgrowIf it happens, usually by age 6-8
Tree nuts~10% outgrowUsually persistent
FishRare to outgrowUsually lifelong
ShellfishRare to outgrowUsually lifelong; often adult-onset
📊 Predictors of Persistence

Higher specific IgE levels, larger skin test wheals, multiple food allergies, comorbid atopic disease (asthma, eczema), and history of severe reactions all predict LESS likelihood of outgrowing the allergy. Serial monitoring with declining IgE may indicate developing tolerance.
