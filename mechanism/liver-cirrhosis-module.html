Liver Disease & Cirrhosis

From portal hypertension to hepatorenal syndrome: understand the pathophysiology of decompensated cirrhosis and its life-threatening complications.

📚 16 concepts ❓ 12 MCQs ⏱️ ~40 min 🎯 GI/Hepatology

1 🔬 Cirrhosis: Definition & Staging
📋 What Is Cirrhosis?

Cirrhosis is the end-stage of chronic liver injury characterized by:

  • Fibrosis: Replacement of normal hepatocytes with scar tissue
  • Nodular regeneration: Abnormal liver architecture with regenerative nodules
  • Vascular distortion: Disrupted blood flow leading to portal hypertension

Common causes: Alcohol (60-70%), NAFLD/NASH (growing), Hepatitis C, Hepatitis B, autoimmune hepatitis, hemochromatosis, Wilson's disease, primary biliary cholangitis.

📊 Compensated vs Decompensated
  • Compensated: Cirrhosis without complications. Liver function adequate despite fibrosis. May be asymptomatic.
  • Decompensated: Development of complications: ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, or jaundice. Once decompensated, median survival drops dramatically.

2 🩸 Portal Hypertension
🔄 Portal Hypertension Pathophysiology

Portal hypertension (>10 mmHg, clinically significant >12 mmHg) drives most complications:

  • Increased resistance: Fibrosis distorts sinusoidal architecture
  • Increased flow: Splanchnic vasodilation → increased portal inflow
  • Collateral formation: Blood finds alternate routes → varices
💔 The Hyperdynamic Circulation
  • Splanchnic vasodilation: NO → pooling in splanchnic bed
  • Decreased effective arterial volume: Arterial system "underfilled"
  • Compensatory activation: RAAS + SNS + ADH → sodium/water retention
  • High cardiac output: High CO, low SVR, but inadequate organ perfusion
⚠️ Varices & Bleeding Risk
Esophageal varices develop when portal pressure >10 mmHg. All cirrhotics need EGD screening. Primary prophylaxis: non-selective beta-blockers or band ligation for high-risk varices.
3 💧 Ascites
SAAG Calculation
SAAG = Serum Albumin − Ascites Albumin
≥1.1 = portal hypertension (97% accuracy). "High SAAG = high pressure"

5 💔 Hepatorenal Syndrome
🎯 HRS Pathophysiology

Hepatorenal syndrome is functional renal failure in advanced cirrhosis:

  • Severe splanchnic vasodilation → profound arterial underfilling
  • Maximal RAAS/SNS activation → intense renal vasoconstriction
  • Kidneys are structurally normal—they "think" they're protecting against shock

💡 HRS vs ATN
HRS: bland urine, very low urine sodium (<10), FENa <1%. ATN: muddy brown casts, FENa >2%.
