📚 GU Development Overview

The genitourinary system develops from intermediate mesoderm and includes:
  • Urinary system: Kidneys, ureters, bladder, urethra
  • Genital system: Gonads, internal ducts, external genitalia
These systems are intimately connected embryologically, explaining why congenital anomalies often affect both.

Key Structures & Origins

🫘 Kidney Development

Humans develop THREE successive kidney systems, each more caudal than the last. Only the third (metanephros) becomes the permanent kidney.

1. PRONEPHROS (Weeks 3-4)

  • Most cranial, in cervical region
  • Non-functional, rudimentary
  • Regresses completely by week 4
  • Clinical: No known anomalies

2. MESONEPHROS (Weeks 4-8)

  • Thoracolumbar region
  • Transiently functional (produces urine)
  • Mesonephric (Wolffian) duct → critical for male development
  • Regresses as kidney, but duct persists in males
Wolffian Duct Derivatives (Males): Epididymis, Vas deferens, Seminal vesicles, Ejaculatory ducts

3. METANEPHROS (Week 5 onwards) = PERMANENT KIDNEY

  • Develops from TWO sources that must interact:
ComponentOriginBecomes
Ureteric BudOutgrowth of Wolffian ductUreter, pelvis, calyces, collecting ducts
Metanephric MesenchymeIntermediate mesodermNephrons (glomerulus → DCT)

Reciprocal Induction

The ureteric bud and metanephric mesenchyme MUST signal to each other:

  • GDNF (from mesenchyme) → RET receptor (on ureteric bud) → bud branching
  • Ureteric bud signals → mesenchyme differentiates into nephrons

Failure of induction = Renal agenesis or dysplasia

Kidney Ascent

Week 6-9: Kidneys "ascend" from pelvis to lumbar region (Actually: differential growth, kidneys stay put, body grows caudally) During ascent: • Kidneys rotate 90° (hilum moves from anterior → medial) • Blood supply changes: Pelvic → Common iliac → Aorta CLINICAL CORRELATIONS: • Pelvic kidney = failed ascent • Horseshoe kidney = fused kidneys trapped by IMA • Accessory renal arteries = persistent embryonic vessels

🔵 Bladder & Urethra Development

The bladder and urethra develop from the urogenital sinus, which forms when the cloaca is divided by the urorectal septum.

Cloacal Division (Weeks 4-7)

Trigone Formation

The trigone (between ureteral orifices and internal urethral orifice) has a unique origin: it's derived from the absorbed mesonephric ducts, not the urogenital sinus. This explains its different histology and smooth muscle arrangement.

⚥ Gonad Development

Gonads develop from the genital ridge (intermediate mesoderm) and are initially bipotential - they can become either testis or ovary depending on genetic signals.

Indifferent Gonad (Weeks 5-7)

  • Genital ridge forms on medial mesonephros
  • Primordial germ cells migrate from yolk sac wall (week 4-6)
  • Germ cells + coelomic epithelium + mesenchyme = indifferent gonad
  • At this stage, male and female gonads are identical

Sex Determination

GENETIC SEX (46,XX or 46,XY) ↓ SRY gene (Y chromosome) ↓ ┌────────────────────────────────────┐ │ SRY PRESENT (46,XY) │ SRY ABSENT (46,XX) │ ↓ │ ↓ │ SRY → SOX9 activation │ WNT4/RSPO1 pathway │ ↓ │ ↓ │ TESTIS DIFFERENTIATION │ OVARY DIFFERENTIATION │ (Week 7) │ (Week 10-12) └────────────────────────────────────┘

Testis Development

Male Pathway (SRY present)

  • Week 7: SRY → SOX9 → Sertoli cells differentiate
  • Sertoli cells organize into testis cords (future seminiferous tubules)
  • Sertoli cells produce AMH (Anti-Müllerian Hormone)
  • Week 8: Leydig cells differentiate → produce testosterone
Two Key Hormones:
  • AMH: Causes Müllerian duct regression
  • Testosterone: Stabilizes Wolffian duct → male internal structures

Ovary Development

Female Pathway (SRY absent)

  • Without SRY, WNT4/RSPO1 pathway dominates
  • Ovary differentiation is LATER (weeks 10-12)
  • No AMH → Müllerian ducts persist
  • No testosterone → Wolffian ducts regress
  • Germ cells enter meiosis → primary oocytes (arrested in prophase I)
🧠 "Ovaries are the DEFAULT pathway"
Without SRY and testosterone, female development occurs.
Male development requires ACTIVE hormonal signaling.

🔀 Genital Duct Development

Both male (Wolffian) and female (Müllerian) duct systems are present in the early embryo. Hormones determine which persists and which regresses.

The Two Duct Systems

FeatureWolffian (Mesonephric)Müllerian (Paramesonephric)
Present inBoth sexes initiallyBoth sexes initially
Persists inMalesFemales
Signal to persistTestosteroneAbsence of AMH
Regresses due toLack of testosteroneAMH from Sertoli cells

Male Duct Development

Wolffian Duct Derivatives (Testosterone-dependent)

🧠 "SEED"
Seminal vesicles, Epididymis, Ejaculatory ducts, Ductus (vas) deferens
  • Testosterone from Leydig cells stabilizes Wolffian duct
  • AMH from Sertoli cells causes Müllerian duct regression

Female Duct Development

Müllerian Duct Derivatives

  • Fallopian tubes (unfused cranial portions)
  • Uterus (fused caudal portions)
  • Upper vagina (fused with urogenital sinus)

Without AMH, Müllerian ducts persist. Without testosterone, Wolffian ducts regress.

Complete Androgen Insensitivity (CAIS): 46,XY with non-functional androgen receptor.
  • Testes present (produce AMH → no Müllerian structures)
  • Testosterone present but can't act → Wolffian ducts regress
  • External genitalia = female (testosterone can't masculinize)
  • Result: Phenotypic female with testes, no uterus

🔄 External Genitalia Development

External genitalia develop from three structures present in both sexes:
  • Genital tubercle
  • Urogenital (urethral) folds
  • Labioscrotal swellings
These are indifferent until week 8; differentiation requires DHT in males.

Homologous Structures

Indifferent StructureMale (DHT present)Female (No DHT)
Genital tubercleGlans penisClitoris
Urogenital foldsPenile urethra (ventral shaft)Labia minora
Labioscrotal swellingsScrotumLabia majora
Urogenital sinusProstate, bulbourethral glandsBartholin's, Skene's glands

Male External Genitalia (DHT-dependent)

Testosterone (from Leydig cells) ↓ 5α-reductase (in genital skin) ↓ DHT (dihydrotestosterone) ↓ • Genital tubercle → elongates → penis • Urogenital folds → fuse ventrally → penile urethra • Labioscrotal swellings → fuse → scrotum Urethral fusion proceeds proximal → distal (Failure at different points → different hypospadias types)

Hypospadias occurs in 1:200-300 males - one of the most common congenital anomalies! Severity depends on where fusion stopped:
  • Glanular (most common, mildest)
  • Penile shaft
  • Penoscrotal
  • Perineal (most severe)

⬇️ Testicular Descent

Testes develop in the abdomen and must descend to the scrotum. This occurs in two phases controlled by different factors.

Two Phases of Descent

Phase 1: Transabdominal (Weeks 10-15)

  • Testes move from posterior abdomen to internal inguinal ring
  • Controlled by INSL3 (Insulin-like 3) from Leydig cells
  • Gubernaculum swells and anchors testis near inguinal region
  • MIS may play permissive role

Phase 2: Inguinoscrotal (Weeks 25-35)

  • Testes pass through inguinal canal to scrotum
  • Controlled by androgens (testosterone)
  • Gubernaculum guides descent
  • Processus vaginalis (peritoneal outpouching) precedes testis
  • Usually complete by birth; may continue until 3-6 months postnatal

Processus Vaginalis

An outpouching of peritoneum that accompanies the testis through the inguinal canal. Normally obliterates after descent.

If persists...Result
Completely patentIndirect inguinal hernia
Partially patentCommunicating hydrocele
Isolated fluid collectionHydrocele of cord or testis

Cryptorchidism Consequences:
  • Increased risk of testicular cancer (5-10x) even after orchiopexy
  • Infertility risk - higher temperature impairs spermatogenesis
  • Testicular torsion risk increased
  • Associated with other GU anomalies (check for hypospadias, hernias)

INSL3 = Intra-abdominal phase
Testosterone = Through the canal (inguinoscrotal)

VACTERL Association: Vertebral, Anal atresia, Cardiac, Tracheo-Esophageal fistula, Renal, Limb

Always check for associated anomalies when one GU anomaly is found!

Key Principles:
  • GU and GI systems develop together (cloaca) - anomalies often coexist
  • One renal anomaly → screen for others (VCUG, echo, spine imaging)
  • Hypospadias + cryptorchidism → evaluate for DSD
  • Prenatal hydronephrosis → postnatal workup required

🧠 Self-Assessment

Question 1

A male infant is born with bilateral undescended testes and a uterus is found on imaging. What hormone deficiency most likely explains this?

A. Testosterone
B. Anti-Müllerian Hormone (AMH)
C. Dihydrotestosterone (DHT)
D. INSL3
Correct: B - AMH
Persistent Müllerian Duct Syndrome occurs when AMH is deficient or its receptor is non-functional. Without AMH, the Müllerian ducts persist (uterus, fallopian tubes) even in a 46,XY male. Testosterone is present (so male external genitalia), but the Müllerian structures remain.

Question 2

Which structure gives rise to the penile urethra?

A. Genital tubercle
B. Urogenital folds
C. Labioscrotal swellings
D. Wolffian duct
Correct: B - Urogenital folds
The urogenital (urethral) folds fuse in the midline ventrally to form the penile urethra. Incomplete fusion results in hypospadias. The genital tubercle becomes the glans penis; the labioscrotal swellings become the scrotum.

Question 3

A horseshoe kidney is "trapped" during ascent by which structure?

A. Aorta
B. Inferior mesenteric artery (IMA)
C. Iliac vessels
D. Ureters
Correct: B - IMA
When the lower poles of the kidneys fuse (horseshoe kidney), the fused portion cannot ascend past the inferior mesenteric artery, which acts as a barrier. The kidney remains low (L3-L5 instead of T12-L3).
