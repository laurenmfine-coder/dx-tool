🩸 GI Bleeding

Upper vs Lower GI Bleeding

Upper GIB (Above Ligament of Treitz)

Presentation
• Hematemesis (bloody or coffee-ground vomit)
• Melena (black, tarry, foul-smelling stool)
• Can have hematochezia if massive
Common Causes
• Peptic ulcer disease (most common)
• Esophageal/gastric varices
• Mallory-Weiss tear
• Erosive gastritis/esophagitis
• Malignancy
• Dieulafoy lesion, AVM

Lower GIB (Below Ligament of Treitz)

Presentation
• Hematochezia (bright red blood per rectum)
• Maroon stools
• May have melena if slow/right-sided
Common Causes
• Diverticulosis (most common)
• Angiodysplasia/AVM
• Colorectal cancer/polyps
• Hemorrhoids
• IBD (UC > Crohn's)
• Ischemic colitis
• Infectious colitis
Pearl: BUN:Cr ratio >30 suggests upper GIB (blood is digested protein → ↑BUN)

🚨 Initial Management (Same for All GIB)

🔌
2 Large-bore IVs
18G or larger
💉
Labs
CBC, BMP, Coags, T&S
💧
Resuscitate
Crystalloid, blood if needed
🩸
Transfuse
Goal Hgb >7 (>8 if CAD)
Hold/Reverse
• Hold anticoagulants (reverse if life-threatening)
• Hold antiplatelets if possible
• Correct coagulopathy (FFP, vitamin K, platelets)
ICU Criteria
• Hemodynamic instability
• Active bleeding with dropping Hgb
• Need for >2 units pRBC
• Significant comorbidities

Upper GIB: Detailed Management

Glasgow-Blatchford Score (Pre-endoscopy)

BUN, Hemoglobin, SBP, HR, Melena, Syncope, Liver disease, Heart failure
Score 0-1: May not need admission/endoscopy

Rockall Score (Post-endoscopy)

Age, Shock, Comorbidities, Diagnosis, Stigmata of recent hemorrhage
Predicts rebleeding and mortality risk

Non-Variceal Upper GIB (PUD)

Medical Management:
  • • IV PPI (high-dose: 80mg bolus → 8mg/hr infusion × 72h)
  • • NPO until endoscopy
  • • NGT controversial (not required)
Endoscopy:
  • • Within 24 hours (12h if high-risk)
  • • Epinephrine injection + thermal/clips
  • • Forrest classification for rebleed risk
Forrest Classification: Ia (spurting) & Ib (oozing) = high risk. IIa (visible vessel) = treat. IIb (clot) = controversial. IIc/III = low risk.

⚠️ Variceal Upper GIB

Immediate Management:
  • • Octreotide (50mcg bolus → 50mcg/hr) - splanchnic vasoconstriction
  • • Antibiotics (ceftriaxone 1g daily × 7 days) - prevents SBP, ↓mortality
  • • Restrictive transfusion (Hgb >7)
  • • Correct INR >1.5, platelets <50K
Endoscopy (within 12h):
  • • Band ligation (preferred for esophageal)
  • • Sclerotherapy if banding not possible
  • • Cyanoacrylate glue for gastric varices
Rescue Therapies (if endoscopy fails):
• Balloon tamponade (Blakemore tube) - bridge to TIPS
• TIPS (Transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt)
• Surgery (last resort)

Lower GIB: Detailed Management

Key Differences from UGIB

  • • Usually self-limited (80% stop spontaneously)
  • • Less often hemodynamically significant
  • • Colonoscopy is diagnostic procedure of choice
  • • May need CT angiography if rapid bleeding

Diagnostic Approach

  • • Rule out upper source first (NGT or EGD if concern)
  • • Colonoscopy within 24h (after prep)
  • • CT angiography if too unstable for colonoscopy
  • • Tagged RBC scan (slower bleeds, localizes)
  • • Angiography (both diagnostic and therapeutic)
Diverticular Bleeding
Most common cause. Usually painless, massive. Right-sided diverticula bleed more. 80% stop spontaneously.
Angiodysplasia
Elderly, CKD, aortic stenosis. Cecum/ascending colon. Recurrent bleeds common.
Ischemic Colitis
Elderly, post-aortic surgery. Crampy pain → bloody diarrhea. Watershed areas (splenic flexure).

Obscure GI Bleeding

Definition: Recurrent bleeding with negative EGD and colonoscopy
Think Small Bowel:
  • • Angiodysplasia (most common)
  • • Small bowel tumors
  • • Crohn's disease
  • • Meckel's diverticulum
  • • NSAID enteropathy
Diagnostic Options:
  • • Capsule endoscopy (first-line)
  • • Push enteroscopy
  • • Balloon-assisted enteroscopy
  • • CT/MR enterography
  • • Meckel's scan (if young)
