🔬 Allergy Testing & Interpretation

From skin prick to component testing - understanding sensitization vs clinical allergy

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Compare skin prick testing (SPT) vs serum specific IgE (sIgE) methodology and interpretation
  • Explain the difference between sensitization and clinical allergy
  • Apply component-resolved diagnostics to stratify risk in food allergy
  • Interpret tryptase levels in acute vs baseline settings
  • Select appropriate testing based on clinical scenario

⚖️ SPT vs Serum Specific IgE

Both methods detect allergen-specific IgE, but through different mechanisms. Neither test alone diagnoses clinical allergy - they demonstrate sensitization. Clinical correlation is essential.

💉 Skin Prick Testing (SPT)

  • Mechanism: Allergen introduced into epidermis → cross-links mast cell IgE → local wheal/flare
  • Read at: 15-20 minutes
  • Positive: Wheal ≥3mm larger than negative control
  • ✓ Higher sensitivity than sIgE
  • ✓ Immediate results
  • ✓ Lower cost per allergen
  • ✗ Requires trained personnel
  • ✗ Affected by antihistamines (hold 5-7 days)
  • ✗ Dermatographism causes false positives
  • ✗ Small systemic reaction risk

🩸 Serum Specific IgE (sIgE)

  • Mechanism: ELISA/FEIA detects circulating IgE antibodies to specific allergens
  • Read at: Days (lab turnaround)
  • Positive: ≥0.35 kU/L (Class 1+)
  • ✓ No risk of systemic reaction
  • ✓ Not affected by medications
  • ✓ Can test during reaction
  • ✓ Standardized/reproducible
  • ✗ Lower sensitivity than SPT
  • ✗ Higher cost per allergen
  • ✗ Results delayed
⚠️ Sensitization ≠ Clinical Allergy

A positive SPT or sIgE means the patient has IgE antibodies to that allergen (sensitized), but 50%+ of sensitized individuals tolerate the food without symptoms. Never diagnose food allergy by testing alone - correlate with clinical history. Unnecessary avoidance leads to nutritional deficiencies and reduced quality of life.

When to Choose Which Test

Clinical Scenario Preferred Test Rationale
Routine food allergy workup SPT (or sIgE if SPT unavailable) Higher sensitivity, immediate results
Patient on antihistamines sIgE Not affected by medications
Severe dermatographism sIgE Avoid false positive wheals
Severe eczema (no clear skin) sIgE Need intact skin for SPT
History of severe anaphylaxis sIgE first (safer) Minimize systemic reaction risk
Risk stratification in peanut allergy Component testing (Ara h 2) Predicts clinical reactivity better than whole peanut

🧬 Component-Resolved Diagnostics (CRD)

CRD tests for IgE to specific allergenic proteins rather than whole allergen extracts. This improves diagnostic accuracy by distinguishing clinically relevant sensitization from cross-reactive IgE.

Peanut Components

Ara h 2
2S Albumin (Storage Protein)
HIGH RISK
Clinical significance: Best predictor of clinical peanut allergy. Heat and digestion stable. Ara h 2 ≥0.35 kU/L has >90% specificity for clinical reactivity. Higher levels correlate with more severe reactions.
Ara h 1, Ara h 3
Storage Proteins (7S/11S Globulins)
HIGH RISK
Clinical significance: Also storage proteins associated with clinical reactivity, but less specific than Ara h 2. Often positive alongside Ara h 2 in truly peanut-allergic patients.
Ara h 8
PR-10 Protein (Pathogenesis-Related)
CROSS-REACTIVE
Clinical significance: Homolog of Bet v 1 (birch pollen). Heat/digestion labile. Patients with ONLY Ara h 8 positivity typically have pollen-food allergy syndrome (oral symptoms only) and often tolerate roasted peanuts. Low risk of systemic reaction.
Ara h 9
Lipid Transfer Protein (LTP)
VARIABLE RISK
Clinical significance: Heat stable, associated with systemic reactions in Mediterranean populations. Cross-reacts with LTPs in many plant foods (peach, apple). Important in Southern Europe, less common in Northern populations.
💎 The Ara h 2 Rule

When evaluating peanut allergy: Ara h 2 positive = true peanut allergy until proven otherwise. Ara h 8 positive only = likely pollen-related cross-reactivity with low systemic risk. This distinction guides whether strict avoidance vs supervised challenge is appropriate.

⚠️ Higher ≠ More Severe

Higher sIgE levels correlate with higher probability of clinical reactivity, NOT severity of reaction. A patient with sIgE of 50 kU/L is more likely to react than one with 2 kU/L, but may have milder symptoms. Severity depends on multiple factors including dose, cofactors, and individual response.

Predictive Decision Points

Studies have established sIgE cutoffs with >95% positive predictive value for clinical reactivity:

Allergen 95% PPV Cutoff Notes
Peanut ≥14 kU/L (or Ara h 2 ≥0.35) Ara h 2 more specific
Egg white ≥7 kU/L Consider ovomucoid for baked egg tolerance
Milk ≥15 kU/L Consider casein for baked milk tolerance
Tree nuts Varies by nut Component testing helpful
💎 When Results are Gray Zone

When sIgE is positive but below 95% PPV cutoff, and history is unclear, an oral food challenge is the gold standard. Supervised challenges can safely determine if the patient truly reacts, preventing unnecessary avoidance.
