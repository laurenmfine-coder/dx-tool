🌿 Allergen Immunotherapy

SCIT, SLIT, and desensitization protocols for allergic disease

📅 Last updated: January 7, 2026 ⏱️ ~45 minutes 📋 Practice Parameter-Based

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Explain the immunologic mechanisms of allergen immunotherapy
  • Identify appropriate candidates for SCIT and SLIT
  • Compare standard, cluster, and rush immunotherapy protocols
  • Recognize and manage local and systemic reactions
  • Understand the role of immunotherapy in specific conditions (allergic rhinitis, asthma, venom allergy)
  • Describe the aspirin desensitization protocol for AERD

1. Mechanism of Action

Allergen immunotherapy (AIT) modifies the immune response to specific allergens, inducing tolerance through multiple mechanisms:

Early Changes (Weeks to Months)

  • Mast cell/basophil desensitization: Reduced mediator release with allergen exposure
  • Decreased immediate-phase response: Less histamine, fewer symptoms

Intermediate Changes (Months)

  • T regulatory (Treg) cell induction: IL-10 and TGF-β production
  • Th2 → Th1 shift: Decreased IL-4, IL-5, IL-13; increased IFN-γ
  • Decreased allergen-specific IgE (long-term)

Late Changes (Years)

  • Blocking antibodies: IgG4 competes with IgE for allergen binding
  • Decreased tissue eosinophilia
  • Sustained tolerance: May persist years after stopping therapy
Key Concept: Unlike medications that suppress symptoms, immunotherapy modifies the underlying disease. Benefits can persist for years after a completed 3-5 year course—the only treatment that alters the natural history of allergic disease.

2. SCIT vs SLIT: Comparison

Feature SCIT (Subcutaneous) SLIT (Sublingual)
Administration Injection in medical office Tablet or drops under tongue at home
Frequency Weekly (buildup) → monthly (maintenance) Daily
Duration 3-5 years 3-5 years (pre-seasonal or perennial)
Allergens Available Any inhalant, venom (custom mixes) Limited: grass, ragweed, dust mite (FDA-approved tablets)
Efficacy Well-established; 30-40% symptom reduction Comparable for approved allergens
Systemic Reaction Risk Higher (0.1-0.2% per injection) Lower (rare anaphylaxis)
Local Reactions Injection site swelling Oral itching, throat irritation (common initially)
Office Visits Required for each injection + 30min observation First dose in office; then home
Ideal Candidate Multiple allergens, venom allergy, prefers less daily adherence Single/few allergens, needle-phobic, wants home administration

FDA-Approved SLIT Tablets

  • Grastek® (Timothy grass): Allergic rhinitis ± conjunctivitis, ages 5-65
  • Ragwitek® (Ragweed): Allergic rhinitis ± conjunctivitis, ages 18-65
  • Odactra® (House dust mite): Allergic rhinitis ± conjunctivitis, ages 18-65; also for dust mite-allergic asthma
Clinical Pearl: SLIT tablets should be started 12 weeks before pollen season (grass, ragweed) for optimal efficacy. Dust mite SLIT is year-round. First dose MUST be given in office with 30-minute observation.

3. SCIT Protocols

📋 Standard (Conventional) Protocol

1
Build-up Phase: Weekly injections with gradually increasing doses over 6-8 months until maintenance dose reached
2
Maintenance Phase: Same dose every 2-4 weeks for 3-5 years

Typical schedule: Start at 1:10,000 or 1:1,000 dilution → increase weekly → reach maintenance (typically 0.5mL of maintenance concentration)

⚡ Cluster Protocol

1
Build-up Phase: Multiple injections per visit (2-4), given 30 min apart, 1-2x/week. Reaches maintenance in 4-8 weeks.
2
Maintenance Phase: Standard maintenance (every 2-4 weeks for 3-5 years)

Advantage: Faster buildup, fewer total visits. Slightly higher reaction rate during buildup.

🚀 Rush Protocol

1
Build-up Phase: Multiple injections over 1-3 days to reach maintenance. Often used for venom immunotherapy.
2
Maintenance Phase: Standard maintenance schedule thereafter

Higher risk: Requires experienced staff, premedication often used. Reserved for venom allergy or urgent cases.

⚠️ Premedication for Cluster/Rush: Consider antihistamines ± omalizumab pretreatment for high-risk patients. H1 blocker (cetirizine 10mg) given 1-2 hours before injections can reduce systemic reactions.

4. Indications & Contraindications

✅ Strong Indications
  • Allergic rhinitis/conjunctivitis (moderate-severe, inadequate medication control)
  • Allergic asthma (controlled, FEV₁ >70%)
  • Venom hypersensitivity (systemic reactions)
  • Atopic dermatitis with aeroallergen sensitization
✅ Requirements for Candidacy
  • IgE-mediated sensitivity confirmed (SPT or sIgE)
  • Clinical correlation (symptoms with exposure)
  • Symptoms inadequately controlled with avoidance + medications
  • Willingness to commit to 3-5 years of treatment
❌ Absolute Contraindications
  • Uncontrolled/severe asthma (FEV₁ <70%)
  • Inability to communicate symptoms
  • Significant cardiovascular disease (high anaphylaxis risk)
  • Treatment with beta-blockers (relative—can continue with caution)
⚠️ Relative Contraindications
  • Pregnancy (don't initiate; can continue if tolerated)
  • Immunodeficiency
  • Autoimmune disease
  • Age <5 years (limited data)
  • Poor adherence likelihood

5. Reactions & Management

Local Reactions

Swelling, redness, and itching at the injection site are common and usually not dangerous:

  • Small local (< 25mm): No action needed; may apply ice
  • Large local (≥ 25mm): May premedicate with antihistamine; consider dose adjustment if persistent
  • Delayed local (>24 hrs): Usually not dose-limiting

Systemic Reactions

Grade Symptoms Management
Grade 1 Pruritus, urticaria, rhinitis, mild asthma (PEF >70%) Antihistamine ± inhaled bronchodilator; observe
Grade 2 Urticaria, angioedema (not laryngeal), moderate asthma (PEF 40-70%) Antihistamine, bronchodilator, consider epinephrine
Grade 3 Severe asthma (PEF <40%), laryngeal edema Epinephrine IM, oxygen, prepare for airway management
Grade 4 Respiratory failure, hypotension, loss of consciousness Epinephrine IM, IV fluids, call emergency services

🚨 Systemic Reaction Protocol

1
Stop injection if in progress; do NOT remove needle until assessment
2
Assess severity - airway, breathing, circulation, skin
3
For Grade 2+: Epinephrine 0.3-0.5mg IM (anterolateral thigh)
4
Position: Supine with legs elevated (unless respiratory distress)
5
Adjuncts: O₂, albuterol for bronchospasm, diphenhydramine, IV access
6
Monitor: Vital signs q5min; observe minimum 30-60 min after resolution
7
Document: Time of onset, symptoms, treatment, outcome, dose adjustment plan

6. Venom Immunotherapy (VIT)

VIT is the most effective form of immunotherapy, with >95% protection rate against future systemic reactions.

Indications for VIT

  • History of systemic reaction (not just large local) to Hymenoptera sting
  • Positive venom skin test or serum specific IgE
  • Adults with systemic reactions should be offered VIT
  • Children: VIT for moderate-severe systemic reactions (not cutaneous-only)

Venoms Used

  • Honeybee (Apis mellifera)
  • Yellow jacket (Vespula species)
  • Wasp (Polistes species)
  • Hornet (Dolichovespula/Vespa)
  • Fire ant (whole body extract—Solenopsis)
Duration of VIT: Typically 5 years. Consider indefinite VIT for: very severe initial reaction (hypotension/LOC), honeybee allergy (higher relapse), systemic mastocytosis, or elevated baseline tryptase.
Clinical Pearl - Mastocytosis Screening: Check baseline serum tryptase in all venom allergy patients. Elevated tryptase (>11.4 ng/mL) suggests mastocytosis and predicts higher risk of severe reactions and VIT treatment failure. These patients may need lifelong VIT.

7. Aspirin Desensitization for AERD

Aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD, Samter's triad) consists of asthma, nasal polyposis, and NSAID sensitivity. Aspirin desensitization can improve disease control.

Components of AERD

  • Chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps (often recurrent despite surgery)
  • Asthma (often severe, eosinophilic)
  • Reactions to COX-1 inhibitors (aspirin, NSAIDs)

Aspirin Desensitization Protocol (Example)

⚠️ Aspirin Challenge/Desensitization (Day 1-2)

Must be performed in monitored setting with emergency equipment available. Asthma must be stable (FEV₁ ≥70%).

1
Day 1 AM: Aspirin 20-40mg → observe 3 hours
2
Day 1 PM: Aspirin 40-60mg → observe 3 hours (if no reaction to AM dose)
3
Day 2: Continue escalating: 60mg → 100mg → 150mg → 325mg (q3h intervals)
4
Maintenance: 325-650mg BID ongoing (must not miss doses—re-sensitization occurs within 48-72 hours)

Benefits of Aspirin Desensitization

  • Improved nasal and sinus symptoms
  • Decreased polyp recurrence (extends time between surgeries)
  • Reduced need for systemic corticosteroids
  • Improved asthma control in some patients
  • Enables NSAID use for cardiovascular or pain indications
⚠️ Critical: Once desensitized, patients must take aspirin DAILY. Missing 48-72 hours results in re-sensitization, and the full desensitization protocol must be repeated before aspirin can be safely resumed.
