Diabetes & DKA

Understanding glucose metabolism, the pathophysiology of diabetic emergencies, and evidence-based management of hyperglycemic crises.

📚 10 Concepts ❓ 8 MCQs ⏱ ~45 min

🔬 Insulin Physiology

Normal Glucose Homeostasis

  • Fed state — Insulin promotes glucose uptake into muscle/fat, glycogen synthesis, lipogenesis
  • Fasting state — Glucagon promotes glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis, lipolysis, ketogenesis
  • Insulin:glucagon ratio — The key determinant of metabolic state

🔄 Insulin Actions

1
Glucose uptake — GLUT4 translocation to cell membrane (muscle, fat)
2
Glycogen synthesis — Activates glycogen synthase in liver and muscle
3
Lipogenesis — Promotes fat storage, inhibits lipolysis
4
Protein synthesis — Anabolic effects on muscle
5
K+ uptake — Drives potassium into cells (important in DKA!)

💡 Clinical Pearl

Insulin's effect on potassium is critical in DKA management. Even with normal serum K+, total body potassium is severely depleted. Giving insulin without K+ replacement causes dangerous hypokalemia.

⚖️ Type 1 vs Type 2 Diabetes

Pathophysiology Comparison

FeatureType 1Type 2
MechanismAutoimmune β-cell destructionInsulin resistance + relative deficiency
OnsetUsually childhood/young adultUsually adult (but increasing in youth)
Insulin levelsAbsent or very lowNormal, high, or eventually low
Ketosis proneYes (insulin-dependent)Usually no (except stress/illness)
C-peptideLow or undetectableNormal or elevated
AutoantibodiesPresent (GAD65, IA-2, etc.)Absent
AssociationOther autoimmune diseasesMetabolic syndrome

💡 Clinical Pearl

LADA (Latent Autoimmune Diabetes in Adults) presents like T2DM but is actually slow-onset T1DM. Suspect it in lean adults with poor response to oral agents. Check GAD65 antibodies and C-peptide.

⚡ DKA Pathophysiology

The Metabolic Cascade

DKA results from absolute or relative insulin deficiency combined with counter-regulatory hormone excess:

🔄 DKA Development

1
Insulin deficiency → Glucose cannot enter cells
2
Counter-regulatory surge → Glucagon, cortisol, catecholamines, GH increase
3
Uncontrolled lipolysis → Free fatty acids flood liver
4
Ketogenesis → FFAs converted to ketones (β-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate)
5
Metabolic acidosis → Ketoacids overwhelm buffering capacity
6
Osmotic diuresis → Hyperglycemia causes massive fluid/electrolyte losses

⚠️ Euglycemic DKA

DKA can occur with glucose <250 mg/dL, especially with:

  • SGLT2 inhibitors (glucosuria prevents hyperglycemia)
  • Pregnancy
  • Recent insulin use
  • Decreased oral intake

Don't rely on glucose alone — check ketones and pH!

💊 DKA Management

The Four Pillars of DKA Treatment

PillarApproachKey Points
1. Fluids NS bolus → maintenance 1-2L in first hour; total deficit 5-10L; switch to D5½NS when glucose <200
2. Insulin Regular insulin drip 0.1 U/kg/hr; goal glucose drop 50-70 mg/dL/hr; continue until gap closes
3. Potassium Replace early and often Add K+ to fluids if K <5.3; hold insulin if K <3.3 until repleted
4. Monitor Frequent labs BMP q2-4h; follow anion gap (not just glucose) for resolution

Potassium Management Algorithm

  • K+ <3.3 mEq/L — Hold insulin! Give 20-40 mEq/hr K+ until >3.3
  • K+ 3.3-5.3 mEq/L — Add 20-40 mEq K+ to each liter of fluids
  • K+ >5.3 mEq/L — No K+ initially; recheck in 2 hours

💡 Clinical Pearl

The anion gap, not glucose, determines DKA resolution. Continue insulin drip until gap closes (<12) and patient can eat. Overlap with subcutaneous insulin for 1-2 hours before stopping drip.

⚠️ Cerebral Edema Risk

More common in pediatric DKA. Risk factors include rapid fluid administration, rapid glucose correction, and bicarbonate use. Correct glucose and osmolality gradually.

💧 Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)

DKA vs HHS

FeatureDKAHHS
Typical patientType 1 (or T2 under stress)Type 2, elderly
Glucose>250 mg/dL>600 mg/dL (often >1000)
pH<7.30>7.30
KetonesStrongly positiveMinimal or absent
OsmolalityVariable>320 mOsm/kg
Fluid deficit~6L~9L (more severe)
Mortality~1-5%~10-20%

Why No Ketosis in HHS?

Patients with HHS have enough residual insulin to prevent lipolysis and ketogenesis, but not enough to facilitate glucose uptake. The result is:

  • Profound hyperglycemia (often >1000 mg/dL)
  • Severe dehydration from osmotic diuresis
  • Hyperosmolality causing altered mental status
  • No significant ketoacid production

💡 Clinical Pearl

HHS has higher mortality than DKA despite less acidosis. The key difference is fluid deficit — HHS patients are profoundly dehydrated. Fluids are even more critical than in DKA.

🔍 Chronic Complications

Microvascular Complications

  • Retinopathy — Nonproliferative → proliferative; leading cause of blindness in working-age adults
  • Nephropathy — Microalbuminuria → macroalbuminuria → ESRD; screen with urine albumin:creatinine ratio
  • Neuropathy — "Stocking-glove" sensory loss; autonomic dysfunction; risk for foot ulcers

Macrovascular Complications

  • Coronary artery disease — 2-4x increased risk; often silent ischemia
  • Cerebrovascular disease — Increased stroke risk
  • Peripheral arterial disease — Combined with neuropathy → high amputation risk

HHS patients are more insulin resistant
HHS has more severe dehydration and often occurs in elderly with comorbidities
The absence of ketones leads to more cellular damage
HHS is more difficult to diagnose, delaying treatment

💉 Diabetes Medications

Key Drug Classes

ClassMechanismKey Points
Metformin Decreases hepatic glucose production First-line for T2DM; hold if eGFR <30; lactic acidosis rare
SGLT2 inhibitors Block renal glucose reabsorption CV and renal protection; risk of euglycemic DKA, UTIs, Fournier's gangrene
GLP-1 agonists Incretin mimetic; glucose-dependent insulin secretion Weight loss, CV benefit; GI side effects; pancreatitis risk
Sulfonylureas Stimulate insulin secretion Hypoglycemia risk; weight gain; avoid in elderly/renal impairment
DPP-4 inhibitors Prolong endogenous incretins Weight neutral; well-tolerated; modest efficacy
Insulin Exogenous replacement Required for T1DM; often needed in advanced T2DM

💡 Clinical Pearl

For T2DM patients with established CV disease or high risk, add SGLT2 inhibitor or GLP-1 agonist with proven CV benefit regardless of HbA1c. For those with CKD or HF, SGLT2 inhibitors have additional benefits.
