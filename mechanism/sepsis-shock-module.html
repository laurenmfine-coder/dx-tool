Sepsis & Shock

Master the pathophysiology of sepsis, recognize shock states, and understand time-critical interventions. From SIRS to septic shock — the mechanisms that save lives.

📚 16 Concepts ❓ 10 MCQs ⏱ ~55 min

🔬 Sepsis Definitions & Pathophysiology

Evolution of Sepsis Definitions (Sepsis-3)

The 2016 Sepsis-3 definitions replaced SIRS criteria with organ dysfunction focus:

  • Sepsis — Life-threatening organ dysfunction due to dysregulated host response to infection (SOFA ≥2 from baseline)
  • Septic Shock — Sepsis with vasopressors required to maintain MAP ≥65 AND lactate >2 despite adequate fluid resuscitation
  • qSOFA — Quick screening: RR ≥22, SBP ≤100, altered mental status (≥2 = high risk)

The Inflammatory Cascade

Sepsis represents a dysregulated host response:

  • PAMPs — Pathogen-associated molecular patterns (LPS, lipoteichoic acid, flagellin)
  • PRRs — Pattern recognition receptors (TLRs, NOD-like receptors) recognize PAMPs
  • Cytokine storm — TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6 trigger systemic inflammation
  • Endothelial dysfunction — Increased permeability → capillary leak → edema
  • Coagulation activation — Microthrombi, DIC, impaired perfusion

🔄 From Infection to Organ Failure

1
Infection → PAMPs released into bloodstream
2
Immune activation → Cytokine release, complement activation
3
Vasodilation → ↓ SVR, distributive shock
4
Capillary leak → Third-spacing, hypovolemia
5
Microvascular dysfunction → Impaired O2 extraction
6
Organ dysfunction → AKI, ARDS, encephalopathy, DIC

💡 Clinical Pearl

Lactate elevation in sepsis has TWO mechanisms: (1) Tissue hypoperfusion → anaerobic metabolism, AND (2) Accelerated aerobic glycolysis from β-adrenergic stimulation. This is why lactate can remain elevated even with adequate perfusion.

💔 Four Types of Shock

Hemodynamic Profiles

Cardiogenic↓↓↑↑↑MI, myocarditis, valvular
Hypovolemic↓↑↓Hemorrhage, dehydration

CO = cardiac output; SVR = systemic vascular resistance; PCWP = pulmonary capillary wedge pressure

Distributive Shock Subtypes

  • Septic — Most common; cytokine-mediated vasodilation, capillary leak
  • Anaphylactic — IgE-mediated histamine release; bronchospasm + angioedema
  • Neurogenic — Loss of sympathetic tone (spinal injury); bradycardia + hypotension
  • Adrenal crisis — Cortisol deficiency; ↓ vascular tone + ↓ catecholamine sensitivity

⚠️ Don't Miss

Septic shock can COEXIST with other shock types! A septic patient with MI has mixed distributive + cardiogenic shock. Look for signs: elevated lactate despite aggressive fluids, pulmonary edema, new ECG changes.

⏱️ Sepsis Management: Hour-1 Bundle

🎯 Surviving Sepsis Campaign Hour-1 Bundle

0-15 minMeasure lactate (repeat if initial >2)
0-15 minObtain blood cultures BEFORE antibiotics
0-60 minAdminister broad-spectrum antibiotics
0-60 minBegin 30 mL/kg crystalloid for hypotension or lactate ≥4
OngoingVasopressors if MAP <65 during/after fluids

Fluid Resuscitation Principles

  • Initial bolus — 30 mL/kg crystalloid (balanced preferred: LR, Plasmalyte)
  • Why balanced? — NS causes hyperchloremic acidosis; RCT data suggest harm with NS
  • Fluid responsiveness — Assess with passive leg raise, pulse pressure variation, IVC ultrasound
  • Avoid overload — After initial resuscitation, use dynamic parameters; excess fluid → pulmonary edema, worse outcomes

Vasopressor Selection

💡 Clinical Pearl

Add vasopressin early (NE dose 0.25-0.5 mcg/kg/min) rather than escalating NE indefinitely. Sepsis depletes endogenous vasopressin stores, and exogenous vasopressin works through a catecholamine-independent pathway.

💊 Antibiotic Principles in Sepsis

Time to Antibiotics

Each hour of delay in appropriate antibiotics increases mortality in septic shock:

  • Target — Within 1 hour of sepsis recognition
  • Blood cultures first — But don't delay antibiotics >45 min for cultures
  • Broad spectrum — Cover likely pathogens based on source; narrow when cultures return

⚠️ MRSA Risk Factors

Add vancomycin for: prior MRSA, recent hospitalization, hemodialysis, indwelling lines, injection drug use, skin/soft tissue source, known colonization. Don't forget to check local antibiogram!

📊 Monitoring & Adjunctive Therapies

Resuscitation Targets

  • MAP ≥65 mmHg — Higher targets (80-85) not beneficial, may be harmful
  • Lactate clearance — Target ≥10% decrease per 2 hours; as good as ScvO2
  • Urine output — Goal >0.5 mL/kg/hr
  • Capillary refill — Peripheral perfusion marker; target <3 seconds

Adjunctive Therapies

  • Corticosteroids — Hydrocortisone 200mg/day for refractory shock (NE >0.25 for ≥4h); no proven mortality benefit but faster shock reversal
  • Blood transfusion — Restrictive (Hgb <7) unless active bleeding, severe hypoxemia, or cardiac ischemia
  • Glucose control — Target 140-180 mg/dL; avoid tight control (<110) due to hypoglycemia risk
  • DVT prophylaxis — LMWH preferred unless contraindicated
  • Stress ulcer prophylaxis — PPI or H2 blocker if risk factors (coagulopathy, mechanical ventilation)

MAP Calculation

MAP = (SBP + 2 × DBP) / 3

💡 Clinical Pearl

Lactate-guided resuscitation is non-inferior to ScvO2-guided. Serial lactates every 2-4 hours help assess response. Persistent lactate elevation despite adequate MAP suggests ongoing tissue hypoperfusion, microcirculatory dysfunction, or non-hypoxic lactate production.

Sepsis-Induced AKI

  • Mechanism — Microvascular dysfunction, inflammation, tubular injury (not just hypoperfusion)
  • Urine output — Often decreased; but AKI can occur with preserved UOP
  • Avoid nephrotoxins — NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast (if possible)
  • RRT indications — Refractory hyperkalemia, acidosis, volume overload, uremic symptoms

Sepsis-Induced Coagulopathy & DIC

  • Mechanism — Tissue factor expression → coagulation activation + impaired anticoagulation + fibrinolysis shutdown
  • Labs — ↓ platelets, ↑ PT/INR, ↑ D-dimer, ↓ fibrinogen
  • Clinical — Microthrombi (organ dysfunction) AND bleeding (consumption)
  • Treatment — Treat underlying infection; FFP/platelets/cryo only if bleeding

👥 Special Considerations

Immunocompromised Patients

  • Neutropenic fever — Medical emergency; antipseudomonal coverage essential
  • Fungal coverage — Consider echinocandin if prolonged neutropenia, TPN, prior colonization
  • Atypical presentations — May lack fever, leukocytosis, or localizing signs
  • PJP prophylaxis failures — Consider PJP in HIV/immunosuppressed with hypoxia

Source Control

Antibiotics alone are insufficient if there's an undrained collection or infected device:

  • Abscess — Drainage (percutaneous or surgical)
  • Cholangitis — ERCP for biliary decompression
  • Infected lines — Remove if possible; line salvage rarely successful
  • Necrotizing fasciitis — Emergent surgical debridement
  • Timing — Within 6-12 hours when feasible

⚠️ Red Flag: Necrotizing Soft Tissue Infection

Pain out of proportion, crepitus, rapid progression, hemodynamic instability, skin changes (dusky, bullae). Don't wait for imaging — surgical exploration is both diagnostic and therapeutic. Mortality increases with every hour of delay.
