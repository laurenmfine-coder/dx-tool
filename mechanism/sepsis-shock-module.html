Sepsis & Shock

From SIRS to septic shock: understanding the inflammatory cascade, hemodynamic profiles, and time-critical resuscitation strategies.

1 🔬 Definitions & Pathophysiology
🎯 Sepsis-3 Definitions (2016)

The Sepsis-3 task force redefined sepsis to emphasize organ dysfunction rather than inflammation:

  • Sepsis: Life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. Operationally defined as a ≥2 point increase in SOFA score.
  • Septic Shock: Sepsis with persistent hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg AND lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation.

The old "SIRS criteria" (temp, HR, RR, WBC) are no longer used to define sepsis but remain useful for identifying patients who may have infection.

💡 Clinical Pearl
qSOFA (Quick SOFA) was designed for bedside screening outside the ICU: Respiratory rate ≥22, altered mental status, SBP ≤100. Score ≥2 suggests high risk but should NOT delay treatment—it's a risk stratification tool, not a diagnostic criterion.
🧬 The Inflammatory Cascade

Sepsis represents a dysregulated host response with both pro-inflammatory and immunosuppressive phases:

  • Recognition: PAMPs (pathogen-associated molecular patterns) bind to TLRs (toll-like receptors) on innate immune cells
  • Cytokine Storm: Release of TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6 → fever, tachycardia, vasodilation
  • Endothelial Dysfunction: Glycocalyx breakdown, capillary leak, edema, microvascular thrombosis
  • Coagulopathy: DIC with simultaneous bleeding and clotting
  • Immunoparalysis: Later phase with lymphocyte apoptosis, monocyte deactivation → secondary infections
SOFA Score Components
Resp (P/F) + Coag (Plt) + Liver (Bili) + CV (MAP/pressors) + CNS (GCS) + Renal (Cr/UOP)
Each organ system scored 0-4 points. Total range 0-24. ≥2 point increase = sepsis.
2 💔 Classification of Shock
📊 The Four Types of Shock

All shock results in inadequate tissue perfusion, but the underlying mechanism differs:

Type Mechanism CVP/Preload CO SVR Examples
Distributive Vasodilation, maldistribution of flow Low/Normal High (early) → Low ↓↓ Sepsis, anaphylaxis, neurogenic
Hypovolemic Volume loss (hemorrhage, dehydration) ↓↓ ↓↓ ↑↑ Hemorrhage, GI losses, burns
Cardiogenic Pump failure ↑↑ ↓↓ ↑↑ MI, cardiomyopathy, arrhythmia
Obstructive Mechanical obstruction to flow ↑ (right) / ↓ (left) ↓↓ ↑↑ PE, tamponade, tension PTX
⚠️ Critical Concept
Septic shock is distributive, but mixed shock is common! A septic patient may also be hypovolemic (third-spacing, poor intake) or develop cardiogenic shock (sepsis-induced cardiomyopathy). Always reassess hemodynamics after initial resuscitation.

3 💉 Hour-1 Bundle & Initial Resuscitation
🚀 Surviving Sepsis Campaign: Hour-1 Bundle
1
Measure Lactate
Re-measure if initial lactate >2 mmol/L. Target: lactate normalization (clearance >10-20%/2hr suggests adequate resuscitation).
2
Obtain Blood Cultures
Before antibiotics if possible, but do NOT delay antibiotics more than 45 minutes. At least 2 sets from separate sites.
3
Administer Broad-Spectrum Antibiotics
Within 1 hour of recognition. Each hour of delay increases mortality 7-8%. Cover Gram-positive, Gram-negative, and consider MRSA/Pseudomonas risk factors.
4
Administer 30 mL/kg Crystalloid
For hypotension (MAP <65) or lactate ≥4. Use balanced crystalloids (LR, Plasmalyte) over NS when possible. Give rapidly (within 3 hours).
5
Vasopressors if Hypotensive
Start norepinephrine if MAP <65 persists during or after fluid resuscitation. Don't wait until fluids are complete—start early through peripheral IV if needed.
💡 Fluid Responsiveness
Not all septic patients need 30 mL/kg! After initial bolus, assess fluid responsiveness before giving more. Dynamic predictors (passive leg raise with CO monitoring, pulse pressure variation >13% if mechanically ventilated) are superior to static measures (CVP is notoriously unreliable).
💊 Vasopressor Selection
  • Norepinephrine (first-line): α1 >> β1. Increases SVR with modest inotropy. Start 0.05-0.1 mcg/kg/min.
  • Vasopressin (second-line): V1 receptor agonist. Add at 0.03-0.04 U/min when NE >0.25-0.5 mcg/kg/min. Catecholamine-sparing, less tachycardia.
  • Epinephrine (alternative): β1 > β2 > α1. More inotropy but more arrhythmias, increases lactate independently.
  • Dopamine (avoid): More arrhythmias than NE. Only consider if bradycardic and low risk of tachyarrhythmia.
  • Phenylephrine (specific situations): Pure α1. Use only when tachyarrhythmias limit other agents or as bridge.
4 🧪 Lactate: Marker & Mechanism
📈 Why Lactate Rises in Sepsis

Lactate elevation in sepsis is multifactorial—it's not simply "anaerobic metabolism from hypoperfusion":

  • Type A (Hypoxic): Tissue hypoperfusion → anaerobic glycolysis → lactate production
  • Type B (Non-hypoxic):
    • Catecholamine surge → accelerated aerobic glycolysis (β2-stimulation)
    • Mitochondrial dysfunction ("cytopathic hypoxia")
    • Decreased hepatic clearance (liver metabolizes 50-70% of lactate)
    • Increased protein catabolism

This is why lactate can remain elevated even with adequate MAP and ScvO2—clearance matters as much as production!

Lactate Clearance
Clearance (%) = [(Initial − Repeat) / Initial] × 100
Target ≥10-20% clearance every 2 hours. Failure to clear suggests ongoing hypoperfusion or hepatic dysfunction.
⚠️ Lactate Pitfalls
Elevated lactate is NOT always sepsis: Consider thiamine deficiency (Wernicke's), metformin toxicity, seizures, severe asthma (β-agonist use), mesenteric ischemia, malignancy, and liver failure. A "normal" lactate doesn't exclude sepsis—early or localized infection may not elevate lactate.
5 🎯 Source Control & Antibiotic Stewardship
🔍 Finding & Controlling the Source

Antibiotics alone won't cure sepsis if there's an undrained abscess or necrotic tissue. Source control should happen within 6-12 hours when feasible:

  • Drain: Abscesses, empyema, cholangitis (ERCP), infected fluid collections (IR drainage)
  • Debride: Necrotizing fasciitis, gangrenous tissue
  • Remove: Infected devices (lines, hardware)—tip culture the line!
  • Divert: Bowel perforation (colostomy), obstructed ureter (nephrostomy)
💊 Empiric Antibiotic Principles

Cover broadly, then narrow based on cultures and clinical response:

  • Community-acquired without risk factors: Ceftriaxone + azithromycin (if pneumonia) or piperacillin-tazobactam
  • Healthcare-associated/risk factors: Anti-pseudomonal β-lactam (pip-tazo, cefepime, meropenem) + vancomycin
  • Intra-abdominal: Add anaerobic coverage (metronidazole or use pip-tazo/meropenem)
  • Neutropenic fever: Cefepime or meropenem monotherapy

De-escalate within 48-72 hours based on cultures. Typical duration: 7-10 days for most infections.

💡 Procalcitonin
PCT can help guide antibiotic discontinuation (not initiation). Consider stopping antibiotics when PCT decreases by ≥80% from peak OR falls to <0.5 ng/mL, combined with clinical improvement. Serial trending is more useful than a single value.
6 🏥 Advanced Management & Adjunctive Therapies
💪 Corticosteroids in Septic Shock

When to consider: Refractory septic shock (NE >0.25 mcg/kg/min for >4 hours despite adequate fluids)

  • Dose: Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day (50 mg q6h or 200 mg continuous infusion)
  • Duration: Until vasopressors discontinued, then taper over several days
  • Rationale: Critical illness-related corticosteroid insufficiency (CIRCI)—not absolute adrenal failure but inadequate response relative to stress
  • Evidence: ADRENAL and APROCCHSS trials showed faster shock reversal; mortality benefit modest/conflicting

No need to check random cortisol or do ACTH stim test—treat empirically if meeting criteria.

🩸 Transfusion & Glucose Management
  • Hemoglobin target: Transfuse for Hgb <7 g/dL in most patients (TRICC, TRISS trials). Exception: active bleeding, ACS, severe hypoxemia—consider higher threshold.
  • Glucose: Target <180 mg/dL. Avoid tight control (<110)—increased hypoglycemia without benefit (NICE-SUGAR).
  • Nutrition: Start enteral nutrition within 48 hours when hemodynamically stable. Trophic feeds OK initially.
🫁 Mechanical Ventilation in Sepsis

Many septic patients develop ARDS. Use lung-protective ventilation:

  • Tidal volume: 6 mL/kg IBW (not actual body weight!)
  • Plateau pressure: <30 cm H₂O
  • PEEP: Higher PEEP for moderate-severe ARDS (P/F <200)
  • Proning: Consider for P/F <150 despite optimization
  • Driving pressure: Target <15 cm H₂O (Pplat - PEEP)
⚠️ Renal Replacement Therapy
Start RRT for conventional indications (severe acidosis, hyperkalemia, volume overload refractory to diuretics, uremia). Recent trials (STARRT-AKI, IDEAL-ICU) show no benefit to "early" RRT for AKI alone—wait for clear indication unless life-threatening.

Question 1 of 15
According to Sepsis-3 criteria, which of the following best defines septic shock?
A SIRS criteria plus suspected infection with hypotension
B Sepsis with MAP <65 mmHg requiring vasopressors
C Sepsis requiring vasopressors for MAP ≥65 AND lactate >2 despite adequate fluids
D qSOFA score ≥2 with positive blood cultures
✓ Correct!
Sepsis-3 defines septic shock as sepsis with both persistent hypotension requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg AND lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation. This dual requirement identifies patients with the highest mortality risk (~40%). The old SIRS criteria and qSOFA are screening tools, not diagnostic criteria for septic shock.
Question 2 of 15
A 62-year-old man presents with fever, confusion, and hypotension (BP 82/50). He is tachycardic to 115 bpm with warm extremities. Labs show WBC 18,000, lactate 4.5 mmol/L, and creatinine 2.1 (baseline 0.9). A central line is placed and initial hemodynamic assessment shows CVP 8 mmHg, ScvO2 75%, and cardiac index 3.8 L/min/m².
Which type of shock is most consistent with these findings?
A Hypovolemic shock
B Distributive (septic) shock
C Cardiogenic shock
D Obstructive shock
✓ Correct!
This is classic distributive (septic) shock: warm extremities, normal-to-high cardiac output (CI 3.8), and adequate ScvO2 (75%) despite hypotension—indicating low SVR (vasodilation). Hypovolemic and cardiogenic shock would show low CO and cold extremities with high SVR as compensation. The fever, elevated WBC, and organ dysfunction (AKI) point to sepsis as the cause.
Question 3 of 15
A patient with septic shock remains hypotensive (MAP 58) after 3L crystalloid. You start norepinephrine and titrate to 0.4 mcg/kg/min. MAP is now 62 mmHg. What is the most appropriate next vasopressor intervention?
A Add vasopressin 0.03 U/min
B Switch to dopamine
C Add phenylephrine 100 mcg/min
D Double the norepinephrine dose
✓ Correct!
Vasopressin is the recommended second-line agent when norepinephrine doses exceed 0.25-0.5 mcg/kg/min. It works via V1 receptors (catecholamine-independent), is "NE-sparing," and may be particularly beneficial as sepsis causes relative vasopressin deficiency. Dopamine causes more arrhythmias. Phenylephrine provides only vasoconstriction without inotropy and is reserved for specific situations. Simply increasing NE without adding a second agent may lead to diminishing returns and more adverse effects.
Question 4 of 15
Which component is NOT part of the SOFA score used to define organ dysfunction in sepsis?
A PaO2/FiO2 ratio
B Platelet count
C Glasgow Coma Scale
D Lactate level
✓ Correct!
The SOFA score assesses 6 organ systems: Respiratory (P/F ratio), Coagulation (platelets), Liver (bilirubin), Cardiovascular (MAP/vasopressors), CNS (GCS), and Renal (creatinine/urine output). Lactate is NOT part of SOFA but is used separately in the definition of septic shock (lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate resuscitation).
Question 5 of 15
You are called to evaluate a 55-year-old woman with suspected cholangitis. She is febrile, tachycardic, and confused. BP is 85/50 mmHg, lactate is 5.2 mmol/L. You have already ordered blood cultures.
According to the Surviving Sepsis Campaign Hour-1 bundle, what is the MOST time-sensitive intervention?
A Arrange emergent ERCP
B Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics
C Complete 30 mL/kg crystalloid bolus
D Place a central venous catheter
✓ Correct!
Antibiotics within 1 hour are the most time-sensitive intervention—each hour of delay increases mortality by 7-8%. While fluids are also critical, they can be given over 3 hours. ERCP for source control is important but typically happens within 6-12 hours (emergent if Tokyo Grade III severity). Central line placement should not delay antibiotics—start vasopressors through a peripheral IV if needed. The Hour-1 bundle emphasizes: lactate, cultures, antibiotics, fluids, and vasopressors if needed.
Question 6 of 15
A septic patient's initial lactate is 6.8 mmol/L. After 2 hours of resuscitation with 2L crystalloid and norepinephrine, repeat lactate is 5.8 mmol/L. What is the lactate clearance and its clinical significance?
A 15% clearance - adequate response, continue current management
B 85% clearance - excellent response, consider de-escalation
C 15% clearance - suboptimal, reassess resuscitation adequacy and source control
D 1% clearance - critical failure, requires immediate ECMO evaluation
✓ Correct!
Lactate clearance = (6.8 - 5.8) / 6.8 × 100 = 15%. Target clearance is ≥10-20% every 2 hours. While 15% meets the minimum threshold, it's at the lower end and the absolute lactate remains quite elevated. This warrants reassessing resuscitation adequacy (fluid responsiveness? need more vasopressors? adequate perfusion pressure?) and ensuring source control is addressed. If clearance continues to be sluggish, consider hepatic dysfunction limiting lactate metabolism.
Question 7 of 15
In sepsis, lactate elevation can occur through mechanisms EXCEPT:
A Increased hepatic gluconeogenesis from lactate
B Catecholamine-driven aerobic glycolysis
C Tissue hypoperfusion with anaerobic metabolism
D Decreased hepatic clearance of lactate
✓ Correct!
Hepatic gluconeogenesis from lactate (the Cori cycle) is a clearance mechanism that REMOVES lactate, not a cause of elevation. In sepsis, this clearance pathway may be impaired, contributing to hyperlactatemia. The other options are all mechanisms that INCREASE lactate: anaerobic metabolism from hypoperfusion (Type A), β2-adrenergic-driven aerobic glycolysis (Type B), and reduced hepatic clearance. Understanding that lactate in sepsis is multifactorial prevents over-reliance on it as a pure perfusion marker.
Question 8 of 15
A 70-year-old diabetic man with pneumonia has been in the ICU for 2 days with septic shock requiring norepinephrine 0.3 mcg/kg/min. Despite MAP 68 mmHg and ScvO2 72%, he remains oliguric with creatinine rising from 1.8 to 3.2 mg/dL. He has received 5L of fluid over 48 hours.
What is the best approach to assess whether additional IV fluids will improve his hemodynamics?
A Check CVP - give fluids if CVP <12 mmHg
B Empirically give another 30 mL/kg bolus
C Assess skin turgor and mucous membranes
D Perform passive leg raise with cardiac output monitoring
✓ Correct!
Passive leg raise (PLR) with CO monitoring is the best dynamic assessment of fluid responsiveness. PLR transiently increases venous return by ~300 mL; if CO increases ≥10%, the patient is likely fluid responsive. CVP is notoriously unreliable for predicting fluid responsiveness—patients can have low CVP and not respond to fluids, or high CVP and still respond. Empiric boluses in an already fluid-resuscitated patient risk pulmonary edema. After the initial resuscitation phase, fluids should be given judiciously based on dynamic predictors.
Question 9 of 15
A patient with healthcare-associated pneumonia develops septic shock. She was recently hospitalized and received antibiotics 3 months ago. Which empiric antibiotic regimen is most appropriate?
A Ceftriaxone + azithromycin
B Piperacillin-tazobactam + vancomycin
C Ampicillin-sulbactam
D Levofloxacin monotherapy
✓ Correct!
This patient has risk factors for resistant organisms (recent hospitalization, recent antibiotics). She needs broad coverage including anti-pseudomonal activity (pip-tazo, cefepime, or meropenem) plus MRSA coverage (vancomycin). Ceftriaxone + azithromycin would be appropriate for community-acquired pneumonia without risk factors. Ampicillin-sulbactam lacks Pseudomonas coverage. Fluoroquinolone monotherapy is inadequate for septic shock with resistant pathogen risk. De-escalate within 48-72 hours once cultures return.
Question 10 of 15
In which patient with septic shock is hydrocortisone therapy MOST appropriate?
A MAP 62 on norepinephrine 0.08 mcg/kg/min, improving lactate
B Hypotensive patient before any vasopressor trial
C Requiring norepinephrine 0.35 mcg/kg/min + vasopressin for 6 hours
D Only after random cortisol level returns <10 mcg/dL
✓ Correct!
Current guidelines recommend hydrocortisone for refractory septic shock—typically defined as requiring high-dose vasopressors (NE >0.25 mcg/kg/min) for several hours despite adequate fluids. This patient meets criteria with high-dose NE + vasopressin for 6 hours. Testing cortisol levels is NOT required—treat empirically. Patients responding to low-dose vasopressors (option A) don't need steroids. Give fluids and vasopressors first before considering steroids (option B is premature).
Question 11 of 15
A septic patient develops ARDS with P/F ratio of 90. She is on volume-controlled ventilation with tidal volume 450 mL (6 mL/kg IBW 75 kg), PEEP 14, and FiO2 100%. Plateau pressure is 32 cm H2O. What is the most appropriate ventilator adjustment?
A Decrease tidal volume to 375-400 mL
B Increase tidal volume to improve ventilation
C Decrease PEEP to reduce plateau pressure
D No changes needed, settings are optimal
✓ Correct!
The plateau pressure (32 cm H₂O) exceeds the target of <30 cm H₂O. Decrease tidal volume further to 5-6 mL/kg IBW to achieve lung-protective ventilation and reduce barotrauma risk. Allow permissive hypercapnia if needed. The driving pressure (Pplat - PEEP = 18) is also above the ideal target of <15. Decreasing PEEP would worsen oxygenation in severe ARDS. In this patient with P/F 90, also consider prone positioning.
Question 12 of 15
Which hemodynamic profile is most consistent with cardiogenic shock?
A CVP high, CO high, SVR low
B CVP high, CO low, SVR high
C CVP low, CO low, SVR high
D CVP low, CO high, SVR low
✓ Correct!
Cardiogenic shock = pump failure. The heart can't eject blood effectively, so: preload backs up (high CVP/PCWP), cardiac output falls (low CO), and the body compensates by vasoconstricting (high SVR). Option A describes early septic shock (distributive). Option C describes hypovolemic shock. Option D doesn't represent any classic shock pattern. Distinguishing cardiogenic from septic shock is critical—the treatments are opposite (cardiogenic needs afterload reduction and inotropes; septic needs vasopressors).
Question 13 of 15
A 58-year-old man with perforated appendicitis has been treated with antibiotics and is on day 5 of ICU stay. Blood cultures are negative. Procalcitonin has decreased from 15 to 2 ng/mL. He has defervesced and is off vasopressors.
What is the most appropriate antibiotic duration strategy?
A Continue antibiotics until PCT <0.1 ng/mL
B Complete 14 days regardless of PCT
C Consider stopping antibiotics given clinical improvement and PCT trending down
D Switch to oral antibiotics for 21 total days
✓ Correct!
PCT-guided antibiotic discontinuation can safely shorten duration. This patient has: clinical improvement (afebrile, off pressors), PCT decreased by >80% from peak, and adequate source control (surgical management of perforation). Guidelines suggest considering stopping antibiotics when PCT drops ≥80% from peak OR reaches <0.5 ng/mL, combined with clinical improvement. A 7-10 day course is typical for complicated intra-abdominal infection with source control. Unnecessarily prolonging antibiotics increases resistance and adverse effects.
Question 14 of 15
A patient with septic shock develops acute kidney injury with oliguria and creatinine 4.2 mg/dL (baseline 1.0). Potassium is 5.4 mEq/L, bicarbonate 18 mEq/L, and he is volume overloaded but responsive to diuretics. According to recent evidence, when should you initiate renal replacement therapy?
A Immediately - early RRT improves outcomes in septic AKI
B When creatinine doubles from current level
C When urine output falls below 200 mL/day
D For emergent indications (refractory acidosis, hyperkalemia, volume overload, or uremia)
✓ Correct!
Recent trials (STARRT-AKI, IDEAL-ICU) showed no benefit to early/prophylactic RRT in AKI without absolute indications—and potential harm from catheter complications and hemodynamic instability. Initiate RRT for conventional emergent indications: refractory hyperkalemia, severe metabolic acidosis (pH <7.1), uremic symptoms (encephalopathy, pericarditis), or volume overload refractory to diuretics. This patient's hyperkalemia and acidosis are mild and volume is diuretic-responsive—continue supportive care and monitor closely.
Question 15 of 15
A 45-year-old woman presents with anaphylaxis after a bee sting. Despite epinephrine and fluids, she remains hypotensive with MAP 55 mmHg. She is flushed and warm.
What type of shock is this, and what is the first-line vasopressor after epinephrine?
A Distributive shock; dopamine
B Distributive shock; norepinephrine
C Hypovolemic shock; phenylephrine
D Cardiogenic shock; dobutamine
✓ Correct!
Anaphylactic shock is distributive shock—massive vasodilation from histamine and other mediators causes low SVR (warm, flushed skin) and relative hypovolemia. Epinephrine is first-line for anaphylaxis (IM 0.3-0.5 mg, can repeat). If refractory to epinephrine, add norepinephrine infusion for its potent α1 vasoconstriction. Dopamine is avoided due to arrhythmia risk. This is not hypovolemic (primary problem is vasodilation) or cardiogenic (heart is working fine, just can't fill due to vasodilation).
