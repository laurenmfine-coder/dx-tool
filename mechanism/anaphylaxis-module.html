🚨 Anaphylaxis: Pathophysiology & Management

Understanding the most severe allergic reaction - from molecular mechanisms to life-saving treatment

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Define anaphylaxis and apply diagnostic criteria
  • Explain the immunologic mechanisms of IgE-mediated anaphylaxis
  • Identify the key mediators and their clinical effects
  • Recognize the timeline of anaphylactic reactions including biphasic risk
  • Describe the mechanism of epinephrine and its role as first-line treatment

📖 Definition & Diagnostic Criteria

Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially life-threatening systemic allergic reaction that occurs rapidly after exposure to a trigger. It involves multiple organ systems and requires immediate treatment with epinephrine.

NIAID/FAAN Diagnostic Criteria for Anaphylaxis
Anaphylaxis is highly likely when ANY ONE of these three criteria is fulfilled:
1 Acute skin/mucosal symptoms + respiratory OR hypotension
Acute onset (minutes to hours) of illness with skin involvement (hives, flushing, angioedema) AND at least one of: respiratory compromise OR reduced BP/end-organ dysfunction
2 Two or more organ systems after likely allergen exposure
After exposure to a likely allergen: skin/mucosal symptoms, respiratory compromise, reduced BP, or persistent GI symptoms (≥2 systems)
3 Hypotension after known allergen exposure
Reduced BP after exposure to KNOWN allergen for that patient (adults: SBP <90 or >30% decrease from baseline)
💎 Clinical Pearl

Up to 20% of anaphylaxis cases present WITHOUT skin findings. Don't rule out anaphylaxis just because there's no urticaria - respiratory or cardiovascular symptoms after allergen exposure should prompt immediate treatment.

🔬 Pathophysiology: The Anaphylaxis Cascade

IgE-mediated anaphylaxis occurs in two phases: sensitization (initial exposure) and elicitation (subsequent exposure triggering the reaction).

Phase 1: Sensitization (First Exposure)
1
Allergen exposure → Antigen-presenting cells process allergen
↓
2
Th2 response → IL-4, IL-13 drive B cell class switching to IgE
↓
3
IgE production → Allergen-specific IgE binds to FcεRI receptors on mast cells/basophils
↓
4
Sensitized state → Patient is now "primed" for reaction upon re-exposure
Phase 2: Elicitation (Re-exposure → Reaction)
1
Re-exposure to allergen → Multivalent allergen binds and CROSS-LINKS surface IgE
↓
2
FcεRI aggregation → Intracellular signaling cascade (tyrosine kinases, calcium influx)
↓
3
Mast cell degranulation → IMMEDIATE release of preformed mediators (seconds to minutes)
↓
4
De novo synthesis → NEW mediators produced via arachidonic acid pathway (minutes to hours)
↓
5
Systemic effects → Multi-organ involvement: skin, respiratory, cardiovascular, GI
⚠️ Key Concept: Cross-Linking is Required

A single IgE molecule binding allergen is NOT enough. The allergen must have multiple epitopes that allow it to cross-link adjacent IgE molecules, aggregating FcεRI receptors and triggering the signaling cascade. This is why monovalent haptens alone don't trigger reactions.

🧪 Mediators of Anaphylaxis

Understanding the mediators helps explain the clinical manifestations and guides treatment choices.

Preformed Mediators (Immediate Release)

Histamine
Preformed
Effects: Vasodilation (H1/H2), increased vascular permeability (H1), bronchoconstriction (H1), increased mucus production, pruritus, tachycardia (H2), gastric acid secretion (H2)
Clinical correlation: Flushing, urticaria, hypotension, bronchospasm, itching
Tryptase
Preformed
Effects: Activates complement, cleaves fibrinogen, activates PAR-2 receptors
Clinical correlation: Serum tryptase is the best biomarker for anaphylaxis - peaks 1-2 hours, returns to baseline by 24 hours. Elevated baseline tryptase suggests mastocytosis.
Heparin & Proteases
Preformed
Effects: Anticoagulation, tissue remodeling, matrix degradation
Clinical correlation: May contribute to prolonged bleeding at injection sites

Newly Synthesized Mediators

Leukotrienes (LTC4, LTD4, LTE4)
Synthesized
Effects: Potent bronchoconstriction (1000x histamine), increased vascular permeability, mucus secretion
Clinical correlation: Prolonged bronchospasm, contributes to late-phase reaction. Target of leukotriene receptor antagonists (montelukast).
Prostaglandin D2 (PGD2)
Synthesized
Effects: Vasodilation, bronchoconstriction, platelet activation
Clinical correlation: Major mast cell product, contributes to flushing and hypotension
Platelet-Activating Factor (PAF)
Synthesized
Effects: Profound hypotension, increased vascular permeability, platelet aggregation, cardiac depression
Clinical correlation: PAF levels correlate with anaphylaxis severity better than histamine. Patients with low PAF acetylhydrolase activity may have more severe reactions.
Organ System Symptoms Primary Mediators
Skin (90%) Urticaria, flushing, angioedema, pruritus Histamine (H1), PGD2
Respiratory (70%) Bronchospasm, stridor, dyspnea, hypoxia Leukotrienes, histamine, PGD2
Cardiovascular (45%) Hypotension, tachycardia, arrhythmia, shock PAF, histamine (H1/H2), nitric oxide
GI (45%) Nausea, vomiting, cramping, diarrhea Histamine, serotonin, PGD2
Neurologic Anxiety, confusion, loss of consciousness Hypoperfusion, direct mediator effects

⏱️ Timeline of Anaphylaxis

0-5 minutes
Immediate Phase Onset
Mast cell degranulation begins. Preformed mediators (histamine, tryptase) released. Initial symptoms: pruritus, flushing, urticaria, throat tightness.
5-30 minutes
Peak Immediate Reaction
Full symptom development. Cardiovascular collapse may occur. This is the critical window for epinephrine administration.
1-2 hours
Tryptase Peak
Serum tryptase peaks. Draw blood for tryptase measurement. Newly synthesized mediators (leukotrienes, PGD2) contribute to ongoing symptoms.
4-12 hours
Biphasic Reaction Risk
Late-phase mediators can cause symptom recurrence WITHOUT re-exposure. Occurs in 5-20% of patients. Risk factors: severe initial reaction, delayed epinephrine.
24-72 hours
Resolution
Tryptase returns to baseline. Symptoms resolve. Patient education, epinephrine prescription, and allergy referral before discharge.
⚠️ Biphasic Anaphylaxis

A second wave of symptoms can occur 4-12 hours after the initial reaction, even without additional allergen exposure. This is caused by late-phase mediators and inflammatory cell recruitment. All anaphylaxis patients should be observed for at least 4-6 hours (longer if severe initial reaction or risk factors present).

💉 Epinephrine: The Mechanism of Action

Epinephrine is the FIRST-LINE and ONLY life-saving treatment for anaphylaxis. No other medication can substitute for it.

Receptor Location Effect Clinical Benefit
α-1 Vascular smooth muscle Vasoconstriction ↑ Blood pressure, ↓ mucosal edema, ↓ urticaria
β-1 Heart ↑ Inotropy, ↑ Chronotropy ↑ Cardiac output, ↑ coronary perfusion
β-2 Bronchial smooth muscle Bronchodilation Relieves bronchospasm
β-2 Mast cells/basophils ↑ cAMP → Stabilization Inhibits further mediator release
💎 Why Intramuscular (IM)?

IM injection into the lateral thigh provides faster absorption and higher peak plasma levels than subcutaneous injection. The thigh has excellent blood supply (especially during anaphylaxis when peripheral vasoconstriction hasn't fully developed). IV epinephrine is reserved for cardiac arrest or profound shock unresponsive to IM dosing.

Dosing

Patient IM Dose Autoinjector Repeat
Adults & Children >30 kg 0.3-0.5 mg (1:1000) EpiPen 0.3 mg q5-15 min PRN
Children 15-30 kg 0.15-0.3 mg (1:1000) EpiPen Jr 0.15 mg q5-15 min PRN
Children <15 kg 0.01 mg/kg (1:1000) EpiPen Jr (may exceed dose) q5-15 min PRN
⚠️ Delayed Epinephrine = Worse Outcomes

Studies consistently show that delayed epinephrine administration is associated with biphasic reactions, prolonged hospitalization, and death. There are NO absolute contraindications to epinephrine in anaphylaxis - the risk of NOT giving it always outweighs potential side effects.

🏥 Adjunctive Treatments

These medications support treatment but do NOT substitute for epinephrine:

Medication Role Important Notes
H1 Antihistamines
(diphenhydramine, cetirizine)		 Relieves urticaria, pruritus Does NOT prevent or treat hypotension or airway obstruction. Give AFTER epinephrine.
H2 Antihistamines
(famotidine, ranitidine)		 May enhance H1 effects Limited evidence. Some protocols include for refractory symptoms.
Corticosteroids
(methylprednisolone)		 May reduce biphasic reaction risk Onset 4-6 hours. No proven mortality benefit. Don't delay epinephrine to give steroids.
Bronchodilators
(albuterol)		 Adjunct for bronchospasm Does not address upper airway edema or cardiovascular collapse.
IV Fluids Volume resuscitation for shock Up to 35% of circulating volume may shift to interstitial space. Give aggressive crystalloid.
Glucagon Patients on beta-blockers 1-5 mg IV for refractory hypotension in beta-blocked patients.
