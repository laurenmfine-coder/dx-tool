🚨 Anaphylaxis

Recognition, diagnosis, and emergency management of the most severe manifestation of Type I hypersensitivity

📅Content last updated: January 9, 2026
⏱️~40 minutes
👥Healthcare Professionals

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Apply diagnostic criteria for anaphylaxis (≥2 organ systems or hypotension after known allergen)
  • Administer epinephrine as first-line treatment with correct dosing and route
  • Recognize and manage refractory anaphylaxis and biphasic reactions
  • Identify common triggers: foods, medications, venom, and idiopathic causes
  • Understand the pathophysiology of anaphylactic shock
  • Implement appropriate post-anaphylaxis management and referral

📋 Diagnostic Criteria

Anaphylaxis is a clinical diagnosis. Do NOT delay treatment to obtain labs or confirmatory tests. The diagnosis is highly likely when ANY ONE of the following criteria is met:

Anaphylaxis Diagnostic Criteria (WAO/EAACI)

1 Acute onset (minutes to hours) with skin/mucosal involvement PLUS ≥1 of:
  • Respiratory compromise (dyspnea, wheeze, stridor, hypoxemia)
  • Hypotension or end-organ dysfunction (syncope, incontinence, collapse)
2 ≥2 of the following after exposure to LIKELY allergen:
  • Skin/mucosal involvement (urticaria, flushing, angioedema)
  • Respiratory compromise
  • Hypotension or associated symptoms
  • Persistent GI symptoms (cramping, vomiting)
3 Hypotension after exposure to KNOWN allergen for that patient:
  • Adults: SBP <90 mmHg or >30% decrease from baseline
  • Children: Age-specific low SBP or >30% decrease
🔑 Skin Signs May Be Absent

Up to 20% of anaphylaxis cases lack skin findings. Don't rule out anaphylaxis just because there's no urticaria. Cardiovascular collapse or respiratory compromise after allergen exposure should prompt immediate epinephrine even without hives.

🔬 Pathophysiology of Anaphylactic Shock

Anaphylaxis represents massive, systemic mast cell and basophil degranulation leading to distributive shock:

MediatorEffectClinical Manifestation
HistamineVasodilation, ↑ permeability, bronchoconstrictionHypotension, urticaria, wheeze
Leukotrienes (LTC₄/D₄)Potent bronchoconstriction, mucus secretionSevere bronchospasm
Prostaglandin D₂Vasodilation, bronchoconstrictionFlushing, hypotension
PAFPlatelet aggregation, ↑ permeabilityCardiovascular collapse
TryptaseComplement activationAmplifies reaction (diagnostic marker)
💡 Why Epinephrine Works

Epinephrine counters ALL major pathophysiologic effects: α₁ = vasoconstriction (reverses hypotension, reduces angioedema); β₁ = ↑ cardiac output; β₂ = bronchodilation + inhibits mast cell degranulation. No other single drug addresses all of these.

💉 Treatment: Epinephrine First

🚨 EPINEPHRINE IS FIRST-LINE - NO EXCEPTIONS

Epinephrine is the ONLY first-line treatment for anaphylaxis. There are NO absolute contraindications in anaphylaxis. Delayed epinephrine is associated with fatal outcomes. Antihistamines and steroids are adjuncts, NOT substitutes.

Anaphylaxis Treatment Algorithm

1ST
Epinephrine IM (anterolateral thigh)
Adults: 0.3-0.5 mg (0.3-0.5 mL of 1:1000)
Children: 0.01 mg/kg (max 0.3 mg)
Repeat every 5-15 minutes as needed
1ST
Position patient supine, legs elevated
Unless respiratory distress (then sitting) or vomiting. DO NOT have patient stand or sit up suddenly.
2ND
Call for help, monitor, IV access, oxygen
High-flow O₂, cardiac monitor, prepare for CPR if needed
2ND
IV fluids for hypotension
NS bolus 1-2 L (adults), 20 mL/kg (children) - may need massive resuscitation
3RD
Adjunctive medications
H1 antihistamine (diphenhydramine 25-50mg IV)
H2 antihistamine (famotidine 20mg IV)
Corticosteroids (methylprednisolone 125mg IV) - may reduce biphasic risk
Nebulized albuterol for bronchospasm

⚠️ Refractory & Biphasic Anaphylaxis

Refractory Anaphylaxis

If no response to 2-3 doses of IM epinephrine:

  • IV epinephrine infusion: 1-10 mcg/min, titrate to effect (requires monitoring)
  • Aggressive IV fluids: May need 5-10 L in severe cases
  • Vasopressors: Norepinephrine, vasopressin if shock persists
  • Glucagon: 1-5 mg IV for patients on beta-blockers (bypasses β-receptor blockade)
  • Airway management: Early intubation if angioedema progressing
🔑 Beta-Blocker Patients

Patients on beta-blockers may have refractory anaphylaxis because epinephrine's β-effects are blocked. They may also have paradoxical bradycardia. Use glucagon 1-5 mg IV which activates adenylyl cyclase independent of β-receptors.

Biphasic Anaphylaxis

FeatureDetails
DefinitionRecurrence of symptoms after initial resolution, without re-exposure
Incidence1-20% of anaphylaxis cases (varies by study)
TimingUsually 4-12 hours (up to 72 hours reported)
Risk factorsSevere initial reaction, delayed epinephrine, unknown trigger

ManagementSame as initial anaphylaxis - epinephrine first
💡 Observation Period

After anaphylaxis treatment, observe patients for 4-6 hours minimum (longer for severe reactions). All patients should be discharged with epinephrine auto-injectors and referred to allergist.

📤 Post-Anaphylaxis Management

  • Prescribe epinephrine auto-injectors (at least 2) with demonstration
  • Anaphylaxis action plan in writing
  • Medical alert identification recommended
  • Allergist/immunologist referral for trigger identification and management
  • Consider tryptase - if baseline elevated, evaluate for mastocytosis
  • Education: Trigger avoidance, early recognition, when to use epinephrine
