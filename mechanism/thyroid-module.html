Thyroid Disorders

Understanding the HPT axis, interpreting thyroid labs, and the pathophysiology of hypo- and hyperthyroidism.

📚 8 Concepts ❓ 8 MCQs ⏱ ~35 min

🔄 The HPT Axis

The Feedback Loop

1
Hypothalamus releases TRH
2
Pituitary releases TSH
3
Thyroid releases T4 (mostly) and T3
4
Periphery converts T4 → T3 (active)
5
Negative feedback — T3/T4 suppress TRH and TSH

Key Points

  • T4 — Main secreted hormone; reservoir for T3 conversion
  • T3 — Active hormone (3-4x more potent)
  • TSH — Most sensitive marker; changes before T4/T3

💡 Clinical Pearl

TSH has a log-linear relationship with T4. Small T4 changes cause large TSH changes — that's why TSH is the best screening test.

🔬 Interpreting Thyroid Labs

TSH-First Approach

TSHFree T4Diagnosis
↑ High↓ LowPrimary hypothyroidism
↑ HighNormalSubclinical hypothyroidism
↓ Low↑ HighPrimary hyperthyroidism
↓ LowNormalSubclinical hyperthyroidism
↓ Low↓ LowCentral hypothyroidism

💡 Clinical Pearl

In primary disease, TSH and T4 move in opposite directions. If they move together, think central/pituitary disease.

🧠 Check Your Understanding

TSH 12 (high), free T4 0.6 (low). Diagnosis?

Primary hypothyroidism — thyroid failing, TSH rises in response
Secondary hypothyroidism — pituitary can't produce TSH
Subclinical hypothyroidism — T4 would be normal
TSH-secreting adenoma — would cause elevated T4

TSH 0.1 (low), free T4 0.5 (low). What to consider?

Primary hyperthyroidism — would have elevated T4
Central hypothyroidism — pituitary/hypothalamic dysfunction
Subclinical hyperthyroidism — T4 would be normal
Primary hypothyroidism — TSH would be elevated

❄️ Hypothyroidism

Causes

  • Hashimoto's — Most common; anti-TPO antibodies
  • Iodine deficiency — Most common worldwide
  • Post-ablative — After RAI or surgery
  • Medications — Amiodarone, lithium

Clinical Features: "Everything Slows Down"

  • Fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain
  • Dry skin, hair loss, constipation
  • Bradycardia, delayed reflexes
  • Menorrhagia, hyperlipidemia

⚠️ Myxedema Coma

Severe hypothyroidism + AMS + hypothermia = emergency. Treat with IV levothyroxine, stress-dose steroids, supportive care.

🔥 Hyperthyroidism

Causes

CauseMechanismRAIU
Graves'TSH receptor antibodies↑ High
Toxic MNGAutonomous nodules↑ Patchy
ThyroiditisRelease of preformed hormone↓ Low
ExogenousExcess levothyroxine↓ Low

Clinical Features: "Everything Speeds Up"

  • Weight loss, heat intolerance, sweating
  • Tachycardia, palpitations, AFib
  • Tremor, anxiety, hyperreflexia
  • Graves': proptosis, pretibial myxedema

💡 Clinical Pearl

RAIU scan distinguishes causes: HIGH uptake = overproduction (Graves', toxic nodules). LOW uptake = release or external source (thyroiditis, exogenous).

🧠 Check Your Understanding

A 35-year-old has hyperthyroidism with diffuse goiter and proptosis. Which antibody causes this?

Anti-TPO — indicates Hashimoto's
TSH receptor antibodies (TRAb) — stimulate thyroid in Graves'
Anti-thyroglobulin — marker of autoimmune thyroid disease
Anti-nuclear antibodies — systemic autoimmune diseases

Hyperthyroidism with tender thyroid and LOW radioiodine uptake. Diagnosis?

Graves' — would have high uptake
Toxic MNG — would have patchy uptake
Subacute thyroiditis — inflammation releases preformed hormone
Toxic adenoma — focal hot spot on scan

💊 Treatment

Hyperthyroidism Options

  • Thionamides (methimazole, PTU) — Block hormone synthesis
  • Radioactive iodine — Destroys thyroid; causes hypothyroidism
  • Surgery — For large goiter, cancer suspicion
  • Beta-blockers — Symptom control

⚠️ Thyroid Storm

Life-threatening: fever, tachycardia, AMS. Treat with PTU, beta-blockers, iodine (after thionamide), steroids.

📍 Thyroid Nodules

Approach

  • Very common (50% of adults on US)
  • Only 5-15% are malignant
  • First: Check TSH → Low TSH? Get RAIU scan
  • Hot nodule = almost never cancer

💡 Clinical Pearl

Hot nodules don't need FNA — they're autonomously functioning and essentially never malignant. Cold nodules need FNA based on size and US features.

🧠 Check Your Understanding

Patient on methimazole develops fever and sore throat. Most important action?

Stop methimazole and check CBC for agranulocytosis
Continue methimazole and treat with antibiotics
Switch to PTU for lower side effect risk
Increase beta-blocker dose

Thyroid nodule with suppressed TSH. RAIU shows "hot" nodule. Next step?

FNA to rule out malignancy
No FNA — hot nodules are almost never malignant
Surgical excision for cancer risk
Repeat ultrasound in 6 months

🔍 Special Situations

Pregnancy

  • Hypothyroidism: ↑ levothyroxine by ~25-30%
  • Hyperthyroidism: PTU in 1st trimester, methimazole after
  • RAI is contraindicated in pregnancy

Sick Euthyroid Syndrome

  • Pattern: Low T3, normal/low T4, normal/low TSH
  • Seen in critical illness
  • Treatment: Address underlying illness, NOT thyroid

🧠 Check Your Understanding
