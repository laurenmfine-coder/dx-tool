🔵 Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia

Anatomy, Pathophysiology & Medical/Surgical Management

📚 Overview

BPH is the non-malignant enlargement of the prostate gland, primarily in the transition zone, causing lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS) due to bladder outlet obstruction.

Epidemiology

AgeHistologic BPH Prevalence
40-49 years~25%
50-59 years~50%
60-69 years~65%
70-79 years~80%
80+ years>90%
Key Distinction: Histologic BPH is nearly universal with age, but only ~50% of men with histologic BPH develop significant LUTS.

🔬 Prostate Anatomy

Prostate Zones (McNeal)

Zone% of GlandClinical Significance
Transition Zone5-10%BPH originates here!
Peripheral Zone70%Where 70-80% of prostate CANCER arises
Central Zone25%Surrounds ejaculatory ducts; rare cancer site
Anterior Fibromuscular StromaVariableNo glandular tissue
Why DRE Misses BPH: The transition zone (where BPH occurs) is anterior and can't be palpated on DRE. DRE assesses the peripheral zone (where cancer occurs). A normal DRE doesn't rule out BPH!

🧬 Pathophysiology

Two-Component Model

LOWER URINARY TRACT SYMPTOMS IN BPH STATIC COMPONENT DYNAMIC COMPONENT (Mechanical obstruction) (Smooth muscle tone) ↓ ↓ Enlarged prostate tissue α1-adrenergic receptors compresses urethra in prostate/bladder neck ↓ ↓ Physical narrowing Increased smooth muscle of prostatic urethra contraction ↓ ↓ Target: 5α-reductase Target: α1-blockers inhibitors (shrink prostate) (relax smooth muscle)

Hormonal Basis

DHT (Dihydrotestosterone) is the primary androgen driving prostatic growth.
  • Testosterone → DHT via 5α-reductase (Type 2 in prostate)
  • DHT is 5x more potent than testosterone at androgen receptor
  • 5α-reductase inhibitors block this conversion → prostate shrinkage

🔍 Clinical Presentation (LUTS)

Storage (Irritative) Symptoms

  • Frequency
  • Urgency
  • Nocturia
  • Urge incontinence

Voiding (Obstructive) Symptoms

  • Weak stream
  • Hesitancy
  • Intermittency
  • Straining to void
  • Incomplete emptying
  • Post-void dribbling

IPSS (AUA Symptom Score)

International Prostate Symptom Score: 7 questions scored 0-5 each (+ 1 QOL question)
  • Mild: 0-7
  • Moderate: 8-19
  • Severe: 20-35
Use to assess severity and track treatment response.

⚠️ Red Flags (Not Typical BPH):
  • Hematuria → rule out bladder cancer
  • Pain → consider prostatitis, cancer
  • Rapid symptom progression → evaluate further
  • Neurologic symptoms → neurogenic bladder

💊 Medical Management

First-Line Agents

ClassDrugsMechanismOnsetSide Effects
α1-BlockersTamsulosin, Alfuzosin, Silodosin, Doxazosin, TerazosinRelax prostatic smooth muscle (dynamic component)Days to weeksOrthostatic hypotension, dizziness, retrograde ejaculation, IFIS
5α-Reductase InhibitorsFinasteride, DutasterideBlock DHT production → shrink prostate (static)3-6 months↓ Libido, ED, gynecomastia, ↓ PSA by 50%

Other Agents

ClassDrugsRole
PDE5 InhibitorsTadalafil 5mg dailyLUTS + ED; relaxes smooth muscle
AntimuscarinicsTolterodine, OxybutyninStorage symptoms (with caution in BOO)
β3-agonistsMirabegronStorage symptoms; safer in BOO
Combination Therapy: α-blocker + 5ARI (e.g., tamsulosin + dutasteride) for men with larger prostates (>40g) and moderate-severe symptoms. MTOPS/CombAT trials showed reduced progression.
⚠️ IFIS (Intraoperative Floppy Iris Syndrome): α-blockers (especially tamsulosin) can cause iris complications during cataract surgery. Inform ophthalmologist!

🔪 Surgical Management

Indications for Surgery

  • Refractory urinary retention
  • Recurrent UTIs due to BPH
  • Recurrent gross hematuria
  • Bladder stones
  • Renal insufficiency from obstruction
  • Failed medical therapy / patient preference

Surgical Options

ProcedureDescriptionProstate Size
TURPTransurethral resection - gold standard<80g
Laser (HoLEP, PVP)Holmium enucleation or GreenLight vaporizationAny size
Simple ProstatectomyOpen or robotic enucleation>80-100g
UroLiftMechanical lifting with implants<80g, no middle lobe
RezūmWater vapor thermal therapy30-80g
AquablationRobotic waterjet ablation30-150g

TURP Complications

  • TUR syndrome: Hyponatremia from irrigation fluid absorption (less common with bipolar)
  • Bleeding (may require transfusion)
  • Retrograde ejaculation (65-75%)
  • Urethral stricture
  • ED (5-10%)
  • Incontinence (rare, <1%)

📋 Key Clinical Pearls

Summary Points:
  • BPH arises from transition zone; cancer from peripheral zone
  • α-blockers work in days (dynamic); 5ARIs take months (static)
  • 5ARIs reduce PSA by ~50% - double measured PSA when interpreting
  • TURP remains gold standard; newer minimally invasive options available
  • Always rule out cancer with PSA and DRE before attributing symptoms to BPH

🧠 Quick Quiz

A 65-year-old man on tamsulosin for BPH is scheduled for cataract surgery. What should you inform the ophthalmologist about?

A. Increased bleeding risk
B. Risk of intraoperative floppy iris syndrome (IFIS)
C. Need to discontinue tamsulosin 2 weeks before
D. No concerns
Correct: B
α1-blockers, especially tamsulosin, are associated with IFIS - a condition where the iris becomes floppy and can prolapse during cataract surgery. The ophthalmologist should be informed so they can take precautions. Stopping the medication doesn't reliably prevent IFIS.
