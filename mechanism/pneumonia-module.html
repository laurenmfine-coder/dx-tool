🫁 Pneumonia

Pneumonia Classification

CAP

Community-Acquired
Acquired outside healthcare setting or <48h after admission

HAP

Hospital-Acquired
≥48h after hospital admission, not incubating at admission

VAP

Ventilator-Associated
≥48h after endotracheal intubation

CAP: Common Organisms

Typical Bacteria
Strep. pneumoniae (#1)
Most common overall. Lobar pneumonia, rusty sputum. Gram+ diplococci.
H. influenzae
COPD patients. Gram- coccobacilli.
Moraxella catarrhalis
COPD, elderly.
Staph. aureus
Post-influenza, IVDU, cavitary. Consider MRSA.
Klebsiella pneumoniae
Alcoholics, diabetics. "Currant jelly" sputum, cavitation, bulging fissure.
Atypical Organisms
Mycoplasma pneumoniae
Young adults, schools, military. "Walking pneumonia." Can cause cold agglutinins, bullous myringitis.
Chlamydophila pneumoniae
Elderly. Biphasic illness (pharyngitis → pneumonia).
Legionella pneumophila
Water sources (cooling towers, hot tubs). GI symptoms, hyponatremia, elevated LFTs. Urine antigen.
Respiratory Viruses
Influenza, RSV, COVID-19, adenovirus. Often atypical CXR (interstitial).
Atypicals: No cell wall → Don't Gram stain, resistant to β-lactams. Need macrolides, fluoroquinolones, or doxycycline.

💊 CAP Treatment

Outpatient (No Comorbidities)

Option 1: Amoxicillin 1g TID × 5 days
Option 2: Doxycycline 100mg BID × 5 days
Option 3: Macrolide (azithromycin) - only if local resistance <25%

Outpatient (With Comorbidities: COPD, DM, CKD, CHF, alcoholism)

Option 1: Respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin or moxifloxacin) monotherapy
Option 2: β-lactam (amox-clav, ceftriaxone) + Macrolide or Doxycycline

Inpatient (Non-ICU)

Option 1: Respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy
Option 2: β-lactam (ceftriaxone, cefotaxime, ampicillin-sulbactam) + Macrolide

Inpatient (ICU / Severe CAP)

Standard: β-lactam (ceftriaxone, cefotaxime, ampicillin-sulbactam) + Macrolide OR Fluoroquinolone
Add MRSA coverage (vanc or linezolid) if: Prior MRSA, recent hospitalization, severe/necrotizing
Add Pseudomonas coverage (pip-tazo, cefepime, meropenem) if: Structural lung disease, recent hospitalization/antibiotics

HAP/VAP: Different Bugs, Different Drugs

Common HAP/VAP Organisms

  • • MRSA
  • • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • • Acinetobacter
  • • ESBL-producing Enterobacteriaceae
  • • Klebsiella
  • • E. coli

Risk Factors for MDR

  • • IV antibiotics in past 90 days
  • • >5 days hospitalization before pneumonia
  • • Septic shock at time of VAP
  • • ARDS before VAP
  • • RRT before VAP
  • • Prior MDR organism isolated

Empiric HAP/VAP Treatment

Cover Gram-negatives including Pseudomonas:
Piperacillin-tazobactam, Cefepime, Ceftazidime, Meropenem, Imipenem, or Aztreonam
Add MRSA coverage if risk factors:
Vancomycin or Linezolid
Consider double Pseudomonas coverage if:
High MDR risk, septic shock, local resistance concerns
De-escalate based on cultures! Narrow spectrum ASAP.

Aspiration Pneumonia

Risk Factors

  • • Altered consciousness (stroke, seizure, sedation)
  • • Dysphagia
  • • GERD
  • • NG tube / PEG tube
  • • Poor dentition

Key Points

  • • Right lower lobe (if upright) or posterior upper lobe (if supine)
  • • Mouth anaerobes + Gram-negatives
  • • Can lead to abscess, empyema
Treatment: Ampicillin-sulbactam OR Clindamycin + Ceftriaxone. Cover anaerobes only if abscess/empyema or severe periodontal disease.

Special Situations

PJP (Pneumocystis jirovecii)
HIV (CD4 <200), immunosuppressed. Subacute, hypoxia out of proportion. CXR: bilateral interstitial/GGO. ↑LDH. Dx: BAL with silver stain.
Tx: TMP-SMX + steroids if severe
Influenza Pneumonia
During flu season. Rapid decline, ARDS. Can have secondary bacterial (S. aureus, Strep).
Tx: Oseltamivir ± antibacterials
Lung Abscess
Cavitary lesion with air-fluid level. Usually aspiration (anaerobes). Foul-smelling sputum.
Tx: Prolonged antibiotics (6-8 weeks), drainage if large/non-responsive
Empyema
Pus in pleural space. Parapneumonic effusion that becomes infected. pH <7.2, glucose <60, LDH >1000.
Tx: Chest tube drainage + antibiotics ± surgery
