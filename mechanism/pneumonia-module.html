Pneumonia & Respiratory Infections

Master pneumonia classification, pathogen patterns, severity assessment, and evidence-based antibiotic selection. From outpatient CAP to ventilator-associated pneumonia.

📚 14 Concepts ❓ 10 MCQs ⏱ ~50 min

📋 Pneumonia Classification

Classification by Setting

  • CAP (Community-Acquired) — Acquired outside hospital; symptom onset before or within 48h of admission
  • HAP (Hospital-Acquired) — Onset ≥48 hours after hospital admission
  • VAP (Ventilator-Associated) — Onset ≥48 hours after endotracheal intubation
  • Aspiration Pneumonia — Following witnessed/suspected aspiration; often polymicrobial

Common CAP Pathogens

PathogenClinical Clues
S. pneumoniae#1 bacterial cause; lobar consolidation; rusty sputum; rapid onset
H. influenzaeCOPD patients; often follows viral URI
MycoplasmaYoung adults; walking pneumonia; bullous myringitis; dry cough
LegionellaGI symptoms; hyponatremia; CNS changes; contaminated water exposure
ChlamydophilaHoarseness; prolonged symptoms; pharyngitis preceding
InfluenzaSeasonal; myalgias; can have bacterial superinfection

💡 Clinical Pearl

"Atypical" pathogens (Mycoplasma, Legionella, Chlamydophila) account for ~15-25% of CAP. The term refers to organisms NOT covered by β-lactams alone. These require macrolide, fluoroquinolone, or doxycycline coverage.

📊 Severity Assessment

CURB-65 Score

CriterionPoints
Confusion (new onset)1
Urea >7 mmol/L (BUN >19 mg/dL)1
Respiratory rate ≥301
Blood pressure: SBP <90 or DBP ≤601
Age ≥651

Score: 0-1 = Outpatient; 2 = Consider admission; ≥3 = Inpatient (≥4 consider ICU)

🏥 ATS/IDSA Severe CAP Criteria (ICU)

Requires 1 major OR ≥3 minor criteria:

  • Major: Mechanical ventilation, Vasopressor-requiring septic shock
  • Minor: RR ≥30, PaO2/FiO2 ≤250, Multilobar infiltrates, Confusion, BUN ≥20, WBC <4K, Platelets <100K, Temp <36°C, Hypotension requiring aggressive fluids

💡 Clinical Pearl

The PSI (Pneumonia Severity Index) uses 20 variables for mortality prediction. CURB-65 is simpler and performs similarly. PSI may underestimate severity in young patients; CURB-65 may miss hypoxemia. Use clinical judgment alongside scores.

💊 CAP Antibiotic Selection

Outpatient CAP

Patient TypeFirst-LineAlternative
Healthy, no comorbiditiesAmoxicillin 1g TID × 5 daysDoxycycline 100mg BID or macrolide*
With comorbidities†Amox-clav or cephalosporin + macrolide/doxyRespiratory FQ monotherapy

*Macrolide only if local pneumococcal resistance <25%
†Comorbidities: chronic heart/lung/liver/renal disease, diabetes, alcoholism, malignancy

Inpatient CAP (Non-ICU)

  • Standard: β-lactam (ceftriaxone, cefotaxime, ampicillin-sulbactam) + macrolide (azithromycin)
  • Alternative: β-lactam + doxycycline, OR respiratory FQ monotherapy
  • Duration: Minimum 5 days; extend if slow to respond

Inpatient CAP (ICU)

  • Standard: β-lactam (ceftriaxone) + azithromycin OR β-lactam + respiratory FQ
  • Pseudomonas risk: Antipseudomonal β-lactam (piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, meropenem) + FQ or aminoglycoside
  • MRSA risk: Add vancomycin or linezolid

⚠️ MRSA/Pseudomonas Risk Factors

MRSA: Prior MRSA infection, recent hospitalization, hemodialysis, injection drug use, post-influenza.
Pseudomonas: Structural lung disease, bronchiectasis, severe COPD, prior Pseudomonas, frequent antibiotics.

🏥 Hospital-Acquired & Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

HAP/VAP Pathogen Profile

Healthcare-associated pathogens require broader coverage:

  • Gram-negatives: Pseudomonas, Klebsiella, Acinetobacter, Enterobacter, E. coli
  • MRSA: Especially after prolonged hospitalization, prior antibiotics
  • Local antibiogram critical: Resistance patterns vary by institution

HAP/VAP Treatment Principles

  • Empiric: Antipseudomonal β-lactam (pip-tazo, cefepime, meropenem) ± aminoglycoside/FQ
  • Add MRSA coverage if: prior MRSA, high unit prevalence (>20%), recent antibiotics, high severity
  • De-escalate: Once cultures return, narrow to targeted therapy
  • Duration: 7 days typically sufficient (shorter than historical 14-21 days)

🧠 MDR Risk Factors (HAP/VAP)

IV antibiotics within 90 days • 5+ days hospitalization before HAP • Septic shock at VAP onset • ARDS before VAP • RRT before VAP

💡 Clinical Pearl

Double antipseudomonal coverage (β-lactam + FQ or aminoglycoside) is recommended for ICU patients with HAP/VAP if: MDR risk factors present, structural lung disease, or locally high Pseudomonas resistance. De-escalate to monotherapy once sensitivities return.

🔬 Special Situations

Aspiration Pneumonia vs. Aspiration Pneumonitis

FeaturePneumonitis (Chemical)Pneumonia (Bacterial)
OnsetHours after aspirationDays after aspiration
CauseSterile gastric contentsColonized oropharyngeal material
TreatmentSupportive; antibiotics NOT indicatedAntibiotics targeting oral flora
PathogensN/AAnaerobes, S. pneumo, GNRs

Aspiration Pneumonia Treatment

  • Community-acquired aspiration: Amoxicillin-clavulanate or ampicillin-sulbactam
  • Hospital-acquired aspiration: Piperacillin-tazobactam or carbapenem
  • Lung abscess/empyema: Prolonged course (4-6 weeks); consider drainage

Immunocompromised Hosts

  • HIV (CD4 <200): PJP (Pneumocystis jirovecii) — diffuse bilateral infiltrates, elevated LDH, hypoxemia out of proportion to CXR
  • Neutropenia: Gram-negatives, fungi (Aspergillus with halo sign); antipseudomonal + antifungal if prolonged
  • Transplant: CMV, PJP, opportunistic molds; timing post-transplant matters

⚠️ When to Suspect PJP

HIV with CD4 <200 or immunosuppressed patients presenting with: subacute dyspnea, dry cough, hypoxemia disproportionate to CXR findings, bilateral interstitial infiltrates, elevated LDH. Diagnosis: induced sputum or BAL with DFA staining or PCR.

🔍 Diagnostics & Clinical Response

Diagnostic Studies

  • CXR: Confirms diagnosis; lobar vs. interstitial pattern may suggest etiology
  • Blood cultures: Obtain before antibiotics; positive in ~10% of CAP
  • Sputum culture: Quality specimen if possible; guides de-escalation
  • Procalcitonin: Helps distinguish bacterial (elevated) vs. viral; guides duration
  • Legionella/pneumococcal urinary antigens: Consider in severe CAP
  • Respiratory viral panel: During flu season; may avoid antibiotics if viral

Expected Clinical Response

  • Clinical improvement: 48-72 hours; fever may persist 3-4 days
  • CXR improvement: Lags behind clinical improvement by weeks
  • Switch to oral: When afebrile, tolerating PO, hemodynamically stable
  • Duration: Minimum 5 days for CAP; 7 days for HAP/VAP

Treatment Failure Considerations

If not improving after 72 hours:

  • Wrong pathogen: Resistant organism, atypical, fungal, viral, TB
  • Wrong diagnosis: PE, malignancy, ARDS, heart failure
  • Complication: Empyema, abscess, superinfection
  • Pharmacokinetic issues: Inadequate dosing, drug interactions

💡 Clinical Pearl

Procalcitonin-guided therapy can safely reduce antibiotic duration. In clinically stable patients, consider stopping antibiotics when procalcitonin <0.25 ng/mL or drops ≥80% from peak. This reduces antibiotic exposure without compromising outcomes.
