Potassium Disorders

Master the workup of hypo- and hyperkalemia, recognize life-threatening ECG changes, and understand the cellular mechanisms that make potassium so dangerous.

1 🔬 Potassium Physiology & Homeostasis
📊 Normal Potassium Distribution

Total body potassium is approximately 50 mEq/kg (about 3500 mEq in a 70 kg adult):

  • 98% intracellular (primarily in muscle, concentration ~140 mEq/L)
  • 2% extracellular (serum K+ = 3.5-5.0 mEq/L)

This 30-40:1 gradient is maintained by the Na+/K+-ATPase pump, which moves 3 Na+ out and 2 K+ into cells. Even small shifts between compartments can dramatically change serum K+!

💡 Clinical Pearl
A serum K+ of 3.0 mEq/L typically represents a 200-400 mEq total body deficit. Don't be fooled by the small numerical change—the intracellular reservoir is massive.
⚖️ Potassium Balance: Input vs Output

Daily intake: ~40-100 mEq/day from diet

Excretion:

  • Kidney (90%): Primary regulator. K+ freely filtered at glomerulus, most reabsorbed in PCT/loop, secreted in collecting duct under aldosterone control.
  • GI tract (10%): Normally minor, but colonic secretion upregulates in CKD ("gut adaptation").
  • Sweat: Usually negligible except in extreme exercise/heat.
🔄 Transcellular Shifts: Acute K+ Changes

These cause rapid changes in serum K+ without affecting total body stores:

K+ INTO Cells (↓ Serum K+) K+ OUT OF Cells (↑ Serum K+)
Insulin Insulin deficiency (DKA)
β2-agonists (albuterol) β-blockers
Alkalosis Acidosis (mineral > organic)
Catecholamine surge Hyperosmolality (glucose drags water & K+)
Hypothermia (rewarming → K+ drops!) Cell lysis (rhabdo, tumor lysis, hemolysis)
⚠️ DKA Potassium Trap
Patients in DKA often present with high or normal serum K+ despite severe total body depletion. Insulin deficiency + acidosis shift K+ out of cells. Once insulin is given and acidosis corrects, K+ plummets. Always check K+ before starting insulin and replace aggressively!
2 ⬆️ Hyperkalemia: When K+ Is Too High
📋 Hyperkalemia Definition & Causes

Hyperkalemia: K+ > 5.0 mEq/L. Life-threatening when K+ > 6.5 or ECG changes present.

Organized by mechanism:

  • Increased intake: Rare as sole cause unless impaired excretion. Watch K+ supplements, salt substitutes, massive transfusion.
  • Decreased excretion: Most common!
    • Kidney failure (acute or chronic)
    • Hypoaldosteronism (Addison's, Type 4 RTA)
    • Medications: ACEi/ARBs, K+-sparing diuretics, TMP-SMX, NSAIDs, heparin
  • Transcellular shift: Acidosis, insulin deficiency, β-blockers, cell lysis (rhabdo, TLS, hemolysis), succinylcholine, digoxin toxicity
  • Pseudohyperkalemia: Hemolyzed sample, prolonged tourniquet, severe leukocytosis/thrombocytosis
💓 ECG Changes in Hyperkalemia (Progressive)

Hyperkalemia causes progressive conduction abnormalities. Memorize this sequence!

Stage 1: Peaked T Waves
K+ 5.5-6.5 mEq/L
Tall, narrow, symmetric T waves ("tenting"). Best seen in precordial leads. Often the earliest sign.
Stage 2: PR Prolongation & P Wave Flattening
K+ 6.5-7.5 mEq/L
P waves flatten and may disappear. PR interval lengthens. Atrial standstill may occur.
Stage 3: QRS Widening
K+ 7.0-8.0 mEq/L
Intraventricular conduction delay. QRS widens progressively. May see bundle branch block patterns.
Stage 4: Sine Wave → V-Fib/Asystole
K+ > 8.0 mEq/L
QRS merges with T wave creating "sine wave" pattern. Imminent risk of V-fib or asystole. EMERGENCY!
💡 Clinical Pearl
ECG changes don't always correlate with K+ level—chronic hyperkalemia may have minimal ECG changes while acute rises at lower levels can cause severe conduction abnormalities. Treat the ECG, not just the number!
🚀 Emergency Hyperkalemia Management
1
Stabilize the Membrane (Immediate)
Calcium gluconate 1-2g IV over 2-3 min. Onset: 1-3 min, duration ~30-60 min. Does NOT lower K+ but prevents arrhythmias. Repeat if ECG doesn't improve. Use calcium chloride (3x more elemental Ca) if central line available.
2
Shift K+ Into Cells
Regular insulin 10 units IV + D50 25g (unless glucose >250). Onset: 15-30 min, lasts 4-6 hrs, drops K+ 0.5-1.5 mEq/L. Also: albuterol 10-20 mg nebulized (drops K+ 0.5-1.0). Sodium bicarbonate only if significant acidosis.
3
Remove K+ From Body
Loop diuretics (furosemide 40-80 mg IV) if adequate urine output. Kayexalate/patiromer/SZC for GI elimination (hours to work). Hemodialysis for severe/refractory cases or renal failure.
Hyperkalemia Treatment Order
C-BIG-K-D
C Calcium
B Bicarb (if acidotic)
I Insulin + Glucose
G β-aGonist
K Kayexalate (binders)
D Dialysis
3 ⬇️ Hypokalemia: When K+ Is Too Low
📋 Hypokalemia Definition & Causes

Hypokalemia: K+ < 3.5 mEq/L. Severe when K+ < 2.5 mEq/L.

Organized by mechanism:

  • Decreased intake: Anorexia, severe malnutrition (usually contributing, not sole cause)
  • GI losses: Vomiting (actually more metabolic alkalosis → renal wasting), diarrhea (direct K+ loss), laxative abuse, villous adenoma
  • Renal losses:
    • Diuretics (loop, thiazide)
    • Hyperaldosteronism (primary or secondary)
    • Hypomagnesemia (impairs K+ reabsorption)
    • RTA Type 1 and 2
    • Bartter and Gitelman syndromes
  • Transcellular shift: Insulin, β-agonists, alkalosis, refeeding syndrome, hypokalemic periodic paralysis
Urine K+ Helps Identify the Cause
Urine K+ > 20 mEq/L = Renal Wasting
Urine K+ < 20 mEq/L = Extrarenal Loss
Check spot urine K+ or 24-hour collection. TTKG (transtubular K gradient) can also help but requires specific conditions.
💓 ECG Changes in Hypokalemia

Hypokalemia prolongs repolarization:

  • T wave flattening/inversion
  • U waves (small positive deflection after T wave)
  • ST depression
  • QT prolongation (actually QU prolongation)
  • Increased risk: Premature ventricular contractions, torsades de pointes, V-fib (especially with digoxin!)
⚠️ Digoxin + Hypokalemia = Disaster
Hypokalemia potentiates digoxin toxicity! Both compete for the Na+/K+-ATPase. Low K+ enhances digoxin binding → increased risk of fatal arrhythmias. Always maintain K+ > 4.0 in patients on digoxin.
💊 Hypokalemia Treatment

Replacement principles:

  • Oral preferred when possible (KCl 40-80 mEq/day in divided doses)
  • IV for severe (K+ < 2.5 or symptomatic/ECG changes):
    • Max rate: 10-20 mEq/hr through peripheral IV
    • Up to 40 mEq/hr through central line with cardiac monitoring
    • Concentration: Max 40 mEq/L peripheral, up to 80 mEq/L central
  • Check and replace magnesium first! Hypomagnesemia causes refractory hypokalemia.
  • Estimate: For each 1 mEq/L decrease in serum K+, total body deficit is 200-400 mEq
💡 The Magnesium Connection
Up to 50% of hypokalemic patients are also hypomagnesemic. Low Mg2+ keeps ROMK channels open in the collecting duct, causing persistent K+ wasting. If K+ won't correct despite replacement, check Mg2+ and replete to >2.0 mg/dL!
4 🎯 Special Clinical Scenarios
🧂 Hypokalemia + Hypertension: Think Aldosterone

This combination suggests mineralocorticoid excess:

  • Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's syndrome, bilateral adrenal hyperplasia) - most common cause of secondary HTN
  • Renovascular hypertension (activates RAAS)
  • Cushing syndrome (cortisol has mineralocorticoid activity)
  • Liddle syndrome (constitutively active ENaC)
  • Licorice ingestion (inhibits 11β-HSD2 → cortisol acts on MR)

Workup: Check aldosterone and renin levels. High aldo + low renin = primary hyperaldosteronism.

💧 Hypokalemia + Metabolic Alkalosis

These two often travel together:

  • Vomiting: Loss of H+ → alkalosis. Volume depletion activates RAAS → Na+ retention at expense of K+. Also, alkalosis itself causes K+ shift into cells.
  • Diuretics: Volume contraction + direct K+ wasting.
  • The chloride story: Low Cl- from vomiting means the kidney can't excrete HCO3- properly (needs Cl- for electroneutrality). Urine Cl- helps: <20 = chloride-responsive (give saline), >20 = chloride-resistant (think hyperaldosteronism).
🔥 Rhabdomyolysis & Potassium

Muscle breakdown releases massive intracellular K+ stores:

  • Can cause severe hyperkalemia within hours
  • Risk factors: trauma, crush injury, prolonged immobilization, statins, extreme exercise
  • Also releases myoglobin (causes AKI) and phosphorus (binds calcium)
  • Treatment: Aggressive IV fluids (200-300 mL/hr), treat hyperkalemia, may need dialysis

Knowledge Check: Potassium Disorders
Test your understanding of K+ physiology and management
Question 1 of 12
A 58-year-old man with CKD stage 4 presents with weakness. K+ is 7.2 mEq/L. ECG shows peaked T waves and QRS widening. What is the FIRST intervention?
ARegular insulin 10 units IV + D50
BCalcium gluconate 1-2g IV
CEmergent hemodialysis
DSodium polystyrene sulfonate 30g
✓ Correct!
Calcium gluconate is always first when ECG changes are present. It stabilizes the cardiac membrane within 1-3 minutes, reducing arrhythmia risk. It does NOT lower K+ but buys time for other interventions. Insulin/glucose shifts K+ into cells (takes 15-30 min). Dialysis removes K+ but takes time to arrange. Kayexalate works over hours.
Question 2 of 12
A patient in DKA presents with glucose 650 mg/dL, pH 7.15, and K+ 5.8 mEq/L.
What is the BEST interpretation of this potassium level?
ATrue hyperkalemia requiring immediate treatment before insulin
BNormal potassium, no concerns
CApparent hyperkalemia masking total body potassium depletion
DPseudohyperkalemia from hemolyzed sample
✓ Correct!
In DKA, serum K+ is often elevated despite severe total body depletion. Insulin deficiency prevents K+ entry into cells, acidosis shifts K+ out, and hyperosmolality causes solvent drag. Once you give insulin and correct acidosis, K+ will plummet. This is why we check K+ before starting insulin and add K+ to IV fluids once K+ < 5.2 mEq/L—to prevent life-threatening hypokalemia!
Question 3 of 12
Which ECG finding is the EARLIEST sign of hyperkalemia?
APeaked T waves
BQRS widening
CSine wave pattern
DAbsent P waves
✓ Correct!
Peaked T waves (tall, narrow, symmetric) appear first, typically at K+ 5.5-6.5 mEq/L. The progression is: Peaked T → PR prolongation/P wave flattening → QRS widening → Sine wave → V-fib/asystole. Recognizing peaked T waves early allows intervention before more dangerous conduction abnormalities develop.
Question 4 of 12
A patient has persistent hypokalemia (K+ 2.8 mEq/L) despite aggressive oral and IV potassium replacement over 3 days. What should you check next?
ASerum phosphorus
BSerum calcium
CSerum sodium
DSerum magnesium
✓ Correct!
Hypomagnesemia causes refractory hypokalemia! Low Mg2+ keeps ROMK channels in the collecting duct constitutively open, leading to ongoing K+ wasting. Up to 50% of hypokalemic patients are also Mg-depleted. Always check and replete Mg2+ to >2.0 mg/dL when K+ won't correct despite adequate replacement.
Question 5 of 12
A 45-year-old woman has hypertension (BP 165/98) and K+ 2.9 mEq/L. She is not on diuretics. What is the most likely diagnosis?
AEssential hypertension with poor diet
BPrimary hyperaldosteronism
CChronic kidney disease
DPheochromocytoma
✓ Correct!
The combination of hypertension + spontaneous hypokalemia (not on diuretics) is classic for primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's syndrome or bilateral adrenal hyperplasia). Aldosterone causes Na+ retention (→ HTN) and K+ wasting. This is the most common cause of secondary hypertension! Workup: aldosterone/renin ratio. If aldo is high and renin is suppressed, consider adrenal CT and confirmatory testing.
Question 6 of 12
Which medication does NOT typically cause hyperkalemia?
ALisinopril
BSpironolactone
CFurosemide
DTrimethoprim
✓ Correct!
Furosemide (a loop diuretic) causes hypoKalemia, not hyperkalemia—it increases K+ excretion in the thick ascending limb. The others all cause hyperkalemia: ACE inhibitors (lisinopril) reduce aldosterone; spironolactone blocks aldosterone receptor; trimethoprim blocks ENaC (similar to amiloride). These are important to remember when managing patients with CKD or on multiple medications.
Question 7 of 12
A patient receiving chemotherapy for lymphoma develops oliguria, K+ 7.0 mEq/L, phosphorus 8.5 mg/dL, calcium 6.2 mg/dL, and uric acid 14 mg/dL.
What is the most likely cause of hyperkalemia?
ATumor lysis syndrome
BChemotherapy-induced nephrotoxicity
CAdrenal insufficiency from metastases
DType 4 RTA
✓ Correct!
This is classic tumor lysis syndrome (TLS): hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia, and hyperuricemia from rapid cell death. The phosphorus binds calcium (→ hypocalcemia). Uric acid and Ca-phos crystals cause AKI (→ oliguria worsens all electrolytes). TLS is most common with hematologic malignancies (leukemia, lymphoma) during initial chemotherapy. Prevention: aggressive hydration, rasburicase, allopurinol.
Question 8 of 12
What percentage of total body potassium is located intracellularly?
A50%
B75%
C90%
D98%
✓ Correct!
98% of total body K+ is intracellular (primarily in muscle), with only 2% in the extracellular space. This is maintained by the Na+/K+-ATPase pump. This massive gradient explains why small transcellular shifts can dramatically change serum K+, and why a serum K+ of 3.0 represents a huge total body deficit (200-400 mEq).
Question 9 of 12
A patient on digoxin develops K+ of 2.8 mEq/L. What is the PRIMARY concern?
AReduced digoxin efficacy
BIncreased digoxin toxicity and arrhythmia risk
CDigoxin-induced hyperkalemia
DNeed to increase digoxin dose
✓ Correct!
Hypokalemia potentiates digoxin toxicity! Both K+ and digoxin compete for binding sites on the Na+/K+-ATPase. Low K+ enhances digoxin's effect, increasing risk of fatal arrhythmias (especially ventricular). Always maintain K+ >4.0 mEq/L in patients on digoxin. Conversely, digoxin overdose can cause hyperkalemia by poisoning the pump.
Question 10 of 12
A patient with severe hypokalemia (K+ 2.2 mEq/L) needs IV replacement. What is the maximum safe rate through a peripheral IV?
A5 mEq/hour
B10 mEq/hour
C10-20 mEq/hour
D40 mEq/hour
✓ Correct!
Through a peripheral IV, maximum rate is 10-20 mEq/hour with concentration ≤40 mEq/L. Higher concentrations cause pain and phlebitis. Through a central line with cardiac monitoring, you can give up to 40 mEq/hour at higher concentrations (up to 80 mEq/L). Always use cardiac monitoring when infusing K+ at rates >10 mEq/hour due to arrhythmia risk.
Question 11 of 12
Which ECG finding is characteristic of hypokalemia?
AU waves
BPeaked T waves
CShortened QT interval
DDelta waves
✓ Correct!
Hypokalemia causes: T wave flattening, U waves (small positive deflection after T wave), ST depression, and QT prolongation. These reflect delayed repolarization. Peaked T waves are seen in hyperkalemia. Delta waves indicate WPW syndrome. Hypokalemia prolongs (not shortens) the QT interval, increasing risk of torsades de pointes.
Question 12 of 12
A patient has vomiting for 3 days and presents with K+ 2.6 mEq/L and metabolic alkalosis (pH 7.52, HCO3 38).
What is the mechanism of hypokalemia in this patient?
ADirect K+ loss in vomit
BDecreased K+ intake
CTranscellular shift from acidosis
DRenal K+ wasting from volume depletion and alkalosis
✓ Correct!
Gastric fluid actually contains little K+ (~10 mEq/L). The hypokalemia from vomiting is primarily due to renal K+ wasting: (1) Volume depletion activates RAAS → aldosterone causes Na+ retention at expense of K+; (2) Alkalosis shifts K+ into cells AND increases renal K+ secretion; (3) Bicarbonaturia (to compensate) obligates K+ loss. Treatment: Give normal saline (volume and chloride) along with K+ replacement.
