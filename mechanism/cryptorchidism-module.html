⬇️ Cryptorchidism

Undescended Testis: Diagnosis, Timing & Management

📚 Overview

Cryptorchidism (from Greek "hidden testis") refers to a testis that has not descended into the scrotum. It is the most common genital anomaly in male newborns.

Epidemiology

ParameterValue
Full-term newborns3-4%
Preterm newbornsUp to 30%
At 1 year of age~1% (spontaneous descent occurs)
Unilateral~90%
Bilateral~10%
Right sideMore common (descends later)
Spontaneous Descent: Most testes that will descend do so by 3-6 months of age. After 6 months, spontaneous descent is rare. Don't wait beyond this point!

🧬 Embryology of Testicular Descent

Two-Phase Descent

PHASE 1: TRANSABDOMINAL (Weeks 10-15) ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ Testis moves from posterior abdomen → internal inguinal ring Controlled by: INSL3 (Insulin-like 3) + MIS (from Leydig and Sertoli cells) Gubernaculum swells, anchors testis near inguinal region PHASE 2: INGUINOSCROTAL (Weeks 25-35) ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ Testis passes through inguinal canal → scrotum Controlled by: ANDROGENS (testosterone/DHT) + Genitofemoral nerve (CGRP) Gubernaculum guides descent Processus vaginalis precedes testis

🔍 Classification & Examination

Types of Undescended Testis

TypeDefinitionPalpable?
True UDTTestis along normal descent pathway but hasn't reached scrotumUsually yes
Ectopic testisTestis outside normal pathway (perineal, femoral, etc.)Yes, but abnormal location
Retractile testisCan be manipulated to scrotum, cremasteric reflex pulls it upYes - can bring to scrotum
NonpalpableIntra-abdominal, atrophic, or absentNo

Locations Along Descent Path

  • Intra-abdominal (above internal ring)
  • Inguinal canal (canalicular)
  • External ring / high scrotal
  • Prescrotal

Physical Examination Technique

Keys to Accurate Exam:
  • Warm room, warm hands
  • Relax cremasteric reflex (frog-leg position, warm bath)
  • Two-handed technique: One hand blocks inguinal canal, other milks down
  • Can it reach the scrotum without tension?
  • Does it stay in scrotum when released?

Retractile vs True UDT

FeatureRetractileTrue UDT
Can bring to scrotumYes, easilyNo, or with tension only
Stays in scrotumTemporarily, then retractsNever reaches or immediately retracts
Cremasteric reflexHyperactiveN/A
ManagementObservation, annual examsSurgical intervention

⚠️ Why It Matters: Consequences

Untreated cryptorchidism has significant consequences:

1. Infertility Risk

  • Elevated temperature impairs spermatogenesis
  • Germ cell degeneration begins by 6-12 months of age
  • Bilateral UDT: 50-70% infertility if untreated
  • Unilateral UDT: Still reduced fertility potential
  • Early orchiopexy improves but may not normalize fertility

2. Malignancy Risk

  • 5-10x increased risk of testicular cancer (vs general population)
  • Risk persists even after orchiopexy
  • Seminoma most common tumor type
  • Orchiopexy allows for easier self-examination/detection
  • Bilateral UDT: Higher risk than unilateral
  • Intra-abdominal testis: Highest risk

3. Testicular Torsion

  • Increased risk due to abnormal fixation
  • More difficult to diagnose (nonpalpable location)

4. Associated Inguinal Hernia

  • 90% have patent processus vaginalis
  • Often repaired at time of orchiopexy

5. Psychological Impact

  • Body image concerns
  • Earlier repair preferred

🔬 Diagnostic Workup

Palpable Testis

No imaging needed! Proceed directly to surgical planning.

Nonpalpable Testis

ModalityUtility
UltrasoundLimited - can't reliably see intra-abdominal testis
MRIBetter but not definitive; not routinely recommended
Diagnostic laparoscopyGOLD STANDARD for nonpalpable testis - diagnostic AND therapeutic

Bilateral Nonpalpable Testes: Special Workup

⚠️ Must rule out Disorders of Sex Development (DSD)!
  • Karyotype (46,XY? 46,XX with CAH?)
  • Hormonal evaluation: LH, FSH, testosterone, AMH, inhibin B
  • hCG stimulation test (does testosterone rise?)
  • If NO testosterone response → anorchia (absent testes)

🔪 Management

Timing of Intervention

AUA/AAP Recommendation: Orchiopexy by 6-12 months of age
  • After 6 months, spontaneous descent unlikely
  • Germ cell deterioration begins early
  • Earlier repair = better fertility potential
  • Should be completed by 18 months at latest

Hormonal Therapy

hCG or GnRH therapy: Generally NOT recommended in US/Europe
  • Low success rates (10-20%)
  • High relapse rates
  • May be considered in select cases (bilateral, near-scrotal position)

Surgical Options

ScenarioProcedure
Palpable testisInguinal or scrotal orchiopexy
Nonpalpable testisDiagnostic laparoscopy first
Intra-abdominal testisFowler-Stephens (1 or 2 stage) or standard laparoscopic orchiopexy
Atrophic/nubbin testisOrchiectomy (remove remnant)
Absent testis (vanishing)Confirm with laparoscopy; no further surgery needed

Fowler-Stephens Procedure

For high intra-abdominal testes where spermatic vessels are too short:
  • Divide spermatic vessels (testis relies on collateral blood supply from vas deferens)
  • Can be done in 1 or 2 stages
  • 2-stage: Clip vessels first, orchiopexy 6 months later (allows collaterals to develop)
  • ~90% testicular survival rate

📈 Follow-Up & Prognosis

Post-Orchiopexy Follow-Up

  • Confirm testis remains in scrotum
  • Monitor for atrophy
  • Counsel on testicular self-examination (lifelong)
  • Discuss fertility implications at appropriate age

📋 Key Clinical Pearls

⚠️ Red Flags Requiring DSD Workup:
  • Bilateral nonpalpable testes
  • Unilateral nonpalpable + hypospadias
  • Any genital ambiguity
Retractile Testis Management:
  • Annual follow-up until puberty
  • 10-30% may "ascend" and become truly undescended
  • If ascends → treat as UDT
Summary:
  • Most common male genital anomaly
  • Surgery by 6-12 months (no later than 18 months)
  • Cancer risk persists after repair → teach self-exam
  • Nonpalpable → laparoscopy (gold standard)

🧠 Quick Quiz

At what age should orchiopexy ideally be performed?

A. Immediately at birth
B. 6-12 months of age
C. At school age (5-6 years)
D. After puberty
Correct: B - 6-12 months
Current guidelines recommend orchiopexy between 6-12 months (completed by 18 months at latest). This timing optimizes fertility potential, as germ cell degeneration begins early. Waiting allows time for spontaneous descent (which is rare after 6 months).
