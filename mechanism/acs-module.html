Acute Coronary Syndrome

Understanding the pathophysiology of plaque rupture, thrombosis, and myocardial injury — from stable angina to STEMI.

📚 12 Concepts ❓ 8 MCQs ⏱ ~45 min 🎯 High Yield

🫀 Coronary Anatomy Foundation

The Coronary Circulation

The heart receives its blood supply from the left and right coronary arteries, which arise from the aortic root just above the aortic valve cusps. Understanding coronary anatomy is essential for predicting which myocardial territories are affected in ACS.

  • Left Main (LM) → bifurcates into LAD and LCx
  • Left Anterior Descending (LAD) → supplies anterior wall, anterior septum, and apex
  • Left Circumflex (LCx) → supplies lateral and posterior walls
  • Right Coronary Artery (RCA) → supplies inferior wall, RV, and (in 70%) the AV node

💡 Clinical Pearl

The LAD is sometimes called "the widow maker" because proximal LAD occlusion affects such a large territory of myocardium that it frequently causes sudden cardiac death or severe heart failure.

Coronary Dominance

Coronary "dominance" refers to which artery supplies the posterior descending artery (PDA) and thus the inferior wall:

  • Right dominant (70%) — RCA gives off the PDA
  • Left dominant (10%) — LCx gives off the PDA
  • Co-dominant (20%) — Both contribute to posterior supply

In right-dominant systems, RCA occlusion causes inferior MI and may affect the AV node, leading to heart block.

🔬 Atherosclerosis: The Underlying Disease

Plaque Formation: A Decades-Long Process

Atherosclerosis begins with endothelial dysfunction and lipid accumulation, progressing through several stages over years to decades:

  • Fatty streak — Earliest lesion; lipid-laden macrophages (foam cells) in intima
  • Fibroatheroma — Lipid core covered by fibrous cap; stable but flow-limiting
  • Vulnerable plaque — Thin fibrous cap, large lipid core, inflammatory cells
  • Ruptured plaque — Cap disruption exposing thrombogenic core

🔄 Atherogenesis Pathway

1
Endothelial injury from HTN, smoking, diabetes, hyperlipidemia
2
LDL particles enter intima and become oxidized (ox-LDL)
3
Monocytes recruited → differentiate into macrophages → engulf ox-LDL → foam cells
4
Smooth muscle cells migrate and secrete collagen, forming fibrous cap
5
Ongoing inflammation weakens cap; plaque becomes "vulnerable"

What Makes a Plaque "Vulnerable"?

Not all plaques are equally dangerous. Vulnerable plaques prone to rupture have these characteristics:

  • Thin fibrous cap (<65 μm)
  • Large necrotic lipid core (>40% of plaque volume)
  • High macrophage density with active inflammation
  • Decreased smooth muscle cell content
  • Neovascularization within plaque (fragile vessels)

Importantly, vulnerable plaques often cause <50% stenosis, meaning they may not cause symptoms until rupture.

💡 Clinical Pearl

Most MIs occur at sites of <50% stenosis. The degree of stenosis does not predict rupture risk — plaque composition matters more than plaque size.

💥 Plaque Rupture and Thrombosis

The Acute Event: From Rupture to Occlusion

When a vulnerable plaque ruptures, the highly thrombogenic lipid core is exposed to flowing blood, triggering rapid thrombus formation:

  • Tissue factor exposure — Released from necrotic core and macrophages
  • Platelet adhesion — Platelets bind to exposed collagen via glycoprotein receptors
  • Platelet activation — Release of ADP, thromboxane A2 → recruits more platelets
  • Coagulation cascade — Tissue factor activates extrinsic pathway → thrombin generation
  • Fibrin formation — Stabilizes platelet plug into mature thrombus

⚠️ Critical Concept: Time = Myocardium

Once coronary occlusion occurs, myocardial necrosis begins within 20-40 minutes and progresses as a "wavefront" from endocardium to epicardium. After 6 hours of complete occlusion, most of the at-risk myocardium is irreversibly injured.

Mechanisms Beyond Plaque Rupture

While rupture is most common, other mechanisms can precipitate ACS:

  • Plaque erosion (~30%) — Endothelial denudation without frank rupture; more common in women and younger patients
  • Calcified nodule (~5%) — Calcified plaque protrudes through fibrous cap
  • Coronary spasm — Vasospasm can cause ischemia even without fixed stenosis (Prinzmetal angina)
  • Supply-demand mismatch — Type 2 MI from tachycardia, hypotension, severe anemia

📊 The ACS Spectrum

Understanding the Continuum

ACS represents a spectrum based on the degree and duration of coronary occlusion:

Condition Coronary Status ECG Finding Troponin
Unstable Angina Partial/transient occlusion ST depression, T-wave changes, or normal Negative
NSTEMI Partial occlusion or complete with collaterals ST depression, T-wave inversion, or normal Elevated
STEMI Complete occlusion ST elevation in contiguous leads Elevated

Why Does STEMI Cause ST Elevation?

Transmural ischemia (affecting the full thickness of the myocardial wall) creates an "injury current" that manifests as ST elevation:

  • Ischemic cells can't maintain normal resting membrane potential
  • Voltage gradient develops between injured and healthy tissue
  • This creates a current of injury that shifts the ST segment upward in leads facing the injured area
  • Reciprocal ST depression appears in leads opposite the injury

💡 Clinical Pearl

NSTEMI can sometimes represent more severe disease than STEMI. A patient with 3-vessel disease and global subendocardial ischemia may have diffuse ST depression without elevation — yet be at very high risk.

🧪 Cardiac Biomarkers

Troponin: The Gold Standard

Cardiac troponins (I and T) are structural proteins of the contractile apparatus that are released when myocytes are injured:

  • High specificity — Cardiac isoforms are distinct from skeletal muscle
  • Detection timeline — Rises 2-4 hours after injury, peaks 12-24 hours, remains elevated 7-14 days
  • High-sensitivity assays — Can detect smaller infarcts earlier; use serial measurements

The 0/1 Hour and 0/3 Hour Algorithms

Modern high-sensitivity troponin assays allow rapid rule-in/rule-out protocols:

  • 0/1h protocol — Baseline + 1-hour troponin; can rule out MI if both very low
  • 0/3h protocol — Alternative if 0/1h not available
  • Rule-in — Very high initial value OR significant delta (rise/fall pattern)
  • Observation zone — Intermediate values require further testing

⚠️ Troponin Elevation ≠ ACS

Many conditions cause troponin elevation without atherosclerotic plaque rupture:

  • Heart failure, myocarditis, takotsubo cardiomyopathy
  • Pulmonary embolism, severe sepsis, renal failure
  • Cardioversion, ablation, cardiac surgery
  • Extreme exertion, rhabdomyolysis

Clinical context and ECG are essential — troponin alone cannot diagnose ACS.

📈 ECG Localization of Infarct

Matching ECG Leads to Coronary Territories

ST elevation in contiguous leads indicates the location of infarct and culprit artery:

ECG Leads Territory Likely Culprit Complications
V1-V4 Anterior/Septal LAD LV dysfunction, VT/VF, cardiogenic shock
V5-V6, I, aVL Lateral LCx or diagonal MR if papillary muscle involved
II, III, aVF Inferior RCA (or LCx) Bradycardia, heart block, RV infarct
V1-V2 (ST depression) Posterior RCA or LCx Often with inferior MI
V1, V4R Right Ventricle Proximal RCA Hypotension with nitrates, volume-dependent

💡 Clinical Pearl

Always get right-sided leads (V4R) in inferior STEMI! RV infarction occurs in ~30-50% of inferior MIs and drastically changes management — these patients are preload-dependent and may crash with nitrates or diuretics.

💊 Mechanistic Basis of Treatment

Antiplatelet Therapy

Since platelet aggregation is central to coronary thrombosis, antiplatelet agents are foundational:

  • Aspirin — Irreversibly inhibits COX-1, blocking thromboxane A2 synthesis
  • P2Y12 inhibitors (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, prasugrel) — Block ADP-mediated platelet activation
  • GP IIb/IIIa inhibitors — Block final common pathway of platelet aggregation

Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) with aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor is standard for ACS.

Anticoagulation

Targeting the coagulation cascade reduces thrombus propagation:

  • Unfractionated heparin — Activates antithrombin III; easily reversible
  • Low molecular weight heparin — More predictable pharmacokinetics
  • Fondaparinux — Selective factor Xa inhibitor; lower bleeding risk
  • Bivalirudin — Direct thrombin inhibitor; used in PCI

Reperfusion: Restoring Flow

The goal in STEMI is to restore coronary blood flow as quickly as possible:

  • Primary PCI — Preferred if available within 120 minutes of first medical contact
  • Fibrinolysis — Alternative if PCI not available within 120 min; most effective within 3 hours
  • Door-to-balloon time — Target <90 minutes for primary PCI
  • Door-to-needle time — Target <30 minutes for fibrinolysis

Secondary Prevention Targets

Long-term management targets the underlying atherosclerotic disease:

  • Statins — Stabilize plaques, reduce LDL, anti-inflammatory effects
  • Beta-blockers — Reduce myocardial oxygen demand, prevent remodeling
  • ACE inhibitors — Prevent adverse remodeling, especially with reduced EF
  • Lifestyle — Smoking cessation, diet, exercise, cardiac rehabilitation
