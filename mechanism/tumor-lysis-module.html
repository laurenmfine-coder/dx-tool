MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

Tumor lysis syndrome (TLS) occurs when rapid destruction of malignant cells releases intracellular contents into the bloodstream, overwhelming the kidneys' capacity to excrete them.

Core Metabolic Cascade

Released ContentsLab AbnormalityDownstream Consequence
Nucleic acids↑ Uric acid (xanthine oxidase converts purines)Uric acid crystallization in renal tubules → AKI
Potassium↑ HyperkalemiaCardiac arrhythmias (peaked T waves, VFib)
Phosphorus↑ HyperphosphatemiaBinds calcium → hypocalcemia; calcium-phosphate deposition in kidneys
Calcium binding↓ Hypocalcemia (secondary)Tetany, seizures, QT prolongation
🔑 Memory aid: TLS = ↑K, ↑PO4, ↑uric acid, ↓Ca. Everything goes UP except calcium (which drops because phosphate binds it). The AKI is driven by both uric acid and calcium-phosphate crystal deposition.

Highest-Risk Malignancies

TLS risk correlates with tumor burden, proliferative rate, and sensitivity to therapy. Burkitt lymphoma carries the highest risk (large tumor burden + exquisitely chemosensitive). ALL (especially with WBC >100K) and DLBCL are also high risk. Solid tumors rarely cause TLS, but bulky small cell lung cancer is a notable exception.

CLINICAL
Diagnosis & Classification

Cairo-Bishop Classification

TypeDefinitionCriteria
Laboratory TLS≥2 metabolic abnormalities within 3 days before or 7 days after treatmentUric acid ≥8, K ≥6, PO4 ≥4.5 (adults), Ca ≤7 — OR 25% change from baseline
Clinical TLSLaboratory TLS PLUS end-organ damageCreatinine ≥1.5x ULN, cardiac arrhythmia, seizure, or death
⚠️ Timing: TLS can occur spontaneously before treatment in high-burden malignancies (especially Burkitt), but most commonly develops 12-72 hours after initiating chemotherapy. Monitor labs every 6-8 hours for the first 48-72 hours.

Lab Monitoring Protocol

For high-risk patients: BMP (K, Ca, PO4, Cr), uric acid, and LDH every 6-8 hours starting before treatment and continuing through 72 hours post-chemotherapy. LDH serves as an indirect marker of cell turnover.

TREATMENT
Prevention & Management

Prevention (KEY to Board Questions)

Risk LevelProphylaxisDetails
Low riskAggressive IV hydration + monitoringNS at 2-3 L/m²/day; monitor labs q12h
Intermediate riskIV hydration + allopurinolAllopurinol blocks xanthine oxidase → prevents NEW uric acid formation
High riskIV hydration + rasburicaseRasburicase converts EXISTING uric acid to allantoin (water-soluble)
🔑 Allopurinol vs Rasburicase: Allopurinol PREVENTS new uric acid formation but does not lower existing levels. Rasburicase BREAKS DOWN existing uric acid (works within hours). For established hyperuricemia or high-risk patients, rasburicase is preferred.
⚠️ Rasburicase contraindication: Absolutely contraindicated in G6PD deficiency — causes severe hemolytic anemia and methemoglobinemia. Must screen before administration. Also: blood samples must be placed on ice immediately (rasburicase continues to degrade uric acid ex vivo, giving falsely low readings).

Established TLS Management

Aggressive IV fluids (goal urine output >2 mL/kg/hr), rasburicase for hyperuricemia, cardiac monitoring for hyperkalemia (treat per standard protocol: calcium gluconate, insulin/glucose, kayexalate), phosphate binders for hyperphosphatemia, and avoid IV calcium unless symptomatic (risk of calcium-phosphate precipitation). Hemodialysis for refractory cases.
