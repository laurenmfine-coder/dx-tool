📖 Teaching Point

The cloaca is an endoderm-lined common chamber that receives the allantois, hindgut, and mesonephric ducts. During weeks 4-7, the urorectal septum grows caudally to divide it into the urogenital sinus (anterior → bladder, urethra) and the anorectal canal (posterior → rectum, upper anal canal). The perineal body forms where the septum meets the cloacal membrane. Incomplete septation → fistulas between rectum and urogenital tract.

📖 Teaching Point

VACTERL: Vertebral (hemivertebra, sacral agenesis), Anorectal (imperforate anus), Cardiac (VSD, ASD, TOF), Tracheo-Esophageal fistula, Renal (agenesis, horseshoe, reflux), Limb (radial ray defects, thumb anomalies). Need 3+ features for diagnosis. Each component should be screened when ANY single VACTERL anomaly is found. Sacral ratio <0.4 predicts poor continence outcomes.

📖 Teaching Point

Male ARM fistula spectrum (low to high): perineal → bulbar urethral → prostatic urethral → bladder neck (vesical). Female spectrum: perineal → vestibular → vaginal → persistent cloaca. Higher fistulas = worse continence prognosis because the rectum bypasses more of the sphincter complex. The puborectalis muscle (voluntary sphincter) is the most important determinant of continence.

📖 Teaching Point

PSARP (Peña procedure): The posterior sagittal approach splits muscles in the exact midline using electrical stimulation to identify the sphincter complex. The rectum is mobilized, the fistula is divided, and the rectum is placed within the center of the sphincter complex. The colostomy is later closed. Long-term continence depends on: 1) quality of the sphincter muscles (sacral development), 2) accurate placement within the muscle complex, 3) sacral innervation (S2-S4). Patients with sacral agenesis have worst outcomes.

📖 Teaching Point

Long-term ARM outcomes: Constipation > incontinence in frequency. Low malformations (perineal fistula) → >90% achieve voluntary continence. High malformations (prostatic/vesical fistula) → only 25-30% achieve full voluntary continence. Bowel management program (Peña protocol): daily enemas that empty the colon completely, keeping the child clean for 24 hours between enemas. Malone appendicostomy (MACE/ACE): allows antegrade enemas through a catheterizable channel in the appendix.
