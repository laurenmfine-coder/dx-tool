👤 Hemifacial Microsomia

Unilateral Underdevelopment of Craniofacial Structures | OMENS Classification

📚 Core Definition & Terminology

Hemifacial Microsomia (HFM) = Spectrum of unilateral (or asymmetric bilateral) underdevelopment of structures derived from the first and second pharyngeal arches

Also known as: Craniofacial microsomia, Oculo-auriculo-vertebral spectrum (OAVS), Goldenhar syndrome (with epibulbar dermoids)

Epidemiology:

  • Incidence: 1 in 3,500 - 5,600 live births
  • Second most common facial birth defect (after cleft lip/palate)
  • Male:Female = 3:2
  • Right side more commonly affected (60%)
  • Unilateral in 70%, bilateral asymmetric in 30%
  • Most cases sporadic (2% familial)

🧬 Pathophysiology

Proposed Mechanisms:

TheoryMechanism
Vascular DisruptionStapedial artery hemorrhage disrupts 1st/2nd arch development (most accepted theory)
Neural Crest DefectAbnormal migration or proliferation of cranial neural crest cells
Genetic FactorsRare familial cases, variable penetrance
TeratogenicThalidomide, retinoic acid exposure
Why Unilateral? The vascular disruption theory explains the characteristic unilateral involvement - hemorrhage from the stapedial artery affects the ipsilateral pharyngeal arches during critical development (weeks 4-8).

📋 OMENS Classification

OMENS = Standardized classification system for HFM severity

Orbit - Mandible - Ear - Nerve - Soft Tissue

O - ORBIT (0-3)

  • O0: Normal orbit
  • O1: Abnormal orbit size
  • O2: Abnormal orbit position
  • O3: Abnormal size AND position

May include: epibulbar dermoid (Goldenhar), coloboma, microphthalmos

M - MANDIBLE (0-3) - Pruzansky Classification

  • M0: Normal mandible
  • M1: Small mandible, normal shape (hypoplasia only)
  • M2a: Abnormal ramus, functional TMJ present
  • M2b: Abnormal ramus, non-functional TMJ
  • M3: Absent ramus, condyle, and TMJ

E - EAR (0-3)

  • E0: Normal ear
  • E1: Minor hypoplasia, all structures present
  • E2: Moderate hypoplasia (anotia with some structures)
  • E3: Severe hypoplasia (absent auricle, lobule remnant only)

Often associated with atretic ear canal, conductive hearing loss

N - NERVE (Facial Nerve, 0-3)

  • N0: No facial nerve involvement
  • N1: Upper face involvement only
  • N2: Lower face involvement only
  • N3: All branches affected

May have incomplete eye closure, asymmetric smile

S - SOFT TISSUE (0-3)

  • S0: No soft tissue deficiency
  • S1: Minimal deficiency
  • S2: Moderate deficiency
  • S3: Severe deficiency (subcutaneous tissue and muscle)

Includes muscles of mastication, parotid gland

🎯 Associated Anomalies

HFM is part of OAVS (Oculo-Auriculo-Vertebral Spectrum) - Always screen for associated anomalies!

Goldenhar Syndrome: HFM + Epibulbar dermoid + Vertebral anomalies. Represents the more severe end of the OAVS spectrum.

🔪 Surgical Management

Principle: Staged reconstruction addressing each component. Timing optimized for growth and function.

Surgical Timeline:

AgeProcedureGoal
0-6 monthsHearing aids / BAHAEarly hearing intervention
2-4 yearsMandibular distraction (if severe)Improve jaw symmetry
6-10 yearsMicrotia reconstruction (Rib graft)Create external ear
8-12 yearsCanaloplasty (if anatomically feasible)Hearing restoration
AdolescenceOrthognathic surgeryFinal jaw alignment
AdolescenceSoft tissue augmentationFacial symmetry (fat grafting, flaps)

Mandibular Options by Severity:

  • M1 (mild): Often observation, orthognathic surgery at maturity
  • M2a: Distraction osteogenesis, costochondral graft
  • M2b/M3: Costochondral rib graft to reconstruct TMJ and ramus

Ear Reconstruction:

  • Autologous rib cartilage (Brent/Nagata technique) - gold standard
  • Porous polyethylene implant (Medpor) - alternative
  • Prosthetic ear - osseointegrated or adhesive
  • Typically performed at age 6-10 when rib cartilage adequate

📊 Prognosis

  • Normal intelligence in most cases
  • Good functional outcomes with staged reconstruction
  • Hearing rehabilitation critical for development
  • Psychosocial support important throughout childhood
  • Multiple surgeries over years - family counseling essential

🧠 Test Your Understanding

In a newborn with hemifacial microsomia, which screening test is MOST important to obtain early?

A. MRI brain
B. Audiology assessment (ABR)
C. CT facial bones
D. Genetic testing
Correct Answer: B

Early audiology assessment is critical because most patients with HFM have conductive hearing loss due to ear canal atresia, middle ear anomalies, or microtia. Early identification allows hearing amplification (hearing aids or bone-anchored devices) during the critical language development period. While cardiac (echo), renal (ultrasound), and spine screening are all important, hearing impacts immediate developmental trajectory and should be prioritized.
