🌬️ Asthma Pathophysiology

Bronchospasm, inflammation, and airway hyperreactivity mechanisms

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the cellular mechanisms of bronchospasm and airway inflammation
  • Explain the role of Th2 inflammation and IgE in allergic asthma
  • Differentiate asthma phenotypes and their treatment implications
  • Apply stepwise therapy based on GINA guidelines
  • Recognize and manage acute asthma exacerbations

The Pathophysiology Triad

Asthma is characterized by three key features that interact to cause symptoms:

1️⃣ Bronchospasm

  • Smooth muscle contraction
  • Triggered by histamine, leukotrienes
  • Rapid onset (minutes)
  • Reversible with bronchodilators
  • Mediates acute symptoms

2️⃣ Airway Inflammation

  • Eosinophils, mast cells, Th2 cells
  • Mucosal edema
  • Mucus hypersecretion
  • Develops over hours-days
  • Responds to corticosteroids

3️⃣ Airway Remodeling

  • Basement membrane thickening
  • Smooth muscle hypertrophy
  • Subepithelial fibrosis
  • Develops over years
  • Partially irreversible
🔑 Why Inflammation Matters Most

Asthma is fundamentally an inflammatory disease. Bronchospasm is just the downstream effect. This is why inhaled corticosteroids (anti-inflammatory) are the cornerstone of therapy, not bronchodilators alone.

The Type 2 Inflammation Cascade

Most asthma (especially allergic) is driven by Type 2 (Th2) immune responses:

Step-by-Step Mechanism

1. Allergen Exposure: Inhaled allergen (dust mite, pollen, pet dander) crosses epithelial barrier

2. Dendritic Cell Activation: DCs present allergen to naive T cells, polarizing them to Th2

3. Th2 Cytokine Release: IL-4, IL-5, IL-13 orchestrate the response

4. IgE Production: IL-4 drives B cell class switching to IgE

5. Mast Cell Sensitization: IgE binds FcεRI on mast cells

6. Re-exposure: Allergen crosslinks IgE → mast cell degranulation → immediate symptoms

💡 The Role of Each Cytokine

IL-4: IgE class switching, Th2 differentiation
IL-5: Eosinophil production, activation, survival (target of mepolizumab, benralizumab)
IL-13: Mucus production, airway hyperreactivity, smooth muscle contraction

Asthma Phenotypes

Not all asthma is the same. Phenotyping guides targeted therapy.

Phenotype Characteristics Biomarkers Targeted Therapy
Allergic Eosinophilic Early onset, atopy, allergen triggers High IgE, ↑eosinophils, ↑FeNO ICS, anti-IgE (omalizumab)
Non-allergic Eosinophilic Adult onset, no atopy, often severe Normal IgE, ↑eosinophils Anti-IL5 (mepolizumab)
Neutrophilic Older, smoking history, steroid-resistant ↑neutrophils, low eosinophils Macrolides, bronchothermoplasty
Obesity-related Late onset, female, minimal eosinophilia Variable Weight loss, ICS

Acute Exacerbation: What's Happening

⚠️ Warning Signs of Severe Exacerbation

• Can't speak in full sentences
• Accessory muscle use, tripod positioning
• Silent chest (no wheezing = no air movement)
• PEF <25% predicted
• SpO₂ <92% on room air
• Altered mental status

Why "Silent Chest" is Ominous

Wheezing requires airflow through narrowed airways. When obstruction becomes so severe that air can barely move, wheezing disappears. A quiet chest in a distressed asthmatic = impending respiratory arrest.

🔑 Acute Management Priorities

1. Oxygen: Target SpO₂ 93-95%
2. SABA: Continuous nebulized albuterol or MDI+spacer q20min x3
3. Ipratropium: Add for severe exacerbations
4. Systemic steroids: Early! Within 1 hour (prednisone 40-60mg or methylpred 125mg IV)
5. Magnesium: 2g IV for severe/refractory cases (bronchodilator effect)
6. Consider BiPAP before intubation if patient can cooperate

🧪 Test Your Understanding

Question 1 of 6
A 25-year-old with asthma presents with acute dyspnea. He is sitting upright, using accessory muscles, and can only speak in 2-word phrases. His chest is nearly silent on auscultation. What does the silent chest indicate?
AThe bronchospasm is resolving
BSevere airflow obstruction with minimal air movement
CThe diagnosis is not asthma
DPneumothorax has developed
✓ Correct!
A silent chest in severe asthma is ominous - it indicates airflow is so severely reduced that there isn't enough air movement to generate wheezing. This patient is in impending respiratory failure. Immediate aggressive treatment (continuous nebs, IV steroids, consider magnesium, prepare for intubation) is critical.
Question 2 of 6
Which cytokine is the primary target of mepolizumab (Nucala) in severe eosinophilic asthma?
AIL-4
BIL-13
CIL-5
DIgE
✓ Correct!
Mepolizumab targets IL-5, which is essential for eosinophil development, activation, and survival. By blocking IL-5, mepolizumab reduces blood and sputum eosinophils, decreasing exacerbations in severe eosinophilic asthma. Omalizumab targets IgE. Dupilumab blocks IL-4/IL-13.
Question 3 of 6
Why are inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) the cornerstone of asthma maintenance therapy?
AThey address the underlying airway inflammation
BThey provide rapid bronchodilation
CThey block mast cell degranulation immediately
DThey reduce mucus production within minutes
✓ Correct!
Asthma is fundamentally an inflammatory disease. ICS reduce eosinophilic inflammation, decrease airway hyperreactivity, and prevent remodeling. They don't provide immediate relief (that's what SABAs do), but they're essential for long-term control. SABA-only therapy is associated with increased mortality.
Question 4 of 6
A patient with severe asthma exacerbation has received 3 rounds of albuterol and ipratropium, IV methylprednisolone, but remains in distress with SpO₂ 89%. What should be given next?
AOral prednisone
BInhaled corticosteroids
CAntibiotics
DIV magnesium sulfate
✓ Correct!
IV magnesium sulfate (2g over 20 minutes) is indicated for severe, refractory asthma exacerbations. Magnesium causes smooth muscle relaxation through calcium channel antagonism and may also have anti-inflammatory effects. It's not routine but is beneficial when standard therapy is insufficient.
Question 5 of 6
Which asthma phenotype would be LEAST likely to respond to anti-IL5 therapy?
AAllergic eosinophilic asthma
BNeutrophilic asthma
CNon-allergic eosinophilic asthma
DAspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease
✓ Correct!
Anti-IL5 therapies (mepolizumab, benralizumab) target eosinophilic inflammation. Neutrophilic asthma has a different inflammatory profile with low eosinophils, so these biologics won't be effective. Neutrophilic asthma is often associated with smoking, older age, and is more steroid-resistant.
Question 6 of 6
What distinguishes asthma from COPD on spirometry?
AAsthma has a lower FEV₁
BCOPD shows complete reversibility
CAsthma shows significant reversibility (≥12% and ≥200mL improvement with bronchodilator)
DCOPD has a normal FEV₁/FVC ratio
✓ Correct!
Asthma classically shows significant bronchodilator reversibility (≥12% AND ≥200mL improvement in FEV₁). COPD has fixed airflow limitation that is NOT fully reversible. Both have reduced FEV₁/FVC ratio. Some patients have asthma-COPD overlap with features of both.
