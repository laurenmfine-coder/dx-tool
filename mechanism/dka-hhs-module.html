DKA & HHS

Diabetic Ketoacidosis and Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State
⏱️ ~50 min📚 18 concepts❓ 4 MCQs
1Pathophysiology
⚡ DKA: The Ketoacidosis Pathway

Trigger: Insulin deficiency + counterregulatory hormone excess

  • ↓ Insulin → ↑ Lipolysis → Free fatty acids to liver
  • Liver converts FFAs to ketone bodies (β-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate)
  • Ketones are acids → metabolic acidosis with increased anion gap
  • Hyperglycemia → osmotic diuresis → dehydration
💧 HHS: The Hyperosmolar Pathway

Key difference: Enough insulin to prevent ketosis, not enough to prevent hyperglycemia

  • Severe hyperglycemia (often >600 mg/dL)
  • Profound osmotic diuresis → severe dehydration (8-10L deficit)
  • Hyperosmolarity (>320 mOsm/kg) → altered mental status
  • No significant ketosis

2Precipitants & Diagnosis
🔍 The "I's" of DKA/HHS
  • Infection: #1 cause - ALWAYS look for it!
  • Insulin: Non-compliance, pump failure, new diagnosis
  • Infarction: MI, stroke
  • Iatrogenic: Steroids, SGLT2 inhibitors (euglycemic DKA)
⚠️ Euglycemic DKA
SGLT2 inhibitors can cause DKA with glucose <250! Check ketones if symptomatic regardless of glucose.
💎 The Potassium Paradox
Initial K+ is often NORMAL or HIGH despite MASSIVE total body depletion. When you give insulin, K+ crashes into cells. Rule: Do NOT give insulin until K+ >3.3
3Treatment
🚨 DKA/HHS Management
1
FLUIDS: NS 1-1.5 L/hr initially, then 250-500 mL/hr
2
POTASSIUM: If K <3.3 → hold insulin, give 20-40 mEq/hr
3
INSULIN: 0.1 U/kg bolus, then 0.1 U/kg/hr drip
4
DEXTROSE: When glucose <200 (DKA) or <300 (HHS), add D5. Continue insulin!
🛑 Critical Pitfalls
  • Don't stop insulin when glucose normalizes - continue until anion gap closes
  • Don't give insulin if K+ <3.3
  • Don't give bicarb unless pH <6.9
✅ DKA Resolution Criteria
  • Glucose <200
  • pH >7.3
  • Bicarbonate ≥15
  • Anion gap ≤12

All four must be met before transitioning to SubQ insulin.
