MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

Rhabdomyolysis = skeletal muscle breakdown → release of intracellular contents (myoglobin, CK, K+, phosphate, uric acid) into circulation.

Cause CategoryExamples
Traumatic/PhysicalCrush injury (#1 in disasters), prolonged immobilization (found down), compartment syndrome, excessive exercise, seizures
Non-traumaticDrugs/toxins: statins (especially + fibrates), alcohol, cocaine, amphetamines. Hyperthermia: NMS, MH, heat stroke. Infections: influenza, HIV, Legionella. Electrolytes: hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia
🔑 Myoglobin Nephrotoxicity: Myoglobin precipitates in renal tubules (especially in acidic urine) → tubular obstruction + direct oxidative injury → pigment nephropathy (ATN). Risk highest when CK >5,000 but AKI can occur at >15,000-20,000. Dark/cola-colored urine (myoglobinuria) — dipstick + for blood but NO RBCs on microscopy.
CLINICAL
Diagnosis & Complications

Classic Triad

Myalgia + weakness + dark urine (but triad present in <10% of cases).

Laboratory Findings

TestFinding
CKMASSIVELY elevated (>5× ULN, often >10,000). Peak 24-72h. Most sensitive marker
UrinalysisDipstick + for blood, but microscopy shows NO RBCs (myoglobin cross-reacts with dipstick)
PotassiumElevated (released from damaged muscle — can be lethal)
PhosphateElevated (intracellular release)
CalciumLOW initially (binds damaged muscle). May rebound HIGH during recovery
Uric acidElevated (purine release from damaged cells)
BUN/CreatinineElevated (AKI from myoglobin nephropathy). BUN:Cr may be <20:1 (intrinsic renal)
⚠️ Life-Threatening Complications: Hyperkalemia (cardiac arrest), AKI (25-33% develop), DIC, compartment syndrome, cardiac arrhythmias.
TREATMENT
Management
PriorityTreatment
1. Aggressive IV fluidsMOST IMPORTANT. Isotonic saline 1-1.5 L/hr initially. Target UOP 200-300 mL/hr (3 mL/kg/hr). Dilutes myoglobin, maintains renal perfusion, prevents tubular precipitation
2. Monitor + treat hyperkalemiaContinuous telemetry. Treat per hyperkalemia protocol if K+ elevated
3. Avoid calcium supplementationHypocalcemia is expected — do NOT replace unless symptomatic (tetany, seizures) or hyperkalemia with EKG changes. Exogenous calcium deposits in damaged muscle
4. Monitor CK trendCK peaks at 24-72h, then declines. If not declining → ongoing muscle injury
5. Dialysis if neededFor refractory hyperkalemia, severe AKI, fluid overload
🔑 Bicarbonate for urine alkalinization (target urine pH >6.5) is sometimes used to prevent myoglobin precipitation in tubules, but evidence is limited. IV fluids remain the mainstay. Avoid with hypocalcemia (alkalosis worsens ionized calcium).
