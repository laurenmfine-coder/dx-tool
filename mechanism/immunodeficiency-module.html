🧬 Primary Immunodeficiency

When to suspect, how to evaluate, and what infections to expect

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Recognize warning signs of primary immunodeficiency
  • Understand the immune defects in major PID categories
  • Match infection patterns to immune deficiency type
  • Apply appropriate laboratory evaluation
  • Understand principles of immunoglobulin replacement therapy

Warning Signs of Immunodeficiency

The Jeffrey Modell Foundation warning signs help identify patients who need evaluation:

🔑 10 Warning Signs (Adults)

1. ≥2 new ear infections within 1 year
2. ≥2 serious sinus infections within 1 year
3. One pneumonia per year for >1 year
4. Chronic diarrhea with weight loss
5. Recurrent viral infections (colds, herpes, warts)
6. Recurrent need for IV antibiotics
7. Recurrent deep abscesses
8. Persistent thrush or fungal skin infections
9. Infection with normally harmless organisms
10. Family history of immunodeficiency

💡 SPUR: When to Think Immunodeficiency

Severe - hospitalization, IV antibiotics, ICU
Persistent - slow response, prolonged treatment
Unusual - opportunistic organisms, atypical sites
Recurrent - same type of infection

Categories of Primary Immunodeficiency

🅱️ B Cell / Antibody Defects

  • Most common (~50% of PIDs)
  • Encapsulated bacteria (S. pneumo, H. flu)
  • Sinopulmonary infections predominate
  • CVID, XLA, IgA deficiency
  • Treat with IVIG/SCIG

🇹 T Cell / Combined Defects

  • Most severe (SCID is emergency)
  • Opportunistic infections (PJP, CMV)
  • Failure to thrive in infants
  • SCID, DiGeorge syndrome
  • May need stem cell transplant

🔵 Phagocyte Defects

  • Catalase-positive organisms
  • Staph, Serratia, Aspergillus
  • Deep-seated abscesses, granulomas
  • CGD, LAD
  • Prophylactic antibiotics/antifungals

🔴 Complement Defects

  • Encapsulated bacteria (especially Neisseria)
  • Recurrent meningococcemia
  • SLE-like syndromes (early complement)
  • Vaccinate against encapsulated organisms
  • CH50, AH50 for screening

Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID)

The most common symptomatic primary antibody deficiency in adults.

Diagnostic Criteria

1. Low IgG (at least 2 SD below mean for age)

2. Low IgA and/or IgM

3. Poor vaccine responses (pneumococcal, tetanus)

4. Onset after age 2 (excludes most genetic defects)

5. Exclusion of other causes

⚠️ Non-Infectious Complications of CVID

• Autoimmune cytopenias (ITP, AIHA) - may precede diagnosis
• Granulomatous disease (lung, liver, spleen)
• Lymphoid hyperplasia, splenomegaly
• Increased risk of lymphoma and GI malignancy
• Enteropathy with malabsorption

💡 Interpreting Vaccine Responses

Check titers to protein antigens (tetanus, diphtheria) AND polysaccharide antigens (pneumococcal serotypes). Some patients make protein but not polysaccharide responses (specific antibody deficiency). Check 4-6 weeks post-vaccination.

Immunoglobulin Replacement Therapy

💉 IVIG (Intravenous)

  • Given every 3-4 weeks
  • Infusion center or home
  • Higher peak levels
  • Headache, chills common
  • Good for rapid correction

💧 SCIG (Subcutaneous)

  • Weekly or biweekly at home
  • More stable IgG levels
  • Fewer systemic reactions
  • Local site reactions
  • Patient independence
🔑 IgG Trough Goals

Target trough IgG ≥500 mg/dL, ideally >700-800 mg/dL. Some patients need higher levels to prevent infections. Adjust dose based on infection frequency, not just IgG level. Check trough level immediately before next infusion.

🧪 Test Your Understanding

Question 1 of 5
A 35-year-old presents with recurrent sinopulmonary infections. Labs show IgG 350 (normal 700-1600), IgA <7, IgM 25. Tetanus titer is protective but pneumococcal titers are low. What is the most likely diagnosis?
ASelective IgA deficiency
BCommon Variable Immunodeficiency
CX-linked agammaglobulinemia
DHyper-IgM syndrome
✓ Correct!
CVID: low IgG + low IgA/IgM + poor vaccine responses. Age rules out XLA (presents in infancy when maternal Ab wanes). IgA deficiency alone wouldn't cause low IgG. Hyper-IgM would have elevated IgM.
Question 2 of 5
A child with recurrent Staphylococcus and Aspergillus infections has liver abscesses and granulomas. What test would confirm the suspected diagnosis?
AQuantitative immunoglobulins
BCH50
CDihydrorhodamine (DHR) flow cytometry
DLymphocyte subsets
✓ Correct!
This is Chronic Granulomatous Disease (CGD). Catalase-positive organisms + granulomas = phagocyte oxidative burst defect. DHR test measures neutrophil respiratory burst. NBT is the older test.
Question 3 of 5
Which infection pattern suggests complement deficiency?
ARecurrent Neisseria meningitidis
BPneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia
CDisseminated BCG
DRecurrent skin abscesses
✓ Correct!
Terminal complement (C5-9) deficiency causes recurrent Neisseria infections. PJP suggests T cell defect. BCG dissemination suggests IL-12/IFN-γ pathway defect. Abscesses suggest phagocyte defect.
Question 4 of 5
A patient on IVIG has trough IgG of 600 mg/dL but continues to have 3-4 sinus infections per year. What should you do?
AContinue same dose - IgG is adequate
BSwitch to SCIG
CAdd prophylactic antibiotics only
DIncrease IVIG dose to achieve higher trough
✓ Correct!
Dose to clinical response, not just IgG level. Many patients need trough >700-800 to prevent infections. If still getting infections despite "adequate" IgG, increase the dose. Some require >1000.
Question 5 of 5
What non-infectious complication of CVID may PRECEDE the diagnosis?
ABronchiectasis
BAutoimmune cytopenias (ITP, AIHA)
CLymphoma
DGranulomatous lung disease
✓ Correct!
Autoimmune cytopenias (especially ITP) may be the presenting feature of CVID, sometimes years before recurrent infections. Check immunoglobulins in patients with "idiopathic" ITP or AIHA!
