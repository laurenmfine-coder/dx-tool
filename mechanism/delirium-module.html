Pathophysiology

Delirium is an acute, fluctuating disturbance of attention and awareness caused by an underlying medical condition. It represents global cerebral dysfunction, NOT a primary psychiatric disorder.

🔑 Core Features (DSM-5): 1) Disturbance in attention and awareness. 2) Develops over hours to days (ACUTE). 3) Fluctuating course. 4) Not better explained by a pre-existing neurocognitive disorder. 5) Evidence of an underlying medical etiology.

Types & Etiologies

Three Subtypes

SubtypeFeaturesDanger
HyperactiveAgitation, restlessness, hallucinations, combativenessEasy to recognize but risk of self-harm, falls
HypoactiveLethargy, withdrawal, flat affect, reduced psychomotor activityMOST COMMON and most MISSED — often misdiagnosed as depression. Worse prognosis
MixedAlternating between hyperactive and hypoactiveFluctuating presentation confuses diagnosis
⚠️ Common Causes (I WATCH DEATH): Infection (UTI, pneumonia #1 in elderly), Withdrawal (alcohol, benzos), Acute metabolic (electrolytes, glucose, hepatic/renal), Trauma (head injury, burns, surgery), CNS pathology (stroke, seizure, meningitis), Hypoxia, Deficiencies (thiamine → Wernicke, B12), Endocrine (thyroid, adrenal), Acute vascular (MI, PE), Toxins/drugs (anticholinergics are #1 medication cause, opioids, benzos, steroids), Heavy metals.

High-Risk Medications (Anticholinergic Burden)

Diphenhydramine, oxybutynin, TCAs (amitriptyline), benzodiazepines, meperidine, scopolamine. The more anticholinergic medications, the higher the delirium risk — especially in elderly.

TREATMENT
Management

Approach: Treat the Cause

Delirium is a medical emergency. The single most important intervention is identifying and treating the underlying cause (infection → antibiotics, urinary retention → catheter, hypoxia → oxygen, medication → discontinue).

Non-Pharmacologic Interventions (FIRST-LINE)

🔑 Prevention & Treatment: Reorientation (clocks, calendars, familiar objects), sleep-wake cycle preservation (lights on during day, minimize nighttime interruptions), minimize tethers (catheters, restraints), correct sensory deficits (glasses, hearing aids), early mobilization, avoid anticholinergics, adequate hydration/nutrition. This reduces delirium by 30-40%.

Pharmacologic Management (Agitation Only)

AgentWhen to UseKey Points
HaloperidolSevere agitation endangering patient/staffLow-dose (0.5-1 mg). Avoid in Parkinson disease, Lewy body dementia (worsens symptoms dramatically), prolonged QTc
Quetiapine/olanzapineAlternative antipsychoticsMay have better side effect profile. Monitor QTc
DexmedetomidineICU delirium (intubated patients)Alpha-2 agonist. Preferred in ICU — less respiratory depression than benzos
BenzodiazepinesONLY for alcohol/benzo withdrawal delirium, hepatic encephalopathyOtherwise WORSEN delirium. Lorazepam preferred (no hepatic metabolism)
