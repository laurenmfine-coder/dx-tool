🔴 Mast Cell Disorders

MechanismDx Module - Mastocytosis, MCAS & Hereditary Alpha-Tryptasemia

📚 Overview: The Mast Cell Disorder Spectrum

Mast cell disorders represent a spectrum of conditions characterized by abnormal mast cell accumulation (mastocytosis) or excessive mast cell activation without clonal expansion (MCAS).

The Three Main Categories

Disorder Key Feature Prevalence
Mastocytosis Clonal mast cell proliferation (KIT mutation) ~1:10,000
MCAS Mast cell activation without clonal disease Unknown (controversial)
HαT Elevated baseline tryptase (genetic) 4-6% of population

Why This Matters

  • Anaphylaxis risk: Patients with mastocytosis have severe anaphylaxis with hypotension more frequently
  • Anesthesia considerations: Certain medications can trigger mast cell degranulation
  • Venom allergy: Higher rates and more severe reactions in mastocytosis
  • Osteoporosis: Systemic mastocytosis causes bone disease even in young patients
🔑 Clinical Pearl: Always check a baseline serum tryptase after anaphylaxis! Elevated baseline (>11.4 ng/mL) suggests mastocytosis or HαT and changes management (lifelong epinephrine, venom immunotherapy evaluation).

🔬 Mastocytosis: Classification & Pathophysiology

WHO Classification (2022)

Category Subtypes Prognosis
Cutaneous Mastocytosis (CM) Urticaria pigmentosa Diffuse CM Mastocytoma Excellent, especially in children (often resolves)
Indolent SM (ISM) Bone marrow involvement No organ damage Normal life expectancy
Smoldering SM (SSM) High mast cell burden ≥2 B findings Risk of progression
Advanced SM SM-AHN Aggressive SM Mast Cell Leukemia Poor, requires cytoreductive therapy

The KIT D816V Mutation

KIT D816V mutation → Constitutive KIT activation → Mast cell proliferation & survival → Clonal accumulation
  • Present in >90% of adult systemic mastocytosis
  • Activating point mutation at codon 816 (Asp→Val)
  • Treatment implication: Resistant to imatinib (use avapritinib, midostaurin)
  • Can be detected by sensitive PCR even at low allele burden

Diagnostic Criteria for Systemic Mastocytosis

WHO Criteria: 1 Major + 1 Minor OR 3 Minor

MAJOR:

  • Multifocal dense mast cell aggregates (≥15 mast cells) in bone marrow or extracutaneous organ

MINOR:

  • >25% of mast cells are spindle-shaped or atypical
  • KIT D816V mutation detected
  • Mast cells express CD25 and/or CD2
  • Serum tryptase >20 ng/mL (not valid if associated hematologic neoplasm)
🔑 Clinical Pearl: The "Darier sign" (urticaria when skin lesion is stroked) is classic for cutaneous mastocytosis but NOT pathognomonic. It results from local mast cell degranulation when lesions are mechanically stimulated.

Triggers for Mast Cell Degranulation

⚠️ Common Triggers to Avoid/Manage

Category Examples
Physical Heat, cold, friction, exercise, stress
Medications NSAIDs, opioids (morphine > fentanyl), vancomycin (red man), muscle relaxants, radiocontrast
Venoms Hymenoptera stings (high anaphylaxis risk!)
Alcohol Direct mast cell degranulation
Anesthesia Atracurium, thiopental (avoid); propofol, fentanyl (safer)

Anaphylaxis in Mastocytosis

  • Hypotension-predominant: Cardiovascular symptoms with ABSENCE of skin findings is classic
  • Hymenoptera venom: Up to 5% of venom anaphylaxis patients have mastocytosis
  • Idiopathic anaphylaxis: Always evaluate for mastocytosis/HαT
  • More severe: Greater hypotension, longer duration, higher epinephrine requirements
🔑 Clinical Pearl: Anaphylaxis WITHOUT urticaria/angioedema (just hypotension and syncope) should raise suspicion for mastocytosis! Up to 50% of mastocytosis patients lack skin findings during anaphylaxis.

🔍 Diagnostic Workup

When to Suspect Mastocytosis

  • Recurrent anaphylaxis (especially hypotension-predominant or idiopathic)
  • Urticaria pigmentosa skin lesions
  • Elevated baseline tryptase (>11.4 ng/mL, ideally >20 ng/mL)
  • Unexplained osteoporosis in young patient
  • Severe hymenoptera anaphylaxis
  • Recurrent flushing + GI symptoms

Diagnostic Algorithm

Step 1: Baseline Serum Tryptase

Draw at baseline (NOT during acute episode). If elevated (>11.4 ng/mL), proceed.

Note: If tryptase 8-11.4, consider HαT genetics

Step 2: Additional Labs

CBC with differential, comprehensive metabolic panel, LDH, vitamin D, DEXA scan

Step 3: Skin Examination

Look for urticaria pigmentosa (red-brown macules/papules). Test Darier sign.

If present → skin biopsy

Step 4: Bone Marrow Biopsy

Gold standard for systemic mastocytosis diagnosis

  • Morphology: Look for mast cell aggregates, spindle shapes
  • Immunophenotype: CD25+, CD2+ mast cells (aberrant)
  • Molecular: KIT D816V mutation testing
Step 5: Stage the Disease

Evaluate B and C findings to classify ISM vs SSM vs Advanced SM

Tryptase Interpretation

Tryptase Level Interpretation Next Steps
<11.4 ng/mL Normal Mastocytosis unlikely (doesn't exclude MCAS)
11.4-20 ng/mL Mildly elevated Consider HαT, repeat, clinical correlation
>20 ng/mL WHO minor criterion High suspicion for SM → bone marrow biopsy
>200 ng/mL Very high Suggests high mast cell burden or advanced SM
🔑 Clinical Pearl: The "20% + 2" rule for acute anaphylaxis: Tryptase is elevated if it's >20% above baseline PLUS 2 ng/mL. This helps distinguish mast cell activation from baseline elevation. A peak:baseline ratio >1.2 + 2 suggests anaphylaxis.

💊 Treatment & MCAS

Management of Indolent Systemic Mastocytosis

Symptom-Directed Therapy

Symptom Treatment
Flushing, pruritus, urticaria H1 antihistamines (high dose if needed, up to 4x)
GI symptoms H2 blockers, PPIs, cromolyn sodium
Anaphylaxis prevention Epinephrine autoinjectors (multiple!), medical alert
Osteoporosis Bisphosphonates, calcium/vitamin D, DEXA monitoring
Refractory symptoms Omalizumab (anti-IgE), consider cytoreduction

Advanced SM Treatment

Cytoreductive Therapy (Hematology-Oncology)

  • Midostaurin: Multi-kinase inhibitor, FDA approved for advanced SM
  • Avapritinib: Selective KIT D816V inhibitor, highly effective
  • Cladribine: For aggressive SM, SM-AHN
  • Imatinib: Only for rare non-D816V KIT mutations

Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS)

Consensus Diagnostic Criteria (2019)

ALL of the following must be met:

  • 1. Typical clinical symptoms affecting ≥2 organ systems (skin, GI, cardiovascular, respiratory)
  • 2. Documented mast cell mediator elevation during symptomatic episode (tryptase increase ≥20% + 2 ng/mL above baseline, or elevated urinary prostaglandins/histamine metabolites)
  • 3. Response to mast cell-targeted therapy (antihistamines, mast cell stabilizers)
  • 4. Exclusion of other diagnoses that better explain symptoms

MCAS Subtypes

Subtype Features
Primary (Clonal) MCAS Meets 1-2 minor criteria for SM but not full SM diagnosis
Secondary MCAS Due to allergy, chronic infection, autoimmune disease
Idiopathic MCAS No identifiable cause, diagnosis of exclusion

Hereditary Alpha-Tryptasemia (HαT)

  • Prevalence: 4-6% of general population (common!)
  • Genetics: Extra copies of TPSAB1 gene (autosomal dominant)
  • Tryptase: Elevated baseline (typically 8-20 ng/mL)
  • Symptoms: Variable - some asymptomatic, others have MCAS-like symptoms, more severe anaphylaxis
  • Testing: Droplet digital PCR for TPSAB1 copy number
  • Management: Symptom-directed, treat like MCAS if symptomatic
🔑 Clinical Pearl: MCAS is real but OVER-diagnosed! The key is DOCUMENTED mediator elevation during symptoms. Many patients are labeled "MCAS" without objective evidence. Insist on proper criteria before the diagnosis.

📝 Knowledge Check

1. A 45-year-old man presents after a bee sting caused syncope and hypotension without any skin findings. Baseline tryptase is 18 ng/mL. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Typical IgE-mediated venom anaphylaxis
B. Systemic mastocytosis with venom anaphylaxis
C. Vasovagal syncope
D. Idiopathic anaphylaxis
Correct! Hypotension-predominant anaphylaxis WITHOUT skin findings + elevated baseline tryptase is classic for mastocytosis. Up to 5% of patients with severe hymenoptera anaphylaxis have underlying mastocytosis. This patient needs bone marrow biopsy and should receive venom immunotherapy.

2. Which mutation is found in >90% of adult systemic mastocytosis and confers resistance to imatinib?

A. BCR-ABL
B. JAK2 V617F
C. KIT D816V
D. FLT3-ITD
Correct! KIT D816V is the hallmark mutation in systemic mastocytosis, present in >90% of adult cases. This specific mutation causes constitutive activation of the KIT receptor and is RESISTANT to imatinib. Avapritinib and midostaurin are effective for D816V-positive disease.

3. A patient has recurrent episodes of flushing, GI cramping, and near-syncope. Tryptase during symptoms was 14 ng/mL; baseline 4 weeks later is 11 ng/mL. Does this meet criteria for MCAS?

A. Yes - tryptase increased >20% + 2 ng/mL above baseline
B. No - the absolute tryptase is normal (<11.4)
C. No - MCAS requires tryptase >20 ng/mL
D. Cannot determine without skin biopsy
Correct! The "20% + 2" formula: 11 × 1.2 + 2 = 15.2 ng/mL threshold. The acute tryptase of 14 is just below this threshold but very close. Technically doesn't quite meet criteria. However, with strong clinical history, consider other mast cell mediators (urinary prostaglandins, histamine metabolites) or repeat testing during a symptomatic episode.

4. Which finding on bone marrow biopsy is the MAJOR criterion for systemic mastocytosis?

A. CD25+ mast cells
B. Multifocal dense aggregates of ≥15 mast cells
C. KIT D816V mutation
D. Serum tryptase >20 ng/mL
Correct! The ONLY major criterion is multifocal dense aggregates of ≥15 mast cells in bone marrow or extracutaneous organs. CD25 expression, KIT mutation, atypical morphology, and elevated tryptase are all MINOR criteria. Diagnosis requires 1 major + 1 minor OR 3 minor criteria.

5. What is hereditary alpha-tryptasemia (HαT)?

A. A rare form of mast cell leukemia
B. An autoimmune disease targeting tryptase
C. A common genetic condition with extra TPSAB1 gene copies causing elevated baseline tryptase
D. An acquired tryptase deficiency
Correct! HαT is caused by extra copies of the TPSAB1 gene (autosomal dominant) and affects 4-6% of the population! It causes elevated baseline tryptase without clonal mast cell disease. Some individuals are asymptomatic; others have MCAS-like symptoms or more severe anaphylaxis. Testing is via droplet digital PCR.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • Mastocytosis = clonal mast cell proliferation; KIT D816V mutation in >90% of adults
  • Anaphylaxis without skin findings + elevated baseline tryptase → think mastocytosis
  • WHO criteria: 1 major + 1 minor OR 3 minor criteria for diagnosis
  • MCAS requires DOCUMENTED mediator elevation during symptoms
  • HαT affects 4-6% of population - common cause of mildly elevated tryptase
  • All mastocytosis patients need: epinephrine, medical alert, venom allergy evaluation, DEXA
