Tachyarrhythmias: The Big Picture

Narrow Complex (QRS <120ms)

Supraventricular origin
Regular: Sinus tach, Aflutter, AVNRT, AVRT, Atrial tach
Irregular: AFib, Aflutter (variable), MAT

Wide Complex (QRS >120ms)

⚠️ Assume VT until proven otherwise!
Ventricular: VT, Polymorphic VT/Torsades, VF
SVT + aberrancy: SVT + BBB, AFib + WPW

SVT: AVNRT vs AVRT

AVNRT

Mechanism: Reentry within AV node (dual pathways)
ECG: P waves buried in QRS or just after (pseudo-R' in V1, pseudo-S in inferior leads)
Most common SVT in adults

AVRT (WPW)

Mechanism: Accessory pathway (Bundle of Kent) outside AV node
Baseline ECG: Short PR, delta wave, wide QRS
Orthodromic: Narrow (down AV, up accessory)
Antidromic: Wide (down accessory, up AV)

SVT Acute Management

1. Vagal maneuvers
→
2. Adenosine 6mg → 12mg → 12mg
→
3. Beta-blocker or CCB

⚠️ WPW + AFib: AVOID AV nodal blockers (adenosine, BB, CCB, digoxin)! Can cause VF. Use procainamide or cardiovert.

Ventricular Tachycardia

VT Clues (vs SVT with aberrancy)

  • • AV dissociation (P waves march through)
  • • Fusion/capture beats
  • • Very wide QRS (>160ms)
  • • Concordance (all precordial leads same direction)
  • • Northwest axis (-90 to -180)
  • • History of structural heart disease

Types

Monomorphic VT: Same QRS morphology. Usually structural heart disease/scar.
Polymorphic VT: Varying QRS. Ischemia or channelopathy.
Torsades de Pointes: Twisting around baseline. Long QT syndrome.
Stable VT
Amiodarone, procainamide, or cardioversion
Unstable VT / Pulseless VT / VF
Immediate defibrillation + CPR + ACLS
Torsades de Pointes Treatment
IV Magnesium (even if Mg normal) + Correct K⁺ + Stop QT-prolonging drugs + Overdrive pacing or isoproterenol (↑HR shortens QT)

Bradyarrhythmias

1° AV Block
PR >200ms, all P waves conducted
Usually benign, no treatment
2° Type I (Wenckebach)
Progressive PR prolongation → dropped beat
Often benign (vagal tone, athletes)
2° Type II (Mobitz II)
Fixed PR, sudden dropped QRS
⚠️ Can progress to 3° block - needs pacing
3° (Complete) Heart Block
Complete AV dissociation. P waves and QRS independent. Escape rhythm determines rate:
Junctional escape: 40-60 bpm, narrow QRS
Ventricular escape: 20-40 bpm, wide QRS
Requires pacemaker
Symptomatic Bradycardia Treatment
Atropine 0.5-1mg
→
Transcutaneous pacing
→
Dopamine or Epinephrine drip
→
Transvenous pacing
Atropine ineffective in 3° block or denervated hearts (transplant)

Long QT Syndrome

QTc Prolongation Causes

Drugs: Antiarrhythmics (sotalol, amiodarone), antipsychotics, antibiotics (macrolides, fluoroquinolones), methadone
Electrolytes: ↓K⁺, ↓Mg²⁺, ↓Ca²⁺
Congenital: Romano-Ward (AD), Jervell Lange-Nielsen (AR + deafness)
Other: Hypothyroidism, hypothermia, bradycardia

Normal QTc

Men: <440ms
Women: <460ms
>500ms = High risk for Torsades
Bazett's: QTc = QT / √RR

Pacemaker Indications (Simplified)

Class I (Definitely Needs Pacemaker)
  • • Symptomatic sinus node dysfunction
  • • 3° AV block (any level)
  • • 2° Type II AV block
  • • Alternating bundle branch block
  • • Sinus pause >3 sec while awake
ICD Indications
  • • Survived VT/VF arrest (not reversible cause)
  • • Sustained VT with structural heart disease
  • • EF ≤35% with NYHA II-III (primary prevention)
  • • EF ≤30% post-MI (>40 days)
  • • HCM, ARVC, Long QT with syncope
