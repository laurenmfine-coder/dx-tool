🎗️ Prostate Cancer

Screening, Gleason Grading, Staging & Treatment

📚 Overview

Prostate cancer is the most common non-skin cancer in men and second leading cause of cancer death. Most are adenocarcinomas arising from the peripheral zone.

Epidemiology

ParameterValue
Lifetime risk~1 in 8 men
Median age at diagnosis66 years
Location70-80% peripheral zone
5-year survival (localized)>99%
5-year survival (metastatic)~30%

Risk Factors

  • Age - risk increases significantly after 50
  • Race - African American men have highest risk, earlier onset
  • Family history - 2-3x risk with first-degree relative
  • BRCA1/2 mutations - especially BRCA2
  • Lynch syndrome

🔍 Screening

PSA Screening Controversy: Shared decision-making is recommended. Discuss risks (overdiagnosis, overtreatment) and benefits (early detection of aggressive cancer) with patients.

PSA (Prostate-Specific Antigen)

PSA is organ-specific, NOT cancer-specific!

Elevated PSA can be caused by:

  • Prostate cancer
  • BPH
  • Prostatitis
  • Recent ejaculation
  • Urinary retention
  • Prostate manipulation (biopsy, cystoscopy)

PSA Interpretation

PSA LevelCancer Probability
0-2.5 ng/mL~10%
2.5-4 ng/mL~15%
4-10 ng/mL~25%
>10 ng/mL~50%

PSA Refinements

TestUse
PSA velocity>0.75 ng/mL/year increase is concerning
PSA densityPSA/prostate volume; >0.15 is concerning

Screening Guidelines (AUA 2023)

  • Age 55-69: Shared decision-making; individualized
  • Age 40-54: Consider in high-risk (African American, family history, BRCA)
  • Age <40: Not recommended
  • Age >70 or life expectancy <10-15 years: Generally not recommended

🔬 Diagnosis

Indications for Biopsy

  • Elevated/rising PSA
  • Abnormal DRE (nodule, asymmetry, induration)
  • Suspicious MRI findings

Biopsy Approaches

ApproachDescription
Transrectal US-guided (TRUS)Traditional 12-core systematic biopsy
MRI-fusion biopsyTargets suspicious lesions on MRI; improving detection
TransperinealLower infection risk; gaining popularity

MRI (Multiparametric)

PI-RADS Score (1-5):
  • 1-2: Clinically significant cancer unlikely
  • 3: Equivocal
  • 4-5: Clinically significant cancer likely → target for biopsy

📊 Gleason Grading

Gleason Score = Primary pattern (most common) + Secondary pattern (second most common). Each pattern graded 1-5 based on glandular differentiation.

Gleason Patterns

PatternDescription
1-2Well-differentiated (rarely assigned now)
3Individual discrete glands; variable size
4Fused/cribriform/poorly formed glands
5No glandular differentiation; sheets, cords, necrosis

Grade Groups (ISUP 2014)

Grade Group 1 = Gleason 3+3=6

Low risk; often managed with active surveillance

Grade Group 2 = Gleason 3+4=7

Favorable intermediate risk; predominantly pattern 3

Grade Group 3 = Gleason 4+3=7

Unfavorable intermediate risk; predominantly pattern 4

Grade Group 4 = Gleason 8 (4+4, 3+5, 5+3)

High risk

Grade Group 5 = Gleason 9-10

Very high risk; poor prognosis

Key Point: 3+4=7 vs 4+3=7 are NOT the same! 4+3 has more high-grade disease and worse prognosis. This is why Grade Groups were introduced.

Metastatic Sites

  • Lymph nodes: Pelvic (obturator, iliac)
  • Bone: Most common distant site; classically osteoBLASTIC
  • Lung, liver: Less common
Osteoblastic Metastases: Prostate cancer classically causes sclerotic (blastic) bone metastases, unlike most cancers which are osteolytic. Look for elevated alkaline phosphatase.

💊 Treatment

Risk Stratification

RiskCriteriaOptions
LowPSA <10, Gleason 6, T1-T2aActive surveillance preferred; radiation; surgery
IntermediatePSA 10-20, Gleason 7, T2b-T2cSurgery, radiation ± ADT
HighPSA >20, Gleason 8-10, T3Radiation + ADT (2-3 years); surgery (select cases)
MetastaticN1 or M1ADT ± chemotherapy ± novel agents

Treatment Modalities

Active Surveillance:
  • For low-risk disease
  • Regular PSA, DRE, repeat biopsies
  • Avoids/delays treatment side effects
  • Intervention if progression
Radical Prostatectomy:
  • Removes entire prostate + seminal vesicles
  • ± Pelvic lymph node dissection
  • Risks: ED (30-70%), incontinence (5-20%)
  • Nerve-sparing when oncologically appropriate
Radiation Therapy:
  • External beam (EBRT) or brachytherapy (seeds)
  • Often combined with ADT for intermediate/high risk
  • Risks: Radiation proctitis, cystitis, ED

Androgen Deprivation Therapy (ADT)

MECHANISM OF ADT: Prostate cancer is ANDROGEN-DEPENDENT Options: • GnRH agonists (leuprolide, goserelin) - Initial testosterone flare, then suppression • GnRH antagonists (degarelix) - No testosterone flare • Orchiectomy - Surgical castration Side effects: • Hot flashes, loss of libido, ED • Osteoporosis, metabolic syndrome • Cognitive changes, fatigue • Gynecomastia

🧠 Quick Quiz

A prostate biopsy shows adenocarcinoma with Gleason 4+3=7. What Grade Group is this?

A. Grade Group 2
B. Grade Group 3
C. Grade Group 4
D. Grade Group 1
Correct: B - Grade Group 3
Gleason 4+3=7 is Grade Group 3 (unfavorable intermediate risk). This is different from 3+4=7, which is Grade Group 2 (favorable intermediate). The primary pattern 4 indicates more aggressive disease.
