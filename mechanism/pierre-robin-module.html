🍼 Pierre Robin Sequence

Micrognathia → Glossoptosis → Airway Obstruction (± Cleft Palate)

📚 Core Definition

Pierre Robin Sequence (PRS) = A SEQUENCE (not syndrome) of:
  1. Micrognathia (small, recessed mandible)
  2. Glossoptosis (posterior displacement of tongue base)
  3. Upper airway obstruction

± U-shaped cleft palate (present in 75-90%, but not required for diagnosis)

Why "Sequence" Not "Syndrome"? A sequence describes a cascade of events stemming from a single initiating problem. In PRS, micrognathia is the primary defect that leads to glossoptosis, which causes airway obstruction and may block palatal fusion.

Epidemiology:

  • Incidence: 1 in 8,500 - 14,000 live births
  • 40% are isolated (nonsyndromic) PRS
  • 60% are syndromic (associated with other syndromes)
  • Most common associations: Stickler syndrome, 22q11.2 deletion

Why U-Shaped Cleft?

The cleft in PRS is typically U-shaped (wide, horseshoe-shaped) rather than V-shaped because the posteriorly positioned tongue physically prevents the palatal shelves from meeting in the midline.

🩺 Clinical Features

1. MICROGNATHIA

  • Small, recessed mandible
  • Retrognathia (positioned posteriorly)
  • Can range from mild to severe

2. GLOSSOPTOSIS

  • Tongue base falls posteriorly into oropharynx
  • Worse in supine position
  • Improves with prone positioning

3. UPPER AIRWAY OBSTRUCTION

  • Stridor, stertor, retractions, cyanosis
  • Worse during feeding and sleep
  • Positional dependency characteristic
  • OSA in 50-100% of patients

🧩 Syndromic Associations (60% of PRS)

CRITICAL: Always evaluate for associated syndromes! They affect prognosis, management, and genetic counseling.

STICKLER SYNDROME (Most Common)

Gene: COL2A1, COL11A1, COL11A2

  • PRS + Severe myopia (>-8 diopters)
  • Retinal detachment risk (12-50%)
  • Sensorineural hearing loss
  • Joint hypermobility

⚠️ Urgent ophthalmology eval required!

VELOCARDIOFACIAL (22q11.2 Deletion)

Features: CATCH-22

  • Cardiac defects
  • Abnormal facies
  • Thymic hypoplasia
  • Cleft palate
  • Hypocalcemia

TREACHER COLLINS SYNDROME

Gene: TCOF1

  • Bilateral mandibular/malar hypoplasia
  • Downslanting palpebral fissures
  • Microtia, conductive hearing loss
Workup for Syndromes: Ophthalmology (Stickler), Genetics/22q11.2 FISH, Echo (cardiac), Audiology, Calcium/PTH, Renal ultrasound

🔪 Management Algorithm

Principle: Start conservative, escalate as needed. Goal: safe airway while allowing mandibular catch-up growth.

STEP 1: Positioning (First-Line)

  • Prone positioning - allows tongue to fall forward
  • Effective for 50-70% of mild-moderate cases
  • Requires continuous monitoring initially

STEP 2: Nasopharyngeal Airway (NPA)

  • Soft tube through nose to posterior pharynx
  • Bypasses tongue base obstruction
  • Bridge therapy to definitive management

STEP 3: Mandibular Distraction Osteogenesis (MDO)

  • Surgical lengthening of mandible
  • Osteotomy + gradual distraction (1mm/day)
  • Success rate: 85-95% avoid tracheostomy
  • Preferred over tracheostomy when feasible

STEP 4: Tracheostomy

  • Reserved for severe cases failing other interventions
  • Usually temporary - decannulation as mandible grows
  • Higher morbidity than MDO
AIRWAY IS PRIORITY #1! PRS infants are difficult airways - prepare for fiberoptic. Avoid sedation without airway control.

🍼 Feeding Management

  • Specialized cleft bottles (Mead Johnson, Pigeon, Haberman)
  • Upright positioning during feeds
  • Calorie fortification (24-27 cal/oz)
  • NG tube if oral intake inadequate
  • G-tube for prolonged feeding support
Goal: 100-120 kcal/kg/day. Feeds >30 minutes often expend more calories than gained.

📊 Prognosis

Mandibular Catch-Up Growth: In isolated PRS, the mandible often shows catch-up growth in the first 2-4 years. Some achieve near-normal size by adolescence.
  • ~40% still need orthognathic surgery at skeletal maturity
  • Nonsyndromic PRS: Generally good outcomes
  • Syndromic PRS: Outcomes depend on associated syndrome
  • VPI common - may need speech surgery

🧠 Test Your Understanding

A 1-day-old with micrognathia and U-shaped cleft palate has SpO2 of 75% supine but 95% prone. What is the FIRST step?

A. Prone positioning and monitoring
B. Immediate mandibular distraction
C. Tracheostomy
D. Cleft palate repair
Correct Answer: A

Prone positioning is first-line treatment for PRS airway obstruction. The SpO2 improvement from 75% to 95% demonstrates position-dependency. Management escalates from positioning → NPA → MDO → tracheostomy only if previous measures fail.

🧠 Question 2

An infant with PRS has severe myopia (-10 diopters). Which syndrome and what major risk?

A. Stickler syndrome - Retinal detachment
B. Velocardiofacial - Cardiac defects
C. Treacher Collins - Hearing loss
D. Nager syndrome - Limb anomalies
Correct Answer: A

Stickler syndrome is the most common PRS-associated syndrome and features severe myopia. The major risk is retinal detachment (12-50%). Urgent ophthalmology referral is essential.
