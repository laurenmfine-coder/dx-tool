📚 Cardiac Development Overview

The heart is the first functional organ in the embryo, beginning to beat at day 22 (week 3). It develops from splanchnic mesoderm in the cardiogenic area, forming a tube that loops, septates, and remodels into the four-chambered heart.

Embryonic Origins

MyocardiumSplanchnic (visceral) mesoderm
EndocardiumMesenchyme (from mesoderm)

🔑 Two Heart Fields: The First Heart Field (FHF) forms the left ventricle and parts of the atria. The Second Heart Field (SHF) adds the right ventricle, outflow tract, and parts of the atria. SHF defects → conotruncal anomalies (TOF, TGA, truncus arteriosus).

Key Timeline

Day 15-18: Cardiogenic Mesoderm

Splanchnic mesoderm forms the cardiogenic crescent anterior to the developing brain. Paired endocardial tubes form within this mesoderm.

Day 21-22: Heart Tube Formation

Lateral embryonic folding brings the paired tubes to the midline → fusion into a single heart tube. Beating begins at day 22.

Day 23-28: Cardiac Looping

The heart tube loops to the RIGHT (D-loop). This rightward looping is critical — failure causes dextrocardia or L-looping associated with transposition.

Weeks 4-5: Septation Begins

Atrial septation (septum primum → septum secundum), ventricular septation (muscular → membranous), and endocardial cushion formation.

Weeks 5-7: Outflow Tract Septation

Neural crest cells migrate to form the aorticopulmonary septum, dividing the truncus arteriosus into aorta and pulmonary trunk. The septum spirals (explaining why the aorta and PA wrap around each other).

Week 8: Septation Complete

Four-chambered heart with separated circulations. Fetal shunts (foramen ovale, ductus arteriosus, ductus venosus) maintain fetal circulation pattern.

⚡ Clinical Pearl: Cardiac defects are the most common congenital malformation, affecting ~1% of live births. VSD is the most common individual defect. Congenital heart disease is the leading cause of infant death from birth defects.

Cardiac Looping (D-Loop)

At day 23, the straight heart tube begins to loop. The ventricular portion bends to the RIGHT (dextro-loop or D-loop). This is controlled by left-right signaling pathways involving Nodal, Lefty, and Pitx2.

🔑 Why D-Looping Matters:
  • Normal D-loop → morphologic RV on the right, LV on the left
  • L-loop (reversed) → ventricular inversion (morphologic RV on left)
  • L-loop + L-TGA → "congenitally corrected TGA" (two wrongs make a right — circulation may be physiologically normal)

Left-Right Axis Determination

Established at the primitive node during gastrulation:

  1. Nodal cilia rotate clockwise → create leftward flow of morphogens
  2. Nodal (a TGF-β family member) concentrated on the LEFT side
  3. Nodal activates Pitx2 (left-side transcription factor)
  4. Pitx2 directs left-sided morphogenesis (heart looping, spleen position, lung lobation)
⚠️ Kartagener Syndrome: Defective nodal cilia (dynein arm defect) → situs inversus (mirror-image organs) + bronchiectasis + chronic sinusitis + male infertility. Same dynein defect affects respiratory cilia and sperm motility. Autosomal recessive.
Heterotaxy Syndromes: Disrupted laterality → ambiguous organ arrangement. Right isomerism (bilateral right-sidedness): asplenia, bilateral trilobed lungs, complex cardiac defects. Left isomerism (bilateral left-sidedness): polysplenia, bilateral bilobed lungs, often less severe cardiac defects.

🧱 Cardiac Septation

Atrial Septation

The single primitive atrium is divided by two septa that create a one-way valve allowing right-to-left shunting in fetal life:

Step 1: Septum Primum

Grows DOWN from the roof of the atrium toward the endocardial cushions. The shrinking opening below it = ostium primum (foramen primum). Before ostium primum closes, perforations appear in the upper septum primum → coalesce to form ostium secundum (foramen secundum).

Step 2: Septum Secundum

A thicker, more rigid septum grows DOWN to the RIGHT of septum primum. It covers most of ostium secundum but leaves an opening = foramen ovale. The thin septum primum acts as a one-way flap valve over the foramen ovale.

At Birth

Increased LA pressure (from pulmonary venous return) pushes septum primum against septum secundum → functionally closes foramen ovale. Anatomic fusion occurs over months.

⚡ Patent Foramen Ovale (PFO): Present in ~25% of adults. Usually asymptomatic, but can cause paradoxical embolism (venous thrombus crosses to arterial circulation → stroke). Associated with migraine with aura and decompression sickness in divers.

Ventricular Septation

The interventricular septum has two components:

  • Muscular septum (large, grows UP from the floor) — forms the majority of the IVS
  • Membranous septum (small, closes the remaining gap) — formed by fusion of: (1) endocardial cushions, (2) muscular septum, (3) aorticopulmonary septum (conotruncal ridges)
🔑 Membranous VSD: The most common type of VSD (~80%). Occurs because the membranous septum requires THREE components to fuse correctly. Failure of any one → persistent opening. Muscular VSDs are the second most common and have a higher rate of spontaneous closure.

Endocardial Cushions

Swellings of mesenchyme in the AV canal region. They contribute to:

  1. Atrial septation (septum primum fuses with them)
  2. Ventricular septation (membranous septum)
  3. AV valve formation (mitral and tricuspid leaflets)
  4. Outflow tract septation (contribute to aorticopulmonary septum)
⚠️ Endocardial Cushion Defect (AV Canal Defect / AVSD): Failure of endocardial cushion fusion → ostium primum ASD + inlet VSD + common AV valve = complete AVSD. Strongly associated with Down syndrome (Trisomy 21) — 40-50% of T21 patients have cardiac defects, and AVSD is the most characteristic.

🩺 Outflow Tract & Great Vessels

Aorticopulmonary Septation

The truncus arteriosus is divided into the aorta and pulmonary trunk by the aorticopulmonary (AP) septum. This septum forms from two sources:

  1. Truncal ridges — mesenchymal swellings within the truncus
  2. Neural crest cells — migrate from the pharyngeal arches to populate the ridges
🔑 The AP septum SPIRALS as it descends through the outflow tract. This spiral is why the aorta and pulmonary artery wrap around each other (aorta posterior and to the right, PA anterior and to the left). Failure of spiraling → Transposition of the Great Arteries (TGA).

Neural Crest Contribution

Cardiac neural crest cells (from rhombomeres 6-8, pharyngeal arches 3, 4, 6) are essential for:

  • AP septum formation (divides truncus into aorta + PA)
  • Aortic arch patterning (remodeling of pharyngeal arch arteries)
  • Parasympathetic innervation of the heart
⚠️ Neural Crest Ablation → Conotruncal Defects:
  • Persistent truncus arteriosus (PTA) — complete failure of AP septum formation → single outflow vessel
  • Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF) — unequal division of outflow tract (anterior deviation of AP septum)
  • Transposition of Great Arteries (TGA) — failure of AP septum spiraling
  • DiGeorge syndrome (22q11.2 deletion) — impaired neural crest migration → conotruncal defects + thymic/parathyroid aplasia

Coarctation of the Aorta

Narrowing of the aorta, typically at or just distal to the ductus arteriosus insertion (juxtaductal). Two theories:

  • Ductus tissue theory — ductal tissue extends into the aortic wall and constricts when the ductus closes
  • Hemodynamic theory — decreased aortic flow during fetal life (e.g., from left-sided obstruction) leads to poor growth of the aortic isthmus
⚡ Coarctation associations: Bicuspid aortic valve (in ~50%), Turner syndrome (45,X), Berry aneurysms of the circle of Willis. Classic presentation: hypertension in upper extremities + weak/absent femoral pulses + rib notching on CXR (from intercostal collateral circulation).

Aberrant Right Subclavian Artery

If the right 4th arch regresses abnormally, the right subclavian arises from the distal aortic arch and courses behind the esophagus → dysphagia lusoria (difficulty swallowing from posterior esophageal compression). Most common aortic arch anomaly (~1% of population). Usually asymptomatic.

🩸 Fetal Circulation

Fetal circulation is designed to bypass the non-functional lungs and deliver oxygenated blood from the placenta to the systemic circulation via three shunts:

Three Fetal Shunts

ShuntFunctionCloses to FormClosure Mechanism
Ductus VenosusBypasses liver (umbilical vein → IVC directly)Ligamentum venosumLoss of placental blood flow → ↓ prostaglandins
Foramen OvaleRight-to-left atrial shunt (bypasses lungs)Fossa ovalis↑ LA pressure from pulmonary venous return presses septum primum against septum secundum
Ductus ArteriosusPA → descending aorta shunt (bypasses lungs)Ligamentum arteriosum↑ O₂ + ↓ prostaglandins → smooth muscle contraction

Fetal Blood Flow Pattern

Placenta (highest O₂) → Umbilical vein (SpO₂ ~80%) → Ductus venosus (bypasses liver) → IVC → Right atrium In the RA, oxygenated IVC blood is directed by the crista dividens: → Through foramen ovale → LA → LV → Ascending aorta → Brain + upper body (receives most oxygenated blood) Deoxygenated SVC blood: → RA → RV → Pulmonary artery → 90% through ductus arteriosus → Descending aorta → Lower body + umbilical arteries → Placenta → 10% to lungs (high PVR keeps lung flow minimal)
🔑 Oxygen Saturations in the Fetus:
  • Umbilical vein: ~80% (highest — coming from placenta)
  • Ascending aorta / brain: ~65% (mixed with some deoxygenated IVC blood)
  • Descending aorta: ~58% (mixed after ductus arteriosus joins)
  • Umbilical arteries: ~58% (lowest — returning to placenta)

Transition at Birth

  1. First breath → alveoli expand → ↓↓ pulmonary vascular resistance → ↑↑ pulmonary blood flow
  2. ↑ LA pressure (from pulmonary venous return) → functionally closes foramen ovale
  3. Cord clamping → ↑ SVR + ↓ prostaglandins → ductus venosus closes
  4. ↑ O₂ + ↓ PGE₂ → ductus arteriosus constricts (functional closure in 12-24h, anatomic by 2-3 weeks)
⚠️ Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA): The ductus stays open, creating a left-to-right shunt (aorta → PA). Common in premature infants (immature smooth muscle, high prostaglandin levels). Treatment: indomethacin/ibuprofen (prostaglandin synthesis inhibitors → close the ductus). Conversely, PGE₁ (alprostadil) keeps the ductus open when needed (duct-dependent lesions like TGA, critical coarctation, pulmonary atresia).
✅ Duct-Dependent Lesions: Some critical CHDs depend on the ductus arteriosus to maintain either pulmonary or systemic blood flow. These neonates collapse when the ductus closes (typically day 2-5). PGE₁ infusion is lifesaving while awaiting surgery. Examples: critical coarctation, interrupted aortic arch, pulmonary atresia, HLHS, critical aortic stenosis, TGA.

⚠️ Congenital Heart Defects

Classified by the embryologic mechanism and the resulting hemodynamic consequence.

Left-to-Right Shunts (Acyanotic — initially)

DefectEmbryologyKey Features
VSDFailed fusion of membranous septum (IVS) — requires endocardial cushions + muscular septum + AP septumMost common CHD overall. Harsh holosystolic murmur at LLSB. Small muscular VSDs often close spontaneously.
ASDSee Septation tab — ostium secundum most commonWide, fixed split S2. Often asymptomatic until adulthood. Right heart volume overload.
PDAFailure of ductus arteriosus closure (↑ O₂ + ↓ PGE₂ normally close it)Continuous "machinery" murmur. Widened pulse pressure. Common in prematurity. Close with indomethacin.
AVSDEndocardial cushion defect — failed cushion fusionASD + VSD + common AV valve. Strong T21 association. Common in Down syndrome.
🔑 Eisenmenger Syndrome: Long-standing left-to-right shunt → chronic pulmonary overcirculation → pulmonary vascular remodeling → irreversible pulmonary hypertension → shunt REVERSES to right-to-left → cyanosis. Once Eisenmenger develops, the defect is generally inoperable (closing it would cause RV failure against fixed high PVR).

Right-to-Left Shunts (Cyanotic)

DefectEmbryologyKey Features
Tetralogy of FallotAnterosuperior deviation of AP septum → (1) RV outflow obstruction, (2) overriding aorta, (3) VSD, (4) RV hypertrophyMost common cyanotic CHD. "Tet spells" (hypercyanotic episodes). Boot-shaped heart on CXR. Squatting increases SVR → forces blood through lungs.
TGA (D-type)Failure of AP septum spiraling → aorta arises from RV, PA from LV → parallel circuitsCyanosis at birth. Incompatible with life unless mixing occurs (PFO, PDA, or VSD). "Egg on a string" CXR. Arterial switch (Jatene) is definitive repair.

DiGeorge Syndrome (22q11.2 Deletion)

⚠️ CATCH-22 Mnemonic:
  • Cardiac defects (conotruncal: interrupted aortic arch, truncus arteriosus, TOF, VSD)
  • Abnormal facies (long face, small jaw, low-set ears)
  • Thymic aplasia/hypoplasia (T-cell deficiency → immunodeficiency)
  • Cleft palate (velopharyngeal insufficiency)
  • Hypocalcemia (absent/hypoplastic parathyroids)

Caused by failure of 3rd and 4th pharyngeal pouch development (neural crest dependent). Most common microdeletion syndrome (~1/4000 births).

📝 Test Your Knowledge

Question 1

The aorticopulmonary septum is formed primarily by cells migrating from which embryonic structure?

A) Endocardial cushions
B) Neural crest (pharyngeal arches 3, 4, 6)
C) Second heart field mesoderm
D) Septum transversum
Neural crest cells from rhombomeres 6-8 migrate through pharyngeal arches 3, 4, and 6 to form the AP septum. Failure → persistent truncus arteriosus, TOF, TGA. DiGeorge syndrome (22q11.2 del) impairs this neural crest migration.

Question 2

A newborn with Down syndrome has an echocardiogram showing a common AV valve with ostium primum ASD and inlet VSD. Which embryologic structure failed to develop properly?

A) Septum secundum
B) Muscular interventricular septum
C) Endocardial cushions
D) Bulbus cordis
Complete AVSD (AV canal defect) = endocardial cushion failure → ostium primum ASD + inlet VSD + common AV valve. Strongly associated with Trisomy 21. Endocardial cushions contribute to atrial septation, ventricular septation, and AV valve formation.

Question 3

Which fetal shunt is kept open by prostaglandins and closed by indomethacin?

A) Foramen ovale
B) Ductus arteriosus
C) Ductus venosus
D) Umbilical vein
The ductus arteriosus is kept patent by PGE₂ (and low O₂). After birth, ↑O₂ + ↓PGE₂ → constriction. Indomethacin/ibuprofen (COX inhibitors) block PG synthesis → close a PDA. PGE₁ (alprostadil) keeps it open for duct-dependent lesions.

Question 4

D-transposition of the great arteries results from failure of which embryologic process?

A) Endocardial cushion fusion
B) Cardiac looping
C) Aorticopulmonary septum spiraling
D) Atrial septation
The AP septum normally spirals as it descends, so the aorta connects to the LV and PA to the RV. Failure of spiraling → aorta from RV + PA from LV = D-TGA (parallel circuits). Incompatible with life without mixing (PFO, PDA, VSD). Arterial switch (Jatene) is definitive.

Question 5

The left 4th aortic arch artery gives rise to which adult structure?

A) Left common carotid artery
B) Aortic arch (between left common carotid and left subclavian)
C) Left pulmonary artery
D) Ductus arteriosus
Left 4th arch = aortic arch. Right 4th arch = proximal right subclavian. 3rd arches = common carotids. Left 6th arch = left PA + ductus arteriosus. Mnemonic: 4th arch = 4 letters in "arch."

Question 6

A neonate presents with cyanosis. CXR shows an "egg on a string" appearance with a narrow mediastinum. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A) Tetralogy of Fallot
B) D-Transposition of the Great Arteries
C) Total Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Return
D) Truncus arteriosus
"Egg on a string" = narrow mediastinum (because the great arteries are stacked anteroposteriorly instead of side by side) with an egg-shaped cardiac silhouette. Classic for D-TGA. TOF = boot-shaped heart. TAPVR (supracardiac) = snowman sign.
