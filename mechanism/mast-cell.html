🔬 Mast Cell Biology & Pathophysiology

Mast cells are tissue-resident immune cells derived from hematopoietic stem cells. They complete maturation in peripheral tissues under the influence of stem cell factor (SCF) binding to the KIT receptor (CD117). Mast cells are strategically located at host-environment interfaces: skin, airways, GI tract, and around blood vessels.

Key Mast Cell Mediators

CategoryMediatorsClinical EffectsTiming
Preformed (granules) Histamine, Tryptase, Heparin, Chymase, TNF-α Immediate: vasodilation, pruritus, bronchoconstriction, ↑vascular permeability Seconds to minutes
Newly synthesized lipids PGD₂, LTC₄/D₄/E₄, PAF Bronchoconstriction, mucus secretion, vasodilation, hypotension Minutes to hours
Cytokines/Chemokines IL-4, IL-5, IL-6, IL-13, TNF-α, VEGF Late-phase inflammation, tissue remodeling, recruitment of eosinophils Hours to days
🔑 Clinical Pearl: Why Tryptase?

Tryptase is relatively mast cell-specific (also in basophils but at much lower levels). It's stored preformed in granules and released during degranulation. Serum tryptase is the most useful biomarker for mast cell activation because: (1) it correlates with mast cell burden, (2) it elevates acutely during reactions, and (3) baseline levels help distinguish MCAS from mastocytosis.

⚠️ MRGPRX2: The "Pseudoallergy" Receptor

The MRGPRX2 receptor on mast cells can be directly activated by certain drugs (fluoroquinolones, neuromuscular blockers, opioids, vancomycin) causing histamine release WITHOUT IgE involvement. This explains "anaphylactoid" reactions on first exposure to these drugs. It also explains why skin testing may be negative despite clinical reactivity.

📋 MCAS Diagnostic Criteria (2019 Consensus)

Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS) requires meeting ALL THREE criteria. This prevents overdiagnosis in patients with nonspecific symptoms.

MCAS Diagnostic Criteria - ALL 3 Required

1️⃣ CLINICAL: Typical Mast Cell Mediator Symptoms

Recurrent, episodic symptoms involving ≥2 organ systems:

  • Skin: Flushing, pruritus, urticaria, angioedema
  • GI: Abdominal cramping, diarrhea, nausea/vomiting
  • Cardiovascular: Hypotension, tachycardia, presyncope/syncope
  • Respiratory: Wheezing, stridor, nasal congestion
  • Neurologic: Headache, cognitive dysfunction ("brain fog")
2️⃣ LABORATORY: Documented Mast Cell Mediator Elevation

Increase in validated mast cell mediator during symptomatic episode:

  • Serum tryptase: Increase ≥20% + 2 ng/mL above baseline
  • 24-hr urine: N-methylhistamine, PGD₂ metabolites, or LTE₄ (alternative if tryptase unavailable)
3️⃣ RESPONSE: Improvement with Mast Cell-Targeted Therapy

Clinical improvement with one or more of:

  • H1 antihistamines
  • H2 antihistamines
  • Mast cell stabilizers (cromolyn)
  • Leukotriene modifiers
💡 The "20% + 2" Rule

Acute tryptase must rise to (1.2 × baseline) + 2 ng/mL to be considered diagnostic. Example: If baseline is 5 ng/mL, acute must be ≥ (1.2 × 5) + 2 = 8 ng/mL. This accounts for both relative change and absolute threshold, reducing false positives from minor fluctuations.

🔑 Timing Matters

Optimal tryptase timing: Draw within 15 minutes to 4 hours of symptom onset. Tryptase peaks at 1-2 hours and returns to baseline within 6-12 hours. A "normal" tryptase drawn >6 hours post-reaction does NOT rule out mast cell activation. Also obtain a baseline tryptase ≥24 hours after symptoms resolve.

🔍 Mastocytosis Classification (WHO 2022)

Mastocytosis is a clonal mast cell disorder characterized by abnormal mast cell accumulation in tissues. The KIT D816V mutation is present in >90% of systemic mastocytosis cases.

WHO Classification

CategoryFeaturesPrognosis
Cutaneous Mastocytosis Skin only (urticaria pigmentosa, MPCM, DCM). No systemic involvement. Common in children. Excellent. Pediatric forms often resolve at puberty.
Indolent Systemic Mastocytosis (ISM) Meets SM criteria. No B or C findings. Most common adult form. Near-normal life expectancy. Risk of anaphylaxis.
Smoldering SM (SSM) 2+ B-findings (high mast cell burden). No C-findings (organ damage). Risk of progression. Close monitoring needed.
SM with Associated Hematologic Neoplasm (SM-AHN) SM + myeloid neoplasm (MDS, MPN, AML, etc.) Prognosis depends on associated neoplasm.
Aggressive SM (ASM) 1+ C-findings (organ damage from mast cell infiltration) Median survival 3-5 years. Requires cytoreductive therapy.
Mast Cell Leukemia (MCL) ≥20% mast cells in bone marrow aspirate smear Poor prognosis. Median survival <1 year without treatment.

WHO Criteria for Systemic Mastocytosis

Diagnosis requires: 1 Major + 1 Minor criterion, OR 3 Minor criteria

MAJOR CRITERION

Multifocal dense infiltrates of mast cells (≥15 cells in aggregates) in bone marrow or other extracutaneous organ

MINOR CRITERIA (need 1 with major, or 3 alone)
  • >25% spindle-shaped or atypical mast cells in bone marrow or other tissue
  • KIT D816V mutation in bone marrow, blood, or other extracutaneous tissue
  • Mast cells expressing CD25 and/or CD2 (aberrant expression)
  • Serum baseline tryptase persistently >20 ng/mL (not applicable if associated myeloid neoplasm)

B-Findings vs C-Findings

B-Findings (Burden)

Indicate high mast cell burden but NO organ damage

  • Bone marrow biopsy >30% mast cell infiltration
  • Serum tryptase >200 ng/mL
  • Hepatomegaly without impairment
  • Splenomegaly without hypersplenism
  • Lymphadenopathy (≥2 cm)

C-Findings (organ damage)

Indicate ORGAN DYSFUNCTION from mast cell infiltration

  • Cytopenias: ANC <1000, Hgb <10, Plt <100K
  • Hepatomegaly WITH impaired function (ascites, elevated LFTs)
  • Splenomegaly WITH hypersplenism
  • Malabsorption with weight loss
  • Osteolytic lesions with pathologic fractures

💊 Treatment: Stepwise Approach

Treatment focuses on symptom control through mast cell mediator blockade. Cytoreductive therapy is reserved for advanced/aggressive forms.

1
Trigger Avoidance + H1 Antihistamine
First-line for all patients. Identify and avoid triggers (foods, medications, physical factors). Start with second-generation H1 blocker at standard dose (cetirizine 10mg, loratadine 10mg, fexofenadine 180mg daily).
2
Increase H1 + Add H2 Antihistamine
Escalate H1 antihistamine up to 4× standard dose (e.g., cetirizine 10mg QID). Add H2 blocker (famotidine 20mg BID) for GI symptoms and enhanced histamine blockade.
3
Add Mast Cell Stabilizer
Cromolyn sodium (oral): 100-200mg QID, 30 min before meals. Works locally in GI tract. Particularly helpful for GI symptoms (cramping, diarrhea, nausea).
4
Add Leukotriene Modifier
Montelukast 10mg daily or zileuton 600mg QID. Blocks leukotriene pathway (LTC₄, LTD₄, LTE₄). Helpful for flushing, respiratory symptoms, and some patients with GI symptoms.
5
Add Aspirin (select patients)
Low-dose aspirin (81-325mg) can help with flushing by blocking PGD₂. Caution: May trigger symptoms in some patients - start with observed challenge. Contraindicated in NSAID-sensitive patients.
6
Omalizumab (Anti-IgE)
Omalizumab for refractory symptoms, especially recurrent anaphylaxis. May work even in non-IgE-mediated mast cell activation. Dosing: 150-300mg SC q2-4 weeks.
7
Cytoreductive Therapy (Advanced SM only)
For aggressive SM, SM-AHN, or MCL: Midostaurin (KIT inhibitor), interferon-α, cladribine, or avapritinib. Referral to hematology/oncology required.
🚨 Anaphylaxis Preparedness

ALL patients with mastocytosis or MCAS should carry epinephrine auto-injectors. Risk of severe anaphylaxis is significantly elevated, especially with Hymenoptera stings (up to 30% risk in mastocytosis). Consider venom immunotherapy for all SM patients with venom allergy (regardless of skin test results). Medical alert bracelet recommended.
