🎗️ Testicular Cancer

The Most Common Solid Malignancy in Young Men

📚 Overview

Testicular cancer is the most common solid tumor in men aged 15-35. The majority (95%) are germ cell tumors (GCTs). Despite being aggressive, it is highly curable even with metastatic disease.

Epidemiology

ParameterValue
Peak age15-35 years
Germ cell tumors95% of testicular cancers
Cure rate (overall)>95%
Cure rate (metastatic)~80%
Risk with cryptorchidism5-10x increased

Risk Factors

  • Cryptorchidism - 5-10x risk (even after orchiopexy)
  • Personal history - 2-3% risk in contralateral testis
  • Family history
  • Testicular dysgenesis/DSD
  • Klinefelter syndrome (mediastinal GCT)
  • HIV infection

🔬 Classification

Germ Cell Tumors (95%)

SEMINOMA (40-50%)

  • Peak age: 30-40 years (slightly older)
  • Radiosensitive and chemosensitive
  • Excellent prognosis
  • Marker: May elevate hCG (10%), NEVER AFP
  • Histology: Sheets of uniform cells, "fried egg" appearance

NON-SEMINOMATOUS GERM CELL TUMORS (NSGCT) (50-60%)

More aggressive than seminoma; may elevate AFP and/or hCG

NSGCT TypeFeaturesMarkers
Embryonal CarcinomaMost undifferentiated; aggressive↑hCG, ↑AFP variable
Yolk Sac TumorMost common in children; Schiller-Duval bodies↑AFP (always)
ChoriocarcinomaMost aggressive; hemorrhagic; early hematogenous spread↑↑hCG
TeratomaAll 3 germ layers; mature vs immature; chemo-resistantNormal markers
Mixed GCTCombination of above (most common presentation)Variable

Non-Germ Cell Tumors (5%)

TypeNotes
Leydig cell tumorProduces testosterone/estrogen; gynecomastia; usually benign
Sertoli cell tumorUsually benign
LymphomaMost common testicular tumor in men >60
Age-Based Differential:
  • Children: Yolk sac tumor most common
  • Young adults (15-35): Mixed GCT, seminoma
  • Older men (>60): Lymphoma most common

🔍 Clinical Presentation

Typical Presentation

  • Painless testicular mass - most common
  • Testicular heaviness or fullness
  • Dull ache in scrotum or lower abdomen
  • Acute pain (10%) - hemorrhage into tumor
  • Incidental finding on imaging or after trauma

Metastatic Symptoms

  • Back pain (retroperitoneal lymphadenopathy)
  • Dyspnea, cough (pulmonary mets)
  • Neck mass (supraclavicular nodes)
  • Gynecomastia (hCG-secreting tumors)
⚠️ Any solid testicular mass is cancer until proven otherwise!
Transillumination negative (solid) = concerning for malignancy

Tumor Markers

MarkerHalf-lifeElevated In
AFP5-7 daysYolk sac, embryonal, mixed; NEVER pure seminoma
hCG24-36 hoursChoriocarcinoma (high), seminoma (10%), embryonal
LDH-Nonspecific; correlates with tumor burden
Key Marker Rules:
  • Elevated AFP = NSGCT (even if pathology says seminoma)
  • Pure seminoma NEVER elevates AFP
  • Markers should normalize after orchiectomy (based on half-life)
  • Persistent elevation = residual disease
⚠️ NEVER perform trans-scrotal biopsy or orchiectomy!
This disrupts lymphatic drainage and can upstage disease. Always use inguinal approach with early cord clamping.

💊 Treatment

Stage I (Confined to Testis)

TypeOptions
SeminomaSurveillance (preferred), single-agent carboplatin, or radiation
NSGCTSurveillance (if low risk), RPLND, or adjuvant BEP x1-2

Stage II-III (Metastatic)

BEP Chemotherapy:
  • Bleomycin
  • Etoposide
  • Platinum (cisplatin)
3-4 cycles depending on risk category. Highly effective!

Post-Chemotherapy Residual Mass

  • Seminoma: Observe if <3cm and PET negative; surgery if PET positive or growing
  • NSGCT: RPLND (retroperitoneal lymph node dissection) for residual mass >1cm

Retroperitoneal Lymph Node Dissection (RPLND)

  • Can be primary treatment (stage I NSGCT) or post-chemo
  • Nerve-sparing technique preserves ejaculation
  • Therapeutic and staging procedure

📋 Key Clinical Pearls

High-Yield Points:
  • Most common solid tumor in young men (15-35)
  • Painless mass = cancer until proven otherwise
  • NEVER trans-scrotal approach; always inguinal
  • AFP elevated = NSGCT (even if biopsy says seminoma)
  • Highly curable even with metastatic disease (BEP chemo)
  • Self-examination and awareness are key
  • Cryptorchidism increases risk 5-10x

🧠 Quick Quiz

A 28-year-old presents with a painless testicular mass. AFP is elevated at 500 ng/mL. Pathology after orchiectomy shows "pure seminoma." What is the correct interpretation?

A. Pure seminoma with paraneoplastic AFP elevation
B. Must contain non-seminomatous elements (treat as NSGCT)
C. Lab error, repeat AFP
D. Seminoma with yolk sac differentiation
Correct: B
Pure seminoma NEVER produces AFP. If AFP is elevated, there MUST be non-seminomatous elements (likely yolk sac tumor or embryonal carcinoma) even if not seen on pathology. Treat as NSGCT, not seminoma.
