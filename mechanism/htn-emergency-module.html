🔴 Hypertensive Emergency

Master the pathophysiology of end-organ damage, recognize clinical presentations, and understand evidence-based BP targets for this critical diagnosis.

⏱️ 20 min 📊 High Yield 🎯 Emergency Medicine

📋 Definitions: Emergency vs Urgency

🚨 Hypertensive Emergency

Definition: Severely elevated BP WITH acute end-organ damage

Management: IV antihypertensives, ICU admission, controlled BP reduction

Requires immediate treatment

⚠️ Hypertensive Urgency

Definition: Severely elevated BP WITHOUT acute end-organ damage

Management: Oral antihypertensives, outpatient follow-up, gradual reduction

Less time-sensitive

🎯 Key Distinction

The absolute BP number matters less than the presence of end-organ damage. A patient with BP 220/120 and no symptoms may be urgency, while BP 180/110 with pulmonary edema is an emergency. Always assess for end-organ damage!

🔬 Pathophysiology

1
Failure of Autoregulation
Normally, cerebral and renal vessels autoregulate to maintain constant blood flow despite BP changes. In hypertensive emergency, BP exceeds the autoregulatory range.
2
Endothelial Dysfunction
Mechanical stress damages endothelium → loss of vasodilatory capacity → further vasoconstriction → vicious cycle of worsening hypertension.
3
Fibrinoid Necrosis
Severe pressure damages arteriolar walls → plasma proteins leak into vessel wall → "onion-skinning" appearance on histology.
4
End-Organ Ischemia
Tissue hypoperfusion despite high BP due to microvascular damage → organ dysfunction manifests clinically.

⚠️ The Autoregulation Curve Shifts

In chronic hypertension, the autoregulation curve shifts rightward. These patients tolerate higher BPs but are vulnerable to hypoperfusion if BP is lowered too quickly. This is why we target 10-20% reduction in the first hour, not normalization!

💊 Management Principles

🎯 BP Reduction Targets

General rule: Reduce MAP by 10-20% in the first hour, then gradually to 160/100-110 over the next 2-6 hours. Avoid rapid normalization which risks watershed infarction!

Condition-Specific Targets

Condition Initial Target Preferred Agent Notes
Aortic Dissection SBP <120, HR <60 Esmolol + Nitroprusside BB first to prevent reflex tachy
Acute Ischemic Stroke <185/110 if tPA candidate Nicardipine, Labetalol Don't treat if not tPA candidate unless >220/120
Hemorrhagic Stroke SBP 140 (per INTERACT2) Nicardipine, Labetalol More aggressive than ischemic
Acute Pulmonary Edema Symptom-guided Nitroprusside, Nitroglycerin Afterload reduction key
ACS Symptom-guided Nitroglycerin, BB Reduce O2 demand
Eclampsia/Preeclampsia SBP <160, DBP <110 Hydralazine, Labetalol Give MgSO4 for seizure prophylaxis
Pheochromocytoma Gradual reduction Phentolamine Never BB alone (unopposed alpha)

IV Antihypertensive Agents

💉 Nicardipine
  • CCB - vasodilator
  • Predictable, titratable
  • Good for most indications
  • Start 5 mg/hr, titrate by 2.5 mg/hr
💉 Labetalol
  • Combined α/β blocker
  • No reflex tachycardia
  • Good for dissection, pregnancy
  • Bolus 20mg or infusion
💉 Esmolol
  • Ultra-short acting β1 blocker
  • Ideal for dissection (rate control)
  • Highly titratable
  • Load 500 mcg/kg, infuse 50-200
💉 Nitroprusside
  • Arterial + venous dilator
  • Most potent, fastest onset
  • Cyanide toxicity risk
  • Requires A-line monitoring

👥 Special Considerations

🤰 Pregnancy (Eclampsia)
  • Labetalol or hydralazine first-line
  • MgSO4 for seizure prophylaxis
  • Avoid ACEi, ARBs, nitroprusside
  • Definitive treatment = delivery
💊 Cocaine/Sympathomimetics
  • Benzodiazepines first!
  • Avoid pure beta-blockers (unopposed alpha)
  • Phentolamine for refractory HTN
  • Nicardipine is safe

📝 Key Takeaways

🎯 Must Know
  • Emergency = elevated BP + end-organ damage
  • Urgency = elevated BP alone
  • Always look for target organ damage
  • The BP number alone doesn't define it
💊 Treatment Pearls
  • Reduce MAP 10-20% in first hour
  • Avoid rapid normalization
  • Condition-specific targets matter
  • Nicardipine is versatile first-line
