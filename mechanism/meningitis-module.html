Meningitis & Encephalitis

CNS Infections: Rapid Recognition, CSF Analysis, and Time-Critical Treatment
⏱️ ~45 minutes 📚 16 concepts ❓ 6 MCQs
1 Classification & Etiology
🧠 Meningitis vs Encephalitis

Meningitis: Inflammation of the meninges

  • Headache, neck stiffness, photophobia
  • Fever
  • Mental status often preserved early (unless severe)

Encephalitis: Inflammation of brain parenchyma

  • Altered mental status, confusion, personality changes
  • Seizures
  • Focal neurological deficits
  • Less prominent meningeal signs

Meningoencephalitis: Both - very common in practice

💎 Don't Forget Listeria!

Add ampicillin for Listeria coverage in:

  • Age >50
  • Immunocompromised (HIV, transplant, steroids)
  • Alcoholism
  • Pregnancy
  • Neonates

Listeria is NOT covered by cephalosporins!

2 Clinical Presentation
🩺 Classic Triad (Often Absent!)

The classic triad of fever + neck stiffness + altered mental status is present in only ~45% of bacterial meningitis cases.

However: ~95% have at least 2 of the following 4:

  • Headache
  • Fever
  • Neck stiffness
  • Altered mental status
🔍 Physical Exam Signs

Kernig's sign: Hip flexed 90°, pain/resistance on knee extension

Brudzinski's sign: Passive neck flexion → involuntary hip/knee flexion

Jolt accentuation: Headache worse with horizontal head rotation (2-3x/sec)

Sensitivity: These signs have poor sensitivity (~5-30%) but reasonable specificity. Absence does NOT rule out meningitis!

🚨 Red Flags for Bacterial Meningitis
  • Rapidly progressive symptoms
  • Petechial/purpuric rash (think N. meningitidis)
  • Seizures
  • Focal neurological deficits
  • Papilledema (increased ICP)
  • Immunocompromised state
3 Diagnostic Approach
🚨 Suspected Bacterial Meningitis Protocol
1
Blood cultures × 2 - before antibiotics if possible (don't delay abx >30 min)
2
Empiric antibiotics + dexamethasone - give IMMEDIATELY if bacterial meningitis suspected
3
CT head - ONLY if indication (see below). Don't delay LP for CT if no contraindication.
4
Lumbar puncture - send CSF for cell count, protein, glucose, Gram stain, culture, +/- PCR panel
🧠 When to CT Before LP

CT head before LP is indicated if ANY of the following (concern for mass lesion/herniation):

  • Immunocompromised state
  • History of CNS disease (mass, stroke, infection)
  • New-onset seizure (within 1 week)
  • Papilledema
  • Altered consciousness
  • Focal neurological deficit

If none present → go straight to LP (don't delay for CT)

💎 CSF Pearls

CSF:Serum glucose ratio <0.4 is highly suggestive of bacterial meningitis

Gram stain sensitivity: ~60-90% for bacterial meningitis (higher if untreated)

PCR panels: FilmArray can detect multiple pathogens in ~1 hour - very helpful!

Traumatic tap: Subtract 1 WBC for every 700-1000 RBCs to correct

5 Treatment
💊 Empiric Antibiotic Therapy
PopulationEmpiric Regimen
Adults 18-50Ceftriaxone 2g q12h + Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg q8-12h
Adults >50, immunocompromisedCeftriaxone + Vancomycin + Ampicillin 2g q4h (Listeria)
Post-neurosurgery/traumaVancomycin + Cefepime (or meropenem)
NeonatesAmpicillin + Cefotaxime (or gentamicin)
💉 Adjunctive Dexamethasone

Dose: 0.15 mg/kg q6h × 4 days

Timing: Give WITH or BEFORE first dose of antibiotics

Benefit: Reduces mortality and neurological sequelae in S. pneumoniae meningitis

Mechanism: Reduces inflammation from bacterial lysis

Discontinue if: Non-bacterial cause confirmed, or pathogen other than S. pneumoniae

⏰ TIME IS BRAIN

Every hour of delay in antibiotics increases mortality.

If bacterial meningitis is suspected:

  • Give antibiotics + dexamethasone IMMEDIATELY
  • Don't wait for CT, LP, or lab results
  • Blood cultures before antibiotics if possible, but NEVER delay antibiotics >30 minutes
6 Viral Meningitis & HSV Encephalitis
🦠 Viral Meningitis

Most common cause: Enteroviruses (coxsackie, echovirus)

Features:

  • Usually self-limited (resolves in 7-10 days)
  • Less severe than bacterial
  • CSF: Lymphocytic pleocytosis, normal glucose

Treatment: Supportive care

🔴 HSV Encephalitis - A Must-Not-Miss

HSV-1 encephalitis is FATAL if untreated (70% mortality) but treatable!

Clinical clues:

  • Altered mental status, personality changes
  • Temporal lobe seizures
  • Fever + focal neurological signs
  • Olfactory/gustatory hallucinations

MRI: Temporal lobe involvement (often bilateral)

Treatment: Acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h × 14-21 days

Key: Start acyclovir empirically if encephalitis suspected - don't wait for HSV PCR results!
