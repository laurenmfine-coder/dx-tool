🫀 Coronary Anatomy Foundation

Understanding coronary anatomy is essential for predicting which myocardial territories are affected in ACS.

🔴

Left Coronary System

Left Main (LM) → bifurcates into:

  • LAD — Anterior wall, anterior septum, apex
  • LCx — Lateral and posterior walls

The LAD is called "the widow maker" due to its large territory.

🔵

Right Coronary System

Right Coronary Artery (RCA) supplies:

  • Inferior wall
  • Right ventricle
  • AV node (in 70% of patients)

RCA occlusion may cause heart block due to AV node involvement.

💡
Clinical Pearl

Proximal LAD occlusion affects such a large territory of myocardium that it frequently causes sudden cardiac death or severe heart failure — hence "the widow maker."

🔄

🔬 Atherosclerosis: The Underlying Disease

Atherosclerosis begins with endothelial dysfunction and lipid accumulation, progressing over decades.

Plaque Formation Stages

● Fatty Streak

Earliest lesion — lipid-laden macrophages (foam cells) accumulate in the intima.

● Fibroatheroma

Lipid core covered by fibrous cap — stable but may become flow-limiting.

● Vulnerable Plaque

Thin fibrous cap, large lipid core, inflammatory cells — prone to rupture.

● Ruptured Plaque

Cap disruption exposes thrombogenic core → acute coronary event.

Vulnerable Plaque Features

High Yield
  • Thin fibrous cap (<65 μm)
  • Large necrotic lipid core (>40% volume)
  • High macrophage density
  • Decreased smooth muscle cells
  • Neovascularization within plaque
🎯
Key Point

Most MIs occur at sites of <50% stenosis. Plaque composition matters more than plaque size — vulnerable plaques may not cause symptoms until rupture.

💥 Plaque Rupture and Thrombosis

The acute event: from rupture to coronary occlusion in minutes.

⚠️
Critical Concept: Time = Myocardium

Once coronary occlusion occurs, myocardial necrosis begins within 20-40 minutes and progresses as a "wavefront" from endocardium to epicardium. After 6 hours of complete occlusion, most of the at-risk myocardium is irreversibly injured.

🩸

Thrombosis Cascade

  1. Tissue factor exposure — from necrotic core and macrophages
  2. Platelet adhesion — bind to exposed collagen
  3. Platelet activation — release ADP, TXA2
  4. Coagulation cascade — thrombin generation
  5. Fibrin formation — stabilizes thrombus
🔀

Other Mechanisms

  • Plaque erosion (~30%) — endothelial denudation; more common in women
  • Calcified nodule (~5%) — calcified plaque protrudes
  • Coronary spasm — vasospasm without fixed stenosis
  • Type 2 MI — supply-demand mismatch (tachycardia, hypotension, anemia)

  • Arbab-Zadeh A. JACC Cardiovasc Imaging. 2015 ↗

📊 The ACS Spectrum

ACS represents a continuum based on degree and duration of coronary occlusion.

Condition Coronary Status ECG Finding Troponin
Unstable Angina Partial/transient occlusion ST depression, T-wave Δ, or normal Negative ❌
NSTEMI Partial occlusion with microemboli ST depression, T-wave inversion Positive ✓
STEMI Complete occlusion ST elevation ≥1mm in 2+ contiguous leads Positive ✓
🎯
Key Distinction

STEMI = complete occlusion → immediate reperfusion needed (PCI <90 min or lysis <30 min)
NSTEMI/UA = partial occlusion → risk stratification, then early invasive vs. conservative strategy
