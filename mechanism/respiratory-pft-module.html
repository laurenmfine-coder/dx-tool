>Capacities are combinations of two or more volumes:

Capacity Components Normal Adult Value Clinical Significance
Total Lung Capacity (TLC) TV + IRV + ERV + RV ~6000 mL ↓ in restriction, ↑ in hyperinflation
Vital Capacity (VC) TV + IRV + ERV ~4800 mL ↓ in both obstruction and restriction
Functional Residual Capacity (FRC) ERV + RV ~2300 mL ↑ in air trapping/hyperinflation
Inspiratory Capacity (IC) TV + IRV ~3500 mL ↓ in hyperinflation

2. Spirometry: The Cornerstone of PFT Interpretation

Key Spirometric Parameters

Essential Spirometry Measurements
1
FVC (Forced Vital Capacity) — Total volume exhaled during a maximal forced expiration
2
FEV₁ (Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 second) — Volume exhaled in the first second of FVC maneuver
3
FEV₁/FVC Ratio — The percentage of FVC exhaled in 1 second (normally ≥0.70 or 70%)
4
FEF₂₅₋₇₅ — Forced expiratory flow at 25-75% of FVC (effort-independent, reflects small airways)

Interpreting the FEV₁/FVC Ratio

Clinical Pearl: The FEV₁/FVC ratio is the FIRST number to evaluate. It determines whether the pattern is obstructive, restrictive, or mixed.
Pattern FEV₁/FVC FEV₁ FVC TLC
Normal ≥0.70 (or LLN) Normal Normal Normal
Obstructive <0.70 ↓↓ Normal or ↓ Normal or ↑
Restrictive ≥0.70 (often ↑) ↓ ↓↓ ↓ (required)
Mixed <0.70 ↓↓ ↓↓ ↓
Important: A low FVC alone does NOT confirm restriction. TLC measurement is required because FVC can be low in severe obstruction due to air trapping.

3. Flow-Volume Loop Interpretation

The flow-volume loop provides a visual representation of airflow during forced expiration and inspiration, plotted against lung volume.

Flow-Volume Loop Patterns
Normal
Obstructive (Scooped)
Restrictive (Narrow)

Pattern Recognition

  • Normal: Triangular expiratory limb with rapid peak flow, smooth inspiratory limb
  • Obstructive: "Scooped" or concave expiratory limb due to dynamic airway collapse
  • Restrictive: Normal shape but reduced in all dimensions (narrow loop)
  • Fixed upper airway obstruction: Flattened both expiratory AND inspiratory limbs
  • Variable extrathoracic obstruction: Flattened inspiratory limb only (e.g., vocal cord dysfunction)
  • Variable intrathoracic obstruction: Flattened expiratory limb only (e.g., tracheomalacia)
Clinical Pearl — Vocal Cord Dysfunction: VCD shows inspiratory flow limitation with a "truncated" inspiratory loop. The expiratory phase is preserved. This pattern helps differentiate VCD from asthma, though they can coexist.

4. Obstructive Lung Disease Pathophysiology

Asthma

Asthma is characterized by reversible airway obstruction, airway hyperresponsiveness, and chronic inflammation.

Asthma Pathophysiology Cascade
1
Allergen/Trigger Exposure → Activation of Th2 lymphocytes and IgE-mediated mast cell degranulation
↓
2
Inflammatory Mediators Released → Histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins cause immediate bronchoconstriction
↓
3
Late Phase Response (4-8 hours) → Eosinophil infiltration, mucus hypersecretion, epithelial damage
↓
4
Airway Remodeling (chronic) → Smooth muscle hypertrophy, basement membrane thickening, goblet cell hyperplasia

Bronchodilator Responsiveness

A positive bronchodilator response supports the diagnosis of asthma and indicates reversible obstruction:

ATS/ERS Criteria for Significant Bronchodilator Response:
• FEV₁ increase ≥12% AND ≥200 mL from baseline (absolute change required)
• Measured 10-15 minutes after short-acting bronchodilator (albuterol 400 mcg)

COPD

COPD is characterized by persistent, poorly reversible airflow limitation due to chronic bronchitis and/or emphysema.

Feature Chronic Bronchitis Emphysema
Definition Productive cough ≥3 months/year for 2+ years Destruction of alveolar walls with airspace enlargement
Pathology Mucus gland hypertrophy, airway inflammation Loss of elastic recoil, alveolar destruction
PFT Pattern ↓ FEV₁/FVC, air trapping ↓ FEV₁/FVC, ↑ TLC, ↓↓ DLCO
Classic Phenotype "Blue bloater" (hypoxemic, hypercapnic) "Pink puffer" (maintains gas exchange longer)

5. Diffusing Capacity (DLCO)

DLCO measures the ability of gases to transfer from alveoli into pulmonary capillary blood. It reflects the integrity of the alveolar-capillary membrane and pulmonary blood volume.

Conditions Affecting DLCO

Decreased DLCO Increased DLCO
Emphysema (alveolar destruction) Pulmonary hemorrhage (blood in alveoli)
Interstitial lung disease (fibrosis) Polycythemia (↑ Hgb)
Pulmonary embolism (↓ blood flow) Left-to-right shunt (↑ pulmonary blood flow)
Pulmonary hypertension Asthma (usually normal or ↑)
Anemia (↓ Hgb) Exercise (↑ cardiac output)
Clinical Pearl — Asthma vs COPD Differentiation:
DLCO is typically normal or elevated in asthma but reduced in emphysema-predominant COPD. This helps differentiate when spirometry alone is unclear.

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Question 1: A 45-year-old woman with dyspnea has the following PFT results: FEV₁ 65% predicted, FVC 90% predicted, FEV₁/FVC 0.58, TLC 115% predicted. What is the pattern?

A. Restrictive
B. Obstructive
C. Mixed obstructive-restrictive
D. Normal
Correct Answer: B. Obstructive
The FEV₁/FVC ratio is 0.58 (<0.70), indicating obstruction. The FEV₁ is disproportionately reduced compared to FVC. The elevated TLC (115%) suggests air trapping/hyperinflation, which is consistent with obstructive disease. Restriction would require a reduced TLC.

Question 2: Which of the following would cause an INCREASED DLCO?

A. Emphysema
B. Pulmonary fibrosis
C. Pulmonary hemorrhage
D. Anemia
Correct Answer: C. Pulmonary hemorrhage
Pulmonary hemorrhage increases DLCO because hemoglobin in the alveoli binds the test gas (carbon monoxide). Emphysema, fibrosis, and anemia all decrease DLCO through different mechanisms: alveolar destruction, membrane thickening, and reduced hemoglobin availability, respectively.

Question 3: A patient's spirometry shows a flattened inspiratory limb with a normal expiratory limb on flow-volume loop. Which condition is most likely?

A. Asthma
B. Tracheomalacia
C. Vocal cord dysfunction
D. Fixed upper airway obstruction
Correct Answer: C. Vocal cord dysfunction
VCD is a variable extrathoracic obstruction. During inspiration, negative intrathoracic pressure causes the dysfunctional vocal cords to adduct (close), limiting inspiratory flow. The expiratory limb is normal because positive pressure during expiration keeps the cords open. Fixed obstruction would flatten both limbs; tracheomalacia flattens the expiratory limb.

Question 4: What defines a positive bronchodilator response per ATS/ERS criteria?

A. FEV₁ increase ≥10% from baseline
B. FEV₁ increase ≥12% AND ≥200 mL from baseline
C. FEV₁ increase ≥15% from predicted
D. Any improvement in symptoms
Correct Answer: B. FEV₁ increase ≥12% AND ≥200 mL from baseline
The ATS/ERS standard requires BOTH a 12% relative increase AND an absolute increase of 200 mL. This dual criterion prevents small percentage changes from being falsely significant and ensures clinically meaningful improvement.
