🫀 Aortic Dissection

Understand the pathophysiology, classification, and management of this cardiovascular emergency where time = aorta.

⏱️ 25 min 📊 High Yield 🎯 Emergency Medicine

📋 Overview

Aortic dissection occurs when blood enters the aortic wall through an intimal tear, creating a false lumen that propagates along the vessel. Without treatment, mortality increases ~1-2% per hour in the first 48 hours for Type A dissections.

🎯 Key Concept: The Intimal Tear

The dissection begins with a tear in the intima (inner layer). Blood under arterial pressure forces its way into the media (middle layer), creating a false lumen that can propagate proximally or distally, compromising branch vessels and causing end-organ ischemia.

📊 Classification Systems

🅰️ Stanford Classification (Preferred)

Type A: Involves ascending aorta (regardless of origin)
Type B: Does NOT involve ascending aorta

Clinical relevance: Type A = Surgery, Type B = Medical (usually)

🔢 DeBakey Classification

Type I: Originates in ascending, extends to descending
Type II: Limited to ascending aorta only
Type III: Originates in descending aorta
(IIIa: above diaphragm, IIIb: below)

Feature Type A (Stanford) Type B (Stanford)

🔬 Pathophysiology

1
Risk Factors → Hypertension (present in 70-90%), connective tissue disorders (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos), bicuspid aortic valve, aortic aneurysm, cocaine use
2
Intimal Tear → Usually occurs at points of greatest hydraulic stress: proximal ascending aorta (65%), just distal to left subclavian (20%)
3
False Lumen Creation → Blood enters media, separating intima from adventitia; can propagate proximally or distally
4
Complications → Branch vessel occlusion (stroke, MI, limb ischemia, renal failure), aortic rupture, tamponade, aortic regurgitation

⚠️ Why Hypertension Matters

Blood pressure exerts force perpendicular to the vessel wall. Higher BP = greater wall stress = faster propagation. This is why immediate BP and heart rate control is critical: target SBP 100-120 mmHg and HR <60.

🩺 Clinical Presentation

⚡ Classic Presentation
  • Pain: Sudden, severe, "tearing/ripping" chest or back pain
  • Maximal at onset (vs crescendo in ACS)
  • May migrate as dissection propagates
  • Anterior chest → ascending; interscapular → descending
🔍 Physical Exam Findings
  • BP differential >20 mmHg between arms
  • Pulse deficits (asymmetric pulses)
  • New aortic regurgitation murmur
  • Neurologic deficits (stroke from carotid involvement)

🔎 Diagnosis

🎯 Imaging of Choice: CT Angiography

CTA is the first-line imaging modality: fast, widely available, highly sensitive/specific. Look for the intimal flap separating true and false lumens. TEE is an alternative when CT is unavailable or contraindicated.

📋 Initial Workup
  • CXR: Widened mediastinum (62%), abnormal aortic contour
  • ECG: Rule out STEMI; may show LVH, ischemia
  • Labs: Troponin, lactate, Cr, CBC, type & screen
  • D-dimer: Sensitive but not specific
🖼️ Definitive Imaging
  • CT Angiography: Gold standard, Sens/Spec ~95-100%
  • TEE: Bedside option, excellent for ascending
  • MRA: Most accurate but time-consuming
  • Aortography: Rarely used now

💡 Clinical Pearl: ADD Risk Score

The Aortic Dissection Detection Risk Score helps stratify pre-test probability:

  • High-risk conditions: Marfan, family hx, known aortic disease, aortic valve disease, recent aortic manipulation
  • High-risk pain features: Abrupt onset, severe intensity, tearing/ripping
  • High-risk exam findings: Pulse deficit, BP differential, focal neuro deficit, new AI murmur, hypotension/shock

≥1 feature in each category = high risk → proceed directly to imaging

💊 Management

1
Immediate Stabilization
• IV access, continuous monitoring, pain control (morphine)
• Type and crossmatch, notify CT surgery
2
Anti-Impulse Therapy (Critical!)
• Target: SBP 100-120 mmHg, HR <60 bpm
• Beta-blocker FIRST: Esmolol (titratable) or labetalol
• Then add vasodilator if needed (nitroprusside, nicardipine)
3
Definitive Treatment
• Type A → Emergent surgical repair
• Type B uncomplicated → Medical management
• Type B complicated → TEVAR or surgery

⚠️ Why Beta-Blocker First?

Vasodilators alone cause reflex tachycardia, which increases dP/dt (the rate of pressure change) and wall stress, potentially extending the dissection. Beta-blockers reduce both HR and contractility, decreasing shear stress on the aortic wall.

Complicated Type B Indications for Intervention

  • Rupture or impending rupture
  • Malperfusion syndrome (visceral, renal, limb ischemia)
  • Refractory pain despite optimal medical therapy
  • Rapid aortic expansion (>1 cm/year)
  • Aneurysm formation >5.5 cm

📝 Key Takeaways

🎯 Must Know
  • Type A = ascending involved = surgery
  • Type B = descending only = medical (usually)
  • Classic: tearing pain, maximal at onset
  • BP differential >20 mmHg is red flag
💊 Treatment Pearls
  • Beta-blocker BEFORE vasodilator
  • Target SBP 100-120, HR <60
  • CTA is imaging of choice
  • Don't delay surgery for Type A
