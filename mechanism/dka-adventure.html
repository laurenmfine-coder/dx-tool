Clinical Decision Point
Classic DKA presentation: Type 1 DM, insulin non-compliance, Kussmaul breathing, fruity breath, altered mental status. What's your first priority?
DECISION 1
Resuscitation
✅
Correct!
Correct! DKA patients are profoundly volume-depleted (average 6-9L deficit). NS at 15-20 mL/kg/hr (1-1.5L in first hour) restores perfusion and improves insulin sensitivity. DO NOT start insulin before fluids — insulin drives potassium intracellularly, and hypovolemia worsens this shift.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Never start insulin before checking potassium and giving fluids! Insulin shifts K+ intracellularly. If K+ is <3.3, insulin can cause fatal hypokalemia and cardiac arrest. Fluid resuscitation must come first.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Bicarbonate is NOT recommended unless pH <6.9. DKA acidosis corrects with insulin (stops ketogenesis) and fluids. Bicarbonate can cause paradoxical CNS acidosis, hypokalemia, and rebound alkalosis. The gap will close with proper DKA management.
DECISION 2

Correct! K+ >5.3 means safe to start insulin. Despite the elevated serum K+, total body potassium is severely depleted (osmotic diuresis + vomiting). Add 20-30 mEq KCl per liter of IV fluids. Monitor K+ every 1-2 hours. Insulin dose: 0.14 U/kg bolus then 0.1 U/kg/hr OR just 0.1 U/kg/hr continuous.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
The K+ of 5.8 is actually pseudo-hyperkalemia from acidosis and insulin deficiency. The TOTAL BODY potassium is depleted. Insulin will shift K+ back intracellularly, so you need to REPLACE it in the fluids, not treat it as hyperkalemia. Insulin is safe to start when K+ >3.3.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
IV insulin drip is standard for DKA (better titration, predictable absorption). SubQ absorption is unreliable in severely dehydrated patients with poor perfusion. SubQ rapid-acting insulin protocols exist for mild DKA but not appropriate for pH 7.12 with hemodynamic instability.
DECISION 3
4 Hours Later
Glucose has dropped to 280, pH improved to 7.24, bicarb is 14, anion gap is 18. K+ is now 4.0. The patient is more alert. Glucose is approaching 200.
✅
Correct!
Correct! When glucose reaches 200-250, add dextrose (D5 in half-NS) but CONTINUE the insulin drip. The goal is to close the anion gap, not just normalize glucose. Reducing the insulin rate to 0.02-0.05 U/kg/hr while adding dextrose prevents hypoglycemia while clearing ketoacidosis.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
NEVER stop insulin while the anion gap is still open (gap = 18, still elevated). Stopping insulin allows ketogenesis to resume and the patient will re-acidose. Continue insulin to clear ketones, and add dextrose to prevent hypoglycemia.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Too early to transition! Criteria for IV-to-subQ transition: anion gap closed (<12), pH >7.3, bicarb >15, patient eating. Her gap is still 18 and pH 7.24. Premature transition risks DKA recurrence — the #1 cause of DKA bounce-back.
RESOLUTION
Resolution
By hour 12: glucose 165 on D5 fluids, pH 7.38, bicarb 22, anion gap 10, K+ 4.2. Patient is eating breakfast and her home insulin regimen has been resumed with overlap.
OUTCOME
🏆 Excellent Clinical Reasoning
Excellent DKA management! Key transition rule: give subQ long-acting insulin (glargine) 2 hours BEFORE stopping the insulin drip to prevent rebound hyperglycemia. Address the root cause (ran out of insulin — ensure prescription access, education, social work consult). Endocrinology follow-up within 1 week.
