Layer 1: Host Response Basics

Understanding the difference between appropriate and dysregulated immune responses

🔑 Core Definition

Sepsis = Life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection.

Key insight: The problem isn't just the infection—it's the body's overreaction to it.

✓ Normal Response

  • • Localized inflammation
  • • Contained to infection site
  • • Appropriate cytokine levels
  • • Pathogen clearance
  • • Resolution & healing

✗ Sepsis Response

  • • Systemic inflammation
  • • Spreads beyond infection
  • • Cytokine storm
  • • Collateral organ damage
  • • Multi-organ dysfunction

SIRS Criteria (Historical Context)

≥2 of the following:

Temperature
>38°C or <36°C
Heart Rate
>90 bpm
Respiratory Rate
>20/min or PaCO2 <32
WBC
>12K or <4K or >10% bands

💡 Clinical Pearl: SIRS is sensitive but not specific—running up stairs can trigger SIRS criteria! That's why Sepsis-3 moved to qSOFA and SOFA scores focusing on organ dysfunction.

Sepsis-3 Definitions (Current)

Sepsis
Infection + SOFA score increase ≥2
Septic Shock
Sepsis + vasopressors needed to maintain MAP ≥65 + lactate >2 despite adequate fluid resuscitation

🧠 Check Your Understanding

A patient has pneumonia with fever (39°C), HR 105, and a new creatinine rise from 1.0 to 2.2. Does this patient have sepsis?

Cytokine Deep Dive

TNF-α (Tumor Necrosis Factor) Pro-inflammatory
  • • Released within minutes by macrophages
  • • Activates endothelium → adhesion molecules
  • • Induces fever (hypothalamic PGE2)
  • • Triggers apoptosis
  • • High levels = poor prognosis
IL-6 (Interleukin-6) Acute Phase
  • • Triggers hepatic acute phase response
  • • ↑ CRP, fibrinogen, ferritin
  • • Correlates with severity
  • • Target for some therapeutics (tocilizumab)

🎯 Why Does Vasodilation Happen?

Cytokines activate inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) in vascular smooth muscle.

↑ NO → ↑ cGMP → smooth muscle relaxation → profound vasodilation

This is why septic shock is "distributive" - blood volume is normal but it's distributed to a vastly expanded vascular space.

Why Each Organ Fails

Sepsis-Induced AKI
Hypoperfusion + Inflammation + Microcirculatory dysfunction
Unlike pure prerenal, septic AKI has direct tubular injury from inflammatory mediators
Sepsis-Induced Cardiomyopathy
Cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1) → myocardial depression → ↓ EF
Usually reversible if patient survives
DIC (Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation)
Tissue factor release → widespread microthrombi → consumption of clotting factors → bleeding
Paradox: Both clotting AND bleeding simultaneously

📈 Why Lactate Rises

Type A (Hypoxic)
  • • Tissue hypoperfusion
  • • Anaerobic metabolism
  • • Can't use oxygen → make lactate
Type B (Non-hypoxic)
  • • Accelerated aerobic glycolysis
  • • Mitochondrial dysfunction
  • • Decreased hepatic clearance

Clinical Pearl: Lactate clearance (>10% decrease in 2-6 hours) predicts survival better than absolute value.

Layer 4: Treatment Rationale

Why we do what we do - mechanism-based therapy

⏱️ Hour-1 Bundle (Surviving Sepsis)

1
Measure Lactate
Marker of tissue hypoperfusion; guides resuscitation
2
Obtain Blood Cultures Before Antibiotics
Identify organism for de-escalation; don't delay abx
3
Administer Broad-Spectrum Antibiotics
Each hour delay increases mortality 7.6%
4
Rapid Fluid Resuscitation (30 mL/kg crystalloid)
Address relative hypovolemia from vasodilation
5
Vasopressors if Hypotensive During/After Fluids
Norepinephrine first-line; counteracts vasodilation
