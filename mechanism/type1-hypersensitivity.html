🔬 Type I Hypersensitivity

IgE-Mediated Allergic Reactions: From Sensitization to Anaphylaxis

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Describe the two-phase process of IgE-mediated allergic reactions (sensitization → elicitation)
  • Identify the key cellular players: mast cells, basophils, Th2 cells, B cells
  • Explain the role of IgE and the high-affinity IgE receptor (FcεRI)
  • List preformed and newly synthesized mediators and their clinical effects
  • Connect mechanism to clinical findings in anaphylaxis and allergic disease

Overview: What is Type I Hypersensitivity?

Type I hypersensitivity (also called immediate hypersensitivity or IgE-mediated allergy) is an immune response that occurs within minutes of allergen exposure in sensitized individuals. It underlies common allergic conditions including anaphylaxis, allergic rhinitis, allergic asthma, food allergy, and drug allergy.

Key Concept: Type I reactions require PRIOR sensitization. The first exposure to an allergen produces IgE antibodies but no symptoms. Subsequent exposures trigger rapid mast cell degranulation and clinical symptoms.

This is fundamentally different from other hypersensitivity types:

Type Mechanism Timing Examples
Type I IgE + mast cells/basophils Minutes Anaphylaxis, allergic rhinitis, food allergy
Type II IgG/IgM + cell surface antigens Hours Hemolytic anemia, Goodpasture's
Type III Immune complex deposition Hours-days Serum sickness, Arthus reaction
Type IV T cell-mediated 24-72 hours Contact dermatitis, TB skin test

Phase 1: Sensitization

The first exposure to an allergen "primes" the immune system but produces no symptoms. This phase establishes the IgE-mediated response that will be triggered on subsequent exposures.

🔄 The Sensitization Pathway
1
Allergen Exposure: Allergen (pollen, food protein, drug, venom) enters via mucosal surface, skin, or injection
↓
2
Antigen Processing: Dendritic cells capture, process, and present allergen peptides via MHC II to naive T cells
↓
3
Th2 Polarization: In allergic-prone individuals, T cells differentiate to Th2 phenotype, secreting IL-4, IL-5, IL-13
↓
4
B Cell Class Switching: IL-4 signals B cells to undergo class switch recombination → produce allergen-specific IgE
↓
5
IgE Binding: Secreted IgE binds to high-affinity FcεRI on mast cells and basophils → patient is now "sensitized"
Clinical Pearl: Sensitization can occur without symptoms. A positive skin test or specific IgE indicates sensitization but NOT necessarily clinical allergy. Clinical allergy requires both sensitization AND symptoms on exposure.

Key Players in Sensitization

Th2 Cells

CD4+ helper T cells

Drive allergic inflammation via IL-4 (IgE switching), IL-5 (eosinophils), IL-13 (mucus, airway hyperreactivity)

IL-4

Th2 cytokine

Essential for IgE class switching in B cells. Also promotes Th2 differentiation (positive feedback loop)

IgE

B cell/plasma cell product

Lowest concentration serum Ig but highest affinity receptor binding. Half-life weeks when bound to mast cells

FcεRI

Mast cell/basophil receptor

High-affinity IgE receptor. Binding IgE "arms" the cell for rapid activation on allergen re-exposure

Phase 2: Elicitation (The Allergic Reaction)

When a sensitized individual encounters the allergen again, the IgE bound to mast cells recognizes and cross-links the allergen, triggering immediate degranulation.

⚡ The Immediate Reaction
1
Re-exposure: Allergen enters body (food ingestion, sting, drug administration)
↓
2
IgE Cross-linking: Multivalent allergen binds to and cross-links adjacent IgE molecules on mast cell surface
↓
3
Signal Transduction: FcεRI aggregation → tyrosine kinase activation → calcium influx
↓
4
Degranulation: Preformed mediators (histamine, tryptase, heparin) released within SECONDS
↓
5
De Novo Synthesis: Arachidonic acid metabolites (prostaglandins, leukotrienes) and cytokines produced over minutes-hours
Why So Fast? Preformed mediators are stored in granules ready for immediate release. This is why anaphylaxis can progress from first symptoms to cardiovascular collapse in minutes.

Mast Cell Mediators and Their Effects

Preformed Mediators (Immediate Release)

Mediator Action Clinical Effect
Histamine H1: vasodilation, increased permeability, smooth muscle contraction, itch
H2: gastric acid, some vasodilation		 Flushing, urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm, GI symptoms, hypotension
Tryptase Serine protease, activates complement, cleaves fibrinogen Marker of mast cell activation (measured to confirm anaphylaxis)
Heparin Anticoagulant, binds histamine Contributes to hemorrhagic manifestations in severe reactions
TNF-α Pro-inflammatory cytokine Sustains inflammation, recruits other immune cells

Newly Synthesized Mediators (Minutes to Hours)

Mediator Pathway Clinical Effect
Prostaglandin D2 Cyclooxygenase (COX) Vasodilation, bronchospasm, mucus secretion
Leukotrienes (LTC4, D4, E4) 5-Lipoxygenase Potent bronchoconstrictors (100x histamine), increased vascular permeability, mucus
PAF Membrane phospholipids Platelet aggregation, severe bronchospasm, hypotension
IL-4, IL-5, IL-13 Gene transcription Perpetuate allergic inflammation, late-phase response
Clinical Pearl - Why Epinephrine Works: Epinephrine counteracts multiple mediator effects simultaneously: α1-agonism causes vasoconstriction (↑BP, ↓angioedema); β1-agonism increases cardiac output; β2-agonism relaxes bronchial smooth muscle and inhibits further mast cell degranulation.

The Late-Phase Response

4-8 hours after the immediate reaction, a "second wave" of symptoms may occur - the late-phase response. This is driven by inflammatory cell recruitment (eosinophils, basophils, Th2 cells) and is responsible for biphasic anaphylaxis.

Biphasic Anaphylaxis: Occurs in 1-20% of anaphylaxis cases. Initial reaction resolves, then symptoms recur hours later WITHOUT re-exposure to allergen. This is why observation periods (4-6+ hours) are recommended after anaphylaxis.

What Drives the Late Phase?

  • Chemokines released during immediate phase recruit eosinophils and basophils
  • Eosinophil granule proteins (MBP, ECP) cause tissue damage
  • Continued leukotriene production sustains bronchoconstriction
  • Th2 cytokines perpetuate inflammation

Clinical Applications

Understanding Treatment Targets

Treatment Mechanism Indication
Epinephrine α + β agonist: reverses vasodilation, bronchospasm; inhibits further degranulation FIRST-LINE for anaphylaxis
H1 Antihistamines Block histamine at H1 receptor Urticaria, rhinitis, adjunct in anaphylaxis
Leukotriene inhibitors Block LT synthesis (zileuton) or receptor (montelukast) Asthma, AERD
Omalizumab Anti-IgE antibody; reduces free IgE, downregulates FcεRI Severe allergic asthma, chronic urticaria
Corticosteroids Inhibit cytokine production, reduce late-phase inflammation Not first-line for immediate reaction; helps late-phase

Diagnostic Testing

  • Skin prick testing: Introduces allergen to mast cells in skin; wheal = IgE-mediated sensitization
  • Serum specific IgE: Measures allergen-specific IgE in blood (ImmunoCAP)
  • Serum tryptase: Confirms mast cell activation; peaks 1-2 hours after anaphylaxis
  • Component-resolved diagnostics: Tests for IgE to specific allergen proteins (e.g., Ara h 2 for peanut)
