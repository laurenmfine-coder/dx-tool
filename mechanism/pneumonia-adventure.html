DECISION 3
ICU Day 2
Despite antibiotics and 4L NS, patient develops worsening hypoxia requiring 6L high-flow nasal cannula. Blood culture grows Streptococcus pneumoniae. Repeat CXR shows developing right-sided pleural effusion.
✅
Correct!
Correct! New pleural effusion in pneumonia = must rule out empyema/complicated parapneumonic effusion. Thoracentesis criteria: pH, glucose, LDH, protein, cell count, Gram stain, culture. If pH <7.2, glucose <60, or frank pus → chest tube drainage. Blood cultures grew S. pneumoniae → narrow to IV penicillin or ceftriaxone monotherapy.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
While simple parapneumonic effusions do resolve with antibiotics, you cannot distinguish simple from complicated (or empyema) without sampling. Untreated empyema has high mortality and morbidity. Any significant pneumonia-associated effusion should be sampled.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
You already have the organism — S. pneumoniae from blood cultures. Broadening is the wrong direction. De-escalate based on culture and sensitivity. Adding unnecessary antibiotics increases C. difficile risk, cost, and antimicrobial resistance without benefit.
RESOLUTION
Recovery
Thoracentesis shows simple parapneumonic effusion (pH 7.35, glucose 68). Narrowed to IV penicillin. By day 5, patient is afebrile, off supplemental O2, and eating. Effusion resolving.
OUTCOME
🏆 Excellent Clinical Reasoning
Excellent management! ATS/IDSA 2019 guidelines: 5-day minimum antibiotic course (can extend to 7 for severe CAP), patient should be afebrile 48h before stopping. Transition IV→oral when improving and tolerating PO. Follow-up CXR at 6-8 weeks to document resolution (especially important in smokers to rule out underlying malignancy). Pneumococcal and influenza vaccination if not up to date.
