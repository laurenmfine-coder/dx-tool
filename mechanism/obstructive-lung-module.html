🫁 Obstructive Lung Disease & Exercise-Induced Bronchoconstriction

Master the pathophysiology, diagnosis, and management of EIB - plus the critical differential of Exercise-Induced Laryngeal Obstruction (EILO)

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Explain the osmotic theory of exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB) pathophysiology
  • Distinguish between EIB with asthma (EIBA) and EIB without asthma (EIBwA)
  • Interpret exercise challenge test results using ATS criteria (≥10% FEV₁ decline)
  • Differentiate EIB from Exercise-Induced Laryngeal Obstruction (EILO) using clinical features
  • Apply evidence-based pharmacologic and non-pharmacologic management strategies
  • Recognize sport-specific risk factors for EIB in athletes

📊 Introduction & Epidemiology

Exercise-induced bronchoconstriction (EIB) describes acute, transient airway narrowing occurring during or after exercise. The terminology has evolved from "exercise-induced asthma" to EIB to emphasize that this phenomenon can occur in individuals with or without underlying asthma.

💡 Key Distinction

EIBA (EIB with Asthma): Up to 90% of asthmatics experience EIB, often reflecting poor asthma control.
EIBwA (EIB without Asthma): Affects up to 20% of the general population and 30-70% of elite athletes, particularly in winter sports and swimming.

🔑 Clinical Pearl

Olympic athletes have won medals despite EIB - proper diagnosis and management enables full athletic participation. The IOC and WADA require objective documentation (challenge testing) before allowing bronchodilator use in competition.

🔬 Pathophysiology: The Osmotic Theory

The osmotic/hyperosmolar theory is the most widely accepted mechanism explaining EIB. During exercise, increased ventilation (up to 100-200 L/min in elite athletes) overwhelms the airways' ability to warm and humidify inspired air.

🔄 EIB Mechanism Cascade

Exercise hyperventilation → Airway water loss → Hyperosmolar airway surface → Mast cell degranulation → Mediator release (histamine, leukotrienes, PGD₂) → Bronchoconstriction

Key Mediators in EIB

🧪 Histamine

  • Released from mast cells within minutes
  • Causes rapid bronchospasm
  • Increases vascular permeability
  • Blocked by antihistamines (limited efficacy)

🧪 Cysteinyl Leukotrienes

  • Potent bronchoconstrictors (1000× histamine)
  • Prolonged action (hours)
  • Increase mucus production
  • Target of LTRAs (montelukast)

🧪 Prostaglandin D₂

  • Mast cell-derived
  • Causes bronchoconstriction
  • Recruits eosinophils and Th2 cells
  • Marker of mast cell activation

The Refractory Period

A unique feature of EIB is the refractory period - approximately 50% of patients experience reduced bronchoconstriction if they exercise again within 1-3 hours of an initial episode. This is due to mast cell mediator depletion and release of protective prostaglandins (PGE₂).

🔑 Clinical Pearl: Warm-Up Effect

Athletes can exploit the refractory period through a "warm-up" strategy: performing interval sprints 20-30 minutes before competition induces a mild EIB response, followed by relative protection during the actual event.

🔍 Diagnosis

Clinical Vignette

A 17-year-old female cross-country runner presents with cough, chest tightness, and shortness of breath that begins about 5 minutes after starting intense training runs. She notes symptoms are worse during cold weather practices. She has no symptoms at rest, no nocturnal awakening, and no symptoms with routine activities. Her older sister has asthma. Physical examination is normal. Peak flow in clinic is 95% predicted.

Exercise Challenge Test: The Gold Standard

Per ATS guidelines, the exercise challenge test is the preferred method for diagnosing EIB. The test should be conducted in controlled conditions with specific protocols.

Exercise Challenge Protocol (ATS Guidelines)

1
Pre-Test Preparation:
  • Withhold SABA ≥8 hours, LABA ≥24 hours, LTRA ≥24 hours
  • Avoid caffeine on test day
  • No vigorous exercise for 4 hours (avoid refractory period)
  • Baseline spirometry must be acceptable (FEV₁ ≥70% predicted)
2
Exercise Protocol:
  • Treadmill or cycle ergometer
  • Achieve 80-90% predicted maximal heart rate (220-age)
  • Duration: 6-10 minutes at target intensity
  • Breathe dry air (≤10 mg H₂O/L) with nose clip
3
Post-Exercise Spirometry:
  • Measure FEV₁ at 5, 10, 15, and 30 minutes post-exercise
  • Record highest acceptable FEV₁ at each time point
  • Calculate percent decline from baseline
📐 ATS Diagnostic Threshold

A ≥10% decline in FEV₁ from baseline confirms EIB diagnosis.

Severity Classification:
Mild: 10% to <25% Moderate: 25% to <50% Severe: ≥50%

⚠️ Important Limitation

Symptoms alone have poor predictive value for EIB diagnosis. Studies show only 50% of patients reporting exercise-induced respiratory symptoms actually have EIB on objective testing. Conversely, many patients with documented EIB are asymptomatic. Always confirm with objective testing.

⚖️ Critical Differential: EIB vs EILO

Exercise-Induced Laryngeal Obstruction (EILO) is a common mimicker of EIB and represents inappropriate narrowing of the larynx during high-intensity exercise. EILO is frequently misdiagnosed as asthma, leading to unnecessary treatment escalation.

Clinical Vignette - Spot the Difference

A 16-year-old female soccer player presents with breathing difficulty during high-intensity drills. She describes a "choking" sensation with difficulty breathing IN. Symptoms occur at peak exertion and resolve within 2-3 minutes of stopping. She has tried multiple inhalers without benefit. Her coach notes a high-pitched noise during her breathing when sprinting. She is anxious about her performance and has missed several games.

🫁 Exercise-Induced Bronchoconstriction (EIB)

  • Location: Lower airways (bronchi)
  • Timing: Peak 5-15 min AFTER exercise stops
  • Breathing phase: Expiratory difficulty
  • Sound: Wheeze (expiratory, from chest)
  • Resolution: 30-90 minutes spontaneously
  • Bronchodilator: Effective (SABA helps)
  • Demographics: Any age, associated with atopy
  • Testing: ≥10% FEV₁ decline post-exercise

🗣️ Exercise-Induced Laryngeal Obstruction (EILO)

  • Location: Upper airway (larynx/vocal cords)
  • Timing: DURING peak exercise intensity
  • Breathing phase: Inspiratory difficulty
  • Sound: Stridor (inspiratory, from throat)
  • Resolution: Rapid (2-5 minutes after stopping)
  • Bronchodilator: NOT effective
  • Demographics: Adolescent females, competitive athletes
  • Testing: CLE test (continuous laryngoscopy during exercise)
⚠️ Key Fact: They Can Coexist!

EIB and EILO coexist in approximately 10-30% of athletes with exertional dyspnea. A poor response to asthma therapy should prompt evaluation for EILO, even if EIB has been documented. Average delay to EILO diagnosis is 2 years.

💊 Management of EIB

ATS Treatment Algorithm

Stepwise Approach (ATS Guidelines)

1
First-Line: Pre-exercise SABA

Albuterol 2 puffs (180-200 mcg) 15-30 minutes before exercise. Provides protection for 2-4 hours. (Strong recommendation)

2
If SABA inadequate, ADD one of:
  • Daily ICS: Reduces airway inflammation (takes 2-4 weeks for full effect)
  • Daily LTRA (montelukast): 10 mg daily; blocks leukotriene pathway
  • Mast cell stabilizer: Cromolyn 2 puffs 15-20 min before exercise
3
Combination therapy:

ICS-LABA combination if underlying asthma is present or symptoms remain uncontrolled

Non-Pharmacologic Strategies

🏃 Warm-Up Protocol

  • High-intensity intervals before competition
  • Exploits refractory period (1-3 hours protection)
  • Example: 30-second sprints × 6-8 reps
  • Allow 20-30 min recovery before event

🌡️ Environmental Modifications

  • Breathe through nose when possible
  • Wear mask/scarf in cold weather
  • Avoid high-pollution training times
  • Consider indoor training in extreme cold
🔑 WADA Considerations for Athletes

Since 2010, SABAs (salbutamol ≤1600 mcg/24h, formoterol ≤54 mcg/24h, salmeterol ≤200 mcg/24h) and all ICS are permitted without a Therapeutic Use Exemption (TUE). However, objective documentation of asthma/EIB is still required by the IOC for international competition.
