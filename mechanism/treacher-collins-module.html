🧬 Treacher Collins Syndrome

Mandibulofacial Dysostosis | TCOF1 Mutation | Bilateral Symmetric Craniofacial Hypoplasia

📚 Core Definition

Treacher Collins Syndrome (TCS) = Autosomal dominant condition causing BILATERAL, SYMMETRIC underdevelopment of structures derived from the first and second pharyngeal arches

Also known as: Mandibulofacial dysostosis, Franceschetti-Zwahlen-Klein syndrome

Epidemiology:

  • Incidence: 1 in 50,000 live births
  • No gender or ethnic predilection
  • 60% are de novo mutations
  • 40% inherited (autosomal dominant)
  • Variable expressivity - even within families
TCS vs HFM: Treacher Collins is BILATERAL and SYMMETRIC, while Hemifacial Microsomia is typically UNILATERAL or asymmetric. This is a key clinical differentiator!

Pathophysiology:

TCOF1 encodes Treacle protein → Essential for ribosome biogenesis in neural crest cells

↓ Treacle → ↓ Ribosome production → Neural crest cell apoptosis → Hypoplasia of 1st & 2nd arch structures

Genetic Counseling:
  • If one parent affected: 50% risk to offspring (AD)
  • Highly variable expressivity - mildly affected parent can have severely affected child
  • 60% have no family history (de novo mutations)
  • Prenatal testing available if mutation known

🩺 Clinical Features

FACIAL BONES (Bilateral & Symmetric)

  • Malar (zygomatic) hypoplasia - Flat cheekbones
  • Mandibular hypoplasia - Micrognathia/retrognathia
  • Maxillary hypoplasia
  • Class II malocclusion, open bite

EYES

  • Downslanting palpebral fissures - Pathognomonic
  • Lower eyelid colobomas (notching) - 70-75%
  • Sparse or absent lower eyelashes (medially)
  • Vision usually normal

EARS

  • Microtia - Bilaterally small/malformed ears
  • External auditory canal atresia
  • Middle ear anomalies - Ossicle malformation
  • Conductive hearing loss - Bilateral, often severe
  • Preauricular hair displacement (sideburns extend toward cheek)

OTHER FEATURES

  • Cleft palate (30%)
  • Choanal atresia/stenosis (rare)
  • Airway compromise - From micrognathia (Pierre Robin phenotype)
  • Normal intelligence in most cases
  • Normal limbs (distinguishes from Nager syndrome)
AIRWAY IS THE #1 CONCERN!
  • Severe micrognathia causes Pierre Robin-like airway obstruction
  • May be life-threatening in neonates
  • Prepare for difficult intubation - always have fiberoptic available
  • May require mandibular distraction or tracheostomy

🔬 Diagnosis

Clinical Diagnosis Based on:

  • Bilateral malar and mandibular hypoplasia
  • Downslanting palpebral fissures
  • Lower eyelid colobomas
  • Ear anomalies (microtia, canal atresia)

Confirmatory Testing:

  • TCOF1 gene sequencing - Detects 78-93%
  • If negative: POLR1C, POLR1D sequencing
  • Deletion/duplication analysis if sequencing negative

Prenatal Diagnosis:

  • Ultrasound may show micrognathia, polyhydramnios
  • 3D ultrasound can reveal facial features
  • Chorionic villus sampling / amniocentesis if mutation known

Differential Diagnosis:

ConditionKey Differentiator
Hemifacial MicrosomiaUnilateral/asymmetric (TCS is bilateral/symmetric)
Nager SyndromeLimb anomalies (preaxial - thumb/radius)
Miller SyndromeLimb anomalies (postaxial - 5th digit)
Goldenhar SyndromeEpibulbar dermoids, vertebral anomalies

🔪 Management

Multidisciplinary team essential: Craniofacial surgery, ENT, audiology, speech therapy, ophthalmology, genetics, psychology

Immediate Neonatal Priorities:

  1. Airway assessment and management
    • Prone positioning if obstruction
    • Nasopharyngeal airway
    • Mandibular distraction osteogenesis (MDO)
    • Tracheostomy if severe
  2. Feeding assessment - May need NG/G-tube
  3. Hearing evaluation - Early amplification

Hearing Management:

  • Early amplification critical for language development
  • Bone conduction hearing aids (soft band BAHA in infancy)
  • Osseointegrated implant (BAHA) after age 5
  • Cochlear implant if sensorineural component
  • Speech therapy ongoing

📊 Prognosis

  • Normal intelligence - Academic support may be needed due to hearing loss
  • Normal life expectancy - If airway managed appropriately
  • Good functional outcomes with staged reconstruction
  • Psychosocial support essential throughout development
  • Multiple surgeries required - family preparation important
Key Points:
  • Hearing loss is the primary cause of developmental delay - early intervention prevents this
  • Appearance improves significantly with staged reconstruction
  • Many patients lead fully independent adult lives

🧠 Test Your Understanding

A newborn has bilateral symmetric malar hypoplasia, downslanting palpebral fissures, lower eyelid colobomas, and microtia. Both hands are normal. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Hemifacial microsomia
B. Treacher Collins syndrome
C. Nager syndrome
D. Goldenhar syndrome
Correct Answer: B

Treacher Collins syndrome is characterized by BILATERAL, SYMMETRIC craniofacial features including malar and mandibular hypoplasia, downslanting palpebral fissures, lower eyelid colobomas, and ear anomalies. The key differentiators: Hemifacial microsomia is unilateral/asymmetric. Nager syndrome has limb anomalies (preaxial - thumb/radius involvement). Goldenhar syndrome has epibulbar dermoids and vertebral anomalies. The normal hands rule out Nager and Miller syndromes.

🧠 Question 2

What is the inheritance pattern and most common gene involved in Treacher Collins syndrome?

A. Autosomal recessive, TCOF1
B. Autosomal dominant, TCOF1 (60% de novo)
C. X-linked recessive, POLR1D
D. Autosomal dominant, FGFR2
Correct Answer: B

Treacher Collins syndrome is autosomal dominant, with TCOF1 mutations accounting for 78-93% of cases. Importantly, 60% are de novo mutations (no family history). TCOF1 encodes the Treacle protein, essential for ribosome biogenesis in neural crest cells. POLR1C (AR) and POLR1D (AD or AR) are rarer causes. FGFR2 is associated with craniosynostosis syndromes (Apert, Crouzon), not Treacher Collins.
