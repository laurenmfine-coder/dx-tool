Acid-Base Disorders

Master the systematic approach to ABG interpretation — from simple disorders to complex triple acid-base disturbances. Essential for critical care and internal medicine.

📚 12 Concepts ❓ 8 MCQs ⏱ ~50 min

🧪 Acid-Base Fundamentals

The Henderson-Hasselbalch Relationship

pH is determined by the ratio of bicarbonate (metabolic) to CO₂ (respiratory):

  • pH = 6.1 + log([HCO₃⁻] / 0.03 × PaCO₂)
  • Normal pH: 7.35-7.45
  • Normal PaCO₂: 35-45 mmHg
  • Normal HCO₃⁻: 22-26 mEq/L

The Four Primary Disorders

DisorderpHPrimary ChangeCompensation
Metabolic acidosis↓↓ HCO₃⁻↓ PaCO₂ (hyperventilation)
Metabolic alkalosis↑↑ HCO₃⁻↑ PaCO₂ (hypoventilation)
Respiratory acidosis↓↑ PaCO₂↑ HCO₃⁻ (renal)
Respiratory alkalosis↑↓ PaCO₂↓ HCO₃⁻ (renal)

💡 Clinical Pearl

Compensation never fully corrects pH. If pH is normal with abnormal CO₂ and HCO₃⁻, suspect a mixed disorder (or the patient just recovered).

📋 The 5-Step Approach

🔄 Systematic ABG Interpretation

1
Acidemia or alkalemia? Check pH (normal 7.40)
2
Respiratory or metabolic? What explains the pH deviation?
3
Is compensation appropriate? Use Winter's formula or expected compensation
4
If metabolic acidosis, calculate anion gap
5
If anion gap elevated, calculate delta-delta

📊 The Anion Gap

Anion Gap Formula

AG = Na⁺ − (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻)

Normal: 8-12 mEq/L (or 3-11 if albumin-corrected)

Albumin-Corrected Anion Gap

Corrected AG = AG + 2.5 × (4 − albumin)

For every 1 g/dL ↓ albumin, AG ↓ by ~2.5

Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis: GOLDMARK

Glycols (ethylene, propylene)
Oxoproline (acetaminophen toxicity)
Lactate
D-lactate
Methanol
Aspirin (salicylates)
Renal failure (uremia)
Ketoacidosis (DKA, starvation, alcoholic)

Non-Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis (NAGMA)

When AG is normal but HCO₃⁻ is low — the chloride is high (hyperchloremic acidosis):

  • GI HCO₃⁻ loss — Diarrhea, fistulas, ureteral diversions
  • Renal HCO₃⁻ loss — RTA types 1, 2, 4
  • Dilutional — Large volume NS resuscitation
  • Early renal failure — Before uremic acids accumulate

💡 Clinical Pearl

Check urine anion gap (UAG = UNa + UK − UCl) to distinguish GI vs renal causes of NAGMA. Negative UAG (< 0) = GI loss (kidney is making NH₄⁺). Positive UAG (> 0) = renal problem (RTA).

🔢 The Delta-Delta Ratio

Delta-Delta Formula

Δ-Δ = (AG − 12) / (24 − HCO₃⁻)

Compares the rise in AG to the fall in bicarbonate

Interpreting Delta-Delta

RatioInterpretationExplanation
1-2Pure anion gap acidosisAG rise matches HCO₃⁻ fall
<1AG acidosis + NAGMAHCO₃⁻ fell more than AG rose
>2AG acidosis + metabolic alkalosisHCO₃⁻ didn't fall as much as expected

💡 Clinical Pearl

The delta-delta is crucial for finding hidden disorders. A DKA patient with a ratio >2 may have concurrent vomiting (metabolic alkalosis). A ratio <1 suggests additional NAGMA (e.g., from diarrhea or RTA).

⬆️ Metabolic Alkalosis

Generation vs Maintenance

Metabolic alkalosis requires two things:

  • Generation — Something that raises HCO₃⁻ (vomiting, diuretics, etc.)
  • Maintenance — Something that prevents renal excretion of excess HCO₃⁻

The kidney normally excretes HCO₃⁻ rapidly. Maintenance factors include: volume depletion, chloride depletion, hypokalemia, mineralocorticoid excess.

Chloride-Responsive vs Chloride-Resistant

FeatureCl-Responsive (UCl <20)Cl-Resistant (UCl >20)
Volume statusHypovolemicEuvolemic or hypervolemic
CausesVomiting, NG suction, diuretics (remote), post-hypercapniaPrimary hyperaldosteronism, Cushing's, Bartter/Gitelman, current diuretic use
TreatmentIV NS (volume and chloride)Treat underlying cause; K+ replacement; sometimes acetazolamide

💡 Clinical Pearl

The most common cause of metabolic alkalosis in hospitalized patients is vomiting or NG suction. Loss of H⁺ and Cl⁻ generates the alkalosis, and volume depletion maintains it. NS fixes both.

🫁 Respiratory Disorders

Respiratory Acidosis

Caused by hypoventilation → CO₂ retention:

  • CNS depression — Sedatives, opioids, stroke, brainstem lesions
  • Neuromuscular — Myasthenia, Guillain-Barré, muscular dystrophy
  • Chest wall — Kyphoscoliosis, obesity hypoventilation
  • Airway — Severe asthma, COPD exacerbation, upper airway obstruction
  • Lung parenchyma — Severe pneumonia, pulmonary edema, ARDS

Respiratory Alkalosis

Caused by hyperventilation → CO₂ washout:

  • Hypoxemia-driven — High altitude, pneumonia, PE, CHF
  • CNS stimulation — Pain, anxiety, fever, sepsis, salicylate toxicity
  • Mechanical ventilation — Excessive minute ventilation
  • Pregnancy — Progesterone-stimulated hyperventilation
  • Liver failure — CNS stimulation from ammonia

💡 Clinical Pearl

Salicylate toxicity causes a characteristic mixed disorder: respiratory alkalosis (direct CNS stimulation) + anion gap metabolic acidosis (interference with oxidative phosphorylation).

🔀 Mixed Acid-Base Disorders

Clues to Mixed Disorders

  • Normal pH with very abnormal CO₂ and HCO₃⁻ — Opposing disorders
  • Compensation doesn't match prediction — Second disorder present
  • Delta-delta outside 1-2 range — Hidden metabolic disorder
  • Clinical context doesn't fit — Look for additional process

Classic Mixed Disorder Scenarios

Clinical ScenarioExpected Disorders
Salicylate toxicityRespiratory alkalosis + AG metabolic acidosis
DKA with vomitingAG metabolic acidosis + metabolic alkalosis
Septic shockRespiratory alkalosis + lactic acidosis
COPD + diureticsRespiratory acidosis + metabolic alkalosis
Renal failure + diarrheaAG acidosis + non-AG acidosis

Triple Acid-Base Disorders

Three simultaneous disorders. Classic example: DKA + vomiting + respiratory compensation failure

  • AG metabolic acidosis (ketoacids)
  • Metabolic alkalosis (vomiting)
  • Respiratory acidosis (aspiration pneumonia)

Key: pH may be near normal despite severe underlying derangements!
