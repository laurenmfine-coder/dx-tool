💉 Epinephrine: The Life-Saving Drug

Understanding the mechanism, dosing, and proper use of epinephrine in anaphylaxis

🚨 CRITICAL CONCEPT

Epinephrine is the ONLY first-line treatment for anaphylaxis.
There are NO absolute contraindications. Delayed administration = worse outcomes.

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Explain the mechanism of epinephrine at α and β receptors
  • Understand why intramuscular injection is preferred over subcutaneous
  • Know appropriate dosing for adults and children
  • Recognize when to repeat doses and when IV epinephrine is indicated
  • Dispel common myths about epinephrine contraindications

⚙️ Mechanism of Action

Epinephrine (adrenaline) is an endogenous catecholamine that acts on multiple adrenergic receptors. Its effects are perfectly suited to reverse the pathophysiology of anaphylaxis.

α-1 Receptors
Vascular smooth muscle
Effects:
• Vasoconstriction
• Increased peripheral resistance
• Decreased mucosal edema
Clinical benefit: Reverses hypotension, reduces angioedema and urticaria, decreases airway swelling
β-1 Receptors
Heart (SA node, myocardium)
Effects:
• Positive inotropy (↑ contractility)
• Positive chronotropy (↑ heart rate)
• Positive dromotropy (↑ conduction)
Clinical benefit: Increases cardiac output, improves coronary perfusion, combats shock
β-2 Receptors
Bronchial smooth muscle, mast cells
Effects:
• Bronchodilation
• ↑ Intracellular cAMP in mast cells
• Stabilizes mast cell membranes
Clinical benefit: Relieves bronchospasm, STOPS further mediator release
💡 Why Epinephrine is Uniquely Effective

No other drug addresses ALL aspects of anaphylaxis simultaneously. Antihistamines only block histamine (one mediator), steroids take hours to work, and bronchodilators don't address cardiovascular collapse or ongoing mast cell degranulation. Epinephrine does it all - immediately.

💉 Route of Administration

Intramuscular (IM) - First Line

IM injection into the lateral thigh (vastus lateralis) is the preferred route for anaphylaxis treatment outside the hospital setting.

💎 Why the Lateral Thigh?

The vastus lateralis has excellent blood supply and large muscle mass, providing rapid absorption. Studies show IM thigh injection achieves higher peak plasma levels faster than subcutaneous injection or IM deltoid injection. The thigh is also easily accessible, even through clothing.

Route Peak Plasma Time Peak Concentration When to Use
IM (thigh) 8 minutes Highest First-line for all anaphylaxis
Subcutaneous 34 minutes Lower NOT recommended for anaphylaxis
IM (deltoid) Slower than thigh Lower than thigh Alternative if thigh inaccessible
IV (infusion) Immediate Titratable Refractory anaphylaxis, cardiac arrest

Intravenous Epinephrine

IV epinephrine is reserved for:

  • Cardiac arrest during anaphylaxis
  • Profound hypotension unresponsive to IM dosing
  • Patients already with IV access and trained personnel available
⚠️ IV Epinephrine Caution
IV epinephrine has a narrow therapeutic window and can cause fatal arrhythmias if given too rapidly. Use diluted solution (1:10,000 or 1:100,000) and give slowly with continuous monitoring. Never give 1:1,000 (IM concentration) IV push.

📊 Dosing

Patient IM Dose Concentration Autoinjector
Adults & adolescents ≥30 kg 0.3-0.5 mg 1:1,000 (1 mg/mL) EpiPen/Auvi-Q 0.3 mg
Children 15-30 kg 0.15-0.3 mg 1:1,000 EpiPen Jr/Auvi-Q 0.15 mg
Children 7.5-15 kg 0.1-0.15 mg 1:1,000 Auvi-Q 0.1 mg (FDA approved) or EpiPen Jr 0.15 mg
Infants <7.5 kg 0.01 mg/kg 1:1,000 Auvi-Q 0.1 mg or drawn-up syringe

2023 AAAAI/ACAAI Guideline: "We suggest clinicians prescribe either the 0.1 mg or the 0.15 mg EAI dose for infants/toddlers weighing less than 15 kg." Multiple organizations (AAAAI, AAP, CSACI, EAACI) support switching to 0.3 mg at 25 kg to limit underdosing.

🔄 When to Repeat

Repeat IM epinephrine every 5-15 minutes if symptoms persist or recur. Many patients require 2+ doses. Prescribe at least 2 autoinjectors for patients with anaphylaxis history - some may need both during a single episode.

Autoinjector Options

EpiPen®
0.3 mg or 0.15 mg (Jr)
  • Most widely recognized
  • Orange tip = needle end
  • Remove blue safety cap, press firmly against outer thigh
  • Hold 10 seconds
Auvi-Q®
0.3 mg, 0.15 mg, or 0.1 mg
  • Audio instructions guide user
  • Compact, wallet-sized
  • Auto-retracting needle
  • Includes 0.1 mg for infants
Generic Epinephrine
0.3 mg or 0.15 mg
  • Lower cost alternative
  • Similar to EpiPen mechanism
  • Check specific device instructions
  • Equally effective

🔧 How to Use an Autoinjector

EpiPen Administration Steps
1
Remove from case - Pull out EpiPen and check expiration date and window (should be clear, not discolored)
2
Form a fist - Grasp EpiPen with orange tip pointing down (toward thigh). Blue safety cap should be on top.
3
Remove blue cap - Pull straight off (don't bend or twist). Device is now armed.
4
Inject into outer thigh - Swing and push orange tip firmly against outer thigh (through clothing if needed). You'll hear a click.
5
Hold for 10 seconds - Keep pressed firmly to ensure full dose delivery.
6
Remove and massage - Pull straight out. Orange cover will extend over needle. Massage injection site for 10 seconds.
7
Call 911 - Seek emergency care immediately. Bring used autoinjector to show medical team.
💎 Through Clothing is OK!

Don't waste time removing pants. The needle on autoinjectors is designed to penetrate clothing. Speed is more important than bare skin. However, avoid injecting through seams, pockets, or thick materials like jeans with heavy belt loops.

🚫 Myths vs Facts

❌ MYTH: "Epinephrine is contraindicated in elderly or cardiac patients"
✓ FACT: There are NO absolute contraindications in anaphylaxis
The risk of NOT giving epinephrine (death from anaphylaxis) always outweighs potential side effects. Elderly patients and those with cardiac disease may have higher risk of adverse effects, but anaphylaxis itself is far more dangerous. Untreated anaphylaxis causes hypoxia and cardiovascular collapse - much worse than transient tachycardia from epinephrine.
❌ MYTH: "Patients on beta-blockers can't receive epinephrine"
✓ FACT: Give epinephrine, then add glucagon if needed
Beta-blockers may blunt the β-receptor effects of epinephrine, but they don't block α-effects and don't make epinephrine dangerous. Give standard epinephrine doses first. If patient remains refractory, glucagon 1-5 mg IV can help by activating cardiac receptors through a non-β pathway.
❌ MYTH: "Antihistamines are just as good as epinephrine"
✓ FACT: Antihistamines are adjunct only - never first-line
Antihistamines only block histamine (one of many mediators) and do nothing for bronchospasm, hypotension, or ongoing mast cell activation. They help with urticaria and itching but cannot save a life. There is no evidence antihistamines prevent or treat anaphylaxis - epinephrine does.
❌ MYTH: "Wait to see if symptoms worsen before giving epinephrine"
✓ FACT: Early epinephrine improves outcomes
Studies consistently show delayed epinephrine is associated with biphasic reactions, hospitalization, and death. When anaphylaxis criteria are met, give epinephrine immediately. It's better to give epinephrine and not need it than to withhold it and have a fatality.

👨‍👩‍👧 Patient Education Points

  • Carry 2 autoinjectors at all times - Biphasic reactions happen, and one dose may not be enough
  • Check expiration dates monthly - Replace before expiration
  • Store at room temperature - Don't refrigerate or leave in hot cars
  • Teach family members and caregivers - They may need to administer if patient is incapacitated
  • Use it if in doubt - Better to use unnecessarily than die from untreated anaphylaxis
  • Always call 911 after use - Need professional monitoring for biphasic reactions
  • Practice with trainer devices - Familiarity improves response time in emergencies
