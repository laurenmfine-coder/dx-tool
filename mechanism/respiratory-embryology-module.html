📚 Respiratory Development Overview

The respiratory system develops from the foregut. During week 4, the respiratory diverticulum (lung bud) appears as a ventral outgrowth from the foregut, which is separated by the tracheoesophageal septum into the trachea (ventral) and esophagus (dorsal).

Embryonic Origins

Structure Embryonic Origin
Laryngeal epithelium, tracheal epithelium, bronchial epithelium, alveolar epithelium Endoderm (foregut)
Cartilage, smooth muscle, connective tissue, pulmonary vasculature Mesoderm (splanchnic mesenchyme)
Laryngeal muscles, laryngeal cartilages Neural crest (pharyngeal arches 4 & 6)

Key Timeline

Week 4: Lung Bud Appears

Respiratory diverticulum forms from ventral foregut. Tracheoesophageal septum divides it from esophagus.

Week 5: Primary Bronchi

Lung bud divides into 2 primary bronchial buds (right larger, more vertical).

Week 5-16: Branching Morphogenesis

Repeated branching creates bronchial tree. By week 16, all conducting airways formed.

Week 24-36: Surfactant Production

Type II pneumocytes mature and begin producing surfactant. Viability threshold ~24 weeks.

Week 36-Birth-8 years: Alveolarization

True alveoli form. Most alveolar development is postnatal!

🎯 Clinical Pearl: Why is the right mainstem bronchus more prone to aspiration? It's wider, shorter, and more vertical than the left - established from the embryonic asymmetry of the lung buds.
💡 Lung Development Stages Mnemonic: "Every Premie Can Surf Alright"
Embryonic → Pseudoglandular → Canalicular → Saccular → Alveolar

📅 Stages of Lung Development

Lung development occurs in 5 overlapping stages. Disruption at different stages causes different types of anomalies. The critical clinical milestone is surfactant production starting at ~24 weeks.
Stage Timing Key Events Clinical Relevance
Embryonic Weeks 4-7 Lung bud → trachea → main bronchi → lobar bronchi TEF/EA, tracheal agenesis, bronchial atresia
Pseudoglandular Weeks 7-17 All conducting airways form (to terminal bronchioles). Looks like a gland histologically. CPAM, sequestration, CDH (lung hypoplasia)
Canalicular Weeks 17-26 Respiratory bronchioles & primitive alveoli form. Capillaries approach airspaces. Type I & II pneumocytes differentiate. Viability threshold (~24 weeks); surfactant begins
Saccular Weeks 26-36 Terminal saccules (primitive alveoli) expand. Blood-air barrier thins. Surfactant production increases. RDS if premature; antenatal steroids help
Alveolar Week 36 - 8 years True alveoli form by septation. Alveoli increase from ~50 million at birth to ~300 million by age 8. Bronchopulmonary dysplasia affects alveolarization

📍 Pseudoglandular Stage Details

Called "pseudoglandular" because the lung looks like an exocrine gland - branching tubules lined by cuboidal epithelium surrounded by mesenchyme. No gas exchange possible.

  • All conducting airways complete by week 16
  • Cartilage, smooth muscle, and mucous glands develop
  • Fetus born at this stage cannot survive

📍 Canalicular Stage - The Viability Threshold

Critical transition to potential viability:

  • Respiratory bronchioles form (gas exchange possible)
  • Capillaries come into close contact with airspaces
  • Type I pneumocytes (gas exchange) and Type II pneumocytes (surfactant) differentiate
  • ~24 weeks = viability threshold with intensive support
⚠️ Premature Birth Complications
  • 22-24 weeks: Borderline viability, very high mortality
  • 24-28 weeks: Surfactant deficiency common → RDS
  • 28-34 weeks: Better outcomes, may still need surfactant
  • 34+ weeks: Usually adequate surfactant
Antenatal corticosteroids (betamethasone) accelerate lung maturation if preterm delivery expected.
🎯 Clinical Pearl: The lecithin/sphingomyelin (L/S) ratio in amniotic fluid indicates lung maturity. L/S ≥ 2.0 suggests adequate surfactant. Phosphatidylglycerol (PG) presence is another marker.

🫀 Diaphragm Development

The diaphragm develops from 4 structures that fuse together. Failure of fusion, especially of the pleuroperitoneal membranes, causes congenital diaphragmatic hernia (CDH).

Components of the Diaphragm

Structure Contribution Adult Derivative
Septum transversum Central tendon Central tendon
Pleuroperitoneal membranes Close pleuroperitoneal canals Posterolateral diaphragm
Body wall mesoderm Peripheral muscular rim Costal part of diaphragm
Esophageal mesenchyme Crura Right and left crura
💡 Diaphragm Components: "Several Parts Build Diaphragm"
Septum transversum, Pleuroperitoneal membranes, Body wall, Dorsal mesentery (esophageal mesenchyme)

Pleuroperitoneal Canal Closure

The pleuroperitoneal canals connect the thoracic and abdominal cavities. They close during week 6 as the pleuroperitoneal membranes fuse with other components.

  • Right side closes first (week 6)
  • Left side closes later → explains why CDH is more common on left
⚠️ Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH)
Failure of pleuroperitoneal membrane fusion → abdominal contents herniate into thorax → pulmonary hypoplasia.

Key Facts:
  • 85-90% are LEFT-sided (Bochdalek hernia - posterolateral)
  • Usually contains stomach, intestines, spleen, sometimes liver
  • Lung compressed → hypoplastic with abnormal vasculature
  • Presents at birth: respiratory distress, scaphoid abdomen, absent breath sounds on affected side
  • DO NOT bag-mask ventilate (inflates stomach, worsens compression)
  • Management: intubate, NG decompression, stabilize → surgical repair

📍 Types of Diaphragmatic Hernias

  • Bochdalek hernia (posterolateral): 85-90%, usually left, most common CDH
  • Morgagni hernia (anterior/parasternal): ~5%, usually right, often asymptomatic until adulthood
  • Hiatal hernia: Esophageal hiatus; sliding vs paraesophageal types
  • Eventration: Thin, weak diaphragm (not true hernia)

Phrenic Nerve

The phrenic nerve (C3-C5) innervates the diaphragm. It descends with the diaphragm as the septum transversum migrates from cervical region to its final position.

🎯 Clinical Pearl: "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive." The long course of the phrenic nerve (from neck to diaphragm) makes it vulnerable to injury during cardiothoracic surgery.

🧪 Surfactant Biology

Surfactant is produced by Type II pneumocytes starting around 24 weeks. It reduces alveolar surface tension, preventing collapse. Deficiency causes Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) in premature infants.

Surfactant Composition

Component Percentage Function
Phospholipids (mainly DPPC - dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine) ~80% Surface tension reduction
Surfactant proteins (SP-A, SP-B, SP-C, SP-D) ~10% SP-B & SP-C: surfactant function
SP-A & SP-D: innate immunity
Neutral lipids (cholesterol) ~10% Membrane fluidity
⚠️ Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) / Hyaline Membrane Disease
Pathophysiology: Surfactant deficiency → high surface tension → alveolar collapse (atelectasis) → hypoxia → ischemia → protein-rich fluid leaks into alveoli → "hyaline membranes"

Risk Factors:
  • Prematurity (most important)
  • Maternal diabetes (insulin inhibits surfactant)
  • C-section without labor (catecholamines promote surfactant release)
  • Male sex
  • Perinatal asphyxia
Presentation: Tachypnea, grunting, nasal flaring, retractions within hours of birth
X-ray: "Ground glass" appearance, air bronchograms, low lung volumes

Prevention & Treatment of RDS

🟢 Prevention: Antenatal Corticosteroids
  • Betamethasone or dexamethasone given to mother
  • Indicated if preterm delivery expected at 24-34 weeks
  • Optimal benefit 24-48 hours after administration
  • Accelerates surfactant production and lung maturation
  • Reduces RDS, IVH, NEC, and mortality
🔵 Treatment: Exogenous Surfactant
  • Given via endotracheal tube
  • Natural (animal-derived) or synthetic preparations
  • Dramatically reduces mortality and morbidity
  • May need multiple doses

Lung Maturity Testing

Test Mature Value Notes
L/S ratio (Lecithin/Sphingomyelin) ≥ 2.0 Traditional gold standard
Phosphatidylglycerol (PG) Present Very specific; appears late (~35 weeks)
Lamellar body count ≥ 50,000/μL Quick, automated
🎯 Clinical Pearl: In diabetic mothers, the L/S ratio may be falsely reassuring - PG is more reliable. Diabetes delays surfactant maturation (insulin inhibits SP-A and SP-B production).

⚠️ Congenital Respiratory Anomalies

Respiratory anomalies can affect the airways (trachea, bronchi), lung parenchyma (CPAM, sequestration), or diaphragm (CDH). Many cause respiratory distress at birth.

Tracheoesophageal Fistula (TEF) / Esophageal Atresia (EA)

⚠️ TEF/EA - Failure of Tracheoesophageal Septum Formation
Most common type (85%): Proximal EA with distal TEF
  • Proximal esophagus ends in blind pouch
  • Distal esophagus connects to trachea
Presentation:
  • Polyhydramnios (can't swallow amniotic fluid)
  • Excessive drooling, "foaming" at mouth
  • Choking, coughing, cyanosis with first feed
  • Unable to pass NG tube (coils in blind pouch)
Associations: VACTERL (Vertebral, Anorectal, Cardiac, TE fistula, Renal, Limb)

Congenital Pulmonary Airway Malformation (CPAM)

Formerly called CCAM (Congenital Cystic Adenomatoid Malformation)

📍 CPAM Features

  • Abnormal bronchial development → cystic or adenomatoid lung tissue
  • Usually affects one lobe
  • Has systemic blood supply (from aorta) vs normal pulmonary supply
  • Types based on cyst size (Type 1 = large cysts, better prognosis)
  • May cause hydrops in utero or respiratory distress at birth
  • Risk of malignancy (pleuropulmonary blastoma)
  • Treatment: surgical resection (lobectomy)

Pulmonary Sequestration

📍 Sequestration = Nonfunctioning lung tissue with systemic blood supply

Feature Intralobar Extralobar
Location Within normal lung (no own pleura) Outside lung (has own pleura)
Frequency 75% 25%
Side Left lower lobe (60%) Left lower (90%)
Presentation Recurrent pneumonia Often asymptomatic, found incidentally
Associations Usually isolated Other anomalies (CDH, cardiac)

Key feature: Systemic arterial supply (usually from aorta) - important for surgical planning!

Other Anomalies

📍 Bronchogenic Cyst

  • Abnormal budding from foregut/tracheobronchial tree
  • Usually mediastinal (near carina) or intrapulmonary
  • Filled with mucus, lined by respiratory epithelium
  • May compress airways or become infected

📍 Tracheomalacia / Bronchomalacia

  • Weakness of tracheal/bronchial wall (deficient cartilage)
  • Airway collapses during expiration
  • May be primary or secondary (compression, intubation)
  • Presents with stridor, "barking" cough, recurrent infections
  • Usually improves with growth (by age 2)

📍 Laryngomalacia

  • Most common cause of stridor in infants
  • Floppy supraglottic structures collapse during inspiration
  • Inspiratory stridor, worse when feeding, supine, or with URI
  • Usually self-limited (resolves by 12-18 months)

Summary Table

Anomaly Key Feature Classic Presentation
TEF/EA Septation failure Drooling, choking with feeds, can't pass NG
CDH Pleuroperitoneal membrane failure Respiratory distress, scaphoid abdomen
CPAM Abnormal bronchial development Cystic lung mass, respiratory distress or recurrent infection
Sequestration Systemic arterial supply Recurrent LLL pneumonia
Laryngomalacia Floppy supraglottis Inspiratory stridor, worse with feeding

📝 Test Your Knowledge

Question 1

The respiratory epithelium (trachea, bronchi, alveoli) is derived from which germ layer?

A. Endoderm
B. Mesoderm
C. Ectoderm
D. Neural crest
Answer: A. The respiratory epithelium is derived from endoderm (foregut). The cartilage, smooth muscle, and blood vessels come from mesoderm.

Question 2

At which gestational age does surfactant production typically begin, marking the threshold of viability?

A. 20 weeks
B. 24 weeks
C. 28 weeks
D. 32 weeks
Answer: B. Surfactant production begins around 24 weeks (canalicular stage), marking the threshold of viability. Type II pneumocytes differentiate and begin producing surfactant.

Question 3

A newborn has respiratory distress, a scaphoid abdomen, and absent breath sounds on the left. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. TEF/Esophageal atresia
B. Congenital diaphragmatic hernia
C. CPAM
D. Pneumothorax
Answer: B. This triad (respiratory distress, scaphoid abdomen, absent breath sounds) is classic for CDH. The scaphoid abdomen occurs because abdominal contents have herniated into the chest.

Question 4

Why is congenital diaphragmatic hernia (Bochdalek type) more common on the left side?

A. The left lung is smaller
B. The left pleuroperitoneal canal closes later
C. The liver protects the right side
D. The heart is on the left side
Answer: B. The left pleuroperitoneal canal closes later than the right, providing a longer window for herniation to occur. While the liver may provide some protection on the right, the primary reason is the timing of closure.

Question 5

A preterm infant has grunting, nasal flaring, and a "ground glass" appearance on chest X-ray. Which cell type is deficient?

A. Type I pneumocytes
B. Type II pneumocytes
C. Clara cells
D. Alveolar macrophages
Answer: B. This is RDS (Respiratory Distress Syndrome) caused by surfactant deficiency. Surfactant is produced by Type II pneumocytes. Treatment includes exogenous surfactant and supportive care.

Question 6

Which lung development stage are conducting airways complete, and what mnemonic letter does it correspond to?

A. Embryonic (E)
B. Pseudoglandular (P)
C. Canalicular (C)
D. Saccular (S)
Answer: B. By the end of the pseudoglandular stage (week 17), all conducting airways down to terminal bronchioles are formed. "Every Premie Can Surf Alright" - P is Pseudoglandular.
