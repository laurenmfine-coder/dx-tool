C Sympathetic nervous system activation

✓
Correct!
BNP (B-type natriuretic peptide) is released primarily in response to ventricular wall stretch from volume or pressure overload. Maria's elevated filling pressures (evidenced by JVP elevation, S3 gallop, and pulmonary congestion) cause myocyte stretching, triggering BNP release.

BNP serves as a counter-regulatory mechanism to RAAS - it promotes natriuresis, diuresis, and vasodilation. However, in heart failure, this protective effect is overwhelmed by RAAS and SNS activation.

Clinical Pearl: The S3 gallop Maria has indicates rapid ventricular filling into a compliant but volume-overloaded ventricle - it's one of the most specific findings for elevated filling pressures.

Maria's heart rate is 102, irregular. Her EKG shows absence of P waves with an irregularly irregular rhythm.
How does Maria's new atrial fibrillation contribute to her heart failure pathophysiology?
A Increases myocardial oxygen demand through tachycardia
B Loss of atrial kick reduces ventricular filling by 15-25%
C Both A and B contribute to worsening cardiac output
D AFib only increases stroke risk, not hemodynamic compromise
How confident are you? Select confidence
✓
Correct!
Atrial fibrillation impairs cardiac output through two mechanisms:

1. Loss of Atrial Kick: The coordinated atrial contraction normally contributes 15-25% of ventricular filling. In AFib, this "atrial kick" is lost, reducing preload and stroke volume.

2. Tachycardia: Rapid ventricular rates shorten diastolic filling time AND increase myocardial oxygen demand. This creates a supply-demand mismatch.

For Maria: Her rate of 102 with AFib means she's losing both the atrial contribution AND has reduced filling time. This is why rate control is a key early intervention.

Maria's echocardiogram returns: EF 32%, dilated LV (LVEDD 6.2 cm), moderate mitral regurgitation, estimated PASP 52 mmHg.
Based on Maria's echocardiogram findings, which classification best describes her heart failure?
A HFpEF (Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction)
B HFrEF (Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction)
C HFmrEF (Heart Failure with Mildly Reduced Ejection Fraction)
D Right heart failure only
How confident are you? Select confidence
✓
Correct!
Maria has HFrEF based on her EF of 32% (≤40% defines HFrEF per guidelines).

Classification Thresholds (2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA):
• HFrEF: EF ≤40%
• HFmrEF: EF 41-49%
• HFpEF: EF ≥50%

Key Echo Findings:
• Dilated LV (6.2 cm) - indicates eccentric remodeling from volume overload
• Moderate MR - often secondary to LV dilation (functional MR)
• Elevated PASP (52 mmHg) - indicates pulmonary hypertension from elevated left-sided pressures

Why This Matters: HFrEF has the strongest evidence for GDMT (ACEi/ARB/ARNI, beta-blockers, MRAs, SGLT2i) with mortality benefit.

📍 Case Connection
Maria's creatinine is 1.4 mg/dL (baseline unknown). She reports decreased urine output over the past week.
Maria's renal dysfunction in the setting of heart failure is MOST likely due to which compensatory mechanism becoming maladaptive?
A RAAS activation causing efferent arteriolar vasoconstriction and reduced GFR
B BNP-mediated natriuresis causing excessive sodium loss
C Direct myocardial toxicity from elevated catecholamines
D Autoimmune glomerulonephritis triggered by cardiac inflammation
How confident are you? Select confidence
✓
Correct!
Cardiorenal Syndrome Type 1 - Acute HF causes renal dysfunction through multiple mechanisms, but RAAS activation is central:

The Maladaptive Cascade:
1. Reduced cardiac output → decreased renal perfusion
2. Kidneys sense "hypovolemia" → activate RAAS
3. Angiotensin II constricts efferent arteriole (initially maintains GFR)
4. But also causes systemic vasoconstriction → increased afterload → worsening HF
5. Aldosterone → sodium retention → more volume overload

Clinical Implication: This is why ACEi/ARBs are cornerstone HFrEF therapy - they break this cycle. The initial Cr bump often seen is acceptable if <30% and stabilizes.

📍 Case Connection
Maria is admitted with acute decompensated HFrEF. Her BP is 168/98, she's hypoxic on room air, and has significant volume overload.
What is the MOST appropriate initial therapy for Maria's acute decompensation?
A IV loop diuretic (furosemide) for decongestion
B Start high-dose beta-blocker for rate control
C Dobutamine infusion for inotropic support
D Emergent cardioversion for atrial fibrillation
How confident are you? Select confidence
✓
Correct!
IV diuretics are first-line for acute decompensated HF with volume overload ("wet" presentation).

Why Loop Diuretics First:
• Maria is "warm and wet" (adequate perfusion + congestion)
• Decongestion improves symptoms AND hemodynamics
• Her elevated BP (168/98) indicates she can tolerate diuresis
• Usually 1-2x home oral dose given IV (or 40-80mg if diuretic-naive)

Why NOT the Other Options:
• B: Never start/increase beta-blockers during acute decompensation
• C: Dobutamine for "cold" profiles (hypoperfusion) - Maria is "warm"
• D: Rate control first; cardioversion not emergent if hemodynamically stable

Next Steps: After initial diuresis, supplemental O2, will add GDMT (ACEi, beta-blocker, MRA, SGLT2i) before discharge.
