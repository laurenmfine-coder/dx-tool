🔀 UPJ Obstruction

The Most Common Cause of Prenatal Hydronephrosis

📚 Overview

Ureteropelvic Junction (UPJ) Obstruction is a blockage at the junction of the renal pelvis and ureter, causing impaired drainage of urine from the kidney. It is the most common cause of significant prenatal hydronephrosis.

Epidemiology

ParameterValue
Incidence1 in 1,000-2,000 births
Male:Female2:1
Left sideMore common (2:1)
Bilateral10-30%

🧬 Etiology

Intrinsic Causes (Most Common)

  • Aperistaltic ureteral segment - abnormal smooth muscle at UPJ
  • Abnormal collagen deposition
  • Ureteral valve or kink
  • High ureteral insertion into renal pelvis

Extrinsic Causes

  • Crossing vessel (lower pole artery) - common in adults/older children
  • Fibrous bands
  • Aberrant renal vessels

Secondary Causes

  • Vesicoureteral reflux (VUR) with high-grade dilation
  • Stones
  • Stricture (post-instrumentation, TB)

🔍 Clinical Presentation

Prenatal/Neonatal

  • Prenatal hydronephrosis - most common presentation today
  • Often detected on routine anatomy scan (18-20 weeks)
  • Usually asymptomatic at birth

Children

  • Abdominal or flank pain (especially with increased fluids)
  • UTI
  • Hematuria (after minor trauma)
  • "Dietl crisis" - intermittent flank pain with nausea/vomiting

Adults

  • Intermittent flank pain (often positional or with fluid intake)
  • Stones (stasis predisposes)
  • Often discovered incidentally
Dietl Crisis: Classic presentation of intermittent UPJ obstruction - episodic flank pain with nausea/vomiting, often triggered by increased fluid intake.

📷 Diagnosis

Imaging Studies

StudyFindingsRole
UltrasoundDilated renal pelvis, NO ureteral dilationInitial imaging; prenatal detection
MAG3 RenogramDelayed washout after furosemideGold standard for function & obstruction
VCUGRules out VURPart of postnatal workup

MAG3 Renogram Interpretation

  • Differential function: Normal >40% each kidney
  • T1/2: Time for 50% washout after furosemide
  • T1/2 <10 min = non-obstructed
  • T1/2 10-20 min = indeterminate
  • T1/2 >20 min = obstructed

💊 Management

Conservative (Observation)

Most infants can be observed:
  • Many resolve spontaneously
  • Serial ultrasounds to monitor
  • MAG3 to assess function
  • Intervene if function declines (<40% or dropping)

Surgical Indications

  • Declining renal function (<40% or dropping)
  • Symptomatic (pain, recurrent UTI)
  • Increasing hydronephrosis
  • Complications (stones, infection)

Surgical Options

ProcedureSuccess Rate
Pyeloplasty (Anderson-Hynes)95%+ - Gold standard
Laparoscopic/RoboticEquivalent outcomes, minimally invasive
EndopyelotomyLower success; select cases

📋 Key Clinical Pearls

Key Differentiator: UPJ obstruction = dilated pelvis WITHOUT dilated ureter. If ureter is dilated, consider VUR or UVJ obstruction.
Summary:
  • #1 cause of significant prenatal hydronephrosis
  • Dilated pelvis WITHOUT dilated ureter
  • MAG3 renogram for function and drainage
  • Many resolve; observe with serial imaging
  • Pyeloplasty for declining function (95%+ success)

🧠 Quick Quiz

A prenatal ultrasound shows left hydronephrosis with a dilated renal pelvis but NO ureteral dilation. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. UPJ obstruction
B. Vesicoureteral reflux
C. Posterior urethral valves
D. UVJ obstruction
Correct: A - UPJ Obstruction
Dilated renal pelvis WITHOUT ureteral dilation is classic for UPJ obstruction. In VUR and UVJ obstruction, the ureter would also be dilated. In PUV, you would see bilateral hydronephrosis with a distended bladder.
