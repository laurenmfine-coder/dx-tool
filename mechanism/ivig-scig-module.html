1. Overview: Why Immunoglobulin Replacement?

Immunoglobulin replacement therapy provides passive immunity to patients who cannot produce adequate antibodies. The goal is to maintain IgG levels sufficient to prevent infections.

Indications for IgG Replacement

  • Primary immunodeficiency: CVID, XLA, hyper-IgM syndrome, specific antibody deficiency (selected cases)
  • Secondary immunodeficiency: CLL with hypogammaglobulinemia, post-HSCT, certain B-cell depleting therapies
  • Other FDA-approved uses: ITP, Kawasaki disease, CIDP, MMN (these use higher "immunomodulatory" doses)
Key Concept: For immunodeficiency, we use replacement doses (400-600 mg/kg/month) to maintain trough IgG levels. For autoimmune/inflammatory conditions, we use immunomodulatory doses (1-2 g/kg) which work through different mechanisms.

2. IVIG vs SCIG: Head-to-Head Comparison

Feature IVIG (Intravenous) SCIG (Subcutaneous)
Route IV infusion Subcutaneous injection/infusion
Frequency Every 3-4 weeks Weekly (conventional) or biweekly (facilitated SCIG)
Setting Infusion center, hospital, or home with nurse Home self-administration
Infusion Duration 2-6 hours depending on product/rate 1-2 hours for weekly infusions
IgG Levels Peak-trough fluctuation (high after infusion, drops before next) Steady-state levels (less fluctuation)
Half-life ~21-35 days Same IgG half-life, but more stable levels due to frequency
Systemic Reactions More common (headache, fever, chills, nausea) Less common systemic reactions
Local Reactions IV site reactions Injection site swelling, redness (usually mild)
Venous Access Required (can be challenging in some patients) Not required
Patient Autonomy Dependent on infusion appointments Self-administered, flexible scheduling
Initial Training Minimal patient training Requires patient education on self-injection
Clinical Pearl: Many patients who experience systemic side effects with IVIG (headaches, fatigue, malaise) feel significantly better with SCIG due to steadier IgG levels and smaller, more frequent doses.

3. Pharmacokinetics

IgG Distribution and Elimination

Infused IgG distributes between intravascular and extravascular compartments:

  • ~45% remains intravascular
  • ~55% distributes to extravascular tissues
  • Half-life: 21-35 days (varies by product and patient)
  • Elimination: Catabolism by endothelial cells; FcRn receptor recycles IgG back to circulation (prolongs half-life)

IVIG Kinetics

With monthly IVIG:

  • Peak: Immediately post-infusion (significant rise)
  • Trough: Just before next infusion (lowest level)
  • Fluctuation: Can vary by 300-400 mg/dL between peak and trough

SCIG Kinetics

With weekly SCIG:

  • Bioavailability: ~65-70% compared to IV (dose may need to be increased)
  • Steady-state: Achieved after 4-6 weeks of weekly dosing
  • Fluctuation: Minimal; steady-state levels with small variations
Dose Conversion IVIG → SCIG:
When converting from IVIG to conventional SCIG, the total monthly dose is typically increased by 37-53% (multiply by 1.37-1.53) to achieve equivalent trough levels, then divided into weekly doses.

Example: IVIG 40g monthly → SCIG: 40g × 1.37 = ~55g monthly ÷ 4 = ~13.75g weekly

Dose Adjustments

  • Check trough IgG before 4th infusion (steady state)
  • Target trough: ≥500 mg/dL minimum; many experts target ≥800 mg/dL for patients with bronchiectasis or frequent infections
  • Adjust dose in increments of 100-200 mg/kg to achieve target
  • More frequent dosing (every 3 weeks instead of 4) can raise trough without increasing total dose

5. Administration Protocols

IVIG Administration

🏥 Nursing Protocol - IVIG Infusion:
  1. Pre-medications (if history of reactions): Acetaminophen 650mg, Diphenhydramine 25-50mg, ± Hydrocortisone 100mg IV
  2. Hydration: Encourage oral fluids; consider IV NS for high-risk patients (renal insufficiency, elderly)
  3. Starting rate: 0.5-1 mg/kg/min (product-specific)
  4. Rate escalation: Increase every 15-30 minutes as tolerated to maximum rate
  5. Monitoring: Vital signs q15min × 1 hour, then q30min, then hourly
  6. Maximum rate: Typically 4-8 mg/kg/min (varies by product)

SCIG Self-Administration

🏠 Patient Teaching - SCIG Self-Injection:
  1. Site selection: Abdomen (avoiding 2 inches around navel), thighs, upper arms, upper back/hip
  2. Rotate sites: Keep sites 2 inches apart; rotate systematically
  3. Clean technique: Wash hands, clean site with alcohol, allow to dry
  4. Needle insertion: 90-degree angle for most; 45-degree if thin subcutaneous tissue
  5. Infusion rate: 15-25 mL/hour per site (pump-assisted) or push over several minutes (manual)
  6. Volume per site: Maximum 20-25 mL conventional SCIG; up to 300 mL for facilitated SCIG
  7. Number of sites: May need 2-4 sites for weekly dose; fewer with facilitated SCIG
  8. Documentation: Log date, dose, sites used, any reactions
Clinical Pearl: Local site reactions (swelling, redness) are common initially but typically improve over 4-8 weeks. Applying warm compress after infusion and using a different site can help.

6. Infusion Reactions & Management

Types of Reactions

Type Symptoms Timing Mechanism
Rate-Related Headache, flushing, chills, myalgias, nausea, fever During or shortly after infusion Complement activation, cytokine release
Anaphylactic Urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm, hypotension Minutes to 1 hour IgE-mediated (rare); anti-IgA antibodies in IgA-deficient patients
Delayed Headache (including aseptic meningitis), fatigue, arthralgia 24-72 hours post-infusion Immune complex formation, cytokines
Renal Acute kidney injury Days after infusion Osmotic nephropathy (sucrose-containing products)
Thrombotic DVT, PE, stroke, MI During or days after Hyperviscosity, Factor XI contamination

⚠️ Reaction Management Protocol

1
STOP the infusion immediately for any significant reaction
2
Assess ABCs: Airway, Breathing, Circulation - call for help if anaphylaxis
3
For mild reactions: Give diphenhydramine 25-50mg IV/PO, acetaminophen, wait 30 min
4
Resume at 50% rate if symptoms resolve; advance slowly
5
For anaphylaxis: Epinephrine 0.3-0.5mg IM, IV fluids, notify physician
6
Document: Reaction type, timing, interventions, outcome
⚠️ IgA Deficiency Warning: Patients with complete IgA deficiency (<7 mg/dL) who have anti-IgA antibodies are at risk for anaphylaxis. Use IgA-depleted products (e.g., Gammagard S/D) or SCIG if possible. Test for anti-IgA antibodies before first infusion in IgA-deficient patients.

7. Monitoring Parameters

Trough IgG Monitoring

  • When to check: Just before the next scheduled infusion (true trough)
  • Initial monitoring: Check before 4th infusion, then every 3-6 months until stable
  • Maintenance: Every 6-12 months once stable
  • Target: ≥500 mg/dL minimum; many experts now recommend ≥800 mg/dL for patients with bronchiectasis

Additional Monitoring

Parameter Frequency Rationale
Serum creatinine/BUN Before each infusion initially; every 6-12 months when stable Monitor for renal toxicity
CBC Every 6-12 months Hemolysis (rare), baseline
LFTs Annually Hepatitis monitoring (rare with current products)
Viral serologies Baseline Document pre-treatment status (Hep B, C, HIV)
Infection frequency Ongoing Clinical efficacy assessment
