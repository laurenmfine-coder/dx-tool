🔵 Diverticulitis & Mesenteric Ischemia

Acute Diverticulitis

  • Pathophysiology: Microperforation of diverticulum → localized inflammation/infection
  • Presentation: LLQ pain (Western); fever; altered bowel habits
  • CT findings: Pericolic fat stranding, diverticulosis, ± abscess, ± free air
  • Hinchey classification: Stage I-IV based on perforation/abscess extent

Diverticulitis Management

SeverityManagement
UncomplicatedOutpatient oral antibiotics (or no antibiotics per recent data); liquid→regular diet
Complicated (abscess <4cm)IV antibiotics; possible drainage
Abscess >4cmPercutaneous drainage + IV antibiotics
Perforation/peritonitisEmergency surgery (Hartmann's or primary anastomosis)

Acute Mesenteric Ischemia

  • Causes: Arterial embolism (50%), arterial thrombosis (25%), venous thrombosis (10%), NOMI (15%)
  • "Pain out of proportion": Severe pain with minimal exam findings early
  • Risk factors: AF, recent MI, atherosclerosis, low-flow states, hypercoagulability
  • Diagnosis: CT angiography (97% sensitive); elevated lactate is late finding
  • Treatment: Anticoagulation, possible revascularization/embolectomy, surgical resection of necrotic bowel

⚠️ Mesenteric Ischemia = TIME CRITICAL

"Pain out of proportion to exam" in patient with AF, vascular disease, or shock should prompt CTA. Lactate elevation is a LATE finding indicating bowel necrosis. Early diagnosis + intervention is key to survival.
