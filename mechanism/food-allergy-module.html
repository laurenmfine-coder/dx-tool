🥜 Food Allergy: Diagnosis & Management

IgE-Mediated & Non-IgE Reactions • Component Diagnostics • OIT & Emerging Therapies

✓ Evidence-Based Content • Open-Access References

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate IgE-mediated food allergy from non-IgE disorders (FPIES, EoE, allergic proctocolitis)
  • Apply component-resolved diagnostics (Ara h 2, etc.) to risk-stratify patients
  • Understand peanut OIT (Palforzia) indications, dosing, and safety management

📊 Classification of Food Allergy

Food allergy is an adverse immune response to food proteins, affecting approximately 5% of children and 4% of adults. The NIAID Guidelines (2010) established the framework for diagnosis and management.

🔴 IgE-Mediated Food Allergy

  • Onset: Minutes to 2 hours
  • Mechanism: IgE → Mast cell degranulation
  • Symptoms: Urticaria, angioedema, anaphylaxis
  • Testing: SPT, sIgE, component testing
  • Common triggers: Peanut, tree nuts, milk, egg, fish, shellfish, wheat, soy

🟣 Non-IgE-Mediated Food Allergy

  • Onset: Hours to days
  • Mechanism: T-cell mediated inflammation
  • Syndromes:
  • • FPIES (enterocolitis)
  • • Allergic proctocolitis
  • • EoE (mixed IgE/non-IgE)
  • Testing: Often negative SPT/sIgE; diagnosis is clinical
⚠️ Critical Distinction: Up to 50-90% of parent-reported food allergies are NOT confirmed by formal evaluation. Self-diagnosis leads to unnecessary dietary restrictions, nutritional deficiencies, and anxiety.

🥛 Major Food Allergens (FALCPA + Sesame)

The FDA requires labeling of the "Top 9" allergens, which account for approximately 90% of food allergies in the US.

Allergen Prevalence Natural History Key Points
🥜 Peanut ~2% children ~20% outgrow Ara h 2 best predictor; OIT available (Palforzia)
🌰 Tree Nuts ~1% children ~10% outgrow Often multiple tree nut allergies; cross-reactivity variable
🥛 Cow's Milk ~2-3% infants ~80% outgrow by age 5 Baked milk tolerance common; can use for OIT
🥚 Egg ~2% children ~70% outgrow by age 5 Baked egg tolerance common; Ova d 1 (ovomucoid) = heat-stable
🌾 Wheat ~0.5% children ~65% outgrow by age 12 Distinguish from celiac; ω-5 gliadin = WDEIA
🫘 Soy ~0.4% children ~50% outgrow by age 7 Highly processed soy often tolerated
🐟 Fish ~0.4% Usually persistent Parvalbumin cross-reactive across species
🦐 Shellfish ~2% adults Usually persistent Tropomyosin; crustaceans vs mollusks separate
🫒 Sesame ~0.2% ~20% outgrow Added to FALCPA 2023; rising prevalence
💡 Clinical Pearl: Early introduction of peanut (4-6 months) in high-risk infants reduces peanut allergy by ~80% (LEAP study). This is now standard guidance per 2017 NIAID Addendum Guidelines.

🔬 Diagnostic Testing for Food Allergy

Skin Prick Testing (SPT)

  • Detects IgE sensitization (not allergy)
  • Wheal ≥3mm greater than negative control = positive
  • Higher sensitivity than specificity
  • Negative predictive value >95%

Serum Specific IgE (sIgE)

  • Quantitative measurement of allergen-specific IgE
  • Does NOT require holding antihistamines
  • Higher levels associated with higher probability of clinical reactivity
  • 95% predictive decision points vary by allergen and age

Component-Resolved Diagnostics (CRD)

Measures IgE to specific allergenic proteins within foods, improving diagnostic accuracy:

🥜 Peanut Components

  • Ara h 2: Best predictor of clinical allergy (2S albumin)
  • Ara h 6: Similar to Ara h 2, cross-reactive
  • Ara h 1, 3: Storage proteins, less predictive
  • Ara h 8: PR-10 protein; pollen cross-reactivity (OAS)
  • Ara h 9: LTP; Southern Europe phenotype

🥛 Milk Components

  • Bos d 8 (casein): Heat-stable; predicts reactivity to baked milk
  • Bos d 4, 5: Whey proteins; heat-labile
  • High casein sIgE → less likely to tolerate baked milk

🥚 Egg Components

  • Gal d 1 (ovomucoid): Heat-stable; predicts reactivity to baked egg
  • Gal d 2 (ovalbumin): Heat-labile
  • Low/negative Gal d 1 → may tolerate baked egg

🌾 Wheat Components

  • Tri a 19 (ω-5 gliadin): WDEIA marker
  • Tri a 14 (LTP): Primary wheat allergy
  • Distinguish IgE wheat allergy from celiac disease
Ara h 2 Diagnostic Thresholds:
• Ara h 2 ≥0.1 kUA/L: 100% sensitivity for peanut allergy
• Ara h 2 ≥5.0 kUA/L: ~98% specificity for peanut allergy
• Ara h 2 ≥1.75 kUA/L: 100% PPV validated in adults

Oral Food Challenge (OFC)

The gold standard for food allergy diagnosis:

  • DBPCFC: Double-blind, placebo-controlled food challenge (research/medicolegal)
  • Open OFC: Commonly used in clinical practice
  • Performed under medical supervision with resuscitation equipment
  • Graduated doses over 1-2 hours with observation period
⚠️ Unvalidated Tests: Do NOT use IgG4 testing, hair analysis, electrodermal testing, or applied kinesiology for food allergy diagnosis. These lack scientific evidence.

🚨 Food Protein-Induced Enterocolitis Syndrome (FPIES)

FPIES is a non-IgE, cell-mediated food allergy presenting with delayed GI symptoms, sometimes progressing to shock. Awareness has increased since the 2017 International Consensus Guidelines.

Clinical Phenotypes

🔴 Acute FPIES

  • Onset: 1-4 hours after ingestion
  • Hallmark: Repetitive, projectile vomiting
  • Pallor, lethargy, hypotonia
  • Diarrhea may follow (5-10 hours)
  • ~15-20% progress to hypovolemic shock
  • NO urticaria, NO respiratory symptoms

🟣 Chronic FPIES

  • Daily exposure to trigger (e.g., formula)
  • Chronic vomiting, diarrhea
  • Failure to thrive, hypoalbuminemia
  • Anemia, dehydration
  • Onset within first 1-3 months of life
  • Resolves 3-10 days after elimination

FPIES Diagnostic Criteria (Major + Minor)

Acute FPIES Diagnosis

MAJOR CRITERION: Vomiting 1-4 hours after food ingestion, absence of IgE-mediated symptoms
MINOR CRITERIA (≥3 of 5):
1. Second episode of vomiting with same food
2. Repetitive vomiting 1-4h after different food
3. Lethargy with reaction
4. Pallor with reaction
5. Need for ER visit or IV fluids
DIAGNOSIS: Major + ≥3 minor criteria, OR positive supervised OFC

Common FPIES Triggers

Region Most Common Triggers
United States/Australia Cow's milk, soy, rice, oat
Italy/Spain Fish (more common than grains)
Adults Shellfish, fish, egg

Acute FPIES Management

  • Mild-Moderate: Oral rehydration, observation
  • Severe (shock): IV normal saline bolus 10-20 mL/kg
  • Ondansetron: 0.15-0.2 mg/kg IV/IM - reduces vomiting significantly
  • Methylprednisolone: 1 mg/kg (max 60-80 mg) IV - may help inflammation
  • Epinephrine NOT effective (non-IgE mechanism)
💡 Prognosis: Most children outgrow FPIES by age 3-5 years. Supervised OFC should be performed periodically (every 12-18 months) to assess for resolution.

💊 Peanut Oral Immunotherapy (Palforzia)

Palforzia (peanut allergen powder-dnfp) is the first FDA-approved treatment for peanut allergy, designed to reduce severity of allergic reactions from accidental exposure.

Key Points

  • FDA Approval: January 2020 (ages 4-17); July 2024 expansion to ages 1-3 years
  • Goal: Desensitization, NOT cure - reduces reaction severity to accidental exposure
  • Mechanism: Daily peanut protein exposure → immune tolerance induction
  • Must continue peanut avoidance even while on therapy

Treatment Phases

Phase Duration Details
Initial Dose Escalation Single day Multiple doses (0.5-6 mg) under medical supervision
Up-dosing ~20-40 weeks 11 dose levels; each new dose given in clinic, then daily at home
Maintenance Indefinite 300 mg daily (~1 peanut equivalent)

Efficacy (PALISADE Trial)

  • 67% of treated patients tolerated 600 mg peanut protein at exit challenge
  • vs 4% of placebo group
  • 100-fold increase in tolerated dose from baseline

REMS Requirements

  • Certified healthcare settings only
  • Initial dose escalation and each up-dose step in clinic
  • 60-minute observation after dose escalation
  • Patients must carry 2 epinephrine autoinjectors
⚠️ Contraindications:
• Uncontrolled asthma
• History of severe anaphylaxis requiring intubation
• Eosinophilic esophagitis
• Active eosinophilic GI disease

Managing Adverse Events

  • GI symptoms most common (abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting)
  • Oral pruritus/tingling common
  • Anaphylaxis occurs in ~10% during treatment
  • Dose adjustments for cofactors: illness, exercise, menses, sleep deprivation

🏥 Food Allergy Management

Allergen Avoidance

  • Label reading education: Identify all forms of allergen names
  • Cross-contact prevention: Separate utensils, cooking surfaces
  • "May contain" labels: Voluntary; recommend avoidance for sensitive patients
  • Restaurant dining: Communication with staff, chef cards

Emergency Preparedness

  • Written Food Allergy Action Plan for school/daycare
  • 2 epinephrine autoinjectors available at all times
  • Medical ID bracelet/necklace
  • Education on epinephrine use - demonstrate with trainer devices
Epinephrine First! For anaphylaxis to food, administer epinephrine IMMEDIATELY. Antihistamines do NOT treat anaphylaxis. Delayed epinephrine is the #1 cause of fatal food anaphylaxis.

Nutritional Considerations

  • Dietitian referral for multiple food allergies
  • Calcium/Vitamin D supplementation if avoiding dairy
  • Ensure adequate protein intake
  • Growth monitoring in children

📝 EBRS-Style Clinical Questions

Question 1: A 2-year-old presents 2 hours after eating oatmeal for the first time with repetitive vomiting, pallor, and lethargy. No urticaria or respiratory symptoms. This is his second such episode with oatmeal. SPT to oat is negative. What is the most likely diagnosis?

Correct Answer: B
This presentation is classic for acute FPIES: delayed (1-4h) repetitive vomiting without cutaneous/respiratory symptoms, with pallor and lethargy. Negative SPT is typical for FPIES (non-IgE mechanism). Oat is a common FPIES trigger. Meets major criterion + ≥3 minor criteria.

Question 2: A 7-year-old with peanut allergy has the following results: Peanut sIgE 45 kUA/L, Ara h 2 = 0.08 kUA/L, Ara h 8 = 38 kUA/L. He also has birch pollen allergic rhinitis. What is the most likely interpretation?

Correct Answer: B
Ara h 8 is a PR-10 protein (Bet v 1 homolog) associated with birch pollen cross-reactivity, not primary peanut allergy. Low Ara h 2 (<0.1 kUA/L) suggests sensitization without clinical allergy. The high whole peanut sIgE is due to Ara h 8 cross-reactivity. This patient may have OAS to peanut or be tolerant. OFC may be considered.

Question 3: A 5-year-old is being considered for Palforzia (peanut OIT). Which of the following is a contraindication to initiating therapy?

Correct Answer: C
Active EoE is a contraindication to Palforzia due to risk of exacerbating eosinophilic inflammation. Prior anaphylaxis (unless requiring intubation) is not a contraindication. Allergic rhinitis is not a contraindication. High Ara h 2 indicates peanut allergy (appropriate candidate), not a contraindication.

Question 4: An infant in the NICU develops vomiting, bloody diarrhea, and feeding intolerance on cow's milk formula at 3 weeks of life. SPT and sIgE to cow's milk are negative. What is the recommended formula switch?

Correct Answer: C
This presentation suggests non-IgE cow's milk protein allergy (possibly chronic FPIES or allergic proctocolitis). Soy formula is NOT recommended due to ~50% cross-reactivity with cow's milk in non-IgE GI allergies. Extensively hydrolyzed (EHF) or amino acid formula (AAF) is appropriate. Goat's milk has high cross-reactivity with cow's milk.
