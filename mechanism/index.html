Pneumonia & Respiratory Infections

From community-acquired to ventilator-associated: master the pathogens, presentations, and evidence-based antibiotic selection for respiratory infections.

1 📋 Pneumonia Classification
🏥 Where Was It Acquired?

Classification drives empiric antibiotic selection:

  • Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP): Onset in community or <48 hours after hospital admission. Most common type.
  • Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia (HAP): Onset ≥48 hours after hospital admission, not intubated.
  • Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP): Onset >48 hours after endotracheal intubation.
  • Healthcare-Associated Pneumonia (HCAP): Term now largely abandoned—use risk factor assessment instead.
Type Setting Common Pathogens Key Concern CAP Community S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, Atypicals, Viruses Severity assessment (CURB-65, PSI) HAP Hospital ≥48h S. aureus (MRSA), Pseudomonas, Enterobacteriaceae MDR risk factors VAP Ventilated >48h Pseudomonas, MRSA, Acinetobacter, Enterobacter Local antibiogram, de-escalation
💡 Risk Factors for MDR Pathogens
IV antibiotics in past 90 days, hospitalization ≥5 days, septic shock, ARDS before VAP, RRT before VAP, prior MDR colonization. These determine whether you need double Gram-negative coverage and/or MRSA coverage.
2 🦠 CAP: Pathogens & Clinical Clues
🎯 Typical vs Atypical Pneumonia

Classic teaching distinction (though overlap is common):

  • Typical: Sudden onset, high fever, productive cough, lobar consolidation. Think S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, S. aureus.
  • Atypical: Gradual onset, dry cough, extrapulmonary symptoms, patchy infiltrates. Think Mycoplasma, Chlamydia pneumoniae, Legionella.

In practice, clinical features poorly distinguish pathogens—empiric coverage should include both!

🔬 Key Pathogens & Clinical Pearls
Streptococcus pneumoniae
#1 cause of CAP. Rust-colored sputum. Lancet-shaped Gram+ diplococci. Associated bacteremia.
Haemophilus influenzae
COPD patients. Gram-negative coccobacilli. Often β-lactamase positive.
Mycoplasma pneumoniae
Young adults, outbreaks. "Walking pneumonia." Bullous myringitis, cold agglutinins.
Legionella pneumophila
Water exposure, travel. GI symptoms, hyponatremia, elevated LFTs. Urinary antigen test.
Staphylococcus aureus
Post-influenza, IVDU, cavitary lesions. Think MRSA if risk factors.
Klebsiella pneumoniae
Alcoholics, diabetics. "Currant jelly" sputum, upper lobe cavitation.
⚠️ Post-Influenza Pneumonia
Bacterial superinfection after influenza is often caused by S. aureus (including MRSA) or S. pneumoniae. Suspect if patient improves from flu then acutely worsens. These patients are often very sick—consider MRSA coverage empirically.
3 📊 Severity Assessment & Disposition
CURB-65 Score (CAP Severity)
CURB-65
CConfusion (new)
UUrea >7 mmol/L (BUN >19)
RRespiratory rate ≥30
BBlood pressure <90/60
65Age ≥65 years
🏠 Disposition Based on CURB-65
  • 0-1: Low risk (mortality ~1%). Outpatient treatment appropriate.
  • 2: Moderate risk (mortality ~9%). Consider short hospitalization or close outpatient follow-up.
  • 3-5: High risk (mortality 15-40%). Hospitalize; consider ICU for score 4-5.
💡 ATS/IDSA Major Criteria for Severe CAP (ICU)
Major criteria (1 = ICU): Septic shock requiring vasopressors OR respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation.

Minor criteria (≥3 = ICU): RR ≥30, P/F ≤250, multilobar infiltrates, confusion, BUN ≥20, WBC <4K, platelets <100K, temp <36°C, hypotension requiring fluids.
4 💊 CAP Antibiotic Selection
🚀 Empiric CAP Treatment (2019 ATS/IDSA Guidelines)
1
Outpatient, No Comorbidities
Amoxicillin 1g TID OR doxycycline 100mg BID OR azithromycin (if local resistance <25%)
2
Outpatient WITH Comorbidities
Amoxicillin-clavulanate + azithromycin OR respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin, moxifloxacin)
3
Inpatient, Non-ICU
β-lactam (ceftriaxone, ampicillin-sulbactam, cefotaxime) + azithromycin OR respiratory FQ monotherapy
4
Inpatient, ICU
β-lactam + azithromycin OR β-lactam + respiratory FQ. Add vancomycin if MRSA risk; add anti-pseudomonal if Pseudomonas risk.
📋 Comorbidities Requiring Broader Coverage
  • Chronic heart, lung, liver, or renal disease
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Alcoholism
  • Malignancy or immunosuppression
  • Asplenia
  • Antibiotic use in prior 3 months (use different class)
⚠️ When to Add MRSA Coverage
Add vancomycin or linezolid for: prior MRSA infection/colonization, recent hospitalization with parenteral antibiotics, severe/necrotizing pneumonia, post-influenza presentation, empyema. Check nasal MRSA swab—high negative predictive value!
5 🏥 HAP & VAP Management
🎯 HAP/VAP Empiric Strategy

Coverage decisions based on MDR risk factors and local antibiogram:

  • Low MDR risk: Single anti-pseudomonal agent (piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, levofloxacin, or meropenem)
  • High MDR risk or high mortality risk: Two anti-pseudomonal agents from different classes + MRSA coverage
  • Always: De-escalate based on cultures within 48-72 hours. Short course (7 days) if responding.
Anti-Pseudomonal Agents
Pip-tazo | Cefepime | Ceftazidime | Meropenem | Levofloxacin | Aminoglycosides
Use 2 from different classes when double-covering. Check local susceptibility patterns!
💡 VAP Prevention Bundle
Head of bed elevation 30-45°, daily sedation vacation + spontaneous breathing trial, DVT prophylaxis, stress ulcer prophylaxis (when indicated), daily oral care with chlorhexidine, minimize ventilator days.
6 🎯 Special Situations & Complications
💧 Parapneumonic Effusion & Empyema

Pleural fluid develops in 20-40% of bacterial pneumonia:

  • Simple parapneumonic: Sterile, pH >7.2, glucose >60, LDH <1000. Treat pneumonia, usually resolves.
  • Complicated parapneumonic: pH <7.2, glucose <60, LDH >1000, or positive Gram stain. Requires drainage.
  • Empyema: Frank pus or positive culture. Always drain (chest tube or VATS). Prolonged antibiotics (3-6 weeks).
🫁 Aspiration Pneumonia vs Pneumonitis
  • Aspiration pneumonitis: Chemical injury from gastric acid. Acute hypoxia, infiltrates (often bilateral, dependent). Usually sterile initially—antibiotics NOT routinely indicated unless fails to improve in 48h.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: Bacterial infection from oropharyngeal flora. More indolent, often in patients with dysphagia/altered consciousness. Cover oral anaerobes (amoxicillin-clavulanate, ampicillin-sulbactam, or clindamycin + FQ).
⏱️ Duration of Therapy
  • Uncomplicated CAP: Minimum 5 days, afebrile ≥48h, clinically stable before stopping
  • HAP/VAP: 7 days (shorter courses work if responding)
  • Pseudomonas/MRSA: May need 7+ days
  • Lung abscess/empyema: 3-6 weeks
  • Procalcitonin: Can guide discontinuation (stop when PCT drops >80% from peak or <0.25)

Question 1 of 12
A 45-year-old healthy woman presents with 3 days of cough, low-grade fever, and malaise. CXR shows patchy bilateral infiltrates. She has no comorbidities and CURB-65 score is 0.
What is the most appropriate empiric treatment?
ACeftriaxone + azithromycin IV
BAmoxicillin 1g TID oral
CPiperacillin-tazobactam IV
DVancomycin + cefepime IV
✓ Correct!
For outpatient CAP without comorbidities, amoxicillin 1g TID is first-line per 2019 ATS/IDSA guidelines. Alternatives include doxycycline or azithromycin (if local pneumococcal resistance <25%). IV antibiotics and hospitalization are unnecessary for CURB-65 = 0.
Question 2 of 12
A 68-year-old man with COPD and diabetes presents with fever, productive cough, and dyspnea. CURB-65 = 2. He was treated with levofloxacin for bronchitis 2 months ago.
What is the most appropriate outpatient regimen?
ALevofloxacin monotherapy
BAmoxicillin monotherapy
CAmoxicillin-clavulanate + azithromycin
DAzithromycin monotherapy
✓ Correct!
This patient has comorbidities (COPD, diabetes) and recent FQ use. Guidelines recommend using a different antibiotic class than prior 3 months. Amoxicillin-clavulanate + azithromycin covers typical and atypical pathogens without repeating the FQ. CURB-65 = 2 is borderline—close follow-up needed if outpatient.
Question 3 of 12
Which pathogen is most commonly associated with pneumonia in a patient with recent influenza infection?
AStaphylococcus aureus
BMycoplasma pneumoniae
CLegionella pneumophila
DPseudomonas aeruginosa
✓ Correct!
S. aureus (including MRSA) and S. pneumoniae are the most common causes of post-influenza bacterial pneumonia. Influenza damages respiratory epithelium and impairs mucociliary clearance. Post-flu staph pneumonia can be rapidly progressive and necrotizing—consider empiric MRSA coverage.
Question 4 of 12
A patient with pneumonia has pleural fluid with pH 7.0, glucose 35 mg/dL, and LDH 1500 U/L. What is the next step?
AContinue antibiotics and observe
BRepeat thoracentesis in 48 hours
CAdd anaerobic coverage
DChest tube drainage
✓ Correct!
This is a complicated parapneumonic effusion (pH <7.2, glucose <60, LDH >1000). These will not resolve with antibiotics alone and require drainage (chest tube or VATS). Delaying drainage leads to loculations and empyema. Simple parapneumonic effusions (pH >7.2, glucose >60) can be observed.
Question 5 of 12
A 35-year-old presents with pneumonia, diarrhea, and confusion. Labs show sodium 128 mEq/L. Which pathogen should you suspect?
AStreptococcus pneumoniae
BLegionella pneumophila
CHaemophilus influenzae
DKlebsiella pneumoniae
✓ Correct!
Legionella classically presents with pneumonia plus extrapulmonary features: GI symptoms (diarrhea), neurologic changes (confusion), and hyponatremia. Also look for elevated LFTs and relative bradycardia. Order urine Legionella antigen (detects serogroup 1). Treat with azithromycin or fluoroquinolone.
Question 6 of 12
Which CURB-65 score indicates the patient can likely be treated as an outpatient?
A3
B2
C0-1
D4
✓ Correct!
CURB-65 of 0-1 indicates low mortality risk (~1%) and outpatient treatment is appropriate. Score of 2 is intermediate—consider short admission or close follow-up. Score ≥3 warrants hospitalization, and 4-5 may need ICU evaluation.
Question 7 of 12
A 72-year-old nursing home resident develops pneumonia on hospital day 5. He received ceftriaxone for a UTI 2 weeks ago.
Which organisms must be covered empirically?
APseudomonas and MRSA
BMycoplasma and Chlamydia
CS. pneumoniae only
DAnaerobes only
✓ Correct!
This is HAP (≥48h after admission) with MDR risk factors (recent IV antibiotics, prolonged hospitalization). Must cover Pseudomonas and MRSA. Use an anti-pseudomonal β-lactam (pip-tazo, cefepime, or meropenem) plus vancomycin. De-escalate based on cultures.
Question 8 of 12
A witnessed aspiration event results in acute hypoxia and bilateral infiltrates. The patient is afebrile with normal WBC. What is the best initial management?
AAmpicillin-sulbactam + azithromycin
BClindamycin + levofloxacin
CPiperacillin-tazobactam
DSupportive care, no antibiotics initially
✓ Correct!
This is aspiration pneumonitis—chemical injury from gastric acid, not bacterial infection. It's initially sterile! Antibiotics are NOT indicated unless the patient fails to improve within 48 hours or develops signs of infection (fever, rising WBC, purulent sputum). Supportive care with oxygen and monitoring is appropriate.
Question 9 of 12
What is the recommended duration of antibiotic therapy for uncomplicated CAP?
A3 days
B5 days minimum, afebrile ≥48h before stopping
C10-14 days
D21 days
✓ Correct!
For uncomplicated CAP, treat for a minimum of 5 days AND until the patient has been afebrile for ≥48 hours and is clinically stable. Longer courses (10-14 days) were traditional but not supported by evidence—shorter is better to reduce resistance and side effects.
Question 10 of 12
An alcoholic presents with upper lobe cavitary pneumonia and "currant jelly" sputum. Which organism is most likely?
AStreptococcus pneumoniae
BMycobacterium tuberculosis
CKlebsiella pneumoniae
DPseudomonas aeruginosa
✓ Correct!
Klebsiella pneumoniae classically causes severe pneumonia in alcoholics and diabetics with upper lobe involvement, cavitation, and thick "currant jelly" sputum (bloody, mucoid). TB is also in the differential for cavitary upper lobe disease, but the acute presentation with characteristic sputum points to Klebsiella.
Question 11 of 12
For inpatient non-ICU CAP, which regimen is recommended?
ACeftriaxone + azithromycin
BVancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam
CAmoxicillin monotherapy
DMetronidazole + gentamicin
✓ Correct!
For inpatient non-ICU CAP, use β-lactam + macrolide (e.g., ceftriaxone + azithromycin) OR respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy. This covers both typical (pneumococcus) and atypical (Mycoplasma, Legionella) pathogens. The combination may have mortality benefit over FQ alone.
Question 12 of 12
Which test has high negative predictive value for ruling out MRSA pneumonia?
ASputum Gram stain
BProcalcitonin level
CBlood cultures
DNasal MRSA PCR swab
✓ Correct!
A negative nasal MRSA PCR has ~95% negative predictive value for MRSA pneumonia. If the nares are negative, you can often safely de-escalate off vancomycin/linezolid. This allows for antimicrobial stewardship without waiting for respiratory cultures.
