Plaque Rupture

Pathophysiology
Pathophysiology

Vulnerable plaques have thin fibrous caps, large lipid cores, and inflammatory cell infiltration. Mechanical stress or inflammation triggers rupture, exposing thrombogenic subendothelium and lipid core. Platelets adhere and activate → thrombus formation → coronary occlusion.

⏱️ Time Is Muscle

STEMI: Door-to-balloon ≤90 min (or ≤120 min if transfer). Every 30-minute delay = 7.5% increase in mortality. "Time is muscle" - myocardial salvage decreases exponentially after onset.

📖 ACC/AHA STEMI Guidelines 2013 (Updated 2023)
  • Aspirin: 162-325 mg (non-enteric) immediately for all ACS
  • P2Y12 inhibitor: Ticagrelor (preferred) or Clopidogrel loading dose
  • STEMI: Primary PCI preferred; fibrinolysis if PCI not available within 120 min
  • NSTEMI: Early invasive (≤24h) if high-risk features
🧠 Mechanism Check

A 58-year-old presents with 30 minutes of crushing chest pain. ECG shows 3mm ST elevation in V1-V4. The nearest PCI-capable hospital is 2 hours away. What is the MOST appropriate reperfusion strategy?

A Transfer for primary PCI (goal door-to-balloon ≤120 min)
B Administer fibrinolysis now, as PCI not available within 120 minutes
C Medical management only with DAPT and anticoagulation
D Wait for troponin results before deciding
✓ Correct!

For STEMI, reperfusion must occur ASAP. If first medical contact-to-PCI >120 minutes, guidelines recommend fibrinolysis (if no contraindications) within 30 min of arrival, followed by transfer for angiography within 3-24 hours. Don't wait - "time is muscle."

Natriuretic Peptides

Biomarker / Compensation
Pathophysiology

BNP and NT-proBNP are released from ventricular myocytes in response to wall stretch. They promote natriuresis, diuresis, and vasodilation - counteracting RAAS activation. Elevated levels correlate with filling pressures and prognosis. Used for diagnosis and monitoring of HF.

📖 2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA Heart Failure Guidelines

Heidenreich PA, et al. Circulation. 2022;145(18):e895-e1032

  • Diagnosis: BNP ≥35 pg/mL or NT-proBNP ≥125 pg/mL supports HF diagnosis
  • Acute HF: BNP ≥100 pg/mL or NT-proBNP ≥300 pg/mL (age-adjusted)
  • Prognosis: Higher levels = worse outcomes; ≥30% reduction with treatment is favorable
  • Obesity: Levels may be lower in obese patients (adjust thresholds)

🧠 Mechanism Check

A 65-year-old with long-standing hypertension presents with dyspnea. Echo shows EF 60%, concentric LVH, and impaired relaxation. BNP is elevated. What is the PRIMARY pathophysiologic mechanism?

A Decreased contractility causing reduced stroke volume
B Impaired ventricular relaxation causing elevated filling pressures
C Mitral regurgitation causing volume overload
D Right ventricular failure causing systemic congestion
✓ Correct!

This is HFpEF (Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction). The preserved EF (60%) with concentric LVH from chronic HTN causes diastolic dysfunction - the stiff ventricle cannot relax properly during diastole, leading to elevated filling pressures and pulmonary congestion despite normal systolic function.

AV Node
Conduction
Mechanism

The AV node provides critical delay (~120-200ms) allowing atrial contraction to complete before ventricular contraction. This delay creates the PR interval on ECG. The AV node has decremental conduction (slows with faster rates) and is influenced by autonomic tone.

💊 Clinical Relevance
  • AV nodal blockers (beta-blockers, CCBs, digoxin) slow conduction
  • Adenosine causes transient AV block (SVT termination)
  • Intrinsic rate 40-60 bpm serves as backup pacemaker
🧠 Mechanism Check

A 68-year-old on metoprolol presents with syncope. ECG shows regular P waves at 80 bpm, regular QRS at 35 bpm, with no relationship between P waves and QRS complexes. What is the diagnosis and treatment?

A Mobitz Type I - observation, hold metoprolol
B Third-degree AV block - transcutaneous pacing, then permanent pacemaker
C Atrial fibrillation with slow ventricular response - rate control
D Sinus bradycardia - atropine
✓ Correct!

Complete (3rd degree) AV block: Complete AV dissociation with independent atrial (80 bpm) and ventricular (35 bpm escape) rhythms. Symptomatic = emergent pacing. Atropine may help if block is at AV node, but usually ineffective. Requires permanent pacemaker.

Hypovolemic Shock

Pathophysiology

Decreased circulating volume → ↓ preload → ↓ stroke volume → ↓ cardiac output. Compensatory ↑ SVR and ↑ HR attempt to maintain BP. Causes: hemorrhage (trauma, GI bleed), dehydration (vomiting, diarrhea), third-spacing (burns, pancreatitis). Treatment: Volume resuscitation (crystalloid, blood products).

🧠 Mechanism Check

A patient has BP 78/52, HR 118, CVP 18, CO 2.8 L/min (low), SVR elevated, EF 25% on echo. What type of shock and first-line treatment?

AHypovolemic; IV fluids
BCardiogenic; inotropes (dobutamine) ± vasopressors
CDistributive; norepinephrine
DObstructive; pericardiocentesis
✓ Correct!

Cardiogenic shock: Low CO + High CVP + High SVR + Low EF = pump failure. Treatment: inotropes (dobutamine) to improve contractility, vasopressors if severely hypotensive, consider mechanical support (IABP, Impella).

Hypertensive Encephalopathy
Mechanism

Failure of cerebral autoregulation. Normally, cerebral blood flow is maintained constant across MAP 60-150 mmHg. In chronic HTN, this curve shifts rightward. When BP exceeds the upper limit of autoregulation, cerebral hyperperfusion occurs → vasogenic edema (especially posterior brain = PRES). Symptoms: headache, confusion, visual changes, seizures.

💊 Management
• Nicardipine or labetalol IV infusion
• Target: 20-25% reduction in first 1-2 hours
• MRI shows posterior reversible encephalopathy (PRES)
• Avoid nitroprusside (may worsen ICP)
🧠 Mechanism Check

A 58-year-old presents with severe chest pain radiating to the back, BP 210/120. CXR shows widened mediastinum. What is the most important initial treatment?

A Nicardipine infusion to lower BP
B Esmolol first, then nitroprusside if needed
C Nitroprusside alone for rapid BP control
D Oral labetalol and observe
✓ Correct!
This is aortic dissection until proven otherwise. Treatment: β-blocker FIRST (esmolol) to reduce HR <60 and dP/dt (shear stress), THEN add vasodilator (nitroprusside) if needed. Starting vasodilator alone causes reflex tachycardia which increases shear stress and worsens dissection. Target SBP <120 within 10-20 min. Get CTA chest STAT.

S. pneumoniae (Pneumococcus)

CAP Pathogen
Pathophysiology

Streptococcus pneumoniae is the most common cause of CAP. Gram-positive diplococci with polysaccharide capsule (virulence factor). Causes lobar pneumonia with dense consolidation. Associated with "rusty" sputum. Risk factors: age >65, COPD, alcoholism, asplenia.

📖 ATS/IDSA CAP Guidelines 2019

Metlay JP, et al. Am J Respir Crit Care Med. 2019;200(7):e45-e67

  • Outpatient (healthy): Amoxicillin OR Doxycycline OR Macrolide
  • Outpatient (comorbidities): Respiratory FQ OR β-lactam + macrolide
  • Inpatient (non-severe): β-lactam + macrolide OR respiratory FQ
  • Inpatient (severe/ICU): β-lactam + macrolide (or FQ)
  • Obtain sputum/blood cultures if severe CAP or ICU admission
🧠 Mechanism Check

A 68-year-old with COPD presents with productive cough, fever, and right lower lobe consolidation on CXR. CURB-65 score is 2. What is the recommended empiric antibiotic regimen per ATS/IDSA guidelines?

A Amoxicillin alone
B β-lactam (ceftriaxone) + macrolide (azithromycin) OR respiratory fluoroquinolone

✓ Correct!

CURB-65 of 2 indicates inpatient treatment. Per ATS/IDSA 2019: for non-severe inpatient CAP, use β-lactam + macrolide (ceftriaxone + azithromycin) OR respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin/moxifloxacin). This covers typical (S. pneumoniae) and atypical pathogens.

Venous Stasis

Virchow's Triad
Pathophysiology

Stagnant blood flow allows accumulation of activated clotting factors and prevents dilution/clearance by flowing blood. Risk factors: immobility (hospitalization, long flights >4h), paralysis, surgery (especially orthopedic), obesity, pregnancy (compressed IVC).

📖 CHEST VTE Guidelines 2021
  • DOACs preferred over warfarin for VTE (except cancer, severe renal)
  • Duration: Provoked = 3 mo; Unprovoked = extended (weigh bleeding risk)
  • Massive PE: Systemic thrombolysis if hemodynamically unstable
  • Submassive PE: Consider catheter-directed therapy if RV dysfunction + biomarkers
🧠 Mechanism Check

A 45-year-old woman 10 days post-knee replacement presents with acute dyspnea and pleuritic chest pain. HR 110, BP 88/60, SpO2 88%. ECG shows S1Q3T3. What is the MOST appropriate next step?

✓ Correct!

This is massive (high-risk) PE with hemodynamic instability (hypotension). D-dimer is not useful when PTP is high. CT-PA delays treatment in unstable patient. Bedside echo can show RV strain. Start anticoagulation immediately; systemic thrombolysis indicated for massive PE with shock.

🧠 Mechanism Check

A patient with severe asthma exacerbation has been receiving continuous albuterol for 1 hour. Initial ABG showed pH 7.48, pCO₂ 32. Repeat ABG shows pH 7.38, pCO₂ 40. The patient appears more tired. What does this indicate?

A Patient is improving - pCO₂ normalizing
B Impending respiratory failure - prepare for intubation
C Metabolic acidosis developing
D Overtreatment with albuterol
✓ Correct!
A "normalizing" pCO₂ in acute asthma is a danger sign. During an asthma attack, patients hyperventilate → low pCO₂. If pCO₂ rises to normal, it means the patient is fatiguing and can no longer maintain the hyperventilation needed to compensate. Combined with appearing tired, this indicates impending respiratory failure. Prepare for intubation and escalate care immediately.

5 Mechanisms of Hypoxemia

1. V/Q Mismatch (Most common!)
↑ A-a

Ventilation and perfusion don't match. Some areas over-perfused (shunt-like), some under-perfused (dead space).

Examples: COPD, asthma, pneumonia, PE

✓ Responds to supplemental O₂

2. Shunt
↑ A-a

Blood bypasses ventilated alveoli entirely. "Wasted" perfusion.

Examples: ARDS, pneumonia (consolidated), AVM, intracardiac shunt

✗ Does NOT respond well to O₂ (blood never sees it!)

3. Diffusion Impairment
↑ A-a

Thickened alveolar-capillary membrane slows O₂ transfer.

Examples: Pulmonary fibrosis, ILD

✓ Responds to O₂, worsens with exercise

4. Hypoventilation
Normal A-a

Not breathing enough → CO₂ rises → O₂ falls (but lung itself is fine)

Examples: Opioids, obesity hypoventilation, NM disease

✓ Responds to O₂ (and ventilatory support)

5. Low Inspired O₂
Normal A-a

Less O₂ in inspired air → Less O₂ in blood

Examples: High altitude, confined space

✓ Responds to supplemental O₂

Most common GN worldwide
Normal C3

Lupus Nephritis

50% of SLE patients develop renal involvement
↓C3, ↓C4

Nephrotic Diseases

Minimal Change Disease (MCD)

#1 cause of nephrotic syndrome in CHILDREN
Presentation: Children 2-6 years. Sudden onset nephrotic syndrome, often after URI. Selective proteinuria (albumin).
Biopsy: Normal on LM. EM: Podocyte foot process effacement. IF negative.
Tx: Excellent response to steroids (90%). Relapse common but steroid-responsive.

Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)

#1 cause of nephrotic syndrome in ADULTS (especially African Americans)
Causes: Primary (idiopathic), HIV, heroin, obesity, sickle cell, reflux nephropathy, genetic (APOL1).
Biopsy: Focal (some glomeruli) + Segmental (part of glomerulus) sclerosis. EM: foot process effacement.
Tx: Steroids (less responsive than MCD). May need calcineurin inhibitors. High recurrence post-transplant.

Membranous Nephropathy

#1 cause of nephrotic syndrome in WHITE adults
Associations: Primary (anti-PLA2R antibody in 70%). Secondary: HBV, SLE, NSAIDs, penicillamine, solid tumors (lung, colon).
Biopsy: Thickened GBM, "spike and dome" on silver stain. Subepithelial deposits. Granular IgG/C3 on IF.
⚠️ High risk of venous thromboembolism (renal vein thrombosis!)
Tx: Conservative if stable. Rituximab or calcineurin inhibitors if progressive. Screen for malignancy in older adults.

Diabetic Nephropathy

#1 cause of ESRD in the US
Progression: Hyperfiltration → Microalbuminuria (30-300 mg/day) → Overt proteinuria → Nephrotic syndrome → ESRD.
Biopsy: Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules, GBM thickening, mesangial expansion.
Tx: ACEi/ARB (even if normotensive), SGLT2 inhibitors, tight glucose control, BP control, finerenone.

Amyloidosis

Types: AL (primary, plasma cell dyscrasia), AA (secondary, chronic inflammation like RA).
Biopsy: Congo red stain → Apple-green birefringence under polarized light. Amyloid deposits in glomeruli.
Tx: Treat underlying cause. AL: Chemo (bortezomib). AA: Control inflammation.

Acetaminophen Toxicity
Mechanism

NAPQI accumulation: Normally, APAP is metabolized via glucuronidation/sulfation. In overdose, these pathways saturate → CYP2E1 produces toxic metabolite NAPQI → normally detoxified by glutathione → glutathione depletion → NAPQI binds hepatocytes → centrilobular necrosis. NAC replenishes glutathione and provides alternative detoxification pathway.

💊 Management
• NAC protocol - start immediately if suspected, don't wait for level
• Check APAP level at 4 hours post-ingestion, plot on Rumack-Matthew nomogram
• Activated charcoal if <4 hours and patient protecting airway
• Monitor INR, creatinine, lactate, pH
• Contact transplant center early if meeting King's criteria

Thrombotic Stroke

Ischemic Stroke
Pathophysiology

Large vessel atherosclerosis causes progressive stenosis of cerebral arteries (ICA, MCA, vertebrobasilar). Plaque rupture triggers in-situ thrombosis → acute occlusion. Often preceded by TIAs. Risk factors: HTN, DM, hyperlipidemia, smoking.

⏱️ Time Is Brain

~1.9 million neurons die per minute during ischemic stroke. "Door-to-needle" goal: ≤60 minutes. Every 15-minute reduction in tPA time = 5% better outcomes.

📖 AHA/ASA Acute Ischemic Stroke Guidelines 2019

Powers WJ, et al. Stroke. 2019;50(12):e344-e418

  • IV Alteplase: ≤4.5 hours; 0.9 mg/kg (max 90 mg), 10% bolus
  • Thrombectomy: LVO (ICA/M1), NIHSS ≥6, within 6-24h if favorable imaging
  • BP pre-tPA: <185/110 before treatment
  • BP post-tPA: <180/105 for 24 hours
  • No tPA: Antiplatelet within 24-48h

Mechanism

Focal seizures originate in one hemisphere. "Aware" means consciousness is preserved throughout. Symptoms depend on the cortical region involved: motor (jerking), sensory (tingling), autonomic (flushing, GI sensation), or psychic (déjà vu, fear). Previously called "simple partial seizure."

Graves' Disease
Mechanism

Autoimmune hyperthyroidism caused by thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins (TSI) that bind and activate TSH receptors. Results in diffuse thyroid hyperplasia and excess T3/T4. Unique features: ophthalmopathy (proptosis), dermopathy (pretibial myxedema), diffuse goiter. RAIU is elevated and diffuse.

💊 Treatment Options
• Antithyroid drugs: Methimazole (preferred) or PTU (pregnancy 1st trimester)
• Radioactive iodine (RAI): Definitive, leads to hypothyroidism
• Surgery: Large goiter, suspicious nodules, severe ophthalmopathy
• Beta-blockers: Symptomatic relief (tremor, tachycardia)

Primary Adrenal Insufficiency (Addison's Disease)
Mechanism

Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)
Mechanism

Systemic activation of coagulation → widespread microthrombi → consumption of platelets and clotting factors → simultaneous thrombosis AND bleeding. Triggered by: sepsis (#1), trauma, malignancy (especially APL), obstetric complications (amniotic fluid embolism, abruption), massive transfusion.

⚠️ DIC = Always Secondary
DIC never occurs in isolation - always find and treat the underlying cause (sepsis, malignancy, etc.). Supportive care alone won't resolve DIC.
💊 Treatment
• Treat underlying cause - most important!
• If bleeding: FFP (factors), cryoprecipitate (fibrinogen <100), platelets (if <50k + bleeding)
• If thrombosis predominant: Consider heparin (controversial)
• Tranexamic acid generally avoided (can worsen thrombosis)

Anaphylaxis
Allergen + IgE on mast cells→ Degranulation→ Histamine release→ Vasodilation + Bronchospasm + Edema
Clinical Features
  • Urticaria, angioedema
  • Bronchospasm, stridor
  • Hypotension, tachycardia
  • GI: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
Treatment
  • Epinephrine IM - FIRST LINE
  • IV fluids
  • H1 + H2 blockers
  • Steroids (prevent biphasic)
  • Glucagon if on β-blockers
Critical: Epinephrine is the ONLY drug that saves lives in anaphylaxis. Give it EARLY, IM into anterolateral thigh.

  • Drug reaction (sulfonamides, anticonvulsants, allopurinol)
  • Mucosal involvement + skin sloughing
  • SJS: <10% BSA
  • TEN: >30% BSA
  • Nikolsky positive

🩺 Reproductive

OB-GYN essentials

Amenorrhea Workup
Rule out pregnancy (β-hCG)→ Check TSH, prolactin→ Progestin challenge→ Check FSH
Primary Amenorrhea

No menses by age 15 (with secondary sexual characteristics) or 13 (without)

  • Turner syndrome: 45,XO, streak gonads, short stature
  • Müllerian agenesis: Absent uterus, normal ovaries
  • Imperforate hymen: Cyclic pain, hematocolpos
Secondary Amenorrhea

No menses for 3 months (regular) or 6 months (irregular)

  • Pregnancy - always first!
  • PCOS - hyperandrogenism, anovulation
  • Hypothalamic - stress, weight loss, exercise
  • Hyperprolactinemia - pituitary adenoma
PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome)
Insulin resistance→ ↑LH (relative to FSH)→ Theca cell hyperandrogenism→ Anovulation
Rotterdam Criteria (need 2 of 3)
  • Oligo/anovulation
  • Clinical or biochemical hyperandrogenism
  • Polycystic ovaries on ultrasound
Management
  • Lifestyle: Weight loss (↑insulin sensitivity)
  • OCPs: Regulate cycles, ↓androgens
  • Metformin: Insulin sensitizer
  • Spironolactone: Anti-androgen for hirsutism
  • Clomiphene/letrozole: If fertility desired
Associations: ↑Risk of endometrial cancer (unopposed estrogen), diabetes, metabolic syndrome
Ectopic Pregnancy
Classic triad: Amenorrhea + vaginal bleeding + pelvic pain. But triad present in <50%!
Risk Factors
  • Prior ectopic
  • PID / tubal damage
  • IUD (doesn't cause, but if pregnant → ↑risk ectopic)
  • Tubal surgery / ligation
  • Infertility treatments
Diagnosis & Management
  • β-hCG + transvaginal US
  • Discriminatory zone: ~1500-2000 mIU/mL
  • Methotrexate: Stable, unruptured, β-hCG <5000
  • Surgery: Unstable, ruptured, high β-hCG

Mood Disorders
Major Depressive Disorder

SIG E CAPS (≥5 for ≥2 weeks):

  • Sleep changes
  • Interest loss (anhedonia)
  • Guilt/worthlessness
  • Energy loss
  • Concentration problems
  • Appetite/weight changes
  • Psychomotor changes

Bipolar Disorder

DIG FAST (manic episode):

  • Distractibility
  • Indiscretion/Impulsivity
  • Grandiosity
  • Flight of ideas
  • Activity increase
  • Sleep decreased
  • Talkativeness

Bipolar I: Full mania. Bipolar II: Hypomania + depression

Treatment: Depression = SSRIs first-line. Bipolar = Mood stabilizers (lithium, valproate, lamotrigine). Antidepressants alone can trigger mania in bipolar!

Psychotic Disorders
Schizophrenia

≥2 symptoms for ≥6 months, ≥1 must be from first 3:

  • Delusions
  • Hallucinations
  • Disorganized speech
  • Disorganized/catatonic behavior
  • Negative symptoms

Negative symptoms: Flat affect, avolition, alogia, anhedonia

Brief vs Schizophreniform
  • Brief psychotic: <1 month, full recovery
  • Schizophreniform: 1-6 months
  • Schizophrenia: ≥6 months
  • Schizoaffective: Schizophrenia + mood episodes
Antipsychotic Side Effects: EPS (acute dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia), NMS (fever, rigidity, AMS, autonomic instability), metabolic syndrome. Clozapine: agranulocytosis (monitor WBC).

Psychiatric Emergencies
Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome
  • Fever, rigidity ("lead pipe")
  • Altered mental status
  • Autonomic instability
  • ↑CK, leukocytosis

Tx: Stop antipsychotic, supportive, dantrolene, bromocriptine

Serotonin Syndrome
  • Hyperthermia, diaphoresis
  • Agitation, AMS
  • Tremor, hyperreflexia, clonus
  • Diarrhea

Tx: Stop serotonergic agents, benzos, cyproheptadine

NMS vs Serotonin Syndrome: NMS = rigidity, slow onset (days). SS = clonus/hyperreflexia, rapid onset (hours).
