🤰 Preeclampsia

Placental Dysfunction → Systemic Endothelial Disease

📚 Core Definition

Preeclampsia = NEW-ONSET hypertension + proteinuria (or end-organ dysfunction) after 20 weeks gestation

Diagnostic Criteria (ACOG):

  • BP ≥140/90 on two occasions ≥4 hours apart after 20 weeks (previously normotensive)
  • PLUS proteinuria (≥300mg/24h or protein:creatinine ≥0.3) OR end-organ dysfunction

End-organ dysfunction (can diagnose without proteinuria):

  • Platelets <100,000
  • Creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or doubling of baseline
  • Liver transaminases >2× normal
  • Pulmonary edema
  • New-onset headache or visual symptoms

⚙️ Pathophysiology

Preeclampsia begins with abnormal placentation in early pregnancy, leading to systemic endothelial dysfunction.

Two-Stage Model:

Abnormal trophoblast invasion
→
Incomplete spiral artery remodeling
→
Placental hypoxia/ischemia
→
Anti-angiogenic factors released
→
Systemic endothelial dysfunction

Why multi-organ involvement? Endothelial dysfunction is SYSTEMIC, affecting kidneys (proteinuria), liver (elevated enzymes), brain (headache, seizures), and vasculature (hypertension).

🔴 Severe Features

Preeclampsia with Severe Features (any one of):
  • SBP ≥160 or DBP ≥110 (on two readings, 4 hours apart)
  • Platelets <100,000/μL
  • Liver transaminases >2× normal OR RUQ/epigastric pain
  • Creatinine >1.1 mg/dL or doubling
  • Pulmonary edema
  • New-onset headache (not responding to meds) or visual disturbances

HELLP Syndrome:

Severe variant of preeclampsia affecting 10-20% of severe cases:

  • Hemolysis (↑LDH, ↑bilirubin, schistocytes)
  • Elevated Liver enzymes (AST/ALT >2× normal)
  • Low Platelets (<100,000)
Clinical Pearl: HELLP can present with minimal hypertension or proteinuria. Always check platelets and LFTs in pregnant patients with RUQ pain, nausea, or malaise.

💊 Treatment Principles

Antihypertensive Therapy:

Goal: SBP <160 and DBP <110 (to prevent stroke)

MedicationDoseNotes
Labetalol20mg IV, then 40mg, 80mg q10minFirst-line; avoid in asthma
Hydralazine5-10mg IV q20minReflex tachycardia possible
Nifedipine10-20mg PO q30minImmediate-release; oral option

Seizure Prophylaxis:

Magnesium Sulfate - Given for ALL severe preeclampsia and during/after delivery
  • Loading: 4-6g IV over 20-30 minutes
  • Maintenance: 1-2g/hour IV infusion
  • Continue 24-48 hours postpartum
  • Monitor: reflexes, respiratory rate, urine output
  • Therapeutic level: 4-7 mEq/L
Magnesium Toxicity Signs:
  • Loss of DTRs (first sign) - occurs at ~10 mEq/L
  • Respiratory depression - occurs at ~15 mEq/L
  • Cardiac arrest - occurs at ~25 mEq/L
Antidote: Calcium gluconate 1g IV

👶 Delivery Decisions

Definitive treatment of preeclampsia = DELIVERY

Timing of Delivery:

ConditionGestational AgeManagement
Preeclampsia without severe features≥37 weeksDeliver

Preeclampsia with severe features<34 weeksSteroids for fetal lung maturity, then deliver; may attempt 48h expectant mgmt in select cases

Don't delay delivery for: Uncontrolled severe hypertension, eclampsia, pulmonary edema, placental abruption, DIC, or non-reassuring fetal status.

⚡ Eclampsia

Definition: New-onset seizures in a patient with preeclampsia (without other cause)

Management of Eclamptic Seizure:

  1. ABCs - Protect airway, position on side
  2. Magnesium sulfate - 4-6g IV bolus (if not already on Mg)
  3. Control BP - Labetalol or hydralazine if severely elevated
  4. Prepare for delivery - Once stabilized
Eclampsia can occur:
  • Antepartum (most common)
  • Intrapartum
  • Postpartum (up to 6 weeks, most within 48 hours)

🧠 Test Your Understanding

A 32-year-old G1P0 at 35 weeks presents with BP 168/108, headache, and RUQ pain. Labs show platelets 82,000, AST 245, LDH 890. What is the most appropriate next step?

A. Expectant management with close monitoring for 48 hours
B. Start labetalol and recheck BP in 4 hours
C. Administer betamethasone and plan delivery in 48 hours
D. Start magnesium, control BP, and plan for delivery
Correct Answer: D

This patient has HELLP syndrome (Hemolysis indicated by elevated LDH, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets) with severe features (BP ≥160/110, symptoms, platelet count <100K). At 35 weeks with HELLP, the management is stabilization (magnesium for seizure prophylaxis, antihypertensives for severe BP) followed by delivery. Expectant management is not appropriate with HELLP. While steroids could be considered, delivery should not be delayed for 48 hours in HELLP syndrome.
