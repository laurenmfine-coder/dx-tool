Grade 3 Severe respiratory (significant bronchospasm) OR GI (vomiting, diarrhea) Epinephrine IM, bronchodilators, IV access Grade 4 Cardiovascular (hypotension, shock, loss of consciousness) Epinephrine IM, IV fluids, recumbent position, emergency care

Contraindications to AIT

Absolute Contraindications:
• Uncontrolled asthma (FEV1 <70% predicted)
• Severe/unstable asthma
• Inability to tolerate epinephrine (relative)
• Pregnancy (do not initiate; may continue if already on maintenance)

Relative Contraindications:
• Beta-blocker use (may reduce epinephrine efficacy)
• ACE inhibitor use (may worsen angioedema)
• Severe cardiovascular disease
• Autoimmune disease (theoretical concern)

Risk Factors for Systemic Reactions

  • Uncontrolled or symptomatic asthma - Most important risk factor
  • History of prior systemic reaction to AIT
  • High degree of allergen sensitivity
  • Dosing errors
  • Injections during peak allergen season
  • Rush or cluster immunotherapy protocols
Fatal Reactions: Rare (~1 per 2.5 million injections). Nearly all fatalities occur in patients with asthma, often uncontrolled. Always assess asthma control before EVERY injection.

📝 EBRS-Style Clinical Questions

Question 1: A 28-year-old with allergic rhinitis to grass pollen is considering immunotherapy. She works long hours and has difficulty attending weekly clinic visits. Her symptoms are primarily in spring and she has negative skin tests to dust mite, ragweed, and tree pollens. What is the most appropriate recommendation?

Correct Answer: B
This patient has monosensitization to grass pollen and difficulty with frequent office visits, making SLIT an ideal option. Grastek (grass pollen) can be self-administered at home after the first dose. She should start 12 weeks before grass season. SCIT with multiple allergens is unnecessary since she's only sensitized to grass.

Question 2: A 45-year-old man had anaphylaxis with hypotension after a yellow jacket sting 2 months ago. Venom skin testing is positive to yellow jacket and hornet. His baseline serum tryptase is 18 ng/mL (normal <11). What is the recommended VIT duration?

Correct Answer: C
This patient has elevated baseline tryptase (>11 ng/mL), suggesting mastocytosis or clonal mast cell disease. Patients with elevated tryptase have higher risk of recurrent severe reactions if VIT is discontinued. Combined with his severe initial reaction (hypotension), lifelong VIT is recommended. He should also be evaluated for systemic mastocytosis.

Question 3: A patient on SCIT maintenance arrives for her injection. She reports increased cough and wheeze over the past week and forgot her rescue inhaler today. Peak flow is 68% of her personal best. What is the appropriate action?

Correct Answer: C
Uncontrolled asthma (symptoms + PEF <70%) is a contraindication to allergen immunotherapy injection. The injection should be withheld until asthma is controlled. Giving the injection—even with pre-treatment or reduced dose—puts this patient at risk for severe bronchospasm or fatal reaction. Optimize her asthma first, then resume SCIT.

Question 4: Which immunologic change is most associated with clinical improvement during allergen immunotherapy?

Correct Answer: B
IgG4 blocking antibodies increase significantly during AIT and compete with IgE for allergen binding, preventing mast cell activation. Allergen-specific IgE may actually increase initially before gradually declining over years. Regulatory T cells increase (not decrease), producing IL-10 and TGF-β. Th2 cytokines decrease (not increase) as the immune response shifts toward tolerance.
