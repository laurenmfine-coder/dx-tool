⚙️ Malignant Hyperthermia

Pathophysiology | Anesthesia/Critical Care

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • RYR1 mutation → uncontrolled calcium release → hypermetabolism
  • Triggers: volatile anesthetics, succinylcholine
  • ETCO2 rise is earliest sign; hyperthermia is LATE
  • Dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg IV is definitive treatment
  • MH Hotline: 1-800-644-9737 (MHAUS)

🔬 Core Pathophysiology

Calcium Release Cascade

Genetic mutation in RYR1 causes uncontrolled calcium release from sarcoplasmic reticulum.

Pathway

Triggering agent exposure→RYR1 channel dysfunction→Uncontrolled Ca²⁺ release from SR→Sustained muscle contraction→Hypermetabolism → heat + CO2 + lactate

⚠️ Triggering Agents

Volatile anesthetics: Sevoflurane, desflurane, isoflurane, halothane

Depolarizing NMB: Succinylcholine

SAFE: Propofol, opioids, nitrous oxide, non-depolarizing NMBs, local anesthetics

🏥 Clinical Correlations

Rising ETCO2 (earliest sign)

Mechanism: Hypermetabolism produces excess CO2

Significance: Often the FIRST sign during anesthesia; unexplained rise should trigger suspicion

Masseter muscle rigidity after succinylcholine

Mechanism: Abnormal calcium handling causes sustained contraction

Significance: May be early warning sign; 50% association with MH susceptibility

Hyperthermia (late sign)

Mechanism: Heat generated from sustained muscle contraction

Significance: Temperature rises 1-2°C every 5 minutes; can exceed 43°C
