⚙️ Toxidromes

Pathophysiology | Toxicology

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Sympathomimetic vs Anticholinergic: WET vs DRY skin
  • Opioid triad: CNS depression, respiratory depression, miosis
  • Cholinergic (SLUDGE): atropine + pralidoxime
  • Know the antidotes: naloxone (opioid), flumazenil (benzo), physostigmine (anticholinergic)
  • Serotonin syndrome: clonus + hyperthermia + AMS → cyproheptadine

🔬 Core Pathophysiology

Sympathomimetic

Excess catecholamine activity.

Pathway

↑ NE/dopamine release or reuptake inhibition→CNS stimulation→Tachycardia, HTN, hyperthermia→Mydriasis, DIAPHORESIS→Agitation, seizures

Anticholinergic

Muscarinic receptor blockade.

Pathway

ACh receptor blockade→Loss of parasympathetic tone→Tachycardia, DRY skin→Mydriasis, urinary retention→Hyperthermia, delirium

Cholinergic (SLUDGE/DUMBELS)

Excess acetylcholine activity.

Pathway

AChE inhibition→ACh accumulation→SLUDGE: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI upset, Emesis→Bradycardia, miosis→Bronchorrhea, bronchospasm

Opioid

Mu-receptor agonism.

Pathway

Mu-receptor activation→CNS depression→Respiratory depression→Miosis (pinpoint pupils)→Decreased GI motility

Sedative-Hypnotic

GABA enhancement.

Pathway

GABA-A potentiation→CNS depression→Respiratory depression→Normal pupils→Hypothermia

🏥 Clinical Correlations

Wet vs Dry skin

Mechanism: Sympathomimetic = diaphoresis; Anticholinergic = dry

Significance: KEY differentiator between these similar-appearing toxidromes

Pinpoint pupils + respiratory depression

Mechanism: Mu-receptor activation

Significance: Pathognomonic for opioid toxicity

SLUDGE symptoms

Mechanism: Cholinergic excess from organophosphate/nerve agent

Significance: Treat with atropine (muscarinic blocker) + pralidoxime
