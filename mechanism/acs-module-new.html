Acute Coronary Syndrome

From plaque rupture to reperfusion: understanding the pathophysiology, ECG patterns, biomarker kinetics, and time-critical management of myocardial ischemia.

0% complete
🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate STEMI, NSTEMI, and unstable angina based on clinical, ECG, and biomarker findings

1 🔬 Pathophysiology of ACS
🩸 Atherosclerotic Plaque Rupture

The fundamental event in most ACS is disruption of an atherosclerotic plaque, exposing thrombogenic material to blood flow:

  • Vulnerable Plaques: Thin fibrous cap (<65 μm), large lipid core (>40% of plaque volume), active inflammation with macrophages
  • Plaque Rupture: Physical stress causes fibrous cap disruption, exposing lipid core and tissue factor
  • Plaque Erosion: Alternative mechanism (more common in women, diabetics) - endothelial denudation without rupture
  • Thrombosis: Platelet adhesion → activation → aggregation → fibrin deposition → occlusive or non-occlusive thrombus
💡 Clinical Pearl
The degree of stenosis does NOT predict plaque rupture risk. Most MIs occur from plaques with <50% stenosis that suddenly rupture. This is why stress testing can miss "vulnerable" plaques—they don't limit flow until they rupture.
⏱️ The Ischemic Cascade

When coronary blood flow is interrupted, a predictable sequence of events occurs:

  1. Seconds: Diastolic dysfunction (impaired relaxation)
  2. ~20 seconds: Systolic dysfunction (wall motion abnormality)
  3. ~30 seconds: ECG changes (ST-T abnormalities)
  4. ~1-2 minutes: Symptoms (chest pain/anginal equivalent)
  5. >20 minutes: Irreversible myocyte death begins (necrosis)
⚡ Key Point: Time = Myocardium
Wavefront phenomenon: Necrosis starts in the subendocardium (most vulnerable to ischemia) and progresses transmurally over 3-6 hours. Early reperfusion salvages myocardium—this is why door-to-balloon time matters!
2 📊 The ACS Spectrum
📈 Classification by ECG and Biomarkers

ACS is a clinical syndrome ranging from unstable angina to STEMI, distinguished by ECG findings and cardiac biomarkers:

🔴 STEMI

  • Pathology: Complete coronary occlusion
  • ECG: ST elevation ≥1mm in 2+ contiguous leads (≥2mm in V2-V3)
  • Troponin: Will be elevated (may be negative initially)
  • Treatment: Emergent reperfusion (PCI or fibrinolysis)
  • Time Goal: Door-to-balloon <90 min

🟡 NSTEMI

  • Pathology: Partial occlusion with distal embolization
  • ECG: ST depression, T-wave inversion, or non-diagnostic
  • Troponin: Elevated (diagnostic criterion)
  • Treatment: Antithrombotic + early invasive strategy
  • Time Goal: Cath within 24-72h based on risk

🔵 Unstable Angina

  • Pathology: Partial occlusion, no necrosis
  • ECG: May have ST-T changes or be normal
  • Troponin: Normal (negative x2)
  • Treatment: Antithrombotic + risk stratification
  • Evolution: Becoming less common with hs-Troponin
⚠️ STEMI Equivalents - Don't Miss!
  • Posterior MI: ST depression V1-V3 (reciprocal) + ST elevation V7-V9
  • de Winter pattern: Upsloping ST depression at J-point with tall, symmetric T waves in precordial leads (= proximal LAD occlusion)
  • Wellens syndrome: Biphasic or deeply inverted T waves in V2-V3 during pain-free interval (= critical LAD stenosis)
  • New LBBB: Consider STEMI equivalent if clinical suspicion high (use Sgarbossa criteria)
3 📍 ECG Localization
🗺️ Correlating ECG Leads with Coronary Anatomy

ECG changes in contiguous leads indicate the location of ischemia and the culprit artery:

ECG Leads Wall/Territory Culprit Artery Complications
V1-V4 Anterior / Septal LAD LV dysfunction, CHF, VT/VF, heart block
V5-V6, I, aVL Lateral LCx or Diagonal Mitral regurgitation
II, III, aVF Inferior RCA (85%) or LCx Bradycardia, heart block, RV infarct
V1-V3 (depression) Posterior RCA or LCx Often with inferior MI
V1, V4R Right Ventricle Proximal RCA Hypotension, JVD, clear lungs
💡 Clinical Pearl: RV Infarct
When you see inferior STEMI, always get right-sided leads (V4R). RV infarct occurs in ~30-50% of inferior MIs. These patients are preload-dependent—nitrates and diuretics can cause severe hypotension. Treat with fluids!
4 🧪 Cardiac Biomarkers
📊 Troponin Kinetics

Cardiac troponins (cTnI, cTnT) are the gold standard biomarkers for myocardial necrosis:

  • Rise: 3-6 hours after symptom onset (hs-Troponin: 1-3 hours)
  • Peak: 12-24 hours
  • Duration: 7-14 days (cTnI) or 10-14 days (cTnT)
High-Sensitivity Troponin Rule-Out Protocols
0/1-hour: hs-cTn < LOD at 0h AND Δ < 3 ng/L at 1h → Rule Out
0/3-hour: hs-cTn < 99th %ile at 0h AND 3h with Δ < 50% → Rule Out
LOD = Limit of Detection. These protocols allow rapid, safe rule-out of MI in low-risk patients.
⚠️ Troponin Elevation ≠ ACS
Many conditions cause troponin elevation without ACS (Type 2 MI or myocardial injury):
  • Tachyarrhythmias, Heart failure, Myocarditis
  • PE, Sepsis, Renal failure (decreased clearance)
  • Hypertensive crisis, Takotsubo cardiomyopathy
  • Cardiac contusion, Post-cardioversion/ablation
Context matters! A rising/falling pattern with ischemic symptoms suggests ACS.
5 💊 Acute Management
🚑 Initial ACS Management Algorithm
1
Immediate: IV access, O₂ if SpO₂ < 90%, continuous monitoring, 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes
2
Antiplatelet: Aspirin 162-325 mg chewed (non-enteric coated) + P2Y12 inhibitor (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, or prasugrel)
3
Anticoagulation: Heparin (UFH or enoxaparin) or bivalirudin if PCI planned
4
Anti-ischemic: Nitroglycerin SL/IV (avoid if hypotensive, RV infarct, or PDE5 inhibitor use), Beta-blocker (if no contraindications)
5
Reperfusion (STEMI): Primary PCI preferred (door-to-balloon <90 min). Fibrinolysis if PCI unavailable within 120 min of first medical contact.
⏰ Time Targets - Know These!
  • ECG: Within 10 minutes of arrival
  • Door-to-balloon (primary PCI): ≤90 minutes
  • First medical contact-to-balloon (transfer): ≤120 minutes
  • Door-to-needle (fibrinolysis): ≤30 minutes
  • NSTEMI high-risk: Cath within 24 hours
