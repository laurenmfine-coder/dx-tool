1 Pathophysiology of VTE
🔺 Virchow's Triad

Three factors predispose to venous thromboembolism:

  • Stasis: Immobility, prolonged travel, bed rest, venous obstruction
  • Endothelial injury: Surgery, trauma, central lines, prior DVT
  • Hypercoagulability: Inherited (Factor V Leiden, Protein C/S deficiency) or acquired (malignancy, pregnancy, estrogen, antiphospholipid syndrome)

Most PEs originate from proximal deep vein thrombosis (iliac, femoral, popliteal veins). Calf DVTs rarely embolize unless they propagate proximally.

💨 Hemodynamic Consequences

When a clot lodges in the pulmonary arteries:

  • Increased pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR): Mechanical obstruction + vasoconstriction (serotonin, thromboxane release)
  • Right ventricular strain: RV is thin-walled and poorly tolerates acute pressure load → RV dilation → interventricular septal shift → impaired LV filling
  • Decreased cardiac output: Results from impaired LV preload
  • Hypoxemia: V/Q mismatch (perfusion defect), shunting, decreased mixed venous O2
⚡ The Death Spiral of Massive PE
PE → ↑PVR → RV dilation → Septal shift → ↓LV filling → ↓Cardiac output → ↓Coronary perfusion → RV ischemia → Further RV failure → Obstructive shock → Death
2 Clinical Presentation
🩺 Signs and Symptoms

Common symptoms:

  • Dyspnea (73%) - often sudden onset, worse with exertion
  • Pleuritic chest pain (66%) - suggests peripheral PE with pleural involvement
  • Cough (37%)
  • Hemoptysis (13%) - suggests pulmonary infarction
  • Syncope (rare but ominous - suggests massive PE)

Common signs:

  • Tachypnea (70%)
  • Tachycardia (30%)
  • Signs of DVT (leg swelling, tenderness) - present in only ~30%
  • Low-grade fever
  • Hypoxemia (not always present!)
💎 Clinical Pearl: The "Normal" PE
A normal oxygen saturation does NOT rule out PE. Up to 20% of patients with PE have SpO2 >95% on room air. The A-a gradient is more sensitive but still not perfect. Clinical suspicion drives workup.
⚠️ Red Flags for Massive/High-Risk PE
  • Syncope or near-syncope
  • Hypotension (SBP <90 or drop ≥40 for >15 min)
  • Severe hypoxemia despite supplemental O2
  • Bradycardia (suggests impending arrest)
  • Elevated JVP, RV heave
  • Altered mental status

  • >4 points: PE likely (proceed to imaging)
🔬 PERC Rule (Pulmonary Embolism Rule-out Criteria)

In low pre-test probability patients, if ALL 8 PERC criteria are negative, PE can be ruled out without D-dimer:

  • Age <50
  • HR <100
  • SpO2 ≥95% on room air
  • No hemoptysis
  • No estrogen use
  • No prior DVT/PE
  • No unilateral leg swelling
  • No surgery/trauma requiring hospitalization in past 4 weeks

Key: Only apply PERC if your gestalt is already "low probability" (roughly <15%). PERC is not a screening tool.

4 Diagnostic Approach
📋 PE Diagnostic Algorithm
1
Assess pre-test probability: Wells score or clinical gestalt
2
Low probability: Apply PERC. If PERC negative → no further testing. If PERC positive → D-dimer
3
Moderate probability: Check D-dimer (use age-adjusted cutoff: age × 10 for patients >50)
4
High probability OR elevated D-dimer: CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA)
5
If CTPA contraindicated: V/Q scan, lower extremity ultrasound, or pulmonary angiography
🔍 D-Dimer: Pearls and Pitfalls

D-dimer is a fibrin degradation product. Elevated when clot is being broken down.

High sensitivity, low specificity:

  • Negative D-dimer effectively rules OUT PE in low/moderate probability
  • Positive D-dimer does NOT rule IN PE - many false positives

False positives (elevated D-dimer without PE):

  • Age >50 (use age-adjusted cutoff)
  • Pregnancy
  • Recent surgery or trauma
  • Malignancy
  • Infection/sepsis
  • Renal failure
  • Hospitalized patients (almost always elevated)
📏 Age-Adjusted D-Dimer Cutoff
For patients >50 years: Cutoff = Age × 10 ng/mL (e.g., for 70-year-old: 700 ng/mL instead of 500). This improves specificity without sacrificing sensitivity, reducing unnecessary CTPAs by ~30% in elderly patients.

🫀 RV Dysfunction: Why It Matters

RV dysfunction identifies patients at higher risk for deterioration even if currently hemodynamically stable.

Signs of RV dysfunction on imaging:

  • CT: RV/LV ratio >0.9 (or >1.0 depending on source)
  • Echo: RV dilation, hypokinesis, McConnell's sign (akinetic RV free wall with spared apex), septal flattening ("D-sign"), TAPSE <16mm

Biomarkers of RV strain:

  • Troponin: RV ischemia from strain
  • BNP/NT-proBNP: RV wall stress
6 Treatment Approach
💊 Anticoagulation: The Foundation

Anticoagulation is the cornerstone of PE treatment. It prevents clot propagation and allows endogenous fibrinolysis.

First-line options:

  • DOACs (rivaroxaban, apixaban): Preferred for most patients. Can start without lead-in heparin. Avoid in severe renal impairment, active cancer (use LMWH), or hemodynamic instability.
  • LMWH (enoxaparin): Preferred in cancer-associated VTE. Weight-based dosing.
  • UFH: Preferred in high-risk PE (may need thrombolysis), severe renal failure, or when rapid reversal needed (surgery planned).
  • Fondaparinux: Alternative in HIT.

Duration:

  • Provoked PE (surgery, immobilization): 3 months
  • Unprovoked PE: Minimum 3 months, then reassess for indefinite therapy
  • Cancer-associated PE: Continue while cancer active (often indefinite)
🚨 High-Risk (Massive) PE: Immediate Actions
  1. Hemodynamic support: IVF cautiously (500mL max - RV is preload sensitive), vasopressors (norepinephrine preferred)
  2. Anticoagulation: IV heparin bolus
  3. Systemic thrombolysis: Alteplase 100mg over 2 hours (or 50mg bolus if arrest imminent)
  4. If thrombolysis contraindicated or failed: Catheter-directed therapy or surgical embolectomy
  5. ECMO: Bridge to intervention in refractory shock
💉 Thrombolysis: When to Consider

Clear indication: High-risk (massive) PE with hemodynamic instability

Consider in: Intermediate-high risk PE with clinical deterioration despite anticoagulation

Absolute contraindications:

  • Prior intracranial hemorrhage
  • Ischemic stroke within 3 months
  • Active bleeding
  • Brain tumor or AVM
  • Recent brain or spinal surgery

Relative contraindications: Recent surgery, recent GI bleed, pregnancy, advanced age (risk-benefit)

🏠 Outpatient PE Treatment
Selected low-risk patients (sPESI = 0, no RV dysfunction, good social situation) can be treated as outpatients with DOACs. This requires: reliable follow-up, ability to return if worsening, no significant comorbidities, adequate home support.
