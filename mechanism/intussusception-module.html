🔄 Intussusception

Telescoping Bowel → Obstruction → Ischemia

📚 Core Definition

Intussusception = Telescoping of one segment of bowel (intussusceptum) into an adjacent distal segment (intussuscipiens)

Epidemiology:

  • Most common cause of intestinal obstruction in children 6 months to 3 years
  • Peak incidence: 5-9 months of age
  • Male:Female ratio = 3:1
  • Most common location: Ileocolic (ileum into colon)
  • ~90% are idiopathic in children

⚙️ Pathophysiology

Lead point or peristalsis
→
Bowel telescopes
→
Mesentery dragged
→
Venous congestion
→
Edema → ischemia

Why "Currant Jelly" Stools?

Venous congestion → mucosal ischemia → sloughing → blood + mucus = "currant jelly" appearance. This is a LATE sign indicating bowel compromise.

🔍 Lead Points

Idiopathic (90% in children)

Thought to be related to lymphoid hyperplasia (Peyer's patches) after viral infection (adenovirus, rotavirus)

Pathologic Lead Points (10%)

Lead PointAge Group

Clinical Pearl: If intussusception occurs outside typical age (>3 years) or recurs multiple times, search for a pathologic lead point.

🩺 Clinical Presentation

Classic Triad (present in <50%):
  1. Intermittent colicky abdominal pain
  2. Currant jelly stools
  3. Palpable "sausage-shaped" mass

More Common Presentation:

  • Episodic pain - Child screams, draws knees to chest for 10-20 min
  • Pain-free intervals - Child may appear normal between episodes
  • Vomiting - Initially non-bilious, may become bilious
  • Lethargy - Between painful episodes ("altered mental status")
  • Mass - RUQ "sausage-shaped," may have "Dance's sign" (empty RLQ)
Late/Ominous Signs:
  • Currant jelly stools (blood + mucus)
  • Bilious vomiting
  • Abdominal distension
  • Shock

🔬 Diagnosis

Ultrasound (Gold Standard)

Classic Findings:
  • "Target sign" (transverse view) - Concentric rings
  • "Pseudokidney sign" (longitudinal view)
  • Sensitivity >95%

Abdominal X-ray:

  • May be normal early
  • Soft tissue mass in RUQ
  • Paucity of gas in RLQ
  • Signs of obstruction (air-fluid levels)
  • Free air = perforation

💊 Management

Initial Stabilization:

  • NPO status
  • IV fluid resuscitation
  • NG tube if vomiting
  • Surgical consultation

Non-Operative Reduction (First-Line)

Air or Contrast Enema:
  • Air enema preferred (safer if perforation)
  • Success rate: 80-95%
  • Performed by radiologist with surgical backup
  • Reduction confirmed by free flow into terminal ileum

Contraindications to Enema Reduction:

  • Peritonitis
  • Perforation (free air on X-ray)
  • Shock unresponsive to resuscitation
  • Prolonged symptoms (>48 hours relative contraindication)

Surgical Reduction:

Required if enema fails or contraindicated. May require bowel resection if necrosis present.

Recurrence: ~10% recurrence rate after enema reduction, usually within 72 hours. Each recurrence can be treated with repeat enema if no contraindications.

🧠 Test Your Understanding

An 8-month-old infant presents with episodes of screaming and drawing knees to chest, followed by periods of lethargy. Ultrasound shows a "target sign" in the RUQ. The child is hemodynamically stable with soft abdomen. What is the next step?

A. Emergency laparotomy
B. Air enema reduction
C. Observation and serial exams
D. CT scan of abdomen
Correct Answer: B

This is a classic presentation of intussusception (episodic pain with lethargy between episodes, target sign on ultrasound). In a hemodynamically stable child without signs of perforation or peritonitis, air enema reduction is the first-line treatment with an 80-95% success rate. Surgery is reserved for failed reduction or when enema is contraindicated.
