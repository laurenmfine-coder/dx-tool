🫁 COPD Pathophysiology

Airflow limitation, gas exchange, and CO₂ retention mechanisms

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the pathophysiology of airflow limitation in COPD
  • Explain the mechanisms of V/Q mismatch and CO₂ retention
  • Differentiate COPD phenotypes (emphysema vs chronic bronchitis)
  • Understand why oxygen must be used carefully in CO₂ retainers
  • Apply GOLD criteria for classification and management

The Core Problem: Airflow Limitation

COPD is characterized by persistent airflow limitation that is usually progressive and associated with an enhanced chronic inflammatory response to noxious particles or gases.

🔴 Emphysema Component

  • Destruction of alveolar walls
  • Loss of elastic recoil
  • Air trapping (can't exhale fully)
  • Enlarged air spaces distal to terminal bronchioles
  • "Pink puffer" phenotype

🔵 Chronic Bronchitis Component

  • Inflammation of airways
  • Mucus hypersecretion
  • Airway wall thickening
  • Productive cough ≥3 months/year for 2 years
  • "Blue bloater" phenotype
🔑 The FEV₁/FVC Ratio

Post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC <0.70 confirms airflow limitation. Unlike asthma, COPD obstruction is NOT fully reversible. FEV₁ determines severity (GOLD 1-4).

Why CO₂ Retention Occurs

Not all COPD patients retain CO₂. Understanding why some do is critical for management.

The V/Q Mismatch Cascade

1. Ventilation-Perfusion Mismatch: Damaged alveoli are poorly ventilated but still perfused → blood passes through without adequate gas exchange.

2. Hypoxic Pulmonary Vasoconstriction Fails: Chronic hypoxia blunts this protective reflex, worsening V/Q matching.

3. Dead Space Increases: Emphysematous destruction creates areas of ventilation without perfusion.

4. Respiratory Muscle Fatigue: Hyperinflation puts diaphragm at mechanical disadvantage.

⚠️ The Oxygen Paradox in CO₂ Retainers

Why high-flow O₂ is dangerous: CO₂ retainers rely on hypoxic drive for ventilation. Give too much O₂ → remove hypoxic drive → hypoventilation → CO₂ narcosis → respiratory arrest. Target SpO₂ 88-92% in these patients!

💡 Clinical Pearl: Haldane Effect

Oxygen also displaces CO₂ from hemoglobin (Haldane effect), releasing more CO₂ into blood. This contributes to hypercapnia when giving O₂ to chronic retainers - it's not just about removing hypoxic drive!

GOLD Classification & Management

GOLD Stage FEV₁ (% predicted) Severity
GOLD 1 ≥80% Mild
GOLD 2 50-79% Moderate
GOLD 3 30-49% Severe
GOLD 4 <30% Very Severe

Pharmacologic Approach

Bronchodilators (Mainstay)

  • LAMA (tiotropium) - once daily
  • LABA (salmeterol, formoterol)
  • LAMA+LABA for more symptoms
  • SABA PRN for rescue

ICS: When to Add

  • Frequent exacerbations (≥2/year)
  • Eosinophils ≥300 cells/μL
  • Asthma-COPD overlap
  • Triple therapy: ICS+LAMA+LABA

COPD Exacerbation Management

An acute worsening of respiratory symptoms requiring additional therapy.

🔑 Exacerbation Treatment: The Essentials

Bronchodilators (SABA ± SAMA nebulized)
Steroids (prednisone 40mg × 5 days)
Antibiotics (if purulent sputum, severe exacerbation, or requiring mechanical ventilation)
Oxygen (target 88-92% in CO₂ retainers)
NIV (BiPAP for respiratory acidosis pH <7.35)

💡 When to Use BiPAP

BiPAP (non-invasive ventilation) in COPD exacerbation: pH <7.35 with PaCO₂ >45 despite initial therapy. Reduces intubation rates and mortality. Contraindicated if patient cannot protect airway or has hemodynamic instability.

A 68-year-old man with severe COPD (FEV₁ 28% predicted) presents with acute dyspnea. ABG on room air: pH 7.31, PaCO₂ 62, PaO₂ 52. You start oxygen. What is the target SpO₂?
A94-98%
B92-96%
C88-92%
D85-88%
✓ Correct!
Target SpO₂ 88-92% in COPD patients at risk for hypercapnic respiratory failure. This patient has CO₂ retention (PaCO₂ 62) and relies partly on hypoxic drive. Higher oxygen targets risk worsening hypercapnia through loss of hypoxic drive and the Haldane effect.
Question 2 of 8
Which finding on spirometry confirms COPD diagnosis?
AFEV₁ <80% predicted
BPost-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC <0.70
CFVC <80% predicted
D>12% improvement with bronchodilator
✓ Correct!
Post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC <0.70 confirms the presence of airflow limitation that is not fully reversible - the hallmark of COPD. FEV₁ alone determines severity (GOLD stage), not diagnosis. Significant bronchodilator response (>12% and >200mL) suggests asthma component.
Question 3 of 8
A COPD patient presents with increased dyspnea and purulent sputum. pH 7.28, PaCO₂ 72 mmHg on 2L NC. What is the most appropriate next step?
AIncrease oxygen to 6L NC
BImmediate intubation
CIV methylprednisolone only
DNon-invasive ventilation (BiPAP)
✓ Correct!
BiPAP is indicated for COPD exacerbation with respiratory acidosis (pH <7.35, elevated PaCO₂). It reduces work of breathing, improves ventilation, and decreases intubation rates and mortality. Increasing O₂ would worsen hypercapnia. Intubation reserved for BiPAP failure or contraindications.
Question 4 of 8
What is the mechanism by which emphysema causes airflow limitation?
ALoss of elastic recoil causing airway collapse during expiration
BBronchospasm from smooth muscle contraction
CFixed upper airway obstruction
DPulmonary fibrosis
✓ Correct!
Emphysema destroys alveolar walls, causing loss of elastic recoil. The airways are normally held open by the elastic pull of surrounding alveoli. Without this "tethering," small airways collapse during expiration, trapping air. This is different from asthma (bronchospasm) or restrictive disease (fibrosis).
Question 5 of 8
When should inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) be added to COPD therapy?
AAll COPD patients at diagnosis
BOnly during exacerbations
CFrequent exacerbations or blood eosinophils ≥300
DNever - ICS are contraindicated in COPD
✓ Correct!
ICS are added for frequent exacerbators (≥2 moderate or ≥1 hospitalization/year) or those with blood eosinophils ≥300 cells/μL. ICS increase pneumonia risk in COPD, so they're not used routinely. The eosinophil count helps predict ICS response - higher eosinophils = more likely to benefit.
Question 6 of 8
The "blue bloater" phenotype in COPD is associated with:
APrimarily emphysema with weight loss
BChronic bronchitis, hypoxemia, and CO₂ retention
CAlpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency
DAsthma-COPD overlap
✓ Correct!
"Blue bloater" = chronic bronchitis phenotype: cyanotic (blue) from hypoxemia, often overweight (bloated), with CO₂ retention, cor pulmonale, and peripheral edema. "Pink puffer" = emphysema: thin, pursed-lip breathing, maintaining oxygenation until late. These are clinical phenotypes; most patients have overlap.
Question 7 of 8
What is the recommended duration of systemic corticosteroids for a COPD exacerbation?
A5 days
B10-14 days
C3 weeks with taper
DUntil symptoms resolve
✓ Correct!
5 days of prednisone 40mg (or equivalent) is as effective as longer courses and reduces steroid side effects. The REDUCE trial showed no difference in outcomes between 5-day and 14-day courses. No taper needed for short courses. Longer courses increase hyperglycemia, infection risk, and adrenal suppression.
Question 8 of 8
What is the Haldane effect and why is it relevant in COPD?
AOxygen shifts the hemoglobin dissociation curve right
BCO₂ increases oxygen affinity to hemoglobin
CLow pH increases oxygen delivery to tissues
DOxygenated hemoglobin carries less CO₂, contributing to hypercapnia when O₂ is given
✓ Correct!
The Haldane effect: oxygenated hemoglobin has reduced affinity for CO₂. When you give oxygen to a CO₂ retainer, hemoglobin releases CO₂ into the blood, raising PaCO₂. This contributes to oxygen-induced hypercapnia beyond just the loss of hypoxic ventilatory drive. Both mechanisms explain why high O₂ is dangerous in chronic CO₂ retainers.
