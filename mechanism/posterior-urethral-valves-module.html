🚫 Posterior Urethral Valves

The #1 Cause of Pediatric End-Stage Renal Disease

📚 Overview

Posterior Urethral Valves (PUV) are obstructing membranous folds in the prostatic urethra that cause bladder outlet obstruction. They occur exclusively in males and are the most common cause of severe obstructive uropathy in children.

Epidemiology

ParameterValue
Incidence1 in 5,000-8,000 male births
SexMales ONLY
Bilateral hydronephrosis~70%
Progress to ESRD25-30%
Prenatal diagnosis~50% detected on prenatal US
⚠️ HIGH STAKES: PUV is the #1 cause of pediatric ESRD from a congenital urologic cause. Early diagnosis and intervention are critical to preserve renal function.

🧬 Embryology & Pathophysiology

Embryologic Origin: PUV result from abnormal integration of the mesonephric ducts into the primitive urethra during development of the urogenital sinus (weeks 5-14).

Valve Types (Young Classification)

TypeDescriptionFrequency
Type IFolds radiating from verumontanum distally to membranous urethra95%
Type IIFolds from verumontanum to bladder neck (likely not true valves)Controversial

Pathophysiologic Cascade

URETHRAL OBSTRUCTION (PUV) ↓ INCREASED BLADDER PRESSURE ↓ ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ │ BLADDER CHANGES UPPER TRACT EFFECTS │ │ • Wall thickening • Bilateral VUR (50%) • Trabeculation • Hydroureteronephrosis • Diverticula • Renal dysplasia • Detrusor dysfunction • "VURD" or "Pop-off" │ │ └────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ ↓ DECREASED FETAL URINE OUTPUT ↓ OLIGOHYDRAMNIOS ↓ PULMONARY HYPOPLASIA + Potter facies

VURD Syndrome

VURD = Valves, Unilateral Reflux, Renal Dysplasia
Paradoxically, unilateral reflux into a dysplastic kidney can be protective - it acts as a "pop-off" valve, decompressing the system and protecting the contralateral kidney. These patients may have better long-term renal function!

🔍 Clinical Presentation

Prenatal (50%+ detected)

  • Bilateral hydronephrosis with dilated bladder
  • "Keyhole" sign: Dilated bladder + dilated posterior urethra
  • Oligohydramnios (severe = poor prognosis)
  • Thickened bladder wall
  • Echogenic kidneys (dysplasia)

Neonatal

  • Palpable distended bladder
  • Weak urinary stream or dribbling
  • Respiratory distress (pulmonary hypoplasia)
  • Failure to thrive
  • Urosepsis
  • Palpable kidneys (hydronephrotic)

Later Childhood (if missed)

  • Recurrent UTIs
  • Voiding dysfunction (incontinence, urgency)
  • Poor urinary stream
  • Chronic kidney disease symptoms
⚠️ Red Flag: Male infant with bilateral hydronephrosis + distended bladder = PUV until proven otherwise!

🔬 Diagnosis

Imaging Workup

StudyFindingsRole
Prenatal USKeyhole sign, bilateral hydro, oligohydramniosInitial detection
Postnatal USHydronephrosis, thick bladder, dilated posterior urethraConfirmation
VCUGDilated posterior urethra, trabeculated bladder, VURDIAGNOSTIC - GOLD STANDARD
MAG3 Renal ScanDifferential renal functionPrognostic
VCUG is ESSENTIAL for diagnosis! Classic findings:
  • Dilated, elongated posterior urethra
  • Abrupt caliber change at valve level
  • Trabeculated bladder
  • ± VUR (often high grade, bilateral)

Labs

  • Serum creatinine (baseline and trend)
  • Nadir creatinine <1.0 mg/dL = better prognosis
  • Electrolytes (hyperkalemia risk)
  • Urinalysis (infection)

🔪 Management

Initial Stabilization

  1. Bladder drainage - Foley catheter (6-8 Fr feeding tube if Foley won't pass)
  2. Monitor urine output, electrolytes, creatinine
  3. Antibiotics if infection suspected
  4. Manage respiratory status if pulmonary hypoplasia

Definitive Treatment: Valve Ablation

Primary Endoscopic Valve Ablation (PEVA) is the treatment of choice.
  • Cystoscopic resection of valve leaflets
  • Using cold knife, Bugbee electrode, or laser
  • Can be done in neonatal period if stable
  • Re-evaluate with VCUG post-procedure

If Ablation Not Feasible (premature, too small)

OptionDescription
VesicostomyTemporary bladder drainage via stoma
UreterostomyIf severe upper tract dilation, poor renal function
PyelostomyRarely used

Long-term Management

  • Bladder dysfunction is lifelong - may persist despite valve ablation
  • CIC (clean intermittent catheterization) often needed
  • Anticholinergics for overactive bladder
  • Monitor renal function throughout life
  • Manage CKD/ESRD if progresses
  • Transition to adult urology care
Valve Bladder Syndrome: Even after successful valve ablation, the bladder may never function normally. Features include:
  • Poor compliance
  • Myogenic failure
  • Incomplete emptying
  • May require augmentation cystoplasty

Outcomes

  • ESRD: 25-30% progress to ESRD
  • Continence: Many achieve daytime continence, nighttime more difficult
  • Fertility: Often preserved if no vas deferens injury
  • Mortality: Related to pulmonary hypoplasia severity

🧠 Quick Quiz

A newborn male has bilateral hydronephrosis, a thick-walled bladder, and oligohydramnios detected prenatally. What is the GOLD STANDARD diagnostic test?

A. Renal ultrasound
B. Voiding cystourethrogram (VCUG)
C. MRI pelvis
D. CT urogram
Correct: B - VCUG
VCUG is the gold standard for diagnosing PUV. It shows the dilated posterior urethra, the point of obstruction, trabeculated bladder, and any associated VUR. Ultrasound is useful for screening but cannot visualize the valves directly.
