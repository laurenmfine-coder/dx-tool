🫁 ARDS Pathophysiology

Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome: Why lungs fail and how to protect them

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the Berlin criteria for ARDS diagnosis
  • Explain the pathophysiology of diffuse alveolar damage
  • Apply lung-protective ventilation strategies
  • Understand the rationale for prone positioning
  • Recognize ventilator-induced lung injury (VILI) mechanisms

Berlin Definition of ARDS

ARDS is defined by acute onset of hypoxemic respiratory failure with bilateral infiltrates not explained by heart failure.

CriteriaDefinition
TimingWithin 1 week of known insult or new/worsening symptoms
ImagingBilateral opacities not fully explained by effusions, collapse, or nodules
Origin of EdemaNot fully explained by cardiac failure or fluid overload
Oxygenation (P/F ratio)Mild: 200-300 | Moderate: 100-200 | Severe: <100 (on PEEP ≥5)
🔑 P/F Ratio: The Key Metric

P/F = PaO₂ ÷ FiO₂. Example: PaO₂ 60 on 60% FiO₂ = 60/0.6 = 100. This patient has moderate ARDS. Normal P/F is ~500. The lower the ratio, the worse the oxygenation.

Pathophysiology: Diffuse Alveolar Damage

The Three Phases

1️⃣ Exudative (Days 1-7)

  • Endothelial & epithelial injury
  • Protein-rich edema floods alveoli
  • Surfactant dysfunction
  • Hyaline membrane formation
  • Neutrophil infiltration

2️⃣ Proliferative (Days 7-21)

  • Type II pneumocyte proliferation
  • Fibroblast migration
  • Organization of exudate
  • Beginning of repair OR...
  • Progression to fibrosis

3️⃣ Fibrotic (>3 weeks)

  • Collagen deposition
  • Alveolar destruction
  • Decreased compliance
  • Prolonged ventilator dependence
  • Variable recovery
💡 The "Baby Lung" Concept

In ARDS, the lung is not uniformly diseased. Only a small portion remains aerated (the "baby lung"). Ventilating with normal tidal volumes overdistends this small functional area, causing further injury. This is why low tidal volume ventilation is critical.

Lung-Protective Ventilation

⚠️ Ventilator-Induced Lung Injury (VILI)

Volutrauma: Overdistension from high tidal volumes
Barotrauma: High airway pressures causing air leaks
Atelectrauma: Repetitive opening/closing of alveoli
Biotrauma: Inflammatory mediator release from mechanical injury

🔑 ARDSNet Protocol Targets

Tidal Volume: 6 mL/kg ideal body weight (IBW, not actual weight)
Plateau Pressure: <30 cm H₂O
Driving Pressure: <15 cm H₂O (Pplat - PEEP)
PEEP: Titrate using PEEP/FiO₂ tables
pH Target: ≥7.20 (permissive hypercapnia acceptable)

Prone Positioning

Proning improves oxygenation and survival in moderate-severe ARDS (P/F <150).

Why Does Proning Work?

1. Recruitment: Posterior lung (compressed in supine) opens up

2. V/Q Matching: Perfusion is more uniform; ventilation improves posteriorly

3. Reduced Compression: Heart no longer compresses left lower lobe

4. Better Drainage: Secretions drain more easily

💡 PROSEVA Trial

Prone positioning for ≥16 hours/day in severe ARDS (P/F <150) reduced 28-day mortality from 32.8% to 16%. Start early (within 48 hours). This is now standard of care for moderate-severe ARDS.

🧪 Test Your Understanding

Question 1 of 5
A patient with pneumonia has PaO₂ 70 mmHg on FiO₂ 0.7 with bilateral infiltrates and PEEP 8. Echo shows normal EF. What is the P/F ratio and ARDS severity?
AP/F 100, Mild ARDS
BP/F 200, Moderate ARDS
CP/F 100, Moderate ARDS
DP/F 70, Severe ARDS
✓ Correct!
P/F = 70 ÷ 0.7 = 100. Berlin criteria: P/F 100-200 = Moderate ARDS. This patient meets all criteria: acute onset, bilateral opacities, not cardiogenic, P/F 100 on PEEP ≥5.
Question 2 of 5
What is the target tidal volume for a patient with ARDS who weighs 100 kg but has an ideal body weight of 70 kg?
A600 mL (6 mL × 100 kg actual)
B420 mL (6 mL × 70 kg IBW)
C840 mL (12 mL × 70 kg IBW)
D500 mL (average)
✓ Correct!
Tidal volume uses IDEAL body weight, not actual. Lungs don't enlarge with obesity. 6 mL/kg × 70 kg IBW = 420 mL. Using actual weight causes volutrauma.
Question 3 of 5
Plateau pressure is 34 cm H₂O, PEEP 14, TV 6 mL/kg IBW. What should you do?
ADecrease tidal volume further
BIncrease PEEP
CIncrease tidal volume
DNo changes needed
✓ Correct!
Pplat >30 risks barotrauma. Reduce TV to 4-5 mL/kg IBW. Accept permissive hypercapnia. Driving pressure (34-14=20) is also too high (goal <15).
Question 4 of 5
Why does prone positioning improve oxygenation in ARDS?
AIncreased cardiac output
BDecreased oxygen consumption
CImproved LV function
DRecruitment of posterior lung and improved V/Q matching
✓ Correct!
In supine position, posterior lung is compressed causing atelectasis. Proning recruits this lung while perfusion is maintained, improving V/Q matching. PROSEVA showed mortality benefit.
Question 5 of 5
Which mechanism of VILI involves repetitive opening and closing of alveoli?
AVolutrauma
BAtelectrauma
CBarotrauma
DBiotrauma
✓ Correct!
Atelectrauma is shear stress from cyclical opening/closing of alveoli. PEEP prevents this by keeping alveoli open. Volutrauma = overdistension, Barotrauma = pressure injury, Biotrauma = inflammatory response.
