Gastrointestinal Bleeding

Upper vs Lower GI Bleeding: Localization, Risk Stratification, and Management
⏱️ ~45 minutes 📚 16 concepts ❓ 6 MCQs
1 Classification of GI Bleeding
📍 Anatomic Classification

Upper GI Bleeding (UGIB): Proximal to ligament of Treitz

  • Peptic ulcer disease (most common - 40%)
  • Esophageal/gastric varices
  • Mallory-Weiss tear
  • Erosive gastritis/esophagitis
  • Malignancy
  • Dieulafoy lesion, angiodysplasia

Lower GI Bleeding (LGIB): Distal to ligament of Treitz

  • Diverticulosis (most common)
  • Angiodysplasia
  • Colitis (ischemic, infectious, IBD)
  • Hemorrhoids
  • Colorectal cancer/polyps
  • Post-polypectomy bleeding
🎯 Clinical Presentations

Hematemesis: Vomiting blood - ALWAYS upper GI

Coffee-ground emesis: Altered blood - upper GI

Melena: Black tarry stool - usually upper GI (>90%), occasionally proximal colon

Hematochezia: Bright red blood per rectum - usually lower GI, BUT can be brisk upper GI bleed

💎 Key Pearl: Hematochezia ≠ Always Lower GI
In a hemodynamically unstable patient with hematochezia, ALWAYS consider brisk upper GI bleeding. Up to 15% of patients with severe hematochezia have an upper GI source. Place NG tube or do upper endoscopy first if concern for upper source.

🔮 Rockall Score (Post-Endoscopy)

Predicts rebleeding and mortality after endoscopy.

Clinical components: Age, shock, comorbidities

Endoscopic components: Diagnosis, stigmata of recent hemorrhage

High-risk stigmata:

  • Active arterial bleeding
  • Visible vessel (non-bleeding)
  • Adherent clot

Low-risk stigmata: Flat pigmented spot, clean ulcer base

3 Initial Management
🚨 Acute GI Bleeding Protocol
1
Assess hemodynamic stability: HR, BP, orthostatics, mental status
2
IV access: Two large-bore (18G+) IVs, fluid resuscitation
3
Labs: CBC, BMP, coags, type & screen (crossmatch if unstable)
4
Transfuse: Target Hgb >7 (or >8-9 if CAD/active bleeding)
5
Reverse anticoagulation if applicable and bleeding is severe
6
GI consult for endoscopy timing
⚠️ Resuscitation Before Endoscopy

Hemodynamically unstable patients need resuscitation FIRST.

Don't rush to endoscopy in an unstable patient - they're more likely to aspirate or arrest during the procedure.

Target: MAP >65, Hgb stabilizing, mental status improving

4 Upper GI Bleeding Management
💊 Medical Therapy

PPI therapy:

  • IV PPI bolus then infusion (e.g., pantoprazole 80mg bolus, then 8mg/hr) if high suspicion for peptic ulcer
  • Continue high-dose IV PPI for 72 hours post-endoscopy if high-risk stigmata
  • Transition to oral PPI after hemostasis achieved

Prokinetics: Erythromycin 250mg IV 30-120 min before EGD improves visualization

H. pylori: Test and treat all peptic ulcer patients

🔴 Variceal Bleeding - SPECIAL MANAGEMENT

Suspect if: Known cirrhosis, stigmata of liver disease, massive bleeding

Specific therapy:

  • Octreotide: 50mcg bolus, then 50mcg/hr infusion (reduces portal pressure)
  • Antibiotics: Ceftriaxone 1g IV daily (reduces mortality!)
  • Restrictive transfusion: Target Hgb 7-8 (over-transfusion increases portal pressure)
  • Avoid excessive fluids

Endoscopic therapy: Band ligation (preferred) or sclerotherapy

Rescue: TIPS if endoscopy fails, balloon tamponade as bridge

💎 Endoscopy Timing
UGIB: Within 24 hours for most patients. Within 12 hours if high-risk features (hemodynamic instability, bloody NG aspirate).
Variceal bleed: Within 12 hours.
LGIB: Within 24 hours if high-risk; can be elective if low-risk and stable.
5 Lower GI Bleeding Management
📋 LGIB Approach

Key difference from UGIB: Most LGIB stops spontaneously (80-85%)

Colonoscopy:

  • Timing depends on severity and stability
  • Requires bowel prep for adequate visualization
  • Can be therapeutic (clips, cautery, injection)

If colonoscopy non-diagnostic and ongoing bleeding:

  • CTA: Can localize active bleeding (requires 0.5-1 mL/min rate)
  • Tagged RBC scan: More sensitive but less specific for localization
  • Angiography: Diagnostic and therapeutic (embolization)
🏥 Common LGIB Etiologies

Diverticular bleeding:

  • Most common cause of significant LGIB in elderly
  • Typically painless, brisk bleeding
  • Usually stops spontaneously; recurrence rate ~25%

Angiodysplasia:

  • Venous malformations, often in right colon
  • Associated with aortic stenosis, CKD
  • Chronic/occult bleeding more common than acute

Ischemic colitis:

  • Crampy abdominal pain FOLLOWED by bloody diarrhea
  • Watershed areas (splenic flexure, rectosigmoid)
  • Risk factors: hypotension, AFib, vasculitis
