🫁 Tension Pneumothorax

A clinical diagnosis requiring immediate intervention. Understand the pressure dynamics, recognize the signs, and master the life-saving decompression technique.

⏱️ 15 min 📊 Critical 🎯 Emergency/Trauma

📋 Overview

Tension pneumothorax occurs when air enters the pleural space through a one-way valve mechanism, progressively accumulating with each breath. Unlike simple pneumothorax, the trapped air compresses the mediastinum, impairs venous return, and causes cardiovascular collapse.

⚠️ Critical Point: Clinical Diagnosis

Tension pneumothorax is a CLINICAL diagnosis. Do NOT delay treatment for imaging. If suspected in an unstable patient, decompress immediately!

🔬 Pathophysiology

1
One-Way Valve Formation
Air enters pleural space during inspiration but cannot escape during expiration (tissue acts as flap valve)
2
Progressive Air Accumulation
Each breath adds more air → intrapleural pressure becomes positive (normally negative)
3
Mediastinal Shift
Rising pressure pushes mediastinum to contralateral side → kinks great vessels
4
Cardiovascular Collapse
Decreased venous return → decreased preload → decreased cardiac output → obstructive shock

🎯 Why Obstructive Shock?

The heart can only pump what it receives. When positive intrathoracic pressure kinks the vena cava and compresses the heart, venous return drops dramatically. This is why patients have distended neck veins (blood can't get into the thorax) and hypotension (heart has nothing to pump out).

🩺 Clinical Presentation

🔍 Classic Findings
  • Severe respiratory distress
  • Hypotension / tachycardia
  • Tracheal deviation (AWAY from affected side)
  • Distended neck veins (JVD)
  • Absent breath sounds on affected side
  • Hyperresonance to percussion
⚠️ Causes
  • Penetrating chest trauma
  • Blunt chest trauma with rib fractures
  • Barotrauma (mechanical ventilation)
  • Central line placement (subclavian, IJ)
  • Thoracentesis complication
  • Spontaneous (rupture of bleb in tall, thin males)
Finding Simple Pneumothorax Tension Pneumothorax
Hemodynamics Stable Unstable - shock
JVD Absent Present (distended)
Tracheal Deviation Midline Deviated away
Treatment Urgency Can wait for imaging Immediate decompression

💊 Management

🎯 Needle Decompression Technique

Site: 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line (or 4th/5th ICS, anterior axillary line)

Needle: 14-16 gauge, at least 5cm long (longer in obese patients)

Direction: Perpendicular to chest wall, just ABOVE the rib (avoid neurovascular bundle)

Confirmation: Rush of air indicates successful decompression

Next step: ALWAYS follow with chest tube (needle is temporizing only)

🚨 Immediate Actions
  • High-flow oxygen
  • Needle decompression if unstable
  • Chest tube placement (definitive)
  • Volume resuscitation if hypotensive
📋 Chest Tube Placement
  • 4th-5th ICS, anterior axillary line
  • 28-32 Fr for trauma
  • Connect to water seal/suction
  • Confirm placement with CXR

📝 Key Takeaways

🎯 Must Know
  • Clinical diagnosis - don't wait for imaging
  • Obstructive shock mechanism
  • JVD + hypotension + absent breath sounds = tension
  • Tracheal deviation is a LATE sign
💊 Treatment Pearls
  • Needle decompression is temporizing
  • Chest tube is definitive treatment
  • 2nd ICS midclavicular or 4th ICS anterior axillary
  • Use long enough needle (5cm+)
