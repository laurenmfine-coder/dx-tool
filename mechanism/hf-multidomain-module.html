Layer 1: Pathophysiology

Why does the heart fail to meet metabolic demands?

🤔

Predict the Core Problem

Heart failure can be caused by systolic OR diastolic dysfunction. What's the fundamental difference in mechanism?

Two Fundamental Patterns

HFrEF (Systolic Failure)
EF ≤ 40%
  • • ↓ Contractility (inotropy)
  • • ↓ Stroke volume
  • • Dilated LV
  • • "Pump failure"
Think: Post-MI, DCM, myocarditis
HFpEF (Diastolic Failure)
EF ≥ 50%
  • • ↓ Relaxation (lusitropy)
  • • ↓ Filling capacity
  • • Stiff, thick LV
  • • "Filling failure"
Think: HTN, HCM, restrictive

Patient Safety Integration

Understanding compensation explains common medication errors:

Error: Starting ACEi at full dose
Why dangerous: Patient is RAAS-dependent for BP → profound hypotension
Error: Aggressive diuresis without monitoring
Why dangerous: ↓ Preload below Starling curve optimum → ↓↓ CO
Error: Adding NSAID for joint pain
Why dangerous: Blocks renal prostaglandins → Na⁺ retention → decompensation

Layer 3: Clinical Manifestations

Connect mechanism to symptoms

Left Heart Failure

  • • Dyspnea (pulmonary congestion)
  • • Orthopnea (↑ venous return supine)
  • • PND (interstitial edema reabsorbed)
  • • Crackles (alveolar fluid)
  • • S3 gallop (rapid ventricular filling)

Right Heart Failure

  • • JVD (↑ venous pressure)
  • • Peripheral edema (capillary leak)
  • • Hepatomegaly (congestion)
  • • Ascites (portal HTN)
  • • Weight gain (fluid retention)

Layer 4: Treatment Logic

Each drug targets a specific pathophysiologic mechanism

ACEi/ARB/ARNI → Block RAAS
Mechanism: ↓ Angiotensin II → ↓ afterload, ↓ remodeling, ↓ aldosterone
Mortality benefit: Yes! Cornerstone of HFrEF therapy
ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan): Also ↑ natriuretic peptides → even better outcomes
Beta-Blockers → Block SNS
Mechanism: ↓ HR, ↓ myocardial O₂ demand, ↓ arrhythmias, reverse remodeling
Paradox: Negative inotrope helps failing heart (long-term)
Key: Start low, go slow - only in STABLE patients
SGLT2i → Newest Addition
Mechanism: Glucosuria → osmotic diuresis + metabolic effects on myocardium
Benefit: Works in HFrEF AND HFpEF (first drug to help HFpEF!)
Bonus: Renal protection, even without diabetes

Global Health Integration

How does HF treatment differ in resource-limited settings?

Medication access: Generic ACEi/BB available, but ARNI may cost $500+/month
Different etiologies: Rheumatic heart disease, Chagas (endemic areas), peripartum cardiomyopathy
Monitoring: Echo/BNP may be unavailable - clinical assessment skills critical

Environmental Medicine Lens

Environmental and occupational factors in heart failure

Environmental Causes of Cardiomyopathy

🧪 Toxic Exposures
  • • Cobalt: "Beer drinker's CM" (historical)
  • • Arsenic: Chronic exposure → CM
  • • Lead: Contributes to HTN → HF
  • • Carbon monoxide: Myocardial injury
💊 Drug/Medication
  • • Anthracyclines: Dose-dependent CM
  • • Trastuzumab: HER2 blockade in heart
  • • Cocaine: Vasospasm + direct toxicity
  • • Alcohol: >7-8 drinks/day chronic
🌡️ Physical Factors
  • • Radiation: Mediastinal XRT → restrictive
  • • Heat stress: Occupational heat → CV strain
  • • Air pollution: PM2.5 → chronic inflammation
👷 Occupational
  • • Shift work → CVD risk ↑
  • • Job strain → HTN
  • • Sedentary work → metabolic syndrome

Patient Safety Lens

Medical errors and prevention strategies in HF care

Common HF Medication Errors

❌ ACEi + ARB + Spironolactone (Triple RAAS blockade)
Risk: Life-threatening hyperkalemia
Root cause: Multiple specialists prescribing without coordination
Prevention: Medication reconciliation, pharmacist review, K⁺ monitoring
❌ Beta-blocker in acute decompensation
Risk: Cardiogenic shock (↓ inotropy when already failing)
Root cause: "Patient is on it at home" without reassessment
Prevention: Hold/reduce BB until euvolemic and stable
❌ Discharge without med reconciliation
Risk: Taking old meds + new meds = overdose or interactions
Root cause: Poor care transitions, time pressure
Prevention: Pharmacist-led discharge counseling, teach-back

Root Cause Analysis Framework

A patient is readmitted 5 days post-discharge with acute HF exacerbation. Apply the "5 Whys":

Why #1: Why did they decompensate? → Didn't take diuretic
Why #2: Why didn't they take it? → Ran out of medication
Why #3: Why did they run out? → Couldn't afford refill
Why #4: Why couldn't they afford it? → Insurance denied, no patient assistance
Why #5: Why no assistance? → Social work not consulted at discharge
System fix: Automatic social work consult for all HF discharges with medication access screening

Social Determinants of Health in HF

💰 Economic Stability
  • • Can't afford medications ($400/mo for ARNI)
  • • Choose between food and meds
  • • Job loss due to disability
  • • Transportation to appointments
🏠 Housing
  • • Homeless → can't store/refrigerate meds
  • • No elevator → can't leave apartment
  • • Mold exposure → respiratory issues
  • • No reliable address for follow-up
🍎 Food Access
  • • Food desert → high sodium options only
  • • Can't afford fresh produce
  • • No cooking facilities
  • • Relies on high-sodium canned food
📚 Health Literacy
  • • Doesn't understand daily weights
  • • Can't read medication labels
  • • Misunderstands "salt restriction"
  • • Language barriers

Global Health Lens

Worldwide variations in HF etiology, presentation, and management

Geographic Variations in HF Etiology

🇺🇸 High-Income Countries
  • • CAD - #1 cause (~60%)
  • • Hypertension
  • • Diabetes-related
  • • Aging population
🌍 Sub-Saharan Africa
  • • Rheumatic heart disease
  • • Hypertensive heart disease
  • • Peripartum cardiomyopathy
  • • Endomyocardial fibrosis
🇧🇷 Latin America
  • • Chagas disease (T. cruzi)
  • • Rheumatic heart disease
  • • CAD (rising)
🌏 South/Southeast Asia
  • • Rheumatic heart disease
  • • CAD (epidemic rise)
  • • Beriberi (thiamine deficiency)

Resource-Stratified Management

Basic Level (No Echo, Limited Labs)
Clinical diagnosis: JVD + crackles + edema + S3
Treatment: Furosemide, ACEi (enalapril generic), BB if available
Limited Level (Echo, Basic Labs)
Add: EF assessment, renal function, electrolytes
Treatment: Guideline-directed + spironolactone
Enhanced Level (Full Workup)
BNP, cardiac MRI, device therapy evaluation
Treatment: ARNI, SGLT2i, ICD/CRT consideration
