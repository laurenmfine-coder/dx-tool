DECISION 1
Resuscitation
✅
Correct!
Correct! Surviving Sepsis Campaign hour-1 bundle: (1) Measure lactate. (2) Blood cultures before antibiotics. (3) Broad-spectrum antibiotics (pip-tazo or meropenem + vancomycin given the rigid abdomen). (4) 30 mL/kg crystalloid for hypotension or lactate ≥4. With rigid abdomen + fever, suspect intra-abdominal catastrophe — this patient likely needs surgery.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Volume resuscitation comes before vasopressors in most shock presentations. Norepinephrine is indicated if MAP remains <65 AFTER adequate fluid resuscitation (30 mL/kg) or if the patient is severely hypotensive. Starting pressors on an empty tank is less effective. However, in extreme cases (MAP <50), early vasopressors alongside fluids is reasonable.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
While this patient may eventually need intubation, premature RSI in septic shock is dangerous — induction agents cause vasodilation and can worsen hypotension catastrophically. Bolus fluids and start vasopressors to stabilize hemodynamics first. If intubation is needed, pre-load with fluids, have push-dose vasopressors ready, and use hemodynamically stable induction agents (ketamine, etomidate).

Correct!
Correct! In septic shock, source control is critical and should happen within 6-12 hours when feasible. A perforated viscus with free air and peritonitis requires emergent surgery — no amount of antibiotics alone will resolve a surgical source. Continue aggressive resuscitation while prepping for OR. Notify anesthesia about the hemodynamic instability.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Antibiotics alone cannot cure a perforated viscus. Without source control (surgical repair), mortality approaches 100%. Yes, she is hemodynamically unstable, but the instability is CAUSED by the ongoing perforation and peritonitis. Stabilize with fluids/pressors AND proceed to OR. Delaying surgery to 'stabilize first' paradoxically worsens outcomes because the source continues to contaminate.
❌
Suboptimal Choice
Percutaneous drainage is appropriate for localized abscesses, not free perforation with generalized peritonitis. Free air on CT with diffuse peritoneal contamination requires open or laparoscopic exploration to identify and repair the perforation, wash out the peritoneal cavity, and potentially create a diversion.
RESOLUTION
Post-Operative
Emergent laparotomy reveals perforated sigmoid diverticulum with fecal peritonitis. Hartmann procedure performed (sigmoid resection + end colostomy). Post-op in ICU on vasopressors, which are weaning. Lactate clearing: 7.2 → 4.1 → 2.0.
OUTCOME
🏆 Excellent Clinical Reasoning
Excellent management of septic shock with surgical source! Post-op priorities: (1) Wean vasopressors as tolerated (target MAP ≥65). (2) De-escalate antibiotics based on culture results (typically 4-7 day course post-source control). (3) Monitor for complications: wound infection, anastomotic issues, ARDS, DIC. (4) Nutrition within 24-48 hours. (5) After recovery: address alcohol use disorder (likely contributed to malnutrition and delayed presentation), ostomy education, and reversal planning.
