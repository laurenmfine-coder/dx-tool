MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

DIC is a consumptive coagulopathy characterized by widespread activation of the coagulation cascade → diffuse microvascular thrombosis → consumption of clotting factors and platelets → paradoxical bleeding. It is always secondary to an underlying condition.

🔑 Key Concept: DIC causes BOTH thrombosis AND bleeding simultaneously. Microvascular thrombi occlude small vessels (organ damage) while consumption of factors/platelets causes hemorrhage. Think of it as "using up all the clotting material on inappropriate clots."

Common Triggers

CategoryCausesMechanism
SepsisGram-negative (most common), meningococcemiaEndotoxin/cytokine-mediated tissue factor release
ObstetricPlacental abruption, amniotic fluid embolism, HELLP, retained dead fetusThromboplastin release from placental/fetal tissue
MalignancyAcute promyelocytic leukemia (APL), mucin-secreting adenocarcinomasTumor procoagulant release; APL granules contain tissue factor
TraumaMassive tissue injury, burns, crush injuryTissue factor release from damaged tissues
OtherTransfusion reaction, snake envenomation, pancreatitisVarious procoagulant triggers
⚠️ APL + DIC: Acute promyelocytic leukemia (t(15;17), PML-RARA) almost always presents with DIC. Start all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA) immediately upon suspicion — don't wait for confirmatory testing. ATRA differentiates the promyelocytes and resolves DIC.

🔑 DIC vs TTP: Both show schistocytes and thrombocytopenia. Key differences: DIC has prolonged PT/PTT and low fibrinogen; TTP has normal PT/PTT and normal fibrinogen. DIC = coagulation problem; TTP = platelet problem.

Clinical Manifestations

Acute DIC: Bleeding predominates — oozing from IV sites, mucosal bleeding, purpura, petechiae. Chronic DIC: Thrombosis predominates — seen with malignancy (Trousseau syndrome — migratory thrombophlebitis).

TREATMENT
Management

Treatment Principles

PriorityAction
#1 Treat underlying causeThis is THE most important step. Antibiotics for sepsis, delivery for obstetric causes, ATRA for APL
Supportive — if bleedingPlatelets (if <10K or active bleeding), FFP (replaces factors), cryoprecipitate (if fibrinogen <100)
AnticoagulationConsider heparin ONLY in chronic/thrombotic DIC (Trousseau). Contraindicated in acute bleeding DIC
⚠️ Do NOT give heparin in acute bleeding DIC. Heparin is only appropriate for chronic, thrombosis-predominant DIC (e.g., cancer-associated). In acute DIC with hemorrhage, heparin worsens bleeding.
