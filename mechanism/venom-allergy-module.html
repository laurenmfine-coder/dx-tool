Venom Allergy Mechanisms

Hymenoptera Venom Immunology & VIT

Venom Components

Major Allergens:

  • Honeybee: Api m 1 (phospholipase A2), Api m 2 (hyaluronidase)
  • Vespids (wasp/hornet): Ves v 1 (phospholipase A1), Ves v 5 (antigen 5)
  • Fire ant: Sol i 1-4 alkaloid venoms

💡 Cross-reactivity: Vespids share ~50% allergens; bee-vespid cross-reactivity is lower (~5%)

Sensitization Cascade

First sting → Sensitization:

  1. Dendritic cells present venom proteins to T-helper cells
  2. Th2 response → IL-4, IL-13 production
  3. B cell class switching to IgE production
  4. IgE binds FcεRI receptors on mast cells/basophils

Subsequent sting → Allergic reaction:

  1. Multivalent allergen cross-links surface IgE
  2. Mast cell degranulation → histamine, tryptase, prostaglandins, leukotrienes
  3. Systemic reaction if sensitized

Venom Immunotherapy (VIT)

Mechanism of tolerance:

  • Shift from Th2 → Th1/Treg response
  • ↑ Blocking IgG4 antibodies
  • ↓ Venom-specific IgE (over years)
  • ↓ Mast cell/basophil reactivity

Efficacy: 95-98% protection after VIT completion

💡 VIT is one of the most effective immunotherapies - can achieve "cure" state

Risk Stratification

Mueller Grading:

  • Grade I: Generalized urticaria, pruritus, malaise
  • Grade II: + Angioedema, GI symptoms
  • Grade III: + Dyspnea, wheeze, stridor
  • Grade IV: + Hypotension, LOC, cardiovascular collapse

Risk factors for severe reactions:

  • Previous systemic reaction (not large local)
  • Mastocytosis/elevated baseline tryptase
  • Beta-blocker/ACE inhibitor use
  • Age (cardiovascular comorbidity)

🎯 Key Points

  • Venom allergens include phospholipases, hyaluronidases, antigen 5
  • IgE sensitization occurs on first sting; reaction on subsequent
  • VIT is 95-98% effective via Th2→Treg shift and blocking IgG4
  • Large local reactions rarely progress to systemic
  • Mastocytosis dramatically increases anaphylaxis risk
