Stage 1 of 8
The Barking Cough
It's 2 AM in the pediatric ED. A 2-year-old boy, Marcus, is brought in by his frightened parents. They report he developed a runny nose and low-grade fever yesterday, but tonight he woke up with a "barking" cough and noisy breathing. Mom demonstrates the sound - it's unmistakably seal-like.

On exam: T 38.2°C, HR 130, RR 32, SpO2 96% on room air. You note inspiratory stridor at rest, mild subcostal retractions, and he's alert but anxious. His voice sounds slightly hoarse.
🔬 Pathophysiology: Why the "Barking" Cough?
Parainfluenza virus (75% of cases) → Subglottic inflammation ↓ Narrowest part of pediatric airway (cricoid cartilage) ↓ Subglottic edema → Turbulent airflow → STRIDOR ↓ Inflamed vocal cords → Characteristic "barky" cough ↓ Peak age: 6 months - 3 years (small airway diameter)
Based on the clinical presentation, what is your initial severity assessment using the Westley Croup Score?
A Mild croup (score 0-2) - can be discharged with supportive care
B Moderate croup (score 3-5) - stridor at rest indicates need for treatment
C Severe croup (score 6-11) - needs immediate airway intervention
D Impending respiratory failure (score >11) - prepare for intubation
📋 Westley Croup Score Components:
• Stridor: 0 (none), 1 (with agitation), 2 (at rest)
• Retractions: 0 (none), 1 (mild), 2 (moderate), 3 (severe)
• Air entry: 0 (normal), 1 (decreased), 2 (markedly decreased)
• Cyanosis: 0 (none), 4 (with agitation), 5 (at rest)
• Level of consciousness: 0 (normal), 5 (altered)

This patient: Stridor at rest (2) + Mild retractions (1) + Normal air entry (0) + No cyanosis (0) + Alert (0) = Score 3 = Moderate
Stage 2 of 8
First-Line Treatment
Marcus continues to have stridor at rest. His parents are worried - "Is he going to be okay? His breathing sounds terrible!" The nurse asks what treatment you'd like to start.
🎯 High-Yield: Croup Treatment Ladder
Mild (score 0-2): Single dose dexamethasone, home with precautions
Moderate (score 3-5): Dexamethasone + nebulized epinephrine
Severe (score 6-11): Dex + epi + close monitoring, repeat epi PRN
Impending failure: Heliox, prepare airway equipment
What is the most appropriate initial treatment for Marcus?
A Nebulized albuterol 2.5 mg
B Oral prednisolone 2 mg/kg
C Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg PO/IM + nebulized racemic epinephrine
D IV methylprednisolone 2 mg/kg + antibiotics
🔑 Key Treatment Principles:

Dexamethasone:
• Dose: 0.6 mg/kg (max 10 mg) - SINGLE DOSE
• Route: PO = IM = IV in efficacy
• Onset: 2-4 hours for full effect
• Duration: 36-72 hours (long half-life = no need to repeat)

Nebulized Epinephrine:
• Racemic: 0.5 mL of 2.25% solution
• L-epinephrine: 0.5 mL/kg of 1:1000 (max 5 mL)
• Onset: 10-30 minutes
• Duration: ~2 hours (observe for rebound!)

⚠️ Albuterol does NOT help croup - wrong receptor!
Stage 3 of 8
Epinephrine Response & Monitoring
You give dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg PO (Marcus weighs 12 kg, so 7.2 mg) and start nebulized racemic epinephrine. Within 15 minutes, his stridor has significantly improved - now only audible when he's upset. The retractions have resolved.

The nurse asks: "He looks so much better! Can he go home now?"
🔬 Why Epinephrine Works in Croup
Epinephrine → α-adrenergic receptor activation ↓ Arteriolar vasoconstriction in subglottic mucosa ↓ ↓ Mucosal edema → ↑ Airway diameter ↓ But... effect wears off in 1-2 hours! ↓ Risk of "REBOUND" stridor when epi wears off (especially if steroids haven't kicked in yet)
How long should Marcus be observed after receiving nebulized epinephrine?
A 30 minutes - if improved, safe to discharge
B 1 hour - standard observation period
C 2-4 hours - watch for rebound after epinephrine wears off
D Admit overnight - all patients receiving epinephrine require admission
📋 Post-Epinephrine Observation:

• Minimum 2 hours (some centers use 3-4 hours)
• Watch for return of stridor as epinephrine wears off
• By 2-4 hours, dexamethasone is starting to work

Safe to discharge if:
• No stridor at rest after observation
• Normal air entry
• SpO2 ≥92% on room air
• Able to take fluids
• Reliable caregivers with return precautions

Note: Need for >1 dose of epinephrine often warrants admission
Stage 4 of 8
The Critical Differential
While Marcus is being observed, another child arrives - 4-year-old Emma. She has stridor too, but something seems different. Her parents report sudden onset while eating dinner, no preceding URI symptoms, and she's sitting forward, drooling, and looks toxic. Temperature is 40.1°C.
⚠️ Red Flags: NOT Typical Croup
• Age >6 years or <6 months
• High fever (>40°C)
• Toxic appearance
• Drooling or difficulty swallowing
• Sudden onset without prodrome
• No response to standard treatment
• Tripod positioning
What is your primary concern for Emma?
A Severe viral croup - give higher dose steroids
B Bacterial tracheitis or epiglottitis - prepare for airway emergency
C Foreign body aspiration - needs bronchoscopy
D Peritonsillar abscess - examine oropharynx

Stage 5 of 8
Imaging in Croup
Back to Marcus - he's doing well at the 2-hour mark. A medical student asks if you should get imaging to confirm the diagnosis.
🎯 Boards Pearl: Imaging in Croup
Croup is a CLINICAL DIAGNOSIS. Imaging is NOT routinely needed!

However, if obtained:
• AP neck X-ray: "Steeple sign" (subglottic narrowing)
• Lateral neck: Rules out epiglottitis ("thumbprint sign")
When IS imaging indicated in a child with stridor?
A All children with stridor should have a neck X-ray
B Atypical presentation, suspected foreign body, or no response to treatment
C All children requiring epinephrine need imaging before discharge
D Only if intubation is anticipated
📋 Indications for Imaging:

Consider imaging if:
• Atypical age (<6 months or >6 years)
• No response to standard treatment
• Suspected foreign body (sudden onset, no URI)
• Concern for retropharyngeal abscess (neck stiffness, torticollis)
• Recurrent croup (consider subglottic stenosis, hemangioma)

Classic Findings:
• Steeple sign = subglottic narrowing (croup)
• Thumbprint sign = swollen epiglottis (epiglottitis)

Remember: Never delay treatment for imaging in respiratory distress!
Stage 6 of 8
Ready for Discharge?
It's now 3 hours since Marcus received epinephrine. He has no stridor at rest, is playful, drank some juice, and SpO2 is 98%. His parents are anxious about taking him home - "What if it comes back?"
What are the KEY discharge instructions for Marcus's parents?
A Continue oral steroids for 3-5 days, use humidifier, return if worse
B Cool mist for comfort, expect symptoms 3-5 days, return for stridor at rest or respiratory distress
C Prescribe albuterol inhaler for home use if stridor recurs
D Antibiotics for 7 days to prevent bacterial superinfection
📋 Discharge Counseling for Croup:

Expected course:
• Symptoms typically last 3-5 days
• Usually worst on nights 2-3
• Single dose dexamethasone provides 36-72 hours coverage

Home management:
• Cool mist (humidifier or outside cool air) - may help comfort
• Hydration, fever control
• Keep child calm (crying worsens stridor)

Return precautions:
• Stridor at rest
• Increased work of breathing
• Difficulty swallowing or drooling
• Color change (blue/pale)
• Not responding to soothing

⚠️ NO additional steroids needed! NO antibiotics! NO albuterol!
Stage 7 of 8
The Return Visit
Two weeks later, Marcus is back. This is his third episode of croup in 6 months. Mom says, "This keeps happening! Is there something wrong with him?"
🔍 Recurrent Croup: Think Beyond Viral!
Consider anatomic or other causes:
• Subglottic stenosis (prior intubation?)
• Subglottic hemangioma (50% have cutaneous hemangiomas)
• Laryngomalacia
• Tracheomalacia
• GERD-related inflammation
• Allergic/spasmodic croup (often no URI symptoms)
What is the appropriate next step for a child with recurrent croup (≥3 episodes)?
A Continue treating episodes as they occur - most children outgrow it
B Start daily inhaled corticosteroids for prevention
C Refer to ENT for direct laryngoscopy/bronchoscopy
D Order neck CT to evaluate for masses
📋 Recurrent Croup Workup:

Definition: ≥2-3 episodes requiring medical attention

Evaluation:
• ENT referral for direct laryngoscopy/bronchoscopy (gold standard)
• Look for: subglottic stenosis, hemangioma, laryngomalacia
• Consider GERD evaluation if suspected

Spasmodic Croup:
• Sudden onset at night, no fever/URI prodrome
• Family history of atopy often present
• May respond to cool air exposure
• Still treat with steroids acutely

Key: Direct visualization is the diagnostic gold standard for recurrent croup!
Stage 8 of 8
Case Synthesis & Pearls
Marcus's parents are reassured and he's referred to ENT for evaluation. Before they leave, the attending asks you to summarize the key management principles of croup.
📝 Croup Management Summary
RECOGNITION: • Age 6mo-3yr + URI prodrome + barky cough + stridor • Worse at night, improves with cool air SEVERITY (Westley Score): • Mild (0-2): No stridor at rest → Dex alone, home • Moderate (3-5): Stridor at rest → Dex + Epi • Severe (6-11): Significant distress → Dex + Epi + Monitor • Impending failure: Heliox, prepare airway TREATMENT: • Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg x1 (PO/IM/IV equivalent) • Nebulized epinephrine for moderate-severe • Observe 2-4 hours post-epinephrine RED FLAGS (Not Croup): • Toxic + drooling + high fever = Epiglottitis • Croup → worsening = Bacterial tracheitis • Sudden onset while eating = Foreign body • Recurrent episodes = Anatomic abnormality
Final question: Which statement about croup is INCORRECT?
A Dexamethasone is effective even in mild croup (reduces return visits)
B Oral prednisolone is as effective as dexamethasone for croup
C L-epinephrine and racemic epinephrine are equally effective
D Humidified air has not been proven beneficial in clinical trials
📋 Final Pearls:

Why dexamethasone over prednisolone?
• Dexamethasone: Longer half-life (36-72 hrs) = single dose
• Prednisolone: Shorter half-life = multiple doses needed
• Studies show dex superior in reducing ED returns

Evidence notes:
• Steroids reduce severity, duration, and need for hospitalization
• Even mild croup benefits from steroids (NNT ~5 to prevent return)
• Humidity/steam: No proven benefit (but low harm, may comfort)
• L-epi and racemic epi are equivalent in efficacy

Take-home: Dexamethasone 0.6 mg/kg x1 is the treatment of choice for ALL severity levels!
