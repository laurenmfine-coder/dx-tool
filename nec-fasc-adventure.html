🧬 Necrotizing Fasciitis Clinical AdventureEMERGENCY MEDICINE

🎮 Case Presentation

A 55-year-old diabetic presents with rapidly worsening left leg pain, swelling, and erythema after a minor skin abrasion 2 days ago. The pain seems disproportionate to examination findings. Vitals: T 39.8°C, HR 125, BP 85/55.

Key Decision: Recognize the Emergency

⚠️ "Pain out of proportion to exam" + systemic toxicity = necrotizing fasciitis until proven otherwise. This is a SURGICAL emergency — survival depends on time to OR, not antibiotics alone.

Red Flags for Necrotizing Fasciitis

  • Pain out of proportion to visible findings
  • Rapid progression despite antibiotics
  • Crepitus (gas in tissues — late finding)
  • Bullae, skin necrosis, ecchymosis (late findings)
  • Systemic toxicity (sepsis, shock)
  • Wooden-hard induration of subcutaneous tissue

Management

PriorityAction
1. ResuscitateIV fluids, vasopressors if needed, ICU admission
2. URGENT surgeryAggressive debridement — "finger test" (blunt dissection confirms fascial necrosis). Often requires multiple returns to OR
3. AntibioticsBroad-spectrum: vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam + clindamycin (clindamycin suppresses toxin production)
🔑 Type I (polymicrobial — diabetics, post-surgical) vs Type II (monomicrobial — Group A Strep in healthy patients). Type II = streptococcal toxic shock. Mortality 20-40% even with optimal treatment.
