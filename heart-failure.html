Heart Failure

From normal cardiac physiology through neurohormonal activation to evidence-based therapy — understand why heart failure develops and how our treatments target each mechanism.

Anatomy & Physiology Pathophysiology Diagnostics Treatment

Anatomy & Physiology

The foundation: How the normal heart works

The Heart as a Pump

The heart's fundamental job is deceptively simple: move blood forward. But achieving this requires exquisite coordination of electrical signals, muscle contraction, valve function, and pressure gradients.

Think of cardiac output as the product of two factors: how often the heart beats (heart rate) and how much it ejects per beat (stroke volume).

Key Equation

Cardiac Output = Heart Rate × Stroke Volume

A normal CO is ~5 L/min at rest. This must increase 3-4x during exercise. When the heart can't meet demand, we call it heart failure.

Determinants of Stroke Volume

Stroke volume depends on three interconnected factors. Understanding these is essential because every heart failure treatment targets one or more of them.

Factor Definition Clinical Correlate
Preload Ventricular wall tension at end-diastole; determined by venous return ↑ with fluid overload; ↓ with diuretics
Afterload Resistance the ventricle must overcome to eject blood ↑ with hypertension, aortic stenosis; ↓ with vasodilators
Contractility Intrinsic ability of myocytes to generate force (independent of load) ↓ in systolic HF; can augment with inotropes

The Frank-Starling Mechanism

This is perhaps the most elegant compensation the heart possesses: stretch the muscle fiber, and it contracts more forcefully. Within physiologic limits, increased preload → increased stroke volume.

Why does this matter? Because in early heart failure, the Frank-Starling mechanism is what keeps cardiac output normal. The ventricle dilates, increasing preload, which augments contractile force. But this compensation has limits — and when exceeded, congestion results.

Clinical Pearl

The "failing" heart isn't always failing to pump. In HFpEF, the heart pumps fine — it just can't fill properly. Always ask: is this a problem of systole or diastole?

Connecting to Pathophysiology

When the heart can no longer maintain adequate output despite Frank-Starling compensation, the body activates powerful neurohormonal systems. These help short-term but cause long-term damage. Let's explore what goes wrong...

Pathophysiology

What goes wrong: The neurohormonal cascade

The Vicious Cycle

Heart failure isn't just a weak pump — it's a systemic syndrome driven by maladaptive neurohormonal activation. The body's "emergency response" becomes the disease itself.

1

Initial Insult

Myocardial injury (MI, HTN, valve disease, toxins) reduces cardiac output

2

Baroreceptor Sensing

Carotid/aortic baroreceptors detect ↓ BP → signal to brainstem

3

SNS Activation

↑ Sympathetic tone → ↑ HR, ↑ contractility, vasoconstriction. Short-term benefit, long-term harm (↑ oxygen demand, arrhythmias, remodeling)

4

RAAS Activation

↓ Renal perfusion → renin release → angiotensin II → aldosterone. Causes vasoconstriction, Na/H₂O retention, fibrosis

5

Ventricular Remodeling

Chronic stretch + neurohormones → myocyte hypertrophy, apoptosis, fibrosis. The ventricle dilates and becomes spherical. Efficiency ↓↓

6

Further ↓ CO

Remodeling worsens function → more neurohormonal activation → the cycle perpetuates

Forward vs. Backward Failure

Symptoms arise from two distinct mechanisms, often occurring together:

Type Mechanism Symptoms
Backward (Congestion) Blood backs up behind the failing ventricle Left: Dyspnea, orthopnea, PND, pulmonary edema
Right: JVD, hepatomegaly, peripheral edema, ascites
Forward (Low Output) Inadequate perfusion to tissues Fatigue, weakness, poor exercise tolerance, prerenal azotemia, cool extremities
Think Mechanistically

When you see a patient with heart failure, ask: "What's backing up? What's not getting enough?" This guides both your physical exam and your treatment approach.

HFrEF vs. HFpEF

Not all heart failure is the same. The ejection fraction tells you about systolic function, but diastolic dysfunction can cause identical symptoms with a normal EF.

Feature HFrEF (EF ≤40%) HFpEF (EF ≥50%)
Primary Problem Impaired contraction Impaired relaxation/filling
Common Causes Ischemia, dilated CMP, valvular HTN, LVH, age, infiltrative
Ventricle Appearance Dilated, thin walls Normal size, thick/stiff walls
Evidence-Based Rx Extensive (GDMT) Limited (SGLT2i, diuretics)

Connecting to Diagnostics

Now that we understand what's happening in heart failure, we can understand why our tests work. BNP rises because of wall stretch. Echo shows us remodeling in real time. Let's see how each test reflects the underlying pathophysiology...

Diagnostics

How we detect it: Tests that reveal the mechanism

BNP / NT-proBNP

Why it works: B-type natriuretic peptide is released by ventricular myocytes in response to wall stretch. More stretch = more BNP. It's a direct biochemical readout of the cardiac stress we discussed in pathophysiology.

Test HF Unlikely HF Likely Notes
BNP <100 pg/mL >400 pg/mL Shorter half-life; affected by nesiritide
NT-proBNP <300 pg/mL >900 pg/mL (age-adjusted) Renally cleared; higher in CKD
Clinical Pearl

BNP can be falsely low in obesity (adipose tissue clears it) and flash pulmonary edema (not enough time to rise). Don't let a "normal" BNP override clear clinical evidence of HF.

Echocardiography

Why it works: Echo lets us see the structural and functional changes we discussed. Every pathophysiologic concept has an echo correlate.

Echo Finding What It Reflects Mechanism Connection
↓ EF Systolic dysfunction Impaired contractility
↑ LV dimensions Ventricular remodeling Chronic volume/pressure overload
↑ LA size Chronic elevated filling pressures Backward failure from LV
Diastolic dysfunction (E/e') Impaired relaxation Stiff ventricle, ↑ filling pressures
↑ RVSP Pulmonary hypertension Transmitted back from LA/LV
MR, TR Annular dilation Secondary to ventricular remodeling

Chest X-Ray Findings

Why it works: Each CXR finding reflects a specific physiologic derangement.

CXR Finding Mechanism
Cardiomegaly Ventricular dilation from remodeling
Cephalization Upper lobe vessels recruit as lower lobe vessels compress from elevated pressures
Kerley B lines Interstitial edema; fluid in interlobular septa
Pleural effusions Elevated venous pressures → transudative fluid
Bat wing/butterfly pattern Alveolar edema in perihilar distribution
Pressure → Findings Timeline

As LA pressure rises, CXR findings progress: cephalization (15-20 mmHg) → interstitial edema/Kerley B (20-25) → alveolar edema (>25). The CXR is a visual representation of hemodynamics.

Connecting to Treatment

Now comes the payoff: every guideline-directed therapy for HFrEF targets a specific mechanism we've discussed. RAAS blockers interrupt neurohormonal activation. Beta-blockers quiet the SNS. Understanding the pathophysiology makes the treatment algorithm intuitive rather than memorized...

Treatment

How we fix it: Targeting the mechanisms

GDMT: The Four Pillars of HFrEF

Guideline-directed medical therapy for HFrEF isn't arbitrary — each drug class interrupts a specific pathophysiologic pathway we discussed.

Drug Class Mechanism Target Why It Works Key Benefit
ACEi/ARB/ARNI RAAS system Blocks Ang II → ↓ vasoconstriction, ↓ aldosterone, ↓ remodeling ↓ Mortality, ↓ hospitalizations, reverse remodeling
Beta-blockers Sympathetic nervous system ↓ HR, ↓ O₂ demand, ↓ arrhythmias, ↓ remodeling signals ↓ Mortality, ↓ sudden death, improve EF
MRAs Aldosterone Blocks aldosterone → ↓ Na retention, ↓ fibrosis, ↓ K wasting ↓ Mortality, ↓ hospitalizations
SGLT2 inhibitors Multiple (Na/glucose, hemodynamics) ↓ Preload (natriuresis), ↓ afterload, metabolic benefits, ↓ fibrosis ↓ HF hospitalizations, ↓ CV death (works in HFpEF too!)
The Mortality Benefit Pattern

Notice something? Three of the four pillars work by blocking neurohormonal activation. The short-term compensations (SNS, RAAS) that "help" acutely are what kill patients long-term. We succeed by turning off the body's own stress response.

Diuretics: Symptom Relief

Mechanism target: Directly addresses backward failure (congestion) by promoting sodium and water excretion.

Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide) block Na-K-2Cl cotransporter in the thick ascending limb. Result: massive natriuresis → ↓ preload → ↓ congestion → symptom relief.

Clinical Pearl

Diuretics improve symptoms but have no mortality benefit. They're essential for quality of life but aren't disease-modifying. The four pillars are what change the disease trajectory.

Advanced Therapies

When GDMT isn't enough, we have options that target the mechanisms at a deeper level:

Therapy Mechanism Indication
CRT (BiV pacing) Resynchronizes LV contraction in LBBB → improves mechanical efficiency EF ≤35%, LBBB, QRS ≥150ms
ICD Prevents sudden cardiac death from VT/VF (common in dilated hearts) EF ≤35% despite GDMT
LVAD Mechanical pump assists/replaces LV function Bridge to transplant or destination therapy
Transplant Replace the failing organ End-stage HF, otherwise good candidate

Putting It All Together

When you approach a heart failure patient, think mechanistically:

  1. What's the EF? → HFrEF vs HFpEF guides your therapy options
  2. What caused it? → Treat reversible causes (ischemia, valve disease, thyroid)
  3. Are they congested? → Diuretics for symptom relief
  4. Are they on all four pillars? → These save lives; optimize doses
  5. Do they need advanced therapy? → CRT, ICD, LVAD, transplant eval
The Big Picture

We've come full circle: from normal cardiac physiology → to what goes wrong → to how we detect it → to how we fix it. Every step connects. The tests diagnose the mechanisms. The treatments target them. Understanding why makes medicine logical rather than memorized.

Test Your Understanding

Why does BNP rise in heart failure? What specific stimulus triggers its release?

BNP is released by ventricular myocytes in response to wall stretch. As the failing ventricle dilates and filling pressures rise, the mechanical stretch signal triggers BNP synthesis and release. It's a direct biochemical marker of the hemodynamic stress.

A patient on maximum GDMT still has symptoms. Their EF is 30% and ECG shows LBBB with QRS 160ms. What therapy might help, and why does it work mechanistically?

CRT (cardiac resynchronization therapy). In LBBB, the LV contracts dyssynchronously — the septum and lateral wall don't contract together, wasting mechanical energy. CRT paces both ventricles to restore coordinated contraction, improving efficiency. This patient meets criteria: EF ≤35%, LBBB, QRS ≥150ms.

Why do we say neurohormonal activation is "maladaptive" in heart failure when it initially helps maintain cardiac output?

Short-term, SNS and RAAS activation maintain CO by increasing HR, contractility, and fluid retention. But chronically, these same mechanisms cause harm: increased myocardial oxygen demand, arrhythmias, vasoconstriction (↑ afterload), fluid overload, and direct promotion of ventricular remodeling and fibrosis. The "rescue" becomes the disease. That's why blocking these systems (beta-blockers, ACEi/ARB, MRAs) improves survival.

A patient has dyspnea, elevated BNP, but a normal EF of 60%. What type of heart failure do they have, and why is treatment more limited compared to HFrEF?

This is HFpEF (heart failure with preserved ejection fraction). The problem isn't impaired contraction but impaired relaxation/filling — a stiff ventricle that can't accept blood properly. Treatment is more limited because the landmark trials (proving mortality benefit for ACEi, beta-blockers, MRAs) were done in HFrEF patients. Only SGLT2 inhibitors have shown clear benefit in HFpEF. Treatment focuses on diuretics for symptoms, managing comorbidities (HTN, obesity, AF), and now SGLT2i.
