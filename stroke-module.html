🧠 Stroke

⏱️ TIME IS BRAIN
1.9 million neurons die per minute in untreated stroke
tPA window: 4.5 hours | Thrombectomy window: up to 24 hours (selected patients)

Ischemic vs Hemorrhagic

Ischemic (87%)

Mechanism
Blocked artery → No blood flow → Infarction
Types:
  • • Large vessel: Carotid stenosis, MCA occlusion
  • • Cardioembolic: AFib, valve disease, PFO
  • • Small vessel (lacunar): HTN, DM → lipohyalinosis
  • • Other: Dissection, hypercoagulable, vasculitis
Treatment: tPA, Thrombectomy, Aspirin

Hemorrhagic (13%)

Mechanism
Vessel rupture → Blood in brain → Mass effect + toxicity
Types:
  • • Intracerebral (ICH): HTN (#1), amyloid angiopathy, AVM, tumor
  • • Subarachnoid (SAH): Aneurysm rupture, trauma
NO tPA! Reverse anticoagulation, BP control, ? Surgery
Cannot distinguish clinically! Must get CT head BEFORE any treatment

Vascular Territories: Localize the Lesion

Anterior Circulation (Carotid System)

MCA (Most Common)
• Contralateral hemiparesis (face + arm > leg)
• Contralateral hemisensory loss
• Homonymous hemianopia
• Dominant (L): Aphasia (Broca/Wernicke)
• Non-dominant (R): Neglect, anosognosia
Gaze deviation TOWARD lesion
ACA
• Contralateral leg weakness > arm/face
• Leg sensory loss
• Urinary incontinence
• Abulia (lack of will/motivation)
• Alien hand syndrome (if corpus callosum)
Lacunar (Small Vessel)
Pure motor: Face/arm/leg (internal capsule)
Pure sensory: Thalamus
Ataxic hemiparesis: Pons/internal capsule
Dysarthria-clumsy hand: Pons
No cortical signs (no aphasia, neglect, visual field cuts)

Posterior Circulation (Vertebrobasilar)

PCA
• Contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing
• Visual agnosia
• Memory impairment (if bilateral)
• Alexia without agraphia (dominant)
Basilar Artery
• "Locked-in syndrome" (aware but can only move eyes vertically)
• Coma
• Quadriplegia
• Cranial nerve palsies
⚠️ Life-threatening emergency
Brainstem/Cerebellum
• Crossed findings (ipsilateral face, contralateral body)
• Vertigo, nystagmus
• Diplopia
• Ataxia
• Dysarthria, dysphagia
The "Dizziness that's actually a stroke"

Classic Brainstem Syndromes

Wallenberg (Lateral Medullary)
PICA or vertebral artery
• Ipsilateral: Horner's, facial sensory loss, ataxia, dysphagia/dysarthria
• Contralateral: Body pain/temp loss
• Vertigo, nystagmus
Medial Medullary
Vertebral or ASA
• Contralateral hemiparesis (arm/leg)
• Contralateral proprioception/vibration loss
• Ipsilateral tongue weakness (XII)
Weber Syndrome (Midbrain)
• Ipsilateral CN III palsy
• Contralateral hemiparesis
Millard-Gubler (Pons)
• Ipsilateral CN VI and VII palsy
• Contralateral hemiparesis

💉 tPA (Alteplase): Know the Criteria

Inclusion

  • • Clinical diagnosis of ischemic stroke
  • • Measurable neurological deficit
  • • Symptom onset <4.5 hours (or last known well)
  • • Age ≥18
  • • CT: No hemorrhage

Major Exclusions

  • • ICH on CT
  • • Recent major surgery/trauma (14 days)
  • • Recent GI/GU bleed (21 days)
  • • Recent stroke/head trauma (3 months)
  • • Platelets <100K, INR >1.7
  • • BP >185/110 despite treatment
  • • Glucose <50 or >400
3-4.5 hour window additional exclusions: Age >80, severe stroke (NIHSS >25), oral anticoagulant use, history of DM + prior stroke

🔧 Mechanical Thrombectomy

Indication: Large vessel occlusion (ICA, M1, sometimes M2, basilar) with salvageable tissue

Standard Window (0-6 hours)

  • • NIHSS ≥6
  • • Pre-stroke mRS 0-1
  • • ASPECTS ≥6 (CT perfusion)
  • • Age ≥18

Extended Window (6-24 hours)

  • • Must have favorable perfusion imaging
  • • Small core, large penumbra
  • • DAWN or DEFUSE 3 criteria
Can be done WITH tPA (if eligible) - don't delay tPA for thrombectomy decision

Secondary Prevention: Prevent the Next One

Antithrombotics

Non-cardioembolic: Aspirin (± clopidogrel for 21 days if minor stroke/TIA)
Cardioembolic (AFib): Anticoagulation (DOACs preferred > warfarin)
Start anticoag 4-14 days post-stroke depending on size

Risk Factor Management

BP: Target <130/80 (after acute phase)
Statin: High-intensity (LDL goal <70)
Diabetes: A1c control
Lifestyle: Smoking cessation, exercise, diet

Carotid Stenosis

Symptomatic 70-99%: CEA or stenting within 2 weeks
Symptomatic 50-69%: Consider CEA (benefit depends on risk factors)
Asymptomatic ≥70%: Medical therapy vs CEA (shared decision)

🩸 Hemorrhagic Stroke Specifics

ICH (Intracerebral Hemorrhage)

Locations by cause:
• Basal ganglia, thalamus, pons, cerebellum → HTN
• Lobar → Amyloid angiopathy (elderly), AVM (young)
Management:
• Reverse anticoagulation (PCC, vitamin K, idarucizumab)
• BP goal: SBP <140 (if presenting 150-220)
• Neurosurgery consult for large/expanding
• ICP management

SAH (Subarachnoid Hemorrhage)

"Thunderclap headache" - worst headache of life, sudden onset
• 85% = ruptured aneurysm
• May have meningismus, photophobia
• CT sensitivity decreases over time (LP if negative)
Complications:
• Rebleeding (highest first 24h)
• Vasospasm (days 3-14) - give nimodipine
• Hydrocephalus
• Hyponatremia (SIADH or cerebral salt wasting)
Tx: Secure aneurysm (coil or clip), nimodipine, BP control
