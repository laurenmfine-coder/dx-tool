CoachDx — AI Feedback

Epinephrine IM is exactly right — it's the FIRST-LINE treatment for anaphylaxis, not a second-line option. Common error: reaching for albuterol or antihistamines first. Those are adjuncts, not primary treatment. Epinephrine addresses bronchospasm AND hypotension AND mucosal edema. Did you know the #1 cause of fatal anaphylaxis outcomes is delayed epinephrine administration?

⏱ After IM Epinephrine

Within 5 minutes: Wheezing decreasing. HR 112. BP improving to 96/60. SpO2 94% on 2L nasal cannula. She's still anxious but breathing is easier. You notice a diffuse urticarial rash on her trunk and extremities that Mom says appeared before the wheezing.

✓ Diagnosis: Anaphylaxis (Peanut Allergy)

Acute onset of respiratory symptoms + hypotension + urticaria following allergen exposure in an atopic child meets diagnostic criteria for anaphylaxis per the WAO/EAACI guidelines. Further history confirmed the child ate a cookie containing peanut butter at the party — a previously undiagnosed allergy. IM epinephrine is the only first-line treatment.

CoachDx — Post-Case Debrief

Reasoning pattern analysis: This case tests whether you can identify a time-critical diagnosis and act decisively. The key clinical reasoning skill here is pattern recognition under pressure — atopic child + allergen exposure + multi-system involvement = anaphylaxis until proven otherwise.

Bias check: If you considered asthma first, that's a common anchoring bias — wheezing triggers the asthma pattern. But the hypotension and urticaria don't fit asthma. Always ask: "What diagnosis explains ALL the findings?"

📚 Key Learning Points

• Anaphylaxis = rapid multi-system involvement (skin + respiratory + cardiovascular)
• IM epinephrine (anterolateral thigh) is FIRST-LINE — never delay for adjuncts
• Biphasic reactions occur in ~20% of cases — observe 4-6 hours minimum
• Discharge requirements: EpiPen prescription, allergy referral, anaphylaxis action plan
• Tryptase level (drawn within 1-2 hrs) supports diagnosis retrospectively

WHAT I OBSERVED
Patient came in for follow-up after stent placement. Doctor listened to heart and lungs, checked ankle swelling. Adjusted medication because BP was still high.
MY QUESTION
Why did they check the ankles? What does swelling there mean?
AI ANALYSIS
The doctor was checking for peripheral edema — fluid buildup in the lower extremities. After stent placement, this can indicate the heart isn't pumping efficiently (heart failure), or it could be a medication side effect (calcium channel blockers like amlodipine commonly cause ankle swelling). The BP adjustment suggests they're optimizing cardiac afterload...

🎯 Diagnostic Challenge
BEGINNER

A 20-year-old college student comes to the clinic because she's been feeling really tired for the past 3 weeks. She says she's been sleeping more but still feels exhausted. She's also noticed she's been craving ice chips and her nails look different.

💡 REASONING SCAFFOLD

When a young person is tired, there are many causes. But craving ice (called pica) and nail changes are very specific clues. What common condition causes all three?

📚 VOCABULARY HELP

Pica: Craving non-food items (ice, dirt, starch). Often a sign of iron deficiency.
Koilonychia: Spoon-shaped nails — another classic iron deficiency sign.
