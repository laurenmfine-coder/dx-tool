MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

Tension PTX = one-way valve mechanism: air enters pleural space on inspiration but cannot exit → progressive air accumulation → lung collapse + mediastinal shift → impaired venous return → obstructive shock → cardiac arrest.

TypeCauseKey Features
Primary spontaneousRupture of apical blebs. Tall, thin young males, smokersSudden pleuritic chest pain + dyspnea. Usually small, often resolves
Secondary spontaneousUnderlying lung disease (COPD, CF, Marfan, TB)Higher morbidity due to limited pulmonary reserve
TraumaticPenetrating/blunt trauma, iatrogenic (central line, thoracentesis, mechanical ventilation/barotrauma)Most common cause of tension PTX
TensionAny PTX can become tension if one-way valve developsCLINICAL DIAGNOSIS — do NOT wait for imaging. Hemodynamic compromise
CLINICAL
Diagnosis & Presentation
⚠️ Tension PTX = CLINICAL DIAGNOSIS:
• Absent breath sounds on affected side
• Tracheal deviation AWAY from affected side
• JVD (impaired venous return)
• Hypotension + tachycardia (obstructive shock)
• Hyperresonance to percussion on affected side
• DO NOT delay treatment for CXR — immediate needle decompression

TREATMENT
Management
ScenarioTreatment
Tension PTXNeedle decompression: 14-16G needle, 2nd ICS MCL (or 5th ICS MAL). Converts tension to simple PTX. Then chest tube
Large simple PTX (>2 cm)Chest tube (tube thoracostomy): 4th-5th ICS MAL (safe triangle)
Small simple PTX (<2 cm, stable)Observation + supplemental O2 (accelerates reabsorption by 4x) + serial CXR
Recurrent PTXVATS (video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery) with pleurodesis or bleb resection
🔑 Needle Decompression: 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line, on affected side. Insert over SUPERIOR border of rib (NVB runs along inferior border). Rush of air confirms diagnosis. Always follow with definitive chest tube placement.
