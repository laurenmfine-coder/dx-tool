MECHANISM
Immunology of Drug Allergy

Penicillin allergy is the most commonly reported drug allergy (~10% of patients), but >95% of labeled patients are NOT truly allergic when formally evaluated. Most reactions represent: non-allergic side effects (GI upset), childhood rashes (likely viral), or tolerance developed over time (IgE-mediated allergy wanes at ~10% per year).

Types of Drug Hypersensitivity (Gell-Coombs)

TypeMechanismTimelineExample
Type I (Immediate)IgE-mediated mast cell degranulationMinutes to 1 hourAnaphylaxis, urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm
Type IIIgG/IgM-mediated cytotoxicityDays-weeksDrug-induced hemolytic anemia, cytopenias
Type IIIImmune complex deposition1-3 weeksSerum sickness, drug-induced vasculitis
Type IV (Delayed)T-cell mediatedDays-weeksMaculopapular exanthem, DRESS, SJS/TEN, AGEP
🔑 Key Concept: IgE-mediated penicillin allergy wanes over time. After 10 years without re-exposure, ~80% of previously allergic patients will have negative skin testing. This is why allergy labels should be periodically reassessed rather than carried indefinitely.
CLINICAL
Evaluation & Risk Stratification

Risk Stratification by History

Risk LevelHistoryApproach
Very Low RiskIsolated GI symptoms, family history only, unknown/remote reaction, headache, itching without rashDirect oral amoxicillin challenge (no skin testing needed)
Low RiskNon-severe cutaneous reaction (maculopapular rash, urticaria) >10 years agoPenicillin skin testing → if negative, oral challenge
Moderate RiskUrticaria/angioedema within last 10 years, reaction to multiple beta-lactamsPenicillin skin testing → if negative, graded oral challenge
High RiskAnaphylaxis (any timeframe), severe reaction (serum sickness, interstitial nephritis)Full penicillin skin testing (major + minor determinants) → if negative, graded challenge in monitored setting
CONTRAINDICATED for delabelingDRESS, SJS/TEN, acute interstitial nephritis, drug-induced hemolytic anemiaDo NOT challenge. Allergy label is permanent. These are non-IgE mediated and do not lose sensitivity over time.
⚠️ Never Challenge: Patients with history of DRESS (Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms), SJS/TEN, or other severe Type IV reactions should NEVER undergo skin testing or oral challenge with the culprit drug. These reactions can be life-threatening on re-exposure and do not reliably desensitize.
TREATMENT
Skin Testing & Delabeling Protocol

Penicillin Skin Testing

StepReagentMethod
1. Prick/PuncturePenicilloyl-polylysine (Pre-Pen, major determinant) + penicillin G (minor determinant) + positive control (histamine) + negative control (saline)Read at 15-20 min. Wheal ≥3 mm greater than negative control = positive.
2. IntradermalSame reagents (if prick negative)Inject 0.02 mL intradermally. Read at 15-20 min. More sensitive than prick.
3. Oral ChallengeAmoxicillin 250 mg (if skin tests negative)Observed for 1 hour. If tolerated → allergy label removed. Full therapeutic dose can follow.

Cross-Reactivity

Drug ClassCross-Reactivity with PenicillinClinical Approach
Cephalosporins~2% (previously overestimated at 10%)Patients with penicillin allergy can generally receive cephalosporins (especially 3rd/4th gen). Risk is higher with 1st gen cephalosporins that share R1 side chain (e.g., cefazolin-amoxicillin).
Carbapenems<1%Safe in penicillin-allergic patients. Can administer without skin testing in most cases.
AztreonamNone (different ring structure)Safe in all penicillin-allergic patients. Exception: cross-reacts with ceftazidime (shared R1 side chain).
🔑 Board Pearl: The 10% penicillin-cephalosporin cross-reactivity rate is a medical myth from contaminated early cephalosporin preparations. Actual cross-reactivity is ~2% and is R1 side-chain dependent, not beta-lactam ring dependent. Amoxicillin cross-reacts with cefadroxil and cephalexin (shared aminobenzyl R1 chain), not with ceftriaxone or cefepime.
