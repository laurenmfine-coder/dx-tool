MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

Venous thromboembolism (VTE) encompasses deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE). The pathogenesis follows Virchow's triad: venous stasis, endothelial injury, and hypercoagulability.

Virchow ElementRisk FactorsMechanism
StasisImmobilization, surgery, long travel, heart failure, obesitySlow flow allows coagulation factor accumulation at valve cusps
Endothelial InjurySurgery, trauma, central lines, prior DVTExposed subendothelial collagen activates coagulation cascade
HypercoagulabilityFactor V Leiden, prothrombin G20210A, malignancy, pregnancy, OCPs, antiphospholipid syndromeInherited or acquired prothrombotic state shifts hemostatic balance
🔑 Key Concept: Factor V Leiden is the most common inherited thrombophilia (5% of Caucasians). It causes activated protein C resistance — APC cannot inactivate Factor Va. Heterozygotes have 5-7× VTE risk; homozygotes have 50-80× risk. However, most people with Factor V Leiden never develop VTE.
CLINICAL
Diagnosis

Wells Score for DVT

Active cancer (+1), paralysis/immobilization (+1), bedridden >3 days or surgery within 12 weeks (+1), localized tenderness (+1), entire leg swollen (+1), calf swelling >3 cm vs. other side (+1), pitting edema (+1), collateral superficial veins (+1), prior DVT (+1), alternative diagnosis as likely (−2).

ScoreRiskNext Step
≤0Low (5%)D-dimer → if negative, DVT excluded. If positive → ultrasound.
1-2Moderate (17%)D-dimer → if negative, DVT excluded. If positive → ultrasound.
≥3High (53%)Compression ultrasound directly (do NOT rely on D-dimer to exclude).
🔑 Board Pearl: D-dimer has high sensitivity but low specificity — it effectively rules OUT VTE when negative (high NPV) but a positive result is nonspecific (elevated in infection, pregnancy, malignancy, post-op, age >50). Use age-adjusted D-dimer in patients >50: threshold = age × 10 ng/mL (e.g., 700 ng/mL for a 70-year-old). This improves specificity without reducing sensitivity.
⚠️ Phlegmasia Cerulea Dolens: Massive iliofemoral DVT causing complete venous outflow obstruction → cyanotic, painful, swollen limb with impending venous gangrene. This is a vascular emergency requiring catheter-directed thrombolysis or surgical thrombectomy — not anticoagulation alone.
TREATMENT
Anticoagulation & Duration

Anticoagulation Options

AgentMechanismKey Considerations
Rivaroxaban/ApixabanDirect Factor Xa inhibitorsFirst-line for most DVT/PE. No monitoring needed. Rivaroxaban: 15 mg BID × 21 days then 20 mg daily. Apixaban: 10 mg BID × 7 days then 5 mg BID.
Heparin → WarfarinAntithrombin enhancer → Vitamin K antagonistPreferred in severe renal impairment, antiphospholipid syndrome, mechanical valves. Overlap for ≥5 days AND INR ≥2.0 for 24 hrs.
LMWH (enoxaparin)Factor Xa > IIa inhibitionPreferred in cancer-associated VTE (or DOACs per recent trials). Weight-based dosing: 1 mg/kg BID. Monitor anti-Xa in renal impairment, obesity, pregnancy.

Duration of Therapy

ScenarioDurationRationale
Provoked VTE (surgery, immobilization)3 monthsTransient risk factor removed; low recurrence
Unprovoked first VTE≥3 months, then reassess for indefiniteAnnual recurrence ~10% if stopped. Extend if male, positive D-dimer after stopping, or residual thrombus.
Recurrent unprovoked VTEIndefiniteRecurrence rate too high to stop
Cancer-associated VTEIndefinite (while cancer active)LMWH or DOAC. Continue until cancer resolved.
⚠️ Warfarin Skin Necrosis: Occurs in protein C/S deficiency. Warfarin depletes protein C faster than procoagulant factors (shorter half-life) → transient hypercoagulable state → thrombosis in dermal vessels → skin necrosis. Prevention: bridge with heparin for ≥5 days when starting warfarin.
