MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

DKA results from absolute or relative insulin deficiency combined with counter-regulatory hormone excess (glucagon, cortisol, catecholamines, growth hormone). This creates a metabolic triad: hyperglycemia, ketosis, and metabolic acidosis.

Metabolic Cascade

StepMechanismClinical Effect
Insulin Deficiency↓ glucose uptake, ↑ hepatic gluconeogenesis/glycogenolysisHyperglycemia (typically 300-800 mg/dL)
Lipolysis↑ free fatty acids → hepatic beta-oxidation → acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetoneKetonemia, ketonuria, fruity breath (acetone)
Metabolic AcidosisKetoacids consume bicarbonate bufferAnion gap metabolic acidosis (AG typically 20-30+)
Osmotic DiuresisGlucose exceeds renal threshold → glycosuriaPolyuria → dehydration (avg 5-7L deficit), electrolyte losses
Potassium ShiftAcidosis + insulin deficiency → K⁺ shifts extracellularlySerum K⁺ may be normal/high despite massive TOTAL body K⁺ depletion
🔑 Key Concept: Euglycemic DKA (glucose <250 mg/dL) occurs with SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin). The mechanism: SGLT2i causes glycosuria → lower glucose → but persistent insulin deficiency + glucagon excess → continued ketogenesis. Board favorite! Also seen in pregnancy, starvation, and alcohol use.
⚠️ Critical: Total body potassium is ALWAYS depleted in DKA (200-500 mEq deficit) even when serum K⁺ is normal or elevated. Insulin drives K⁺ intracellularly — starting insulin with K⁺ <3.3 can cause fatal hypokalemia and cardiac arrest. ALWAYS check K⁺ before starting insulin.
CLINICAL
Diagnosis & Precipitants

Diagnostic Criteria

Glucose >250 mg/dL (or euglycemic with SGLT2i), pH <7.30, bicarb <18 mEq/L, anion gap >12, positive serum ketones (beta-hydroxybutyrate >3 mmol/L preferred).

Common Precipitants (5 I's)

Infection (most common — UTI, pneumonia), Insulin nonadherence, Infarction (MI, stroke, mesenteric), Intoxication (alcohol, cocaine), Iatrogenic (steroids, SGLT2i).

🔑 Board Pearl: Use beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) over the urine ketone dipstick. Dipstick detects acetoacetate, NOT BHB. During treatment, as DKA resolves, BHB converts to acetoacetate — making the dipstick appear WORSE even as the patient improves (paradoxical ketonuria). BHB directly tracks resolution.
TREATMENT
Management Protocol

Treatment Algorithm

StepInterventionDetails
1. FluidsNS 1-1.5 L/hr × 1-2 hrsThen 250-500 mL/hr. Switch to 0.45% NS when corrected Na normalizes. Total deficit typically 5-7 L.
2. PotassiumCheck K⁺ BEFORE insulinK⁺ <3.3: Hold insulin, replace K⁺ aggressively (40 mEq/hr). K⁺ 3.3-5.3: Add 20-40 mEq/L to each liter of fluids. K⁺ >5.3: Hold K⁺, recheck in 2 hrs.
3. InsulinRegular insulin drip 0.1-0.14 U/kg/hrNo bolus needed per recent evidence. Do NOT start until K⁺ ≥3.3. Goal: ↓ glucose 50-75 mg/dL/hr.
4. D5 BridgeAdd D5 when glucose <250Switch to D5-0.45%NS. Continue insulin drip — the goal is to close the anion gap, NOT just normalize glucose. Reducing insulin prematurely allows ketosis to continue.
5. BicarbOnly if pH <6.9100 mEq NaHCO₃ in 400 mL water + 20 mEq KCl over 2 hrs. Routine bicarb worsens outcomes (paradoxical CNS acidosis, hypokalemia, delayed ketosis resolution).

Resolution Criteria (all must be met)

Glucose <200 mg/dL AND at least 2 of: bicarb ≥15 mEq/L, pH >7.30, anion gap ≤12. Then overlap SubQ insulin 1-2 hrs before stopping drip.

⚠️ Cerebral Edema: Most feared complication in pediatric DKA. Risk factors: new-onset diabetes, age <5, severe acidosis, rapid fluid administration, rapid glucose correction. Monitor for headache, altered mental status, bradycardia, hypertension (Cushing reflex). Treat with mannitol 0.5-1 g/kg or hypertonic saline. Mortality 20-25%.
