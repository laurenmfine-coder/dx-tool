Teaching Point

Thyroglossal duct cyst: Most common congenital neck mass in children (~70% of congenital neck masses). Arises from remnants of the thyroglossal duct that normally obliterates by week 7. The duct traces the thyroid's descent path: foramen cecum → through/around hyoid bone → anterior neck → thyroid. Cyst can occur anywhere along this path. Most common location: at or just inferior to the hyoid bone (65%).

Teaching Point

Sistrunk procedure (1920): The gold standard for thyroglossal duct cyst. Steps: (1) excise the cyst, (2) remove the central portion of the hyoid bone, (3) excise the duct tract all the way to the foramen cecum (core of tissue from the base of the tongue). Without this complete excision, recurrence is ~50%. With Sistrunk, recurrence is ~3-5%. Before surgery: always confirm the patient has a normal functioning thyroid gland (ultrasound or scan) — the cyst may contain the ONLY thyroid tissue.

Teaching Point

Lingual thyroid: Failure of thyroid descent from the foramen cecum. Incidence: ~1/100,000. F:M = 7:1. In 70% of cases, it's the ONLY thyroid tissue. Presentation: usually asymptomatic, but can cause dysphagia, dysphonia, airway obstruction (especially during puberty/pregnancy when TSH-stimulated growth occurs). Management: suppressive levothyroxine (shrinks the tissue); surgical excision only if symptomatic and normal thyroid confirmed elsewhere.

Teaching Point

Branchial cleft cysts by type: 1st cleft (~5%): near EAC/parotid, close to facial nerve. 2nd cleft (~95%): anterior to upper SCM, external opening at anterior SCM border, internal at tonsillar fossa. 3rd/4th cleft (rare): left side predominance, may present as recurrent thyroid abscess (pyriform sinus fistula to thyroid). Treatment: complete surgical excision. Recurrence increases with prior infection or incomplete excision.

Teaching Point

DiGeorge (22q11.2 deletion): TBX1 gene is the critical gene in the deleted region. Neural crest cells require TBX1 signaling for proper pharyngeal development. CATCH-22: Cardiac (conotruncal: interrupted aortic arch type B, truncus arteriosus, TOF), Abnormal facies, Thymic aplasia (T-cell deficiency), Cleft palate, Hypocalcemia (parathyroid aplasia). Most common microdeletion syndrome (~1/4000). ~90% de novo. Variable expressivity — spectrum from severe (classic DiGeorge) to mild (velocardiofacial syndrome).
