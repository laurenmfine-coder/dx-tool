📖 Teaching Point

Esophageal atresia (EA) occurs from failure of the tracheoesophageal septum to properly divide the foregut. The foregut normally separates into the ventral trachea and dorsal esophagus during weeks 4-5. In EA, the esophagus ends as a blind pouch.

📖 Teaching Point

TEF classification: Type A (8%) = pure EA, no fistula. Type B (1%) = EA + proximal fistula. Type C (85%) = EA + distal fistula. Type D (1%) = EA + both fistulae. Type E/H (5%) = H-type fistula, no atresia. Type C is the 'classic' presentation with coiled OG tube AND gastric air.

📖 Teaching Point

VACTERL association occurs in ~10-15% of EA/TEF patients. Always screen: spine X-ray (V), anorectal exam (A), echocardiogram (C), already diagnosed (TE), renal ultrasound (R), limb exam (L). Need ≥3 features for the association. The cardiac defect here gives this baby 2 features (C + TE).

📖 Teaching Point

Preoperative management of EA/TEF: (1) NPO, IV fluids; (2) Replogle tube on continuous low suction in upper pouch; (3) Head-up positioning (30-45°) to reduce reflux through the fistula; (4) Minimize bag-mask ventilation (air goes through fistula → stomach distension → respiratory compromise). Intubate with tip beyond the fistula if possible.

📖 Teaching Point

EA/TEF complications timeline: EARLY (days): anastomotic leak (10-20%), pneumothorax. INTERMEDIATE (weeks): recurrent fistula (5-10%), GERD. LATE (months-years): esophageal stricture (30-40%), tracheomalacia (20%), GERD requiring fundoplication. Long-gap EA requires staged repair or esophageal replacement.

📖 Teaching Point

Esophageal stricture is the most common long-term complication of EA repair (30-40%). It responds well to endoscopic balloon dilation (average 2-3 dilations needed). Tracheomalacia occurs because the tracheal cartilage in the fistula segment is often abnormal/deficient. Most cases resolve by age 2 as cartilage strengthens.
