Ectopic Pregnancy Risk Factors

Ectopic pregnancy risk factors (by strength of association): prior ectopic (10× risk), prior tubal surgery (including tubal ligation — 'post-tubal ectopic'), PID/STI history (chlamydia is the most common cause of tubal damage globally), IUD use (IUDs prevent intrauterine pregnancy more effectively than ectopic — so if pregnancy occurs with IUD, it's more likely ectopic), infertility treatments (IVF paradoxically increases ectopic risk), smoking (impairs tubal motility). Chlamydia screening in sexually active women <25 is one of the most cost-effective preventive interventions — it reduces PID and subsequent ectopic risk.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: The incidence of ectopic pregnancy has increased over the past 30 years (paralleling STI rates and IVF utilization), but mortality has decreased due to earlier diagnosis with transvaginal ultrasound and beta-hCG monitoring. In resource-limited settings where ultrasound is unavailable, ruptured ectopics remain a leading cause of maternal death. Heterotopic pregnancy (simultaneous IUP and ectopic) is rare naturally (~1:30,000) but occurs in ~1:100 with IVF — always check the adnexa even if an IUP is seen in IVF patients.

TVUS + beta-hCG: The Ectopic Workup

The ectopic pregnancy algorithm relies on TVUS + quantitative beta-hCG: Discriminatory zone: at beta-hCG >1,500-2,000 mIU/mL, a normal IUP should be visible on TVUS. If the uterus is empty at this level → pregnancy of unknown location (PUL) = ectopic until proven otherwise. TVUS can visualize: empty uterus (most common finding), adnexal mass (tubal ring sign, 'blob sign'), live ectopic (with cardiac activity — rare), and free fluid in the cul-de-sac (hemoperitoneum if ruptured). CT has no role in stable first-trimester evaluation — radiation exposure + IV contrast in pregnancy without benefit over US.

Ectopic TVUS Findings

TVUS findings in ectopic: (1) Empty uterus (pseudogestational sac — a fluid collection without the 'double decidual sign' — can be confused with early IUP), (2) Adnexal mass (most commonly a 'tubal ring' — hyperechoic ring surrounding a small gestational sac), (3) Free fluid (echogenic fluid in cul-de-sac suggests hemoperitoneum from rupture or tubal leakage). The finding of an empty uterus at beta-hCG above the discriminatory zone, combined with an adnexal mass, has >95% specificity for ectopic.

TVUS Findings Guide Medical vs Surgical Treatment

The imaging finding directly determines the management pathway: Methotrexate criteria (all must be met): hemodynamically stable, unruptured, no fetal cardiac activity, beta-hCG <5,000 (some use <10,000), reliable follow-up. Surgical indications: hemodynamic instability, ruptured ectopic, fetal cardiac activity, beta-hCG >5,000, failed methotrexate, or patient unable to comply with methotrexate monitoring protocol. For this patient (beta-hCG 2,400, stable, left adnexal mass), either methotrexate OR surgery is appropriate — shared decision-making is key.

Placenta Previa Risk Factors

Placenta previa (placenta covering the cervical os) occurs in ~0.5% of pregnancies but is dramatically more common with prior cesarean deliveries. Risk increases exponentially with number of prior C-sections: 1 prior CD → 1% previa risk; 2 prior CDs → 2%; 3 prior CDs → 3-4%; ≥4 prior CDs → up to 10%. The most dangerous complication is placenta accreta spectrum (PAS) — where the placenta invades through the myometrium due to deficient decidua at the uterine scar. PAS + previa with prior CDs has life-threatening hemorrhage potential. Smoking and cocaine cause relative placental insufficiency, leading to compensatory placental enlargement and higher previa rates.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: The rising cesarean delivery rate worldwide (~30% in the US) is directly driving increasing rates of placenta previa and accreta spectrum disorder. This is a major downstream consequence of primary cesarean deliveries — each additional CD exponentially increases risk of abnormal placentation in subsequent pregnancies. Reducing the primary cesarean rate is a public health priority for this reason.
A.Transabdominal AND transvaginal ultrasound —
B.MRI pelvis for placental evaluation
C.CT abdomen/pelvis
US for Previa + Accreta Screening

TVUS is safe and is the gold standard for placenta previa classification: complete previa (placenta covers the internal os), marginal previa (placental edge reaches but doesn't cover the os), low-lying (placental edge within 2cm of os but not reaching it). CRITICAL: with two prior CDs + previa, ultrasound must evaluate for PAS: look for loss of the normal retroplacental clear zone, myometrial thinning, placental lacunae ('Swiss cheese' appearance), and increased vascularity at the uterine-bladder interface. MRI is second-line for PAS when US is equivocal. NEVER perform digital cervical exam in known or suspected previa — it can trigger massive hemorrhage.

A.Normal placental position — the previa resolved
B.Placental abruption (retroplacental hematoma)
C.Complete placenta previa (placenta fully covering the internal cervical os)
D.Cervical insufficiency with funneling
Previa + Accreta Spectrum Screening

The US findings tell the complete story: complete previa (delivery must be by cesarean) with possible PAS at the site of prior uterine scar. PAS signs on US: placental lacunae (irregular vascular spaces within the placenta — positive predictive value >90% when multiple lacunae seen), loss of retroplacental clear zone (placenta invading myometrium), myometrial thinning to <1mm, and hypervascularity at the uterine serosa-bladder interface. If PAS is suspected, delivery planning changes dramatically — multidisciplinary team, blood bank preparation, possible cesarean hysterectomy.

A.Attempt vaginal delivery with close monitoring
B.US confirms complete previa with suspected PAS → this requires multidisciplinary
C.Expectant management until term (39 weeks)
Imaging Drives Delivery Planning

Complete previa = mandatory cesarean delivery (vaginal delivery would cause life-threatening hemorrhage). Timing: 36-37 weeks for previa alone, 34-35 weeks for previa + suspected PAS (earlier delivery balances maternal hemorrhage risk against neonatal prematurity). If PAS is confirmed: the planned surgery may include cesarean hysterectomy (the placenta is left in situ and the uterus removed to control hemorrhage). Average blood loss with PAS is 3-5 liters — the massive transfusion protocol must be activated. Interventional radiology may place uterine artery balloon catheters preoperatively.

Ovarian Torsion Risk Factors

Ovarian torsion occurs when the ovary (with or without the fallopian tube) rotates on its vascular pedicle, compromising blood flow. Risk factors: ovarian enlargement >5cm (cysts, tumors, hyperstimulation), fertility treatment (clomiphene, gonadotropins — cause multiple large follicles), pregnancy (corpus luteum cysts, relaxin-mediated ligament laxity), prior tubal ligation (alters adnexal mobility), and long utero-ovarian ligament (congenital). In children and adolescents: normal ovaries can torse (more mobile due to underdeveloped utero-ovarian ligaments), and torsion is the #1 gynecologic surgical emergency in pediatric patients. Dermoid cysts (mature teratomas) are the most common ovarian neoplasm to cause torsion.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: Ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS) from fertility treatment can massively enlarge the ovaries (>10cm), creating very high torsion risk. With the increase in IVF cycles worldwide, fertility treatment-related torsion is rising. Also: in pregnancy, ovarian torsion peaks in the first trimester (large corpus luteum) and is a diagnostic challenge because RLQ pain mimics appendicitis and ectopic pregnancy.
A.Pelvic ultrasound with Doppler flow assessment —
B.CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast
C.MRI pelvis
US with Doppler for Torsion

Pelvic US (transvaginal + transabdominal) with color Doppler is first-line. Findings suggestive of torsion: (1) enlarged ovary (>5cm), (2) peripherally displaced follicles ('string of pearls'), (3) free fluid in the cul-de-sac, (4) 'whirlpool sign' (twisted vascular pedicle), (5) absent or reduced arterial and/or venous Doppler flow. CRITICAL CAVEAT: normal Doppler flow does NOT rule out torsion — the ovary has dual blood supply (ovarian artery from aorta + tubal branch of uterine artery), so one may maintain flow even when torsed. If clinical suspicion is high, proceed to surgery regardless of Doppler findings.

A.Normal right ovary with normal Doppler flow
B.Enlarged right ovary (>5cm) with multiple large follicles
C.Ruptured ovarian cyst only
Torsion US Findings

The classic US findings in torsion: enlarged, edematous ovary with peripherally displaced follicles (stromal edema pushes follicles to the periphery), free fluid (from venous congestion and transudation), and the whirlpool sign if the twisted pedicle is visualized. Absent Doppler flow is specific but not sensitive — Doppler can be normal in early or intermittent torsion. The enlarged ovary in the context of fertility treatment is the key predisposing finding.

A.US findings + clinical presentation = emergent surgical exploration
B.Pain management and observation for 24 hours
C.Antibiotics for PID
Clinical Suspicion → Surgery

Ovarian torsion is a clinical + imaging diagnosis, and clinical suspicion trumps Doppler findings. The surgical approach: laparoscopic detorsion with ovarian conservation (even if the ovary appears dusky/blue — most ovaries recover after detorsion). Oophorectomy is reserved for clearly nonviable ovaries (black, necrotic) or suspected malignancy. Ovarian conservation is especially important in this patient who is undergoing fertility treatment — preserving ovarian tissue preserves her reproductive potential.

Oophoropexy (surgically shortening the utero-ovarian ligament to prevent re-torsion) can be considered during the same surgery if recurrence risk is high. Molar Pregnancy Epidemiology

Gestational trophoblastic disease (GTD) incidence varies dramatically by geography: 1:1,000 pregnancies in the US/Europe vs 1:100-200 in Southeast Asia, Japan, and Mexico — driven by both genetic and nutritional factors (folate and beta-carotene deficiency). Age is bimodal: highest rates in adolescents (<20) and women >40. Complete moles (46,XX — all paternal chromosomes, no fetal tissue) present earlier and more dramatically than partial moles (69,XXY — triploid, some fetal tissue). The markedly elevated hCG causes secondary effects: hyperthyroidism (TSH receptor cross-stimulation), hyperemesis, and early preeclampsia — all of which are clinical clues.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: After molar pregnancy, 15-20% of complete moles develop gestational trophoblastic neoplasia (GTN) requiring chemotherapy — making serial beta-hCG monitoring mandatory for 6-12 months. Reliable contraception during monitoring is essential (pregnancy would confound hCG tracking). The cure rate for GTN with chemotherapy exceeds 95%, making it one of the most curable malignancies — but only if diagnosed through proper surveillance.
A.Pelvic ultrasound (transvaginal)
B.CT abdomen/pelvis
C.MRI pelvis
TVUS Confirms Molar Pregnancy

TVUS findings in complete molar pregnancy: 'snowstorm' pattern — diffuse echogenic intrauterine tissue with multiple small cystic spaces (hydropic villi), no fetal parts, no amniotic fluid. Partial mole: Swiss cheese pattern in a thickened placenta, possibly with fetal parts or amniotic fluid. TVUS also evaluates for bilateral theca lutein cysts (ovarian enlargement from hCG hyperstimulation — present in 25-50% of complete moles). Chest X-ray is also indicated — to evaluate for trophoblastic pulmonary metastases (lung mets occur in 4-5% at diagnosis).

Complete Mole US Pattern

The 'snowstorm' pattern is classic for complete moles. The cystic spaces represent hydropic (fluid-filled) chorionic villi — the hallmark of molar pregnancy. Modern high-resolution US can detect moles earlier (8-12 weeks) than the classic 'grape-like vesicle' stage. Theca lutein cysts result from extreme hCG stimulation — they can be massive (>10cm) and may cause ovarian torsion. These cysts resolve spontaneously after the mole is evacuated and hCG normalizes.

A.Medical management with methotrexate
B.Observation and repeat ultrasound in 2 weeks
C.US confirms molar pregnancy → urgent suction curettage
Evacuation + Complication Management

Suction curettage is the standard treatment for molar pregnancy (preserves fertility). Pre-evacuation checklist: stabilize hypertension (early preeclampsia — resolves after evacuation), manage hyperthyroidism (beta-blocker for symptoms, resolves after hCG drops), type and crossmatch blood (hemorrhage risk), and chest X-ray for staging. Pathology differentiates complete from partial mole, which determines surveillance intensity. Hysterectomy is an option for patients who have completed childbearing — it eliminates the risk of local invasion but does NOT eliminate metastatic risk (surveillance still required).

Placental Abruption Epidemiology

Abruption occurs in ~1% of pregnancies but causes disproportionate mortality. Risk factors: chronic hypertension (#1, 2.4-5×), preeclampsia, cocaine/methamphetamine use (acute vasospasm), cigarette smoking (1.5-2.5×), prior abruption (10× recurrence risk), abdominal trauma (MVC, domestic violence — always screen), advanced maternal age, polyhydramnios/multiple gestation (rapid uterine decompression), short umbilical cord, and thrombophilia. Cocaine is the most common illicit drug cause of abruption — it causes acute hypertensive crisis and intense vasospasm of uterine arteries. The critical distinction: abruption = painful, dark bleeding, rigid uterus, fetal distress; previa = painless, bright red bleeding, soft uterus, usually reassuring fetal status.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: Domestic violence screening is essential in ALL cases of abruption — abdominal trauma from intimate partner violence is a significant cause, particularly in the third trimester when the uterus is large and vulnerable. Abruption from trauma can occur even without obvious external injury. Also: the rising rates of chronic hypertension in younger women (driven by obesity) are contributing to increasing abruption rates.
A.CT abdomen to visualize the abruption
B.MRI pelvis for better placental evaluation
C.Placental abruption is primarily a CLINICAL diagnosis, not an imaging diagnosis.
Abruption — Clinical Diagnosis, Not Imaging Diagnosis

This is one of the most important teaching points in obstetrics: ultrasound MISSES 50-75% of abruptions. The retroplacental hemorrhage is frequently isoechoic to the placenta and invisible on US. A normal ultrasound in a patient with clinical abruption should NOT provide false reassurance. When US is helpful: it can visualize large retroplacental or preplacental hematomas, confirm previa (the other major cause of third-trimester bleeding), assess fetal presentation, and estimate gestational age if unknown. The clinical triad drives management: painful vaginal bleeding + rigid/tender uterus + fetal distress = abruption, regardless of ultrasound findings.

A.US will definitively show the abruption
B.US may show a retroplacental hypoechoic or mixed echogenicity collection
C.Complete placenta previa
US Sensitivity Is Poor for Abruption

When US does detect abruption, findings include: retroplacental collection (hematoma between placenta and uterine wall — may be hypoechoic acutely, becoming more echogenic over days), placental thickening (>5cm, from concealed hemorrhage expanding the placenta), subchorionic hematoma, and elevated amniotic fluid echogenicity (blood in the amniotic fluid). Concealed abruption (blood trapped behind the placenta without external bleeding) is the most dangerous form — it can cause massive hemorrhage and DIC without obvious vaginal bleeding, making clinical assessment (rigid uterus, fetal distress, maternal tachycardia) even more critical.

Emergent Delivery + Hemorrhage/DIC Management

Severe abruption with fetal distress = emergent cesarean section. Maternal stabilization and delivery happen simultaneously — do NOT delay delivery for steroids or diagnostic workup. DIC management: abruption is the most common obstetric cause of DIC — tissue thromboplastin release from the damaged placenta activates the coagulation cascade. Labs: fibrinogen (<200 mg/dL = DIC), PT/PTT, platelets, D-dimer. Treatment: cryoprecipitate for fibrinogen <150, FFP for prolonged PT/PTT, platelets if <50,000, packed RBCs for hemorrhage. Massive transfusion protocol should be activated for significant abruption.
