📚 Renal Development Overview

The urinary system develops from intermediate mesoderm through three successive kidney systems that develop in a cranial-to-caudal sequence. Only the third system (metanephros) becomes the permanent kidney. The development involves a critical reciprocal induction between the ureteric bud and the metanephric mesenchyme.

🔑 Critical Concept — Reciprocal Induction: The ureteric bud (UB) and metanephric mesenchyme (MM) MUST interact for either to develop. UB signals (including GDNF/RET) induce MM to condense into nephrons. MM signals induce UB to branch (forming the collecting system). Failure of this interaction → renal agenesis.

Key Timeline

Week 4: Pronephros

Rudimentary, non-functional. Appears in the cervical region and degenerates by end of week 4. Duct persists as the pronephric duct → becomes the mesonephric (Wolffian) duct.

Weeks 4-8: Mesonephros

Functional interim kidney in the thoracolumbar region. Produces urine briefly. Mesonephric tubules degenerate in females; in males, some become efferent ductules of the testis. Mesonephric (Wolffian) duct persists → gives rise to the ureteric bud + male reproductive tract.

Week 5: Ureteric Bud Formation

The ureteric bud sprouts from the distal mesonephric duct and grows into the metanephric mesenchyme. This is the critical initiating event for permanent kidney development.

Weeks 5-36: Metanephros (Permanent Kidney)

Reciprocal induction between UB and MM. UB branches ~15 times → collecting system. MM condenses into nephrons (through MET: mesenchymal-to-epithelial transition). Nephrogenesis complete by ~36 weeks.

Weeks 6-9: Ascent & Rotation

Kidneys "ascend" from the pelvis to the lumbar region (actually differential growth — the body grows caudally relative to the kidneys). During ascent, kidneys rotate 90° medially (hilum faces medially in adult).

⚡ Clinical Pearl: Nephrogenesis is complete by ~36 weeks of gestation. Premature infants born before this have fewer nephrons, which may contribute to hypertension and chronic kidney disease in adulthood (Brenner hypothesis / fetal programming of adult disease).

🔄 Three Kidney Systems

Three sequential kidney systems develop from intermediate mesoderm, progressing cranial → caudal. Each degenerates as the next forms, except the metanephros which becomes permanent.

1. Pronephros (Week 4)

  • Location: Cervical region
  • Function: Non-functional in humans (vestigial)
  • Fate: Degenerates completely by end of week 4
  • Legacy: Its duct persists → becomes the pronephric duct → mesonephric (Wolffian) duct

2. Mesonephros (Weeks 4-8)

  • Location: Thoracolumbar region
  • Function: Functional interim kidney — produces urine, has glomeruli and tubules
  • Fate: Most tubules degenerate; some persist in males as efferent ductules
  • Legacy: Mesonephric (Wolffian) duct → ureteric bud + epididymis + vas deferens + seminal vesicle + ejaculatory duct

3. Metanephros (Week 5 → Adult)

  • Location: Initially sacral, ascends to lumbar
  • Function: Permanent kidney — begins producing urine by week 10-12
  • Two components: Ureteric bud (collecting system) + metanephric mesenchyme (nephrons)
  • Nephrogenesis: ~1 million nephrons per kidney, complete by 36 weeks

"Please Make Me a kidney" = Pronephros → Mesonephros → Metanephros
Cranial → Caudal | Transient → Transient → Permanent

🔬 Metanephric Development

Ureteric Bud & Collecting System

The ureteric bud (UB) sprouts from the distal mesonephric duct at week 5 and invades the metanephric mesenchyme. It undergoes ~15 generations of dichotomous branching:

Ureteric bud (from mesonephric duct) → Stalk = URETER → First branches = RENAL PELVIS → MAJOR CALYCES → Next branches = MINOR CALYCES → Subsequent branches = COLLECTING DUCTS (papillary → cortical) Key: Everything from UB = collecting system (ureter → pelvis → calyces → collecting ducts)

Metanephric Mesenchyme & Nephron Formation

The metanephric mesenchyme (MM) is induced by UB signals to undergo mesenchymal-to-epithelial transition (MET) — one of the best-studied examples of this process in development.

Metanephric mesenchyme (induced by UB) → Condensation (cap mesenchyme) → Renal vesicle → Comma-shaped body → S-shaped body → Mature nephron: Bowman's capsule → PCT → Loop of Henle → DCT Key: Everything from MM = excretory nephron (glomerulus through DCT) The nephron CONNECTS to the collecting duct (from UB) at the DCT-CD junction

GDNF/RET Signaling

🔑 GDNF/RET is THE critical pathway:
  • GDNF (Glial cell-Derived Neurotrophic Factor) — secreted by metanephric mesenchyme
  • RET — receptor tyrosine kinase on the ureteric bud
  • GDNF from MM activates RET on UB → stimulates UB outgrowth and branching
  • RET mutations → failure of UB outgrowth → renal agenesis
  • RET is also a proto-oncogene: gain-of-function mutations → MEN2A/2B (medullary thyroid carcinoma, pheochromocytoma)
🧪 Other Key Signaling:
  • Wnt9b / Wnt4 — from UB, induces MET in the mesenchyme
  • WT1 (Wilms' tumor 1) — transcription factor essential for MM survival; mutations → Wilms' tumor + WAGR syndrome
  • PAX2 — required for UB outgrowth and nephron differentiation; mutations → renal coloboma syndrome
  • BMP4 — inhibits ectopic UB budding (prevents duplex systems)

Nephron Endowment

Each kidney contains approximately 200,000-1,800,000 nephrons (average ~1 million), with wide variation. Nephrogenesis begins at ~week 8-9 and is complete by ~36 weeks. NO new nephrons form after birth.

⚡ Brenner Hypothesis: Low nephron number at birth (from prematurity, IUGR, maternal malnutrition, or genetic factors) → compensatory hyperfiltration in remaining nephrons → progressive glomerulosclerosis → hypertension and CKD in adulthood. This links low birth weight to adult cardiovascular and renal disease.

⬆️ Renal Ascent & Rotation

The "Ascent"

From weeks 6-9, the kidneys appear to migrate from the sacral region to the lumbar region (L1-L3). This is not true migration — the body grows caudally (elongates) while the kidneys remain relatively fixed, creating the appearance of ascent.

During Ascent:

  • Kidneys pass through the fork of the umbilical arteries
  • Blood supply changes: initially from common iliac → progressively higher aortic branches → final renal arteries from L1-L2 aorta
  • Lower vessels degenerate as new, higher vessels form
  • Accessory renal arteries (~25% of people) = persistent lower vessels that failed to degenerate

Rotation

During ascent, each kidney rotates 90° medially. Initially, the hilum faces anteriorly. After rotation, the hilum faces medially (adult position).

🔑 Horseshoe Kidney — Board Favorite:
  • Lower poles fuse across the midline (90% are lower pole fusion)
  • Fused kidney gets caught on the inferior mesenteric artery (IMA) during ascent → sits low at L3-L5
  • Associated with Turner syndrome (45,X) — ~7% of Turner patients have horseshoe kidney
  • Increased risk of: UPJ obstruction, kidney stones, UTIs, Wilms' tumor (slight increase)
  • Usually asymptomatic and found incidentally on imaging

🫗 Bladder & Lower Urinary Tract

Urogenital Sinus Division

The cloaca (common chamber for the hindgut and allantois) is divided by the urorectal septum into:

  • Anterior: Urogenital sinus → bladder + urethra + prostate (males) / lower vagina (females)
  • Posterior: Anorectal canal → rectum + upper anal canal
Cloaca │ ├── Urorectal septum divides (week 7) │ ├── ANTERIOR → Urogenital sinus │ ├── Upper (vesical) → BLADDER body │ ├── Middle (pelvic) → Prostatic + membranous urethra (M) / entire urethra (F) │ └── Lower (phallic) → Penile urethra (M) / vestibule (F) │ └── POSTERIOR → Anorectal canal → Rectum + Anal canal (above pectinate line)

⚠️ Urachal Anomalies:
  • Patent urachus — complete persistence → urine drains from umbilicus (fistula)
  • Urachal cyst — focal persistence → fluid-filled cyst between bladder and umbilicus (risk of infection)
  • Urachal sinus — opens at the umbilical end only → drains at umbilicus
  • Vesicourachal diverticulum — opens at the bladder end only
  • Urachal remnants have a small risk of adenocarcinoma (urachal carcinoma — very rare but high-yield)

Posterior Urethral Valves (PUV)

🔑 Most common cause of lower urinary tract obstruction in male neonates:
  • Abnormal folds of mucosa in the prostatic urethra (type I most common — from the verumontanum)
  • Causes bilateral hydronephrosis, megacystis (distended bladder), oligohydramnios
  • Oligohydramnios → Potter sequence: pulmonary hypoplasia + limb deformities + flattened facies
  • Only in MALES (urethra anatomy)
  • Prenatal: bilateral hydronephrosis + distended bladder + oligohydramnios on ultrasound
  • Treatment: postnatal valve ablation (endoscopic); prenatal vesicoamniotic shunt if severe

Potter Sequence

⚠️ Potter Sequence (Oligohydramnios Sequence): Any cause of severe oligohydramnios → fetal compression + lack of amniotic fluid for lung development:
  • Pulmonary hypoplasia (most lethal consequence — lungs need amniotic fluid to expand)
  • Limb deformities (compression: clubfoot, joint contractures)
  • Flattened facies (Potter facies: recessed chin, flattened nose, low-set ears)

Causes: bilateral renal agenesis (classic Potter syndrome), PUV, bilateral MCDK, prolonged PROM, uteroplacental insufficiency.

🔬 ARPKD vs ADPKD — Board Comparison:
  • ARPKD: AR, infantile, PKHD1, collecting duct cysts, hepatic fibrosis, often fatal early
  • ADPKD: AD, adult, PKD1/PKD2, variable-sized cysts, berry aneurysms + hepatic cysts, ESRD by 50-60y
  • Key distinguisher: ARPKD = both kidneys uniformly enlarged and echogenic (prenatal US); ADPKD = bilateral cysts of different sizes, typically detected in adulthood

Duplex Collecting System

Embryology & Weigert-Meyer Rule

Early bifurcation of the ureteric bud (or two separate buds) → two separate collecting systems draining one kidney. Each has its own ureter.

  • Upper pole ureter: inserts LOW and MEDIALLY → prone to OBSTRUCTION (ureterocele) → hydronephrosis
  • Lower pole ureter: inserts HIGH and LATERALLY → prone to REFLUX (short intramural tunnel)
🧠 Weigert-Meyer Rule:
Upper pole = obstructs (inserts low → ureterocele) | Lower pole = reflux (inserts high → incompetent)

Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR)

  • Retrograde flow of urine from bladder → ureter → kidney
  • Caused by short or absent intramural ureter → incompetent flap valve at UVJ
  • Most common in young girls
  • Risk of recurrent pyelonephritis → reflux nephropathy → renal scarring → CKD
  • Grading I-V (I = reflux into ureter only → V = massive dilation + intrarenal reflux)
  • Low grades (I-III) often resolve spontaneously with growth; high grades may need surgery (ureteral reimplantation)

📝 Test Your Knowledge

Question 1

A horseshoe kidney fails to ascend to its normal position because it gets trapped by which structure?

A) Superior mesenteric artery
B) Inferior mesenteric artery
C) Aortic bifurcation
D) Common iliac arteries
The fused lower poles of a horseshoe kidney get caught on the IMA as they try to ascend past it, trapping the kidney at L3-L5 (below its normal L1-L2 position). Associated with Turner syndrome (45,X).

Question 2

The ureteric bud gives rise to all of the following EXCEPT:

A) Collecting ducts
B) Renal pelvis
C) Proximal convoluted tubule
D) Ureter
The PCT develops from metanephric mesenchyme (along with glomerulus, Bowman's capsule, loop of Henle, and DCT). The ureteric bud forms the collecting system: ureter → pelvis → calyces → collecting ducts.

Question 3

Bilateral renal agenesis causes Potter sequence. What is the most lethal component?

A) Pulmonary hypoplasia
B) Limb deformities
C) Flattened facies
D) Adrenal insufficiency
Pulmonary hypoplasia is the lethal component. Fetal breathing movements of amniotic fluid are essential for lung development. Without amniotic fluid (from absent urine production), the lungs cannot expand and develop → fatal respiratory failure at birth.

Question 4

Which receptor on the ureteric bud is activated by GDNF from the metanephric mesenchyme to initiate kidney development?

A) FGFR2
B) RET
C) WT1
D) PAX2
GDNF (from MM) → RET receptor (on UB) → UB outgrowth and branching. RET loss-of-function → renal agenesis. RET gain-of-function → MEN2A/2B (medullary thyroid carcinoma). This dual role makes RET a high-yield board topic.

Question 5

An infant with a distended bladder, bilateral hydronephrosis, and oligohydramnios is most likely male. What is the diagnosis?

A) Vesicoureteral reflux
B) Posterior urethral valves
C) Ureterocele
D) Ureteropelvic junction obstruction
PUV is the most common cause of lower urinary tract obstruction in male neonates. The triad of distended bladder + bilateral hydronephrosis + oligohydramnios in a male = PUV until proven otherwise. Treatment: postnatal endoscopic valve ablation.

Question 6

In a duplex kidney system, which pole's ureter is prone to vesicoureteral reflux per the Weigert-Meyer rule?

A) Upper pole (inserts low and medially)
B) Lower pole (inserts high and laterally)
C) Both equally
D) Neither — duplex systems don't reflux
Weigert-Meyer rule: upper pole ureter inserts low → obstructs (ureterocele). Lower pole ureter inserts high → refluxes (short intramural tunnel = incompetent flap valve).
