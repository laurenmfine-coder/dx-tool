💨 Part 1: Mechanics of Breathing

Breathing is driven by pressure gradients created by changes in thoracic cavity volume. Understanding these mechanics is essential for recognizing why patients with lung disease experience dyspnea and how treatments work.

Live Breathing Animation

Diaphragm contracts (flattens) → Thoracic volume ↑ → Pressure ↓ → Air flows IN

Diaphragm relaxes (domes up) → Thoracic volume ↓ → Pressure ↑ → Air flows OUT

💡 Boyle's Law: The Foundation of Breathing

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ — At constant temperature, pressure and volume are inversely proportional. When the thoracic cavity expands (↑ volume), intrapulmonary pressure decreases below atmospheric pressure, and air flows into the lungs down its pressure gradient.

Inspiration: An Active Process

During quiet inspiration, muscle contraction increases thoracic volume, decreasing intrapulmonary pressure below atmospheric pressure (about -3 cmH₂O), causing air to flow into the lungs.

🔵 Diaphragm (Primary)
Contracts → Flattens → Descends

Increases vertical dimension of thorax. Responsible for ~75% of air movement during quiet breathing. Innervated by phrenic nerve (C3, C4, C5 - "C3, 4, 5 keeps the diaphragm alive").

🔵 External Intercostals (Primary)
Contract → Elevate ribs → "Bucket handle" motion

Increase anteroposterior diameter of thorax. Fibers run obliquely downward and forward. Innervated by intercostal nerves (T1-T11).

🟡 Scalenes (Accessory)
Elevate ribs 1 & 2

Active during deep breathing and exercise. Elevate upper rib cage.

🟡 Sternocleidomastoid (Accessory)
Elevates sternum

Recruited during respiratory distress. Visible as "neck breathing" - sign of increased work of breathing.

🟡 Pectoralis Major/Minor (Accessory)
Pull ribs outward

Used when arms are fixed (tripod position). Patients with COPD often lean forward on arms to use these muscles.

Expiration: Usually Passive

During quiet expiration, no muscle contraction is required. The inspiratory muscles relax, and the natural elastic recoil of the lungs and chest wall returns the thorax to its resting position, increasing intrapulmonary pressure above atmospheric pressure and pushing air out.

🔴 Rectus Abdominis (Forced Expiration)
Increases intra-abdominal pressure

Pushes diaphragm upward. Active during coughing, sneezing, exercise, and in patients with airway obstruction.

🔴 Internal Intercostals (Forced Expiration)
Depress ribs → Decrease thoracic volume

Fibers run obliquely downward and backward (opposite to external intercostals).

🔑 Clinical Pearl: Signs of Increased Work of Breathing

When a patient uses accessory muscles, it indicates respiratory distress. Look for: nasal flaring, tracheal tug, suprasternal/intercostal retractions, tripod positioning, pursed-lip breathing, and visible sternocleidomastoid contraction. In severe cases, patients develop paradoxical abdominal movement (abdomen moves inward during inspiration) - a sign of impending respiratory failure.

📊 Part 2: Lung Volumes and Capacities

The lung is divided into four volumes (non-overlapping) that combine to form four capacities (combinations of volumes). Understanding these is essential for interpreting pulmonary function tests.

Lung Volumes Visualization

IRV (Inspiratory Reserve Volume) ~3,000 mL
TV (Tidal Volume) ~500 mL
ERV (Expiratory Reserve Volume) ~1,200 mL
RV (Residual Volume) - Cannot be exhaled ~1,200 mL

Note: RV cannot be measured by spirometry - requires body plethysmography or gas dilution techniques.

The Four Volumes

Volume Definition Normal Value Clinical Significance
Tidal Volume (TV) Air moved in/out during quiet breathing ~500 mL Basis for minute ventilation (TV × RR)
Inspiratory Reserve Volume (IRV) Extra air that CAN be inhaled after normal inspiration ~3,000 mL Reduced in restrictive disease
Expiratory Reserve Volume (ERV) Extra air that CAN be exhaled after normal expiration ~1,200 mL Reduced in obesity, pregnancy
Residual Volume (RV) Air remaining after maximal expiration (cannot be exhaled) ~1,200 mL ↑ in obstructive disease (air trapping)

The Four Capacities

Capacity Formula Normal Value Clinical Significance
Total Lung Capacity (TLC) IRV + TV + ERV + RV ~6,000 mL ↓ in restriction, ↑ in hyperinflation (COPD)
Vital Capacity (VC) IRV + TV + ERV ~4,800 mL Maximum air that can be moved; ↓ in both obstruction & restriction
Inspiratory Capacity (IC) IRV + TV ~3,500 mL Important for exercise capacity
Functional Residual Capacity (FRC) ERV + RV ~2,400 mL Air remaining after quiet expiration; ↑ in hyperinflation
💡 Why RV Exists: Airway Closure

You cannot exhale all the air from your lungs because small airways collapse at low lung volumes due to loss of radial traction (airway tethering). The closing capacity is the lung volume at which small airways begin to close. In elderly and obese patients, closing capacity increases and may exceed FRC, causing V/Q mismatch even during normal breathing.

📈 Part 3: Spirometry & PFT Interpretation

Spirometry measures how much air you can move and how fast you can move it. The key parameters are FVC, FEV₁, and the FEV₁/FVC ratio.

Key Spirometry Parameters

Parameter Definition Normal Value Interpretation
FVC Forced Vital Capacity - total volume exhaled forcefully after maximal inspiration ≥80% predicted or ≥LLN ↓ in restriction (or pseudorestriction from air trapping)
FEV₁ Volume exhaled in first second of forced expiration ≥80% predicted or ≥LLN ↓ in obstruction; used to grade severity
FEV₁/FVC Ratio Percentage of FVC exhaled in first second ≥0.70 (or ≥LLN) <0.70 = Obstruction
FEF₂₅₋₇₅% Forced expiratory flow during middle 50% of FVC ≥65% predicted Sensitive for small airway disease but highly variable
PEF Peak Expiratory Flow - maximum flow rate achieved Varies by age/sex/height Effort-dependent; useful for asthma monitoring

The Interpretation Algorithm

🔄 Step-by-Step PFT Interpretation

  1. Step 1: Is FEV₁/FVC reduced?
    • YES (≤0.70) → Obstructive pattern → Go to Step 2
    • NO (>0.70) → Not obstructive → Go to Step 3
  2. Step 2: If obstructive, what severity? Use FEV₁ % predicted:
    • Mild: FEV₁ ≥80%
    • Moderate: FEV₁ 50-79%
    • Severe: FEV₁ 30-49%
    • Very Severe: FEV₁ <30%
  3. Step 3: If not obstructive, is FVC reduced?
    • YES (FVC <80% or <LLN) → Possible restriction → Need TLC to confirm
    • NO → Normal spirometry
  4. Step 4: Is there bronchodilator response?
    • Positive if FEV₁ or FVC increases by ≥12% AND ≥200 mL (or ≥10% of predicted per 2022 guidelines)

Obstructive vs. Restrictive Patterns

🔴 Obstructive Pattern

  • FEV₁/FVC ratio: ↓↓ (diagnostic)
  • FEV₁: ↓ (grades severity)
  • FVC: Normal or ↓ (air trapping)
  • TLC: Normal or ↑ (hyperinflation)
  • RV: ↑ (air trapping)
  • Examples: Asthma, COPD, bronchiectasis

🟡 Restrictive Pattern

  • FEV₁/FVC ratio: Normal or ↑
  • FEV₁: ↓ (proportional to FVC)
  • FVC: ↓↓
  • TLC: ↓↓ (confirms restriction)
  • RV: Normal or ↓
  • Examples: Pulmonary fibrosis, chest wall disease, neuromuscular disease
🔑 Watch for "Pseudorestriction"

A low FVC with normal FEV₁/FVC can look restrictive but actually be obstructive with air trapping. The RV is so elevated that the patient can't fully exhale, reducing FVC. Always check TLC: if TLC is normal or elevated, it's not true restriction - it's air trapping from obstruction!

📉 Part 4: Flow-Volume Loops

Flow-volume loops plot airflow (Y-axis) against volume (X-axis) during a complete forced expiration followed by forced inspiration. The shape of the loop reveals the location and nature of airway obstruction.

Understanding the Flow-Volume Loop

The top half (above X-axis) represents expiration; the bottom half represents inspiration. Peak expiratory flow (PEF) occurs early and is effort-dependent. The descending limb represents effort-independent flow limited by dynamic airway compression.

Normal

Sharp PEF with smooth, convex expiratory curve; symmetric inspiratory limb

Obstructive

"Scooped" concave expiratory limb; reduced PEF; prolonged expiration

Restrictive

Normal shape but narrower (reduced volumes); may be "tall and thin"

Fixed Obstruction

Flattening of BOTH inspiratory AND expiratory limbs (e.g., tracheal stenosis)

Variable Extrathoracic

Flattened inspiratory limb only (e.g., vocal cord dysfunction, laryngeal tumor)

Variable Intrathoracic

Flattened expiratory limb only (e.g., tracheomalacia)

Dynamic Airway Obstruction: Why Location Matters

🔬 Transmural Pressure and Airway Collapse

The location of an obstruction relative to the thorax determines when airflow is affected:

During Inspiration

  • Intrathoracic pressure becomes negative
  • This negative pressure holds intrathoracic airways open
  • But negative pressure pulls extrathoracic airways inward
  • → Variable extrathoracic obstruction worsens during inspiration

During Forced Expiration

  • Intrathoracic pressure becomes positive
  • This positive pressure compresses intrathoracic airways
  • But positive pressure pushes extrathoracic airways open
  • → Variable intrathoracic obstruction worsens during expiration
💡 Clinical Application: Stridor vs. Wheeze

Inspiratory stridor suggests extrathoracic obstruction (above thoracic inlet) - think EILO, croup, epiglottitis, foreign body in larynx.

Expiratory wheeze suggests intrathoracic obstruction (within thorax) - think asthma, COPD, bronchitis.

🔒 Part 5: Why Airways Obstruct - Mechanisms

Understanding WHY airways obstruct helps explain symptoms and guides treatment selection.

Components of Airway Obstruction

Mechanism What Happens Resulting Symptom Treatment Target
Bronchospasm Smooth muscle contraction narrows airway lumen Acute wheeze, chest tightness, dyspnea Bronchodilators (β₂-agonists, anticholinergics)
Airway Inflammation Edema of airway wall; inflammatory cell infiltration Increased reactivity, persistent symptoms Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS)
Mucus Hypersecretion Goblet cell hyperplasia; excess mucus production Productive cough, mucus plugging, chest congestion Mucolytics, hydration, airway clearance
Airway Remodeling Structural changes: fibrosis, smooth muscle hypertrophy Fixed obstruction (irreversible) Prevention (early ICS use)
Loss of Elastic Recoil Destruction of alveolar attachments (emphysema) Air trapping, hyperinflation, expiratory collapse Limited - lung volume reduction surgery

Airway Tethering and Collapse

Small airways (< 2mm) lack cartilage and depend on radial traction from surrounding alveolar attachments to stay open. This is called "airway tethering."

Normal Airways

  • Alveolar septa attach to airway walls
  • During inspiration, expanding alveoli pull airways open
  • During expiration, elastic recoil provides driving pressure AND maintains some tethering
  • Airways remain patent throughout breathing cycle

In Emphysema

  • Alveolar destruction → loss of tethering attachments
  • Loss of elastic recoil → reduced driving pressure for expiration
  • During forced expiration, positive pressure causes dynamic airway collapse
  • Air becomes trapped distal to collapsed airways
🔑 Why COPD Patients Use Pursed-Lip Breathing

Pursed-lip breathing creates back-pressure that stents small airways open during expiration, preventing dynamic collapse. This allows more complete exhalation and reduces air trapping. It's a natural compensatory mechanism that patients often develop unconsciously.

⚖️ Part 6: Asthma vs. COPD - The Critical Distinctions

While both are obstructive lung diseases, asthma and COPD have fundamentally different pathophysiology, cellular mediators, and treatment responses.

🟣 Asthma

  • Definition: Chronic inflammatory airway disease with variable, reversible airflow obstruction and bronchial hyperresponsiveness
  • Onset: Usually childhood/young adult
  • Etiology: Allergic (atopic) or non-allergic; genetic + environmental triggers
  • Inflammation: Type 2 (Th2) - eosinophilic predominant
  • Key cytokines: IL-4, IL-5, IL-13
  • Airway changes: Inflammation, bronchospasm, mucus; usually reversible
  • Reversibility: Typically significant (≥12% FEV₁ improvement)
  • DLCO: Normal
  • Response to ICS: Excellent

🟠 COPD

  • Definition: Persistent, progressive airflow limitation associated with enhanced chronic inflammatory response to noxious particles/gases
  • Onset: Usually > 40 years old
  • Etiology: Smoking (90%+), α1-antitrypsin deficiency, occupational/pollution exposure
  • Inflammation: Neutrophilic, CD8+ T cells, macrophages
  • Key cytokines: IL-8, TNF-α, LTB4
  • Airway changes: Small airway fibrosis + emphysema; largely irreversible
  • Reversibility: Minimal or absent
  • DLCO: Reduced (emphysema)
  • Response to ICS: Variable; best in eosinophilic COPD

Pathophysiology Deep Dive

🟣 Asthma: The Type 2 Inflammatory Cascade

Allergen exposure triggers a Th2-dominant immune response:

  1. Allergen exposure → Dendritic cell activation → Th2 cell differentiation
  2. IL-4 → B cell class switching → IgE production
  3. IgE binds mast cell FcεRI → sensitization
  4. Re-exposure → IgE cross-linking → mast cell degranulation
  5. Immediate phase: Histamine, leukotrienes, PGD₂ → bronchospasm (minutes)
  6. IL-5 → Eosinophil recruitment & activation
  7. Late phase: Eosinophilic inflammation → sustained symptoms (4-8 hours)
  8. IL-13 → Goblet cell metaplasia → mucus hypersecretion

🟠 COPD: Neutrophilic Inflammation and Tissue Destruction

Chronic irritant exposure triggers a self-perpetuating inflammatory cycle:

  1. Cigarette smoke/particles → Epithelial cell injury → Alarmin release
  2. Alveolar macrophage activation → IL-8, LTB4 → neutrophil recruitment
  3. Neutrophil degranulation → Neutrophil elastase, MMP-9
  4. Protease-antiprotease imbalance → Alveolar wall destruction (emphysema)
  5. Oxidative stress → Inactivates α1-antitrypsin → Accelerates destruction
  6. CD8+ T cells → Cytotoxic damage to alveoli
  7. Small airway fibrosis → Fixed airflow obstruction
  8. Mucus gland hypertrophy → Chronic bronchitis phenotype

Symptoms and Their Pathophysiologic Basis

Symptom Pathophysiologic Cause More Prominent In
Wheeze Turbulent airflow through narrowed airways (bronchospasm + inflammation) Both (more variable in asthma)
Chest tightness Bronchospasm activating vagal afferents; hyperinflation stretching chest wall Asthma (especially)
Dyspnea Increased work of breathing; dynamic hyperinflation; hypoxemia Both
Cough Airway irritant receptors; mucus stimulating cough reflex Both
Sputum production Goblet cell hyperplasia; mucus gland hypertrophy COPD (chronic bronchitis)
Nocturnal symptoms Circadian variation in airway tone; decreased cortisol overnight Asthma (classic)
Exercise intolerance Dynamic hyperinflation; ventilatory limitation; deconditioning COPD (progressive)
💡 Asthma-COPD Overlap (ACO)

~10-20% of patients have features of both diseases: they're smokers with asthma history, or have COPD with eosinophilic inflammation (blood eosinophils ≥300 cells/μL). These patients often have more exacerbations but respond better to ICS than "pure" COPD. Identifying eosinophilic phenotype guides therapy.

💊 Part 7: Respiratory Medications - Mechanism to Therapy

Each medication class targets a specific component of airway pathophysiology. Understanding the mechanism helps you predict efficacy and side effects.

Bronchodilators: Opening the Airways

SABA - Short-Acting β₂-Agonist
Albuterol (Salbutamol)
Target: Bronchospasm

Mechanism: Binds β₂-receptors on airway smooth muscle → ↑ cAMP → smooth muscle relaxation → bronchodilation within 5 minutes. Duration 4-6 hours.

Use: Rescue inhaler; pre-exercise in EIB

LABA - Long-Acting β₂-Agonist
Salmeterol, Formoterol
Target: Sustained bronchospasm

Mechanism: Same as SABA but lipophilic side chain allows prolonged receptor binding. Formoterol: fast onset + long duration (12h). Salmeterol: slow onset + long duration.

Use: NEVER as monotherapy in asthma (↑ death risk). Always combine with ICS.

SAMA - Short-Acting Muscarinic Antagonist
Ipratropium
Target: Vagal-mediated bronchoconstriction

Mechanism: Blocks M3 muscarinic receptors → prevents acetylcholine-induced bronchoconstriction. Onset 15-30 min, duration 4-8 hours.

Use: COPD; acute asthma exacerbation (with SABA)

LAMA - Long-Acting Muscarinic Antagonist
Tiotropium, Umeclidinium
Target: Sustained vagal tone

Mechanism: Prolonged M3 blockade (24h duration). Also reduces mucus secretion. Dissociates slowly from receptor.

Use: COPD maintenance; add-on in uncontrolled asthma

Anti-Inflammatory Agents: Treating the Root Cause

ICS - Inhaled Corticosteroid
Fluticasone, Budesonide, Beclomethasone
Target: Airway inflammation (especially eosinophilic)

Mechanism: Enters cells → binds glucocorticoid receptor → translocates to nucleus → ↓ inflammatory gene transcription (cytokines, chemokines, adhesion molecules). ↑ anti-inflammatory proteins (lipocortin-1). Takes 1-4 weeks for full effect.

Use: CORNERSTONE of asthma therapy. In COPD: for eosinophilic phenotype or frequent exacerbations.

LTRA - Leukotriene Receptor Antagonist
Montelukast, Zafirlukast
Target: Leukotriene-mediated inflammation

Mechanism: Blocks CysLT1 receptor → prevents leukotriene (LTC4, LTD4, LTE4) effects: bronchospasm, mucus secretion, eosinophil recruitment, vascular leak.

Use: Asthma add-on; especially in aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD) and EIB. FDA neuropsychiatric warning (monitor for behavioral changes).

Biologic - Anti-IgE
Omalizumab
Target: IgE-mediated allergic inflammation

Mechanism: Monoclonal antibody binds free IgE → prevents IgE binding to mast cells/basophils → reduces degranulation and allergic response.

Use: Moderate-severe allergic asthma uncontrolled on ICS-LABA. Requires positive allergen sensitivity and elevated IgE.

Biologic - Anti-IL-5/IL-5R
Mepolizumab, Benralizumab
Target: Eosinophilic inflammation

Mechanism: Mepolizumab binds IL-5 → ↓ eosinophil survival/activation. Benralizumab binds IL-5 receptor → directly depletes eosinophils (ADCC).

Use: Severe eosinophilic asthma; now also approved for eosinophilic COPD (mepolizumab).

Biologic - Anti-IL-4/IL-13
Dupilumab
Target: Type 2 inflammation broadly

Mechanism: Blocks IL-4Rα subunit (shared by IL-4 and IL-13 receptors) → inhibits both IL-4 and IL-13 signaling → ↓ IgE production, ↓ eosinophil recruitment, ↓ mucus secretion.

Use: Moderate-severe eosinophilic asthma OR steroid-dependent asthma. Also approved for atopic dermatitis, nasal polyps.

ICS+LABA Combinations: Why They Work Together

💡 The ICS-LABA Synergy

ICS enhances LABA: Corticosteroids upregulate β₂-receptor expression, preventing tachyphylaxis (tolerance) to LABAs.

LABA enhances ICS: β₂-agonists activate glucocorticoid receptors via cAMP-dependent phosphorylation, increasing corticosteroid sensitivity.

This bidirectional synergy makes ICS-LABA combinations more effective than either drug alone, even at higher doses. Common combinations: Fluticasone-salmeterol (Advair), Budesonide-formoterol (Symbicort), Fluticasone-vilanterol (Breo).

📋 Part 8: Treatment Algorithms

Asthma: GINA Stepwise Approach (2024)

1
Step 1: Mild Intermittent

Preferred: As-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol (SMART approach)
Alternative: As-needed SABA + ICS taken together
No longer recommended: SABA-only treatment

2
Step 2: Mild Persistent

Preferred: Daily low-dose ICS + as-needed SABA, OR as-needed low-dose ICS-formoterol
Alternative: Daily LTRA (less effective)

3
Step 3: Moderate Persistent

Preferred: Low-dose ICS-LABA maintenance + as-needed ICS-formoterol (SMART)
Alternative: Medium-dose ICS + as-needed SABA

4
Step 4: Severe Persistent

Preferred: Medium-dose ICS-LABA + as-needed ICS-formoterol
Consider adding: LAMA (tiotropium)

5
Step 5: Severe Refractory

Add: Biologic therapy based on phenotype:
• Allergic: Omalizumab (anti-IgE)
• Eosinophilic: Mepolizumab, Benralizumab (anti-IL-5), Dupilumab (anti-IL-4Rα)
• TSLP-high: Tezepelumab (anti-TSLP)
Referral: Specialist for phenotyping, bronchial thermoplasty consideration

COPD: GOLD 2024 Algorithm

A
Group A: Low symptoms, low exacerbation risk

mMRC 0-1, CAT <10, ≤1 moderate exacerbation/year
Treatment: Bronchodilator (SABA, SAMA, or LAMA or LABA)

B
Group B: More symptoms, low exacerbation risk

mMRC ≥2, CAT ≥10, ≤1 moderate exacerbation/year
Treatment: LAMA + LABA (dual bronchodilation)

E
Group E: High exacerbation risk (any symptoms)

≥2 moderate or ≥1 severe (hospitalization) exacerbation/year
Treatment: LAMA + LABA
If blood eosinophils ≥300: Consider adding ICS (triple therapy)
If still exacerbating: Consider adding roflumilast, azithromycin, or biologic (mepolizumab if eos ≥300)

🔑 When to Add ICS in COPD

ICS is NOT automatically added in COPD (unlike asthma). Add ICS when:
• Blood eosinophils ≥300 cells/μL (strong indication)
• Blood eosinophils 100-300 with frequent exacerbations (consider)
• History of asthma/ACO overlap

Avoid ICS if history of pneumonia or eosinophils <100 (increased pneumonia risk without benefit).
