MECHANISM
Classification

Bipolar disorder involves pathological mood episodes: mania, hypomania, and depression. Neurobiology involves dysregulated dopamine, serotonin, and glutamate signaling with altered prefrontal-limbic circuitry.

TypeDefining FeatureKey Points
Bipolar I≥1 manic episodeMania is sufficient for diagnosis. Depressive episodes common but NOT required. Most severe form
Bipolar II≥1 hypomanic + ≥1 major depressive episodeNo history of full mania. NOT "milder bipolar I" — high suicide risk and functional impairment from depression
Cyclothymic≥2 years of hypomanic + depressive symptoms (not meeting full criteria)Chronic mood fluctuation. Risk of converting to Bipolar I or II

Mania vs Hypomania

FeatureManiaHypomania
Duration≥7 days (or hospitalization)≥4 consecutive days
FunctioningMARKED impairment, hospitalization, psychosisNo marked impairment, no psychosis
Psychotic featuresCan be present (grandiose delusions)ABSENT by definition
CLINICAL
Manic Episode
🔑 DIG FAST Mnemonic for Mania (≥3 criteria + elevated/irritable mood for ≥7 days):
Distractibility
Indiscretion / Impulsivity (spending sprees, sexual indiscretions)
Grandiosity (inflated self-esteem)
Flight of ideas / racing thoughts
Activity increase (goal-directed activity or psychomotor agitation)
Sleep decreased (reduced need — feels rested after 2-3 hours)
Talkativeness (pressured speech)

Mixed Features

DSM-5 specifier: mania with simultaneous depressive symptoms (or vice versa). High suicide risk — the impulsivity of mania combined with the hopelessness of depression is dangerous.

Rapid Cycling

≥4 mood episodes per year. More common in Bipolar II, women, and hypothyroidism. Associated with worse prognosis. Antidepressants can trigger rapid cycling.

⚠️ Critical Pearl: Bipolar depression is frequently misdiagnosed as unipolar MDD. Antidepressant monotherapy in bipolar disorder can trigger mania, rapid cycling, or mixed episodes. ALWAYS screen for mania history before prescribing antidepressants. Average delay to correct bipolar diagnosis: 8-10 years.
TREATMENT
Pharmacotherapy

Acute Mania

AgentKey Points
LithiumGold standard mood stabilizer. Anti-suicidal properties (ONLY psychiatric drug proven to reduce suicide). Narrow therapeutic index (0.6-1.2 mEq/L). Toxicity: tremor, polyuria, hypothyroidism, nephrogenic DI, Ebstein anomaly in pregnancy. Monitor: lithium levels, renal function, TSH
Valproate (Divalproex)Effective for acute mania, mixed episodes, rapid cycling. Side effects: weight gain, tremor, hepatotoxicity, pancreatitis, thrombocytopenia. TERATOGENIC (neural tube defects — absolutely avoid in pregnancy/women of childbearing age)
Atypical antipsychoticsOlanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole, risperidone. Effective for acute mania. Faster onset than lithium. Metabolic side effects
CarbamazepineAlternative mood stabilizer. Many drug interactions (CYP3A4 inducer). Risk of aplastic anemia, DRESS/SJS (screen HLA-B*1502 in Asian patients)

Bipolar Depression

🔑 Treatment differs from unipolar MDD: First-line: quetiapine, lurasidone, lamotrigine, olanzapine-fluoxetine combination (OFC). Lithium also effective. If antidepressant needed, ALWAYS combine with mood stabilizer. Lamotrigine is particularly effective for bipolar depression prevention but NOT for acute mania. Titrate slowly (risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome).

Maintenance

Lithium (best evidence for suicide prevention), valproate, lamotrigine (better for depression prevention), or atypical antipsychotics. Lifelong treatment recommended after 2+ episodes.
