Electrolyte Disorders

Sodium, Potassium, Calcium, Magnesium, Phosphate

🧂 Sodium Disorders

Sodium disorders are really water disorders. Hyponatremia = too much water relative to sodium. Hypernatremia = too little water relative to sodium.

📉 Hyponatremia (Na <135 mEq/L)

Most common electrolyte disorder. First step: Check serum osmolality to classify.

Step 1: Serum Osmolality

OsmolalityCategoryCauses
>295 (High)HypertonicHyperglycemia, mannitol (dilutional — water shifts to ECF)
280-295 (Normal)IsotonicPseudohyponatremia: hyperlipidemia, paraproteinemia
<280 (Low)HypotonicTRUE hyponatremia — proceed to volume status

Step 2: Volume Status (for Hypotonic)

Hypovolemic (↓ TBW, ↓↓ Na)
  • Renal loss (UNa >20): Diuretics, salt-wasting nephropathy, adrenal insufficiency
  • Extrarenal (UNa <20): Vomiting, diarrhea, burns, third-spacing
Euvolemic (↑ TBW, normal Na)
  • SIADH (most common)
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Adrenal insufficiency
  • Psychogenic polydipsia
Hypervolemic (↑↑ TBW, ↑ Na)
  • CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome (edematous states — dilutional)
SIADH Criteria: Hypotonic hyponatremia + euvolemia + UOsm >100 + UNa >40 + normal thyroid/adrenal function. Common causes: CNS disease, lung disease, drugs (SSRIs, carbamazepine), malignancy.

Treatment Principles

⚠️ Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome (ODS)

  • Caused by correcting sodium TOO FAST in chronic hyponatremia
  • Limit: ≤8-10 mEq/L per 24 hours (some say ≤6-8 if high risk)
  • High risk: Na <105, alcoholism, malnutrition, hypokalemia, liver disease
  • Presents 2-6 days later: dysarthria, dysphagia, quadriparesis, "locked-in"
ScenarioTreatment
Severe symptoms (seizures, coma)Hypertonic saline (3%) — raise Na 4-6 mEq/L in first few hours
HypovolemicNormal saline (volume repletion)
SIADHFluid restriction, salt tabs ± loop diuretic; tolvaptan if refractory
HypervolemicFluid + sodium restriction, diuretics, treat underlying cause

📈 Hypernatremia (Na >145 mEq/L)

Always hyperosmolar. Usually a water deficit problem — patient couldn't access or drink water.

Causes

  • Inadequate water intake: Altered mental status, intubated, elderly, infants
  • Water loss: Diabetes insipidus (central or nephrogenic), osmotic diuresis, diarrhea, burns
  • Sodium gain: Hypertonic saline, sodium bicarbonate (iatrogenic)

Treatment

Water deficit = TBW × [(Na/140) - 1]
TBW = weight(kg) × 0.6 (men) or 0.5 (women)
  • Replace free water (D5W or oral water)
  • Correct slowly: ≤10-12 mEq/L per 24 hours to avoid cerebral edema
  • If hypovolemic: Start with NS to restore volume, then switch to hypotonic

⚡ Potassium Disorders

Potassium is the major intracellular cation. Small changes in serum K+ can cause fatal arrhythmias. Always check K+ with acid-base status (acidosis shifts K+ out of cells).

📉 Hypokalemia (K <3.5 mEq/L)

Causes

Transcellular Shift (into cells)
  • Alkalosis
  • Insulin
  • Beta-agonists
  • Refeeding syndrome
True Deficit
  • GI loss: Diarrhea, vomiting, NG suction
  • Renal loss: Diuretics, hyperaldosteronism, RTA, hypomagnesemia

Clinical Features

  • Muscle: Weakness, cramps, rhabdomyolysis (severe)
  • Cardiac: U waves, flattened T waves, prolonged QT, arrhythmias
  • GI: Ileus, constipation
Hypomagnesemia: Always check Mg in refractory hypokalemia! Low Mg causes renal K+ wasting. Must replace Mg first or K+ won't correct.

📈 Hyperkalemia (K >5.0 mEq/L)

Medical emergency when severe. Can cause fatal arrhythmias without warning.

Causes

🧠 "MACHINE"

Medications (ACEi, ARBs, K-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs) • Acidosis • Cellular destruction (hemolysis, rhabdo, tumor lysis) • Hypoaldosteronism • Intake excess • Nephropathy (renal failure) • Excretion impaired

ECG Changes (Progressive)

  1. Peaked T waves (earliest)
  2. Prolonged PR interval
  3. Flattened P waves
  4. Widened QRS
  5. Sine wave → VFib/asystole

⚠️ Hyperkalemia Treatment — In Order!

  • 1. Stabilize membrane: Calcium gluconate (doesn't lower K+, protects heart)
  • 2. Shift K+ into cells: Insulin + glucose, beta-agonists (albuterol), bicarbonate (if acidotic)
  • 3. Remove K+ from body: Loop diuretics, kayexalate/patiromer, hemodialysis

🦴 Calcium Disorders

Always check albumin-corrected calcium or ionized calcium. For every 1 g/dL drop in albumin below 4, add 0.8 to total calcium.

Corrected Ca = Measured Ca + 0.8 × (4 - albumin)

📉 Hypocalcemia (Corrected Ca <8.5 mg/dL)

Causes

  • Hypoparathyroidism: Post-thyroidectomy (#1), autoimmune, DiGeorge
  • Vitamin D deficiency: Malabsorption, CKD, liver disease
  • Hyperphosphatemia: CKD, tumor lysis, rhabdomyolysis
  • Acute pancreatitis: Calcium saponification
  • Hypomagnesemia: Impairs PTH secretion and action

Clinical Features

🧠 "CATs go Numb"

Convulsions • Arrhythmias (prolonged QT) • Tetany • Numbness/paresthesias

  • Chvostek sign: Facial twitch when tapping facial nerve
  • Trousseau sign: Carpal spasm with BP cuff inflation (more specific)

📈 Hypercalcemia (Ca >10.5 mg/dL)

Causes — 90% are Hyperparathyroidism or Malignancy

🧠 "Stones, Bones, Groans, Psychiatric Overtones"

Kidney stones • Bone pain (osteitis fibrosa) • Abdominal pain, constipation, pancreatitis • Confusion, depression, fatigue

CausePTHFeatures
Primary hyperparathyroidism↑ or inappropriately normalOutpatient, usually mild, adenoma
Malignancy (PTHrP, mets, myeloma)↓ (suppressed)Inpatient, usually severe, sick patient

Treatment

  • IV fluids (NS): First-line — dilute calcium, promote calciuresis
  • Loop diuretics: After volume repletion (furosemide promotes Ca excretion)
  • Bisphosphonates: Zoledronic acid — for malignancy-associated (takes 2-4 days)
  • Calcitonin: Rapid but transient effect (tachyphylaxis)
  • Dialysis: If severe/refractory or renal failure

✨ Magnesium Disorders

📉 Hypomagnesemia (Mg <1.7 mg/dL)

Often overlooked but causes refractory hypokalemia and hypocalcemia!

Causes

  • GI loss: Diarrhea, malabsorption, PPI use (chronic)
  • Renal loss: Diuretics, alcohol, aminoglycosides, cisplatin, amphotericin
  • Other: DKA, refeeding syndrome
The "Refractory" Electrolyte: If hypokalemia or hypocalcemia doesn't correct with replacement, CHECK MAGNESIUM. Must fix Mg first!

Clinical: Similar to hypocalcemia

  • Neuromuscular: Tremor, tetany, seizures
  • Cardiac: Prolonged QT, torsades de pointes, arrhythmias

📈 Hypermagnesemia (Mg >2.5 mg/dL)

Rare — almost always iatrogenic (Mg-containing antacids/laxatives in renal failure) or obstetric (Mg for eclampsia).

Clinical (Progressive)

  • Loss of deep tendon reflexes (early warning sign)
  • Hypotension, bradycardia
  • Respiratory depression
  • Cardiac arrest

Treatment

  • Stop Mg-containing products
  • Calcium gluconate — antagonizes Mg effects
  • IV fluids + loop diuretics
  • Dialysis if severe/renal failure

🔬 Phosphate Disorders

📉 Hypophosphatemia (PO4 <2.5 mg/dL)

Causes

  • Refeeding syndrome: Classic! Insulin drives PO4 into cells
  • Respiratory alkalosis: Shifts PO4 intracellularly
  • DKA treatment: Insulin effect
  • Alcohol withdrawal
  • Hyperparathyroidism: Increased renal excretion

Clinical (Severe <1.0)

  • Weakness, rhabdomyolysis
  • Respiratory failure (diaphragm weakness)
  • Hemolysis (ATP depletion in RBCs)
  • Confusion, seizures

📈 Hyperphosphatemia (PO4 >4.5 mg/dL)

Causes

  • Renal failure: Most common (decreased excretion)
  • Tumor lysis syndrome: Cell lysis releases PO4
  • Rhabdomyolysis: Same mechanism
  • Hypoparathyroidism: Decreased renal excretion

Clinical

Main concern: Binds calcium → hypocalcemia → tetany, arrhythmias. Calcium-phosphate deposition in tissues.

Treatment

  • Dietary phosphate restriction
  • Phosphate binders (calcium acetate, sevelamer, lanthanum)
  • Dialysis if severe/renal failure
