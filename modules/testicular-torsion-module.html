🚨 Testicular Torsion

A Urologic Emergency - Time is Testicle!

⚠️ SURGICAL EMERGENCY - Salvage rate drops dramatically after 6 hours ⚠️

📚 Overview

Testicular torsion is the twisting of the spermatic cord, leading to venous then arterial occlusion and testicular ischemia. It is a clinical diagnosis that requires emergent surgical exploration.

Epidemiology

ParameterValue
Incidence1 in 4,000 males under 25
Peak agesNeonatal and 12-18 years (puberty)
LateralityLeft slightly more common
Bilateral (synchronous)Rare (~2%)

🧬 Anatomy & Types

Normal Anatomy

Normally, the tunica vaginalis attaches to the posterolateral testis, anchoring it and preventing rotation. The "bell clapper" deformity allows torsion when this attachment is abnormally high.

Types of Testicular Torsion

TypeDescriptionAge Group
IntravaginalTorsion within tunica vaginalis; "bell clapper" deformityAdolescents/adults (most common overall)
ExtravaginalEntire cord twists above tunica vaginalis attachmentNeonates (tunica not yet fixed)

Bell Clapper Deformity

NORMAL ATTACHMENT: Tunica vaginalis attaches posterolaterally → Testis anchored, limited mobility → Low torsion risk BELL CLAPPER DEFORMITY: Tunica vaginalis envelopes entire testis → Testis hangs freely like a "bell clapper" → Can rotate freely on spermatic cord → HIGH torsion risk Present bilaterally in 80%! → ALWAYS fix contralateral testis

⏱️ Time is Testicle!

⏰ SALVAGE RATES BY DURATION

Time from OnsetSalvage Rate
<6 hours90-100%
6-12 hours50%
12-24 hours10-20%
>24 hoursNear 0%
⚠️ BOTTOM LINE: If torsion is suspected, go to the OR. Do NOT delay for imaging if clinical suspicion is high. A negative exploration is acceptable; a missed torsion is not.

🔍 Clinical Presentation

Classic Symptoms

  • Sudden onset severe testicular pain
  • Often wakes patient from sleep
  • May have prior episodes of intermittent torsion/detorsion
  • Nausea and vomiting (common)
  • Lower abdominal pain (referred)
  • NO urinary symptoms typically

Physical Examination

FindingDescription
High-riding testisTestis pulled up due to cord shortening from twist
Transverse lieLong axis horizontal instead of vertical
Absent cremasteric reflexMost sensitive sign (~99%)
Diffuse tendernessEntire testis tender (vs epididymitis = posterior tenderness)
Swelling, erythemaDevelops over time
Prehn Sign: Elevation of scrotum DOES NOT relieve pain in torsion (but may in epididymitis). However, this sign is unreliable - don't use it to rule out torsion!

🔬 Differential Diagnosis

ConditionKey Distinguishing Features
Testicular torsionSudden onset, absent cremasteric reflex, high-riding testis
Torsion of appendix testis"Blue dot sign," point tenderness at upper pole, cremasteric reflex intact
EpididymitisGradual onset, dysuria/pyuria, posterior tenderness, cremasteric reflex intact
OrchitisOften viral (mumps), systemic symptoms, bilateral possible
Incarcerated herniaInguinal mass, bowel symptoms
TraumaHistory of injury
Testicular tumorUsually painless mass, gradual onset
Torsion of Appendix Testis:
  • Most common cause of acute scrotum in prepubertal boys
  • "Blue dot sign" = infarcted appendage visible through skin
  • Self-limited; managed conservatively (NSAIDs, rest)
  • If diagnosis certain, no surgery needed

📷 Imaging

Doppler Ultrasound

Role of imaging: If clinical diagnosis is clear and suspicion is HIGH, go directly to OR. Ultrasound is useful when diagnosis is uncertain but should NOT delay surgery if torsion is likely.
US FindingInterpretation
Absent/decreased blood flowStrongly suggests torsion
Whirlpool signTwisted spermatic cord
Increased blood flowMore consistent with epididymitis/orchitis
Reactive hydroceleNon-specific
⚠️ Limitations of Ultrasound:
  • Sensitivity 82-100%, specificity 97-100%
  • Early or intermittent torsion may have preserved flow
  • Operator dependent
  • Small/prepubertal testis may be difficult to assess
If clinical suspicion is high, explore regardless of US findings!

🔪 Management

Surgical Exploration (Definitive)

Standard of Care: Emergent scrotal exploration
  1. Scrotal incision
  2. Detorsion of testis
  3. Assess viability (color, bleeding, Doppler)
  4. If viable → bilateral orchiopexy (3-point fixation)
  5. If non-viable → orchiectomy + contralateral orchiopexy
Why Bilateral Orchiopexy?
Bell clapper deformity is bilateral in ~80% of cases. Always fix the contralateral testis to prevent future torsion!

Manual Detorsion (Temporizing)

"Open the book" technique:
  • Rotate testis outward (lateral) like opening a book
  • Right testis: rotate counterclockwise
  • Left testis: rotate clockwise
  • May require 1-3 full rotations
  • Success indicated by pain relief and cord lengthening
Still requires surgical exploration and fixation!

Neonatal Torsion

  • Usually extravaginal
  • Often prenatal; testis usually non-viable at birth
  • Management controversial (explore vs observe)
  • Contralateral fixation recommended
  • Salvage rate very low if prenatal

📋 Key Clinical Pearls

⚠️ NEVER Miss Torsion:
  • Absent cremasteric reflex = most sensitive sign
  • Don't rely on Prehn sign
  • If in doubt, explore
  • A negative exploration is acceptable
History Tips:
  • Prior similar episodes (intermittent torsion)
  • Activity at onset (though can occur at rest/sleep)
  • Duration of symptoms (critical for prognosis)
Summary:
  • <6 hours = 90%+ salvage
  • Clinical diagnosis → OR, not imaging
  • Always bilateral orchiopexy
  • Absent cremasteric reflex is key finding

🧠 Quick Quiz

A 14-year-old presents with 3 hours of sudden left testicular pain, nausea, and a high-riding left testis. Cremasteric reflex is absent. What is the next step?

A. Doppler ultrasound
B. Emergent scrotal exploration
C. Trial of antibiotics for epididymitis
D. Urinalysis and urine culture
Correct: B - Emergent scrotal exploration
This presentation (sudden onset, high-riding testis, absent cremasteric reflex) is classic for testicular torsion. With only 3 hours of symptoms, salvage rate is >90% with immediate surgery. Do NOT delay for imaging when clinical suspicion is high. Go directly to OR.
