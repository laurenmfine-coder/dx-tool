🔬 Understanding the Embryology

💡 Midgut Rotation - Normal Development

During weeks 6-10, the midgut herniates into the umbilical cord, rotates 270° counterclockwise around the SMA, and returns to the abdomen. The duodenojejunal junction (ligament of Treitz) ends up in the left upper quadrant, and the cecum ends up in the right lower quadrant.

In malrotation, the gut fails to rotate properly. Why does this lead to volvulus?

📍
The cecum is in the wrong position
Position alone causes problems
🔗
Narrow mesenteric base allows the entire midgut to twist
SMA becomes the axis of rotation → ischemia
🧬
Neural crest cells fail to migrate
Like Hirschsprung disease
🫁
The diaphragm doesn't form properly
Bowel herniates into chest

💡 Key UGI Findings in Malrotation

Normal: DJ junction (ligament of Treitz) is LEFT of spine, at level of pylorus

Malrotation: DJ junction is RIGHT of spine or midline

Volvulus: "Corkscrew" or "bird's beak" appearance from twisted bowel

🔑 Key Learning Points

  • Bilious vomiting in newborn = surgical emergency until proven otherwise
  • Malrotation: Failure of 270° CCW rotation during weeks 6-10
  • Narrow mesenteric base allows entire midgut to twist around SMA
  • UGI is gold standard: Shows DJ junction position + corkscrew sign
  • Ladd procedure: Reduce volvulus, lyse bands, widen mesentery, appendectomy
  • Bloody stool = ischemia: Indicates vascular compromise, increases urgency

📚 Embryology Connection

Malrotation occurs when the midgut fails to complete its normal 270° counterclockwise rotation around the SMA. The result is a narrow mesenteric pedicle that can twist (volvulus), cutting off blood supply to the entire midgut (duodenum to mid-transverse colon).
