Teaching Point

Hirschsprung disease: Absence of ganglion cells (from the myenteric/Auerbach and submucosal/Meissner plexuses) in a segment of the colon. The aganglionic segment cannot relax → functional obstruction. Incidence: ~1/5000 live births, M:F = 4:1. The aganglionic segment is ALWAYS contiguous from the rectum proximally (it never 'skips'). In 80% of cases, the rectosigmoid is affected (short-segment). In 5-10%, the entire colon is aganglionic (total colonic aganglionosis).

Teaching Point

Enteric nervous system development: Vagal neural crest cells (from rhombomeres 1-7) enter the foregut and migrate as a wavefront through the entire GI tract, colonizing it cranial → caudal over weeks 5-12. They differentiate into the neurons and glia of the myenteric (Auerbach's) and submucosal (Meissner's) plexuses. These plexuses control peristalsis, secretion, and blood flow. Key signaling: GDNF/RET (same pathway as kidney development!) + Endothelin-3/EDNRB + SOX10.

Teaching Point

RET in Hirschsprung: Loss-of-function mutations account for ~50% of familial and ~15-20% of sporadic Hirschsprung cases. Other genes: EDNRB/EDN3 (endothelin signaling — ~5%), SOX10 (Waardenburg-Shah syndrome + Hirschsprung), PHOX2B. Hirschsprung associations: Down syndrome (T21) is the most common chromosomal association (~2-10% of Hirschsprung patients have T21). Also: Waardenburg syndrome (SOX10), MEN2A (rare co-occurrence), congenital central hypoventilation (PHOX2B).

Teaching Point

Diagnostic workup: (1) CONTRAST ENEMA: shows transition zone (narrow aganglionic segment → dilated normal bowel). The transition zone may not be visible in total colonic aganglionosis or in neonates. (2) ANORECTAL MANOMETRY: absent rectoanal inhibitory reflex (RAIR) — normally, rectal distension causes internal anal sphincter relaxation. In Hirschsprung, the aganglionic sphincter cannot relax. (3) SUCTION RECTAL BIOPSY (gold standard): absent ganglion cells + hypertrophied nerve trunks + increased AChE staining. Calretinin staining (absent in Hirschsprung) is increasingly used.

Teaching Point

Hirschsprung complications: (1) HIRSCHSPRUNG-ASSOCIATED ENTEROCOLITIS (HAEC) — most dangerous complication (~20-30%). Presents with explosive diarrhea, abdominal distension, fever, sepsis. Can occur pre- or post-operatively. Thought to be from stasis → bacterial overgrowth → mucosal inflammation. Treatment: rectal irrigations + IV antibiotics. (2) Post-operative: soiling/incontinence (~10-20%), constipation, stricture, enterocolitis. Long-term outcomes are generally good for short-segment disease.

Stage 6 of 6
🎓 Integration & Key Takeaways
The baby undergoes a successful transanal pull-through at 3 months of age. At 2 years, he has regular bowel movements with occasional soiling managed with dietary adjustments.

Key Takeaways

✓Hirschsprung: absent ganglion cells (Auerbach + Meissner plexuses) from failed neural crest migration
✓Vagal neural crest cells migrate cranial → caudal (weeks 5-12); rectum colonized LAST
✓Rectosigmoid affected in 80% (short-segment); always contiguous from the anus proximally
✓RET: loss-of-function → Hirschsprung; gain-of-function → MEN2A/2B (same gene!)
✓Diagnosis: suction rectal biopsy (gold standard) — absent ganglion cells + hypertrophied nerves
✓Contrast enema: transition zone; Manometry: absent RAIR (rectoanal inhibitory reflex)
✓Treatment: pull-through surgery (Swenson/Duhamel/Soave) — resect aganglionic segment
✓Down syndrome (T21) is the most common chromosomal association
