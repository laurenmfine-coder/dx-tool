👶 Cleft Lip & Palate

Embryology, Classification, Multidisciplinary Care & Surgical Timing

📚 Core Definition & Epidemiology

Cleft Lip and/or Palate (CL/P) = Failure of fusion of facial processes during embryonic development, resulting in a gap in the upper lip, palate, or both

Epidemiology:

  • Incidence: 1 in 500-2500 live births (varies by ethnicity)
  • One of the most common congenital craniofacial anomalies
  • Cleft lip ± palate: Male > Female (2:1)
  • Isolated cleft palate: Female > Male
  • Distribution: 46% have both CL and CP, 33% have CP alone, 21% have CL alone

Racial/Ethnic Variation:

PopulationIncidence (per 1,000)
Native American/Asian1.5 - 4.0 (highest)
Caucasian0.9 - 2.7
African descent0.2 - 1.7 (lowest)
Clinical Pearl: Unilateral cleft lip occurs on the left side twice as often as the right (Left:Right:Bilateral = 6:3:1)

🧬 Embryology: Why Clefts Happen

Normal Facial Development Timeline:

Critical Period: Weeks 4-12 of gestation
Facial development is complete by week 12. Any disruption during this window can result in clefting.
Week 4: Five facial prominences surround the developing mouth: frontonasal prominence (1), paired maxillary prominences (2), paired mandibular prominences (2)
Week 5: Nasal placodes appear; frontonasal prominence divides into medial and lateral nasal processes
Week 6: Upper lip forms - Medial nasal processes fuse with maxillary and lateral nasal processes. Failure here → Cleft Lip
Week 6-7: Primary palate forms - Medial nasal processes fuse in midline to create intermaxillary segment (philtrum + primary palate anterior to incisive foramen)
Week 8-9: Palatal shelves elevate from vertical to horizontal as tongue descends; shelves grow toward midline
Week 9-10: Secondary palate fuses - Palatal shelves fuse with each other and nasal septum in "zipper" fashion (anterior → posterior). Failure here → Cleft Palate
Week 12: Palatal fusion complete; oral and nasal cavities now separate

Why Fusion Can Fail:
  • Inadequate growth of facial processes
  • Failure of palatal shelf elevation (tongue obstruction)
  • Failure of epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition at fusion site
  • Premature ossification preventing midline fusion
  • Genetic mutations affecting growth factors (TGF-β, BMP, FGF, SHH pathways)

Memorize: Lip forms during Week 6, Palate forms during Weeks 8-12. Cleft lip and cleft palate are embryologically distinct events!

📋 Classification Systems

Anatomical Classification:

By Structure Involved

  • Cleft Lip Only (CL) - 21%
  • Cleft Palate Only (CP) - 33%
  • Cleft Lip + Palate (CLP) - 46%

By Laterality

  • Unilateral - One side affected (80%)
  • Bilateral - Both sides affected (20%)
  • Left side more common than right

Cleft Lip Severity:

TypeDescription
Incomplete (Partial)Cleft does not extend to the nasal floor; nasal sill intact
CompleteCleft extends through entire lip into nasal floor; nasal deformity present
MicroformMinor notch in vermilion border; "forme fruste"

Cleft Palate Classification:

TypeDescription
Primary PalateAnterior to incisive foramen (includes alveolus)
Secondary PalatePosterior to incisive foramen (hard and soft palate)
CompleteInvolves both primary and secondary palate
IncompleteInvolves only part of the palate
SubmucousMuscle/bone defect covered by intact mucosa (occult)
Submucous Cleft Palate Triad:
  1. Bifid uvula
  2. Zona pellucida (bluish midline streak - thin mucosa)
  3. Notch in posterior hard palate

LAHSHAL Classification (International Standard):

Written from examiner's perspective looking at patient:

Lip (right) - Alveolus (right) - Hard palate - Soft palate - Hard palate - Alveolus (left) - Lip (left)

Uppercase = Complete cleft | Lowercase = Incomplete cleft

Example: LAHSAL = Complete left-sided unilateral cleft lip, alveolus, and palate

🧩 Syndromic Associations

Rule Out Syndromes: Up to 50% of isolated cleft palates and 30% of CL/P cases are syndromic. Always perform a thorough genetic evaluation!

Pierre Robin Sequence (PRS)

Triad: Micrognathia → Glossoptosis → Airway obstruction (± Cleft palate)

Mechanism: Small mandible fails to descend properly, tongue blocks palatal fusion

Key points: U-shaped cleft palate; 60% are syndromic (Stickler, VCF most common)

Van der Woude Syndrome

Gene: IRF6 mutation (chromosome 1q32-q41)

Features: CL/P + Lower lip pits (pathognomonic)

Inheritance: Autosomal dominant

Note: Most common syndromic cause of CL/P

Velocardiofacial Syndrome (22q11.2 Deletion / DiGeorge)

Gene: 22q11.2 microdeletion (TBX1)

Features: CATCH-22 - Cardiac defects, Abnormal facies, Thymic hypoplasia, Cleft palate, Hypocalcemia

Key points: 10% of isolated CP have 22q11.2 deletion; velopharyngeal insufficiency common

Stickler Syndrome

Gene: COL2A1, COL11A1, COL11A2 mutations

Features: CP + Severe myopia + Hearing loss + Joint hypermobility + Midface hypoplasia

Warning: High risk of retinal detachment - ophthalmology eval required in first year

Treacher Collins Syndrome

Gene: TCOF1, POLR1C, POLR1D mutations

Features: Bilateral mandibular/zygomatic hypoplasia + CP + External ear anomalies + Downslanting palpebral fissures

Inheritance: Autosomal dominant (60% de novo)

Clinical Pearl: Lip pits = Van der Woude. Cardiac defect + CP = Think 22q11.2. Severe myopia + CP = Stickler (urgent eye exam needed!)

👥 The Multidisciplinary Cleft Team

Standard of Care: Multidisciplinary team approach from birth through adulthood. No single specialist can address all needs.

🩺 Plastic/Craniofacial Surgeon

Lip repair, palate repair, rhinoplasty, revision surgeries

👂 Otolaryngologist (ENT)

PE tubes, hearing management, velopharyngeal surgery

🗣️ Speech-Language Pathologist

Speech assessment, VPI evaluation, therapy

🍼 Feeding Specialist

Specialized bottles, breastfeeding support, nutrition

🦷 Orthodontist

Presurgical molding, alignment, palatal expansion

🦷 Oral/Maxillofacial Surgeon

Alveolar bone grafting, orthognathic surgery

🧬 Geneticist

Syndrome diagnosis, recurrence risk counseling

🧠 Psychologist/Social Worker

Psychosocial support, bullying intervention, adjustment

👶 Pediatrician

Primary care coordination, growth monitoring

👩‍⚕️ Cleft Nurse Coordinator

Family liaison, care coordination, education

Why Multidisciplinary Care Matters:

  • Feeding: Nasal regurgitation, poor latch, increased work of feeding
  • Hearing: 90%+ have Eustachian tube dysfunction → otitis media with effusion → conductive hearing loss
  • Speech: 20-30% have velopharyngeal insufficiency even after repair
  • Dental: Missing teeth, misaligned teeth, need for bone grafting
  • Growth: Midface hypoplasia may require orthognathic surgery
  • Psychosocial: Appearance concerns, bullying, self-esteem

🔪 Surgical Timing & Techniques

The "Rule of 10s" (Traditional Guideline):

Cleft Lip Repair Readiness:
  • ≥10 weeks of age
  • ≥10 pounds weight
  • ≥10 g/dL hemoglobin

Note: Now considered a guideline, not a strict rule. Modern anesthesia allows earlier repair in select cases.

Surgical Timeline:

AgeProcedurePurpose
0-3 monthsPresurgical orthopedics (NAM)Narrow cleft, align alveolus, improve nasal shape
3 monthsCleft Lip RepairRestore orbicularis oris continuity, cosmesis
3-6 monthsPE tubes (if needed)Ventilate middle ear, prevent hearing loss
9-12 monthsCleft Palate RepairEnable speech development (before first words)
4-6 yearsSpeech surgery (if VPI)Pharyngeal flap or sphincter pharyngoplasty
8-12 yearsAlveolar bone graftEnable canine eruption, stabilize maxilla
AdolescenceRhinoplasty / Lip revisionFinal cosmetic refinement
16-18 yearsOrthognathic surgery (if needed)Correct midface hypoplasia, jaw alignment

Cleft Lip Repair Techniques:

Rotation-Advancement (Millard)

  • Most commonly used technique
  • Rotation flap on medial side
  • Advancement flap from lateral side
  • Scar mimics philtral column
  • Allows nasal tip access

Triangular Flap (Tennison-Randall)

  • Z-plasty based technique
  • Adds lip length via triangular flaps
  • Good for wide clefts
  • Scar crosses philtral column

Cleft Palate Repair Techniques:

TechniqueDescriptionAdvantage
Furlow Z-PlastyDouble opposing Z-plasty of soft palateLengthens palate, reorients levator muscles
Von LangenbeckBipedicled mucoperiosteal flapsSimple, minimal raw areas
V-Y PushbackV-Y advancement to lengthen palateGood for short palates
Two-Flap PalatoplastyVeau-Wardill-Kilner modificationGood closure of wide clefts
Goal of Palate Repair: Create an intact palate BEFORE speech develops (by 12 months) to enable normal velopharyngeal closure for speech sounds.
Why Timing Matters: Earlier palate repair (by 9-12 months) optimizes speech development. Late repair is associated with higher rates of velopharyngeal insufficiency and speech problems.

🎯 Long-Term Outcomes & Follow-up

Expected Outcomes with Optimal Care:

  • Normal or near-normal speech in 70-80% of patients
  • Acceptable cosmetic appearance
  • Normal hearing with appropriate management
  • Functional dentition with orthodontics/grafting
  • Normal life expectancy (unless syndromic)

Common Complications & Sequelae:

IssueIncidenceManagement
Velopharyngeal Insufficiency (VPI)20-30%Speech therapy, pharyngeal flap surgery
Oronasal Fistula5-30%Secondary palate surgery
Midface Hypoplasia25-30%Orthognathic surgery (Le Fort I)
Otitis Media with Effusion>90%PE tubes, audiologic monitoring
Dental AnomaliesVariableOrthodontics, prosthodontics
Nasal DeformityCommon in CLSecondary rhinoplasty

Lifelong Follow-up Schedule:

  • Infancy: Feeding, growth, genetics, surgery planning
  • Preschool (2-5 years): Speech evaluation, VPI assessment, hearing
  • School Age (6-12 years): Bone grafting, orthodontics, psychology
  • Adolescence (13-18 years): Orthognathic surgery, final rhinoplasty, transition planning
  • Adulthood: Maintenance, genetic counseling if planning family
Recurrence Risk Counseling:
  • One affected child, unaffected parents: ~4% risk for next child
  • Two affected children: ~9% risk
  • One affected parent: ~4% risk for each child
  • One affected parent + one affected child: ~17% risk
  • Syndromic forms follow Mendelian inheritance patterns
Key Message: Cleft care is a marathon, not a sprint. Coordinated multidisciplinary care from birth through adulthood optimizes functional and psychosocial outcomes.

🧠 Test Your Understanding

A newborn is found to have micrognathia, glossoptosis with airway obstruction, and a U-shaped cleft palate. What is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the next best step?

A. Van der Woude syndrome - Check for lip pits
B. Pierre Robin sequence - Secure the airway and position prone
C. Velocardiofacial syndrome - Order echocardiogram
D. Stickler syndrome - Urgent ophthalmology consult
Correct Answer: B

This classic triad of micrognathia, glossoptosis, and airway obstruction defines Pierre Robin sequence (PRS). The U-shaped cleft palate is characteristic but not required for diagnosis. The most urgent initial step is to address the airway obstruction - prone positioning allows the tongue to fall forward and often relieves obstruction. While PRS can be associated with syndromes like Stickler (requiring eye exam) or VCF (requiring cardiac eval), the immediate priority is airway management. Van der Woude is characterized by lower lip pits, not this triad.

🧠 Question 2

During which gestational week does the upper lip normally complete fusion?

A. Week 4
B. Week 6
C. Week 10
D. Week 12
Correct Answer: B

The upper lip forms during gestational week 6 when the medial nasal processes fuse with the maxillary and lateral nasal processes. Week 4 is when the facial prominences first appear. The secondary palate (hard and soft palate posterior to the incisive foramen) completes fusion by weeks 10-12. Understanding this timeline explains why cleft lip and cleft palate can occur independently - they are separate developmental events occurring at different times.

🧠 Question 3

A 6-month-old with isolated cleft palate is found to have severe myopia on ophthalmologic examination. Which syndrome should be suspected, and what other evaluation is critical?

A. Velocardiofacial syndrome - Echocardiogram
B. Stickler syndrome - Retinal examination for detachment risk
C. Treacher Collins - CT of temporal bones
D. Van der Woude - Check for lip pits
Correct Answer: B

Stickler syndrome is characterized by cleft palate, severe myopia (nearsightedness), hearing loss, and joint hypermobility due to mutations in collagen genes (COL2A1, COL11A1). The critical concern is the high risk of retinal detachment, which can cause blindness if not monitored. All patients with isolated cleft palate should be evaluated by a pediatric ophthalmologist in the first year. VCF syndrome typically presents with cardiac defects and hypocalcemia, not eye problems.
