❤️ Chest Pain: 5 Diagnoses Every Premed Should Know

Why Chest Pain Matters

"Chest pain" is the second most common reason for emergency department visits in the United States. It's also one of the most dangerous chief complaints — because the same symptom can mean anything from a pulled muscle to a life-threatening heart attack.

As a premed shadowing in any clinical setting, you will see chest pain. This module teaches you the five diagnoses that physicians think about FIRST when they hear those two words — and how they tell them apart.

The Key Principle: In chest pain, the physician's first question isn't "What's most likely?" — it's "What can kill this patient in the next hour?" The dangerous diagnoses must be ruled out before benign ones are even considered.

🫀 Acute Coronary Syndrome — The #1 Concern

ACS is an umbrella term covering three conditions on a spectrum: unstable angina, NSTEMI, and STEMI. They share the same mechanism but differ in severity.

The Mechanism (Connect to Your Prereqs)

Biochemistry: Atherosclerotic plaque is a lipid-laden lesion in the arterial wall. When the fibrous cap ruptures, collagen is exposed → platelet adhesion → thrombus formation. This is the coagulation cascade you learn in biochemistry, happening in real time.

Physiology: Coronary occlusion → myocardial oxygen demand exceeds supply → anaerobic metabolism → lactate production → chest pain. The myocardium can survive ~20 minutes of ischemia before irreversible necrosis begins.

How to Recognize It

Classic presentation: Substernal chest pressure ("elephant on my chest"), radiation to left arm or jaw, associated with diaphoresis (sweating), nausea, and shortness of breath. Worse with exertion. NOT sharp, NOT positional, NOT reproduced by palpation.

The Diagnostic Workup

EKG (within 10 minutes): ST elevation = STEMI → emergent cath lab. ST depression or T-wave changes = possible NSTEMI. Normal EKG does NOT rule out ACS.

Troponin: Cardiac-specific biomarker released when myocytes die. Takes 3-6 hours to rise — serial measurements needed. High-sensitivity troponin can detect smaller amounts earlier.

Why Timing Is Everything

"Time is myocardium" — every 30 minutes of coronary occlusion increases infarct size and mortality. Door-to-balloon time goal: less than 90 minutes. This is why STEMI activates the cath lab immediately — you don't wait for troponin.

🫁 Pulmonary Embolism & 🩸 Aortic Dissection

Pulmonary Embolism

Physiology: A thrombus (usually from a DVT in the legs) embolizes to the pulmonary vasculature. Depending on size, it can cause anything from mild hypoxia to sudden cardiac arrest. The right ventricle acutely dilates trying to pump against the obstruction.

Virchow's Triad (the three conditions that promote clot formation):

1. Stasis: Immobility (long flights, bedrest, surgery)
2. Endothelial injury: Trauma, surgery, smoking
3. Hypercoagulability: Pregnancy, OCPs, cancer, genetic thrombophilias

Risk factors MULTIPLY, not add. OCP (3-4×) + smoking (1.5×) + long flight (2-3×) = 20-40× baseline risk.

Key distinction: PE causes PLEURITIC chest pain (sharp, worse with breathing) while ACS causes PRESSURE-like pain (dull, crushing). This single feature helps differentiate them at the bedside.

Aortic Dissection

Anatomy: The aortic wall has three layers: intima, media, adventitia. In dissection, blood enters through a tear in the intima and tracks through the media, creating a false lumen. This can propagate proximally (toward the heart) or distally (toward the legs).

The three classic findings:

1. "Tearing" or "ripping" chest pain radiating to the BACK (the dissection tracking through the aorta)
2. Blood pressure differential >20 mmHg between arms (the false lumen compresses one subclavian artery)
3. Widened mediastinum on chest X-ray (the expanding false lumen)

Critical point: Aortic dissection can mimic STEMI if it extends to the coronary ostia. Giving anticoagulants or thrombolytics to a dissection patient can be fatal. This is why dissection must always be considered in chest pain before giving blood thinners.

💨 Tension Pneumothorax & 🔥 Esophageal Rupture

Tension Pneumothorax

Physics: Air enters the pleural space through a one-way valve mechanism — it gets in but can't get out. With each breath, more air accumulates. Increasing intrathoracic pressure collapses the lung, then shifts the mediastinum to the opposite side, compressing the heart and great vessels.

Why it's a clinical diagnosis: Tension pneumothorax can kill in minutes. You do NOT wait for a chest X-ray. The classic findings — absent breath sounds on one side, tracheal deviation, hypotension, distended neck veins — tell you to act immediately with needle decompression.

Prereq connection (Physics): Boyle's Law (P₁V₁ = P₂V₂) explains the mechanism. As air volume increases in the closed pleural space, pressure rises. This pressure compresses the vena cava → decreased venous return → decreased cardiac output → shock. Treatment creates a hole to equalize pressure.

Esophageal Rupture (Boerhaave Syndrome)

The rarest but most dramatic: Forceful vomiting (or instrumentation) causes a full-thickness tear in the esophagus, usually the left posterolateral distal esophagus. Gastric contents spill into the mediastinum, causing chemical mediastinitis and rapid sepsis.

Mackler Triad: Vomiting + chest pain + subcutaneous emphysema (air crackling under the skin of the neck/chest). Subcutaneous emphysema is nearly pathognomonic — air from the esophageal tear tracks through tissue planes.

Why it matters: Mortality without treatment approaches 100% at 48 hours. With prompt surgical repair, survival improves dramatically. It's rare enough that physicians must actively consider it — pattern recognition won't help because most physicians have seen very few cases.

Case 1

A 62-year-old male with hypertension and diabetes presents with 90 minutes of substernal chest pressure radiating to his left jaw. He is diaphoretic and nauseated. EKG shows ST elevations in V1-V4. Troponin is pending. What is the next step?
A.Wait for troponin results to confirm myocardial injury before intervening
B.Order CTA chest to rule out aortic dissection before any anticoagulation
C.Activate catheterization lab for emergent percutaneous coronary intervention
D.Administer thrombolytics intravenously and admit to the coronary care unit
E.Start heparin infusion and obtain serial troponins every three hours
✅ Activate the cath lab. ST elevations in V1-V4 indicate an anterior STEMI — the LAD artery is occluded. The EKG IS the diagnostic test. Do NOT wait for troponin (takes hours to rise). Door-to-balloon time goal is under 90 minutes. Every minute of delay means more myocardium dying.

Case 2

A 29-year-old woman on oral contraceptives presents with sudden-onset sharp chest pain that worsens when she breathes in. She flew back from Europe 2 days ago. HR 118, O2 sat 92%, CXR normal. What diagnosis is highest on the differential?
A.Spontaneous pneumothorax based on sudden onset and pleuritic quality
B.Acute pericarditis with pleuritic chest pain and tachycardia on exam
C.Panic attack with hyperventilation causing chest tightness and tachycardia
D.Musculoskeletal chest wall strain from carrying heavy luggage during travel
E.Pulmonary embolism given risk factors, pleuritic pain, and normal chest X-ray
✅ Pulmonary embolism. Three risk factors align with Virchow's triad: OCPs (hypercoagulability), long flight (stasis), and pleuritic chest pain + tachycardia + hypoxia. A normal CXR is actually expected in PE — it helps rule out other causes. Next step: CTA chest (not D-dimer, because clinical probability is high).

Case 3

A 72-year-old male with poorly controlled hypertension presents with sudden "tearing" pain between his shoulder blades. BP in the right arm is 188/102 and in the left arm is 152/88. CXR shows a widened mediastinum. Heparin is ordered for suspected ACS. What should you do?
A.Hold the heparin — the presentation is consistent with aortic dissection
B.Proceed with heparin because ACS is more common than aortic dissection
C.Administer thrombolytics to address the likely coronary artery thrombus
D.Perform needle decompression of the left chest for suspected pneumothorax
E.Obtain EKG and troponin to confirm ACS before changing the treatment plan
✅ Hold the heparin. Tearing pain radiating to the back + 36 mmHg BP differential between arms + widened mediastinum = aortic dissection until proven otherwise. Anticoagulation can be catastrophic — it prevents clot formation in the false lumen and can worsen hemorrhage. This is why dissection must ALWAYS be considered before giving blood thinners for chest pain.

Case 4

A 22-year-old male suddenly collapses at a concert clutching his chest. He is tall and thin. Paramedics find absent breath sounds on the right, tracheal deviation to the left, hypotension, and distended neck veins. What is the immediate action?
A.Transport urgently to hospital for CT angiography of the chest
B.Administer aspirin and nitroglycerin for presumed acute coronary syndrome
C.Obtain a portable chest X-ray to confirm the diagnosis before intervention
D.Perform needle decompression of the right chest at the second intercostal space
E.Initiate CPR and attach AED pads for evaluation of cardiac rhythm
✅ Needle decompression. This is a tension pneumothorax — a CLINICAL diagnosis. Absent breath sounds (right) + tracheal deviation (left, away from the affected side) + hypotension + JVD = classic presentation. Young, tall, thin males are at highest risk for spontaneous pneumothorax. You do NOT wait for imaging — needle decompression at the 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line, right side.

What You've Learned

You can now recognize the clinical "fingerprints" of the five most dangerous chest pain diagnoses. Here's what to carry with you into every clinical experience:

The One-Sentence Summaries

ACS:

Crushing pressure + arm/jaw radiation + diaphoresis → EKG in 10 minutes, cath lab if ST elevation.

PE:

Pleuritic pain + dyspnea + risk factors (Virchow's triad) + normal CXR → CTA chest.

Dissection:

Tearing pain to back + BP differential + widened mediastinum → CTA, NO anticoagulation.

Tension pneumothorax:

Sudden pain + absent breath sounds + tracheal deviation → needle decompression NOW.

Boerhaave:

Vomiting + chest pain + subcutaneous emphysema → emergency surgery.

The Pattern: Each diagnosis has a unique pain quality (pressure vs. sharp vs. tearing vs. sudden vs. post-vomiting) and a unique key finding. Learn those 10 features and you'll be ahead of most premeds — and many first-year medical students.
