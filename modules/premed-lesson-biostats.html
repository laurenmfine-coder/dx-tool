📊 Biostatistics That Actually Matter in Medicine

Why You Need This

In your stats class, you learn about p-values and standard deviations. In medicine, you need to answer a different question: "Does this test actually help my patient?"

Every day, physicians interpret diagnostic tests. A positive result doesn't always mean the patient has the disease, and a negative result doesn't always mean they're healthy. Understanding WHY requires the concepts in this module.

What you'll learn: Sensitivity, specificity, PPV, NPV, pre-test probability, likelihood ratios, and NNT — the seven concepts that separate someone who understands medical evidence from someone who just memorizes it.

The 2×2 Table: Foundation of Everything

Every diagnostic test produces four possible outcomes. The 2×2 table organizes them:

Disease PresentDisease Absent
Test PositiveTrue Positive (TP)
✅ Test correctly detected disease		False Positive (FP)
❌ Test said yes, but no disease
Test NegativeFalse Negative (FN)
❌ Test missed the disease		True Negative (TN)
✅ Test correctly ruled out disease

Every statistic in this module is derived from this table. If you can draw it from memory, you can derive sensitivity, specificity, PPV, and NPV on the spot.

Memory trick: The columns are truth (disease present or absent). The rows are what the test tells you. Good tests make the diagonals (TP and TN) large and the off-diagonals (FP and FN) small.

Sensitivity & Specificity

Sensitivity: "Does the test catch the disease?"

Sensitivity = TP ÷ (TP + FN) = "Of everyone WITH the disease, how many tested positive?"

A highly sensitive test (≥95%) has very few false negatives. If the test is negative, you can be confident the patient doesn't have the disease. Mnemonic: SnNOUT — Sensitive test, Negative result, rules OUT.

Example: The D-dimer for PE has ~97% sensitivity. If it's negative, you can confidently rule out PE. But many things cause a positive D-dimer (surgery, pregnancy, infection) — so a positive result doesn't confirm PE.

Specificity: "Does the test correctly identify the healthy?"

Specificity = TN ÷ (TN + FP) = "Of everyone WITHOUT the disease, how many tested negative?"

A highly specific test (≥95%) has very few false positives. If the test is positive, you can be confident the patient HAS the disease. Mnemonic: SpPIN — Specific test, Positive result, rules IN.

Example: Biopsy-proven pathology is nearly 100% specific for cancer. If the biopsy says cancer, it's cancer. But biopsies can miss the tumor (false negative) — so sensitivity isn't perfect.
The tradeoff: Sensitivity and specificity are inversely related. Making a test more sensitive (lower cutoff) catches more disease but also produces more false positives. Making it more specific (higher cutoff) reduces false positives but misses more disease. This tradeoff is a core tension in diagnostic medicine.

PPV & NPV: What the Patient Actually Wants to Know

Patients don't ask "What's the sensitivity of this test?" They ask: "My test came back positive — do I have the disease?" That question is answered by Positive Predictive Value.

Positive Predictive Value (PPV)

PPV = TP ÷ (TP + FP) = "If my test is positive, what's the probability I actually have the disease?"

Negative Predictive Value (NPV)

NPV = TN ÷ (TN + FN) = "If my test is negative, what's the probability I'm actually healthy?"
The critical insight that most premeds miss: PPV and NPV depend on disease PREVALENCE. The same test with the same sensitivity and specificity will have completely different predictive values depending on how common the disease is in the population you're testing.
Why this matters clinically: A screening mammogram has ~90% sensitivity and ~90% specificity. Breast cancer prevalence in screened women is ~0.5%. Run the numbers:

In 10,000 women: 50 have cancer, 9,950 don't.
TP = 45 (90% of 50), FP = 995 (10% of 9,950)
PPV = 45 ÷ (45 + 995) = 4.3%

A positive mammogram means only a 4.3% chance of actually having cancer. This is why positive screening tests require CONFIRMATION — and why physicians don't panic over a single positive result.

Pre-Test Probability: The Art Before the Science

Before ordering any test, the physician forms an estimate: "How likely is it that this patient has the disease?" This is pre-test probability, and it determines whether a test is even worth ordering.

Three Zones of Pre-Test Probability

Low pre-test probability (<10%): A healthy 25-year-old with sharp chest pain reproduced by pressing on the chest wall. Probability of heart attack is very low. Even a positive troponin might be a false positive. A sensitive test with a negative result is useful (rules out).
Moderate pre-test probability (10-80%): A 55-year-old smoker with exertional chest pressure. Could be cardiac, could be GERD. Testing is most valuable here — it changes management.
High pre-test probability (>80%): A 70-year-old diabetic with crushing substernal pressure, diaphoresis, and ST elevation on EKG. You don't need a troponin to know this is a heart attack. Testing delays treatment. Act first.
The clinical reasoning connection: Pre-test probability is what the physician calculates (often intuitively) when they build a differential diagnosis. The history, exam, and risk factors all feed into this estimate. Tests are most powerful when pre-test probability is in the middle — where the result actually changes what you do.

Number Needed to Treat (NNT): Does This Treatment Actually Work?

Drug companies love to report "relative risk reduction" because it sounds impressive. Physicians need to think in terms of absolute benefit — and NNT is the tool for that.

NNT = 1 ÷ Absolute Risk Reduction = 1 ÷ (Control event rate − Treatment event rate)

NNT answers: "How many patients do I need to treat for ONE patient to benefit?"

Example that changes perspective:

A drug reduces heart attack risk from 4% to 3% over 5 years.
Relative risk reduction: 25% (sounds great!)
Absolute risk reduction: 1% (one percentage point)
NNT = 1 ÷ 0.01 = 100

You need to treat 100 patients for 5 years to prevent ONE heart attack. The other 99 get no benefit but still experience side effects and costs. Is it worth it? That depends on the drug's side effects, cost, and patient values — this is where evidence meets clinical judgment.
NNT benchmarks: NNT of 2-5 = highly effective treatment (like antibiotics for strep throat). NNT of 20-50 = moderately effective (statins for high-risk patients). NNT of 100+ = marginal benefit (many screening interventions). Whenever you see a study claiming a drug "works," ask: what's the NNT?

Question 1

A rapid strep test has 95% sensitivity and 98% specificity. A physician in a busy urgent care sees a patient with a sore throat. The rapid strep test comes back NEGATIVE. Using the SnNOUT mnemonic, what can the physician conclude?
A.The patient definitely does not have strep and needs no further testing
B.The negative result on a sensitive test makes strep pharyngitis unlikely
C.The specificity is more relevant and the result is therefore inconclusive
D.The pre-test probability was too low for this test to be meaningful
E.A throat culture is always required regardless of rapid test results
✅ SnNOUT — Sensitive test, Negative result, rules OUT. With 95% sensitivity, a negative rapid strep has only a 5% false negative rate, making strep unlikely. Note: "unlikely" not "impossible" — some guidelines recommend backup throat culture in children because even a 5% miss rate matters in pediatrics.

Question 2

A screening test for a rare disease (prevalence 1 in 10,000) has 99% sensitivity and 99% specificity. If 100,000 people are screened and a person tests positive, what is the approximate probability they actually have the disease?
A.Approximately 99%, matching the test's sensitivity and specificity values
B.Approximately 50%, because false positives equal true positives at this prevalence
C.Approximately 1%, because low prevalence dramatically reduces positive predictive value
D.Approximately 10%, because specificity determines PPV in low-prevalence populations
E.Cannot be determined without knowing the test's likelihood ratio and confidence interval
✅ About 1%. Of 100,000 people: 10 have the disease (TP ≈ 10), 99,990 don't (FP ≈ 1,000). PPV = 10/(10+1,000) ≈ 1%. Even an excellent test produces mostly false positives when the disease is rare. This is why mass screening for rare diseases is problematic — and why confirmatory testing exists.

Question 3

A pharmaceutical company reports their new blood pressure drug reduces stroke risk by 33% compared to placebo. On closer inspection, the absolute risk decreased from 3% to 2% over 5 years. What is the NNT, and what does it mean?
A.NNT = 3, meaning three patients must be treated to prevent one stroke event
B.NNT = 33, corresponding directly to the relative risk reduction percentage
C.NNT = 50, meaning the drug has minimal clinical significance for most patients
D.NNT = 100, meaning 100 patients treated for 5 years to prevent one stroke
E.NNT cannot be calculated from relative risk reduction and requires hazard ratios
✅ NNT = 100. ARR = 3% − 2% = 1% = 0.01. NNT = 1/0.01 = 100. You treat 100 patients for 5 years to prevent one stroke. The 33% relative risk reduction sounds impressive, but the absolute benefit is small. This is why you should always look at absolute numbers, not just relative risk reduction, when evaluating treatments.

Question 4

A physician is deciding whether to order a D-dimer for a patient with possible PE. The patient has no risk factors, no tachycardia, and normal oxygen saturation. Pre-test probability is estimated at less than 5%. In which clinical scenario is the D-dimer test MOST useful?
A.High pre-test probability where a positive result would confirm the diagnosis
B.Any clinical scenario because D-dimer has excellent sensitivity for PE
C.Moderate pre-test probability where the result would change clinical management
D.Post-treatment monitoring to confirm adequate response to anticoagulation
E.Low pre-test probability where a negative result confidently rules out PE
✅ Low pre-test probability. D-dimer is highly sensitive (~97%) but poorly specific. In low-probability patients, a negative D-dimer rules out PE (SnNOUT) and avoids a CT scan with radiation and contrast. In high-probability patients, D-dimer is useless — even a negative result doesn't change management, and a positive one is expected. Tests are most valuable when they change what you do.

Your Biostatistics Toolkit

You now have the seven concepts that physicians use every day to interpret evidence and make decisions:

Sensitivity — Does the test catch the disease? (SnNOUT: sensitive negative rules out)
Specificity — Does the test correctly identify the healthy? (SpPIN: specific positive rules in)
PPV — If positive, what's the real probability of disease? (Depends on prevalence!)
NPV — If negative, what's the real probability of being healthy? (Also depends on prevalence)
Pre-test probability — How likely is disease BEFORE the test? (Determines if testing is even useful)
NNT — How many patients treated for one to benefit? (Cuts through marketing hype)
The 2×2 table — Draw it from memory and you can derive everything above on the spot.

These aren't abstract statistics — they're the cognitive tools physicians use to decide: Should I order this test? What does this result mean? Should I start this treatment? Your MCAT will test these concepts, and your clinical rotations will demand them.
