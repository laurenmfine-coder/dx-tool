🧠 Neurogenic Bladder

Lesion Localization & Urodynamic Patterns

📚 Overview

Neurogenic bladder refers to bladder dysfunction caused by neurological disease or injury. The pattern of dysfunction depends on the level of the lesion in the nervous system.

Common Etiologies

LevelConditions
BrainStroke, Parkinson's, dementia, brain tumor, MS
Spinal cordSCI, MS, transverse myelitis, spinal stenosis, tumors
Peripheral nervesDiabetes, pelvic surgery (radical hysterectomy, APR), herniated disc
CongenitalMyelomeningocele (spina bifida), tethered cord, sacral agenesis

🧬 Neuroanatomy of Micturition

NORMAL VOIDING NEUROANATOMY CEREBRAL CORTEX (frontal lobe) ├── Conscious control, social awareness ├── Inhibits pontine micturition center until appropriate │ PONTINE MICTURITION CENTER (PMC) ├── Coordinates voiding reflex ├── "ON/OFF switch" for micturition │ SPINAL CORD ├── T11-L2: Sympathetic (hypogastric nerve) │ • Bladder relaxation (β receptors) │ • Sphincter contraction (α receptors) │ • STORAGE function │ ├── S2-S4: Parasympathetic (pelvic nerve) │ • Detrusor CONTRACTION (M3 receptors) │ • VOIDING function │ └── S2-S4: Somatic (pudendal nerve) • External sphincter (voluntary control) • "Guarding reflex"
The Guarding Reflex: When the bladder fills, afferent signals trigger reflexive contraction of the external sphincter (via pudendal nerve) to prevent leakage. This is disrupted in various neurogenic conditions.

📍 Classification by Lesion Level

SUPRAPONTINE LESIONS (Brain - above PMC)

Examples: Stroke, Parkinson's, dementia, brain tumor

PatternDetrusor overactivity WITH coordinated sphincter
MechanismLoss of cortical inhibition of PMC; voiding reflex intact
SymptomsUrgency, frequency, urge incontinence
VoidingUsually complete (no significant residual)
RiskLow risk to upper tracts (sphincter relaxes with voiding)

SUPRASACRAL SPINAL CORD LESIONS (Between PMC and S2-4)

Examples: Spinal cord injury above S2, MS, transverse myelitis

PatternDetrusor overactivity WITH detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (DSD)
MechanismLoss of coordination between bladder and sphincter
DSDSphincter contracts DURING bladder contraction (should relax!)
SymptomsIncomplete emptying, high pressures, incontinence
RiskHIGH RISK to upper tracts (high pressure → hydro, reflux, renal damage)
⚠️ DSD is DANGEROUS! High bladder pressures (>40 cmH2O) can cause hydronephrosis, VUR, and renal failure. Close monitoring and treatment essential.

SACRAL/INFRASACRAL LESIONS (S2-4 and below)

Examples: Cauda equina, myelomeningocele, pelvic surgery, diabetic neuropathy

PatternDetrusor areflexia/underactivity; sphincter weakness may occur
MechanismDamage to sacral voiding center (parasympathetic outflow)
SymptomsUrinary retention, overflow incontinence, stress incontinence
VoidingPoor emptying, high PVR
RiskUTI (stasis), overflow incontinence; upper tract risk if high storage pressures

🔍 Evaluation

Clinical Assessment

  • Detailed neurological history
  • Voiding diary
  • Post-void residual (PVR)
  • Neurological exam (perineal sensation, anal tone, bulbocavernosus reflex)

Urodynamic Studies

Urodynamics is ESSENTIAL in neurogenic bladder:
  • Characterizes detrusor function (over/underactive)
  • Identifies DSD
  • Measures bladder compliance and pressures
  • Guides treatment decisions
  • Monitors for dangerous high-pressure patterns

Upper Tract Monitoring

  • Renal ultrasound (hydronephrosis)
  • Serum creatinine
  • VCUG if VUR suspected
  • Nuclear renal scan for differential function

💊 Management

Goals of Treatment

  1. Protect the upper urinary tract (most important!)
  2. Achieve low-pressure urine storage
  3. Ensure adequate bladder emptying
  4. Maintain continence
  5. Improve quality of life

Treatment Options

ProblemTreatment Options
Detrusor overactivityAntimuscarinics, β3-agonists, Botox injection
DSDα-blockers, Botox to sphincter, sphincterotomy
Poor emptyingClean intermittent catheterization (CIC), indwelling catheter
Low complianceAntimuscarinics, augmentation cystoplasty
IncontinenceSling, artificial urinary sphincter, diversion

Clean Intermittent Catheterization (CIC)

CIC is the gold standard for bladder emptying in neurogenic bladder:
  • Usually every 4-6 hours
  • Maintains low bladder pressures
  • Reduces UTI risk vs indwelling catheter
  • Preserves upper tract function
  • Requires adequate hand function or caregiver

Augmentation Cystoplasty

  • For refractory low compliance or small capacity
  • Uses intestinal segment to enlarge bladder
  • Requires CIC afterward
  • Complications: mucus, stones, metabolic issues, perforation

📋 Specific Conditions

Spinal Cord Injury (SCI):
  • Spinal shock: Initial areflexic bladder (weeks); requires catheter
  • After recovery: Pattern depends on level (above/below S2-4)
  • Autonomic dysreflexia: Emergency! Triggered by bladder distension in T6+ injuries
Myelomeningocele (Spina Bifida):
  • Most common cause of neurogenic bladder in children
  • Lifelong management from infancy
  • CIC often started in infancy
  • Goal: Protect kidneys, achieve continence for social integration
Diabetes Mellitus:
  • Autonomic neuropathy → impaired bladder sensation
  • Often presents late with large capacity, incomplete emptying
  • Detrusor underactivity common

🧠 Quick Quiz

A patient with T10 spinal cord injury has urodynamics showing detrusor overactivity with simultaneous external sphincter contraction during voiding. What is this pattern called?

A. Detrusor areflexia
B. Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (DSD)
C. Stress incontinence
D. Urge incontinence
Correct: B - DSD
Detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia occurs with suprasacral spinal cord lesions (above S2-4, like T10). The sphincter contracts instead of relaxing during bladder contraction. This creates high pressures and is dangerous to the upper tracts.
