FAST in the Trauma Algorithm

The FAST exam is the single most important bedside imaging tool in acute trauma. It answers ONE question: is there free fluid (blood) in the abdomen or pericardium? Four windows: (1) RUQ (Morison's pouch — most sensitive, detects as little as 200mL), (2) LUQ (splenorenal recess), (3) suprapubic (pouch of Douglas/rectovesical), (4) subxiphoid (pericardial effusion). The FAST determines the algorithm: Unstable + positive FAST → OR (emergent laparotomy). Stable + positive FAST → CT for grading/surgical planning. Unstable + negative FAST → consider non-abdominal hemorrhage (pelvis, chest, retroperitoneum — retroperitoneal bleeding is NOT detected by FAST). Extended FAST (eFAST) adds bilateral thoracic views for pneumothorax and hemothorax.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: Trauma is the leading cause of death for ages 1-44 in the US. Hemorrhagic shock accounts for 30-40% of trauma deaths, and most are potentially preventable with rapid hemorrhage control. The 'golden hour' concept drives emergency care: definitive hemorrhage control within 60 minutes significantly improves survival. The FAST exam takes <3 minutes and can be performed during the primary survey — it does NOT delay resuscitation. Every emergency physician, surgeon, and medical student should be able to perform and interpret a FAST exam.

FAST — Concurrent with Resuscitation

The FAST exam is performed simultaneously with the primary survey — it does not require transport, contrast, radiation, or patient cooperation. Technique: low-frequency curvilinear probe (3.5-5 MHz), patient supine. The exam is BINARY: free fluid present (positive) or absent (negative). Free fluid appears as: anechoic (black) stripes between organs and in dependent spaces. eFAST adds bilateral lung assessments: absence of lung sliding + A-lines without B-lines + 'stratosphere sign' on M-mode = pneumothorax (can be detected before CXR). Hemothorax: anechoic fluid above the diaphragm.

B.POSITIVE FAST: anechoic free fluid (blood) in Morison's pouch

Positive FAST — Hemoperitoneum Confirmed

FAST sensitivity for hemoperitoneum: ~85-95% when >200-500mL of free fluid is present (sensitivity decreases for smaller volumes). Morison's pouch (hepatorenal recess) is the most sensitive window — fluid accumulates here first in the supine patient. False negatives: early injury with <200mL of hemorrhage, retroperitoneal bleeding (duodenum, kidneys, pancreas, great vessels — NOT detected by FAST), adhesions preventing fluid migration, and obesity limiting image quality. False positives: pre-existing ascites, ruptured ovarian cyst, peritoneal dialysis fluid. In this unstable patient, any positive FAST finding = hemoperitoneum = OR.

A.Positive FAST + hemodynamically unstable = EMERGENT LAPAROTOMY (no CT).
B.CT abdomen to grade the injury before surgery
C.Angiographic embolization first
Positive FAST + Unstable = OR (Not CT)

The FAST exam answered the critical question: WHERE is he bleeding? The answer (abdomen) directs him to the OR. Damage control surgery principles: (1) stop hemorrhage (packing, ligation, resection), (2) control contamination (staple bowel injuries), (3) temporary abdominal closure (negative pressure dressing), (4) ICU resuscitation (correct coagulopathy, acidosis, hypothermia — the 'lethal triad' of trauma), (5) planned return to OR in 24-72h for definitive repair and closure. A stable patient with positive FAST would get CT for injury grading — many solid organ injuries (spleen, liver) can be managed non-operatively with angiographic embolization if the patient responds to resuscitation.

Damage Control Surgery + Staged Injury Management

The FAST exam — a 3-minute bedside test — was the critical decision tool that sent this patient to the OR instead of the CT scanner (which would have delayed hemorrhage control and likely resulted in death). After surgical hemorrhage control and ICU stabilization, the SECONDARY survey with complete CT imaging identifies all injuries. Motorcycle crashes frequently produce polytrauma: TBI (the leading cause of motorcycle death — helmet use reduces mortality by 37%), splenic/hepatic laceration, pelvic fractures, and extremity fractures. Injury prevention (helmet use, alcohol avoidance) is the ultimate upstream intervention — motorcycle crashes with helmets have dramatically different injury patterns and survival.
