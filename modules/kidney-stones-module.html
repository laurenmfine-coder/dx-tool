💎 Nephrolithiasis

Stone Types, Imaging, and Management Algorithm

📚 Overview

Nephrolithiasis affects ~10% of the population lifetime. Understanding stone composition guides both acute management and long-term prevention.

🔬 Stone Types & Composition

CALCIUM OXALATE (75-80%)

AppearanceEnvelope-shaped crystals; brown/black stones
X-rayRADIOPAQUE
CausesHypercalciuria, hyperoxaluria, hypocitraturia, low urine volume
AssociationsHigh oxalate foods (spinach, rhubarb, nuts), Crohn's (enteric hyperoxaluria), gastric bypass
Prevention↑ Fluids, ↓ sodium, thiazides (for hypercalciuria), citrate supplementation

CALCIUM PHOSPHITE (Hydroxyapatite/Brushite) (5-10%)

X-rayRADIOPAQUE
CausesHigh urine pH (>7.0), RTA Type 1, hyperparathyroidism
Key PointForm in ALKALINE urine (opposite of uric acid)

URIC ACID (5-10%)

AppearanceRhomboid/rosette crystals; yellow-orange stones
X-rayRADIOLUCENT (not seen on X-ray!)
CausesLow urine pH (<5.5), gout, high purine diet, tumor lysis, myeloproliferative disorders
PreventionUrine alkalinization (K-citrate to pH 6.5-7), allopurinol, ↓ purines, ↑ fluids
SpecialCan be DISSOLVED with alkalinization!

STRUVITE (Magnesium Ammonium Phosphate) (5-15%)

Appearance"Coffin lid" crystals; staghorn calculi
X-rayRADIOPAQUE
CausesUrease-producing bacteria (Proteus, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas) → high ammonia → high pH
Key PointINFECTION stones; require complete removal + antibiotics
Risk FactorsWomen, chronic catheters, neurogenic bladder, recurrent UTIs

CYSTINE (1-2%)

AppearanceHexagonal crystals; yellow waxy stones
X-rayFaintly radiopaque ("ground glass")
CauseCystinuria - autosomal recessive defect in dibasic amino acid transport
PreventionVery high fluid intake (>3L/day), urine alkalinization, tiopronin or D-penicillamine
Key PointSuspect in YOUNG patients with recurrent stones
Memory Aid - Radiolucency:
"U Can't See" = Uric acid and Cystine (and pure xanthine) are radiolucent on X-ray
(Actually cystine is faintly opaque, but uric acid is truly radiolucent)

🔍 Clinical Presentation

Classic Symptoms

  • Renal colic: Severe, colicky flank pain radiating to groin/testicle/labia
  • Pain location follows stone location (flank → lower abdomen → groin)
  • Nausea and vomiting (common)
  • Hematuria (85-90%, may be microscopic)
  • Restlessness - can't find comfortable position (vs peritonitis = still)
  • Urinary urgency/frequency if stone at UVJ

Stone Location & Symptoms

LocationTypical Symptoms
Renal pelvis/UPJDeep flank/CVA pain
Mid-ureterPain radiates anteriorly to abdomen
Distal ureterPain radiates to groin, testicle/labia
UVJ/intramuralBladder irritation (frequency, urgency)
⚠️ Complicated Stone = EMERGENCY:
  • Obstructing stone + INFECTION (pyonephrosis) - sepsis risk!
  • Obstruction of solitary kidney
  • Bilateral obstruction
  • Acute renal failure
→ Requires URGENT decompression (ureteral stent or nephrostomy tube)

📷 Imaging

Imaging Modalities

ModalitySensitivityProsCons
CT (non-contrast)95-100%Gold standard; sees all stone types; fastRadiation; cost
Ultrasound45-85%No radiation; good for pregnancy; shows hydronephrosisMisses ureteral stones; operator dependent
KUB (X-ray)45-60%Low radiation; follow known radiopaque stonesMisses radiolucent stones; low sensitivity
CT with contrastVariableEvaluates anatomy, tumorsContrast may obscure small stones
Imaging Choice:
  • First episode, adult: Non-contrast CT (low-dose if available)
  • Pregnant: Ultrasound first; MRI if needed
  • Pediatric: Ultrasound first; low-dose CT if needed
  • Follow-up of known stone: KUB (if radiopaque) or US

CT Findings

  • Direct visualization of stone (measure size, location)
  • Hydroureter/hydronephrosis (obstruction)
  • Perinephric stranding (inflammation)
  • Stone density (HU) may predict composition

Acute Management Algorithm

Medical Expulsive Therapy (MET)

For stones ≤10mm:
  • Alpha-blockers (tamsulosin) - relax ureteral smooth muscle
  • Pain control (NSAIDs first-line; opioids if needed)
  • Hydration
  • Strain urine for stone analysis
Passage rates by size:
  • <5mm: ~90% pass spontaneously
  • 5-10mm: ~50% pass
  • >10mm: Unlikely to pass; intervention usually needed

Surgical Interventions

ProcedureIndicationNotes
Ureteroscopy (URS)Ureteral stones, lower pole renal stones <2cmLaser lithotripsy + basket extraction; high success
Shock Wave Lithotripsy (SWL)Renal stones <2cm, proximal ureteral stonesNon-invasive; may need multiple sessions
PCNLLarge renal stones (>2cm), staghorn calculiPercutaneous access; highest stone-free rate for large stones
Stent placementTemporizing for obstruction, post-procedureRelieves obstruction; not definitive treatment

🔄 Prevention & Metabolic Workup

When to Do Metabolic Workup

  • Recurrent stone formers
  • First-time former with high risk (family history, young age, solitary kidney)
  • Non-calcium stones (uric acid, cystine, struvite)
  • Children

24-Hour Urine Analysis

ParameterGoalIf Abnormal
Volume>2.5 L/dayIncrease fluids
Calcium<250 mg/day (women), <300 (men)Thiazide diuretic, ↓ sodium
Oxalate<40 mg/day↓ Oxalate foods, calcium WITH meals
Citrate>450 mg/dayPotassium citrate
Uric acid<800 mg/day (men)↓ Purines, allopurinol
pH6.0-6.5Alkalinize if <5.5 (uric acid risk)
Sodium<150 mEq/day↓ Salt intake
Universal Prevention:
  • Drink enough to produce >2.5L urine/day
  • Limit sodium (<2300 mg/day)
  • Normal calcium intake (don't restrict!)
  • Limit animal protein
  • Increase fruits/vegetables (citrate)

🧠 Quick Quiz

A patient with history of gout presents with renal colic. KUB shows no stone. CT shows a 6mm stone in the distal ureter. What type of stone is most likely?

A. Calcium oxalate
B. Uric acid
C. Struvite
D. Cystine
Correct: B - Uric Acid
Uric acid stones are RADIOLUCENT (not seen on KUB) but visible on CT. The history of gout strongly suggests uric acid stone. These stones form in acidic urine (pH <5.5) and can potentially be dissolved with urine alkalinization.
