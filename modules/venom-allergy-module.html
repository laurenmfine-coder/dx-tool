MECHANISM
Pathophysiology of Insect Venom Hypersensitivity

Insect venom allergy is an IgE-mediated Type I hypersensitivity reaction. On initial sensitization, venom proteins (phospholipase A2, hyaluronidase, melittin) stimulate IgE production. On re-exposure, venom cross-links IgE on mast cells and basophils, triggering degranulation and systemic mediator release.

🔑 Large Local vs Systemic: Large local reactions (swelling >10 cm lasting >24 hours) have only a 5-10% risk of future systemic reaction. Systemic reactions (urticaria, anaphylaxis) have up to 60% risk of recurrence on re-sting — this is why venom immunotherapy (VIT) is indicated for systemic reactions, not large local reactions.

CLINICAL
Evaluation & Diagnosis

Timing of Reactions

IgE-mediated venom reactions occur within minutes to 1-2 hours of the sting. Delayed reactions (serum sickness-like, nephritis, vasculitis) are rare and not IgE-mediated. Biphasic anaphylaxis can occur up to 72 hours later in ~5% of cases.

Diagnostic Testing

Testing should be performed 4-6 weeks after the sting event (to allow IgE replenishment after anaphylaxis) in patients who had systemic reactions. Both skin testing and serum specific IgE (sIgE) should be performed — some patients are positive on one test but not the other.

⚠️ Elevated baseline tryptase: Patients with elevated baseline serum tryptase (>11.4 ng/mL) or known mastocytosis have higher risk of severe anaphylaxis to stings. Always check baseline tryptase in patients with venom anaphylaxis — occult mastocytosis is found in up to 5% of adults with venom anaphylaxis.

Special Populations

Children with cutaneous-only systemic reactions (hives without cardiorespiratory symptoms) have <5% risk of severe anaphylaxis on re-sting and generally do NOT need VIT. Adults with the same presentation have higher risk and VIT should be considered, especially with frequent exposure.

TREATMENT
Management & Venom Immunotherapy

Acute Management

IM epinephrine (0.3 mg adult, 0.15 mg pediatric) is the first-line treatment for systemic venom reactions. All patients with prior systemic reactions should carry an epinephrine auto-injector. For honeybee stings, remove the stinger quickly (method of removal does not matter — speed matters).

Venom Immunotherapy (VIT)

FeatureDetails
IndicationHistory of systemic reaction + positive venom skin test or sIgE
Duration3-5 years minimum; lifelong if mastocytosis or very severe reaction
Efficacy97% effective for yellow jacket/wasp; ~80% for honeybee
Maintenance dose100 mcg per relevant venom, every 4-8 weeks
🔑 VIT Efficacy: VIT reduces the risk of systemic reaction from 60% to <5%. It is one of the most effective forms of allergen immunotherapy. Protection persists for years after discontinuation in most patients, except those with mastocytosis (who should continue indefinitely).
⚠️ Beta-blocker caution: Beta-blockers can make anaphylaxis more severe and resistant to epinephrine treatment. Consider switching to alternative antihypertensives in patients with venom allergy, though this must be balanced against cardiovascular need.
