🤔 How Doctors Think

1. Two Systems of Thinking

Every physician uses two cognitive systems — and the interplay between them is the foundation of clinical reasoning.

System 1: Pattern Recognition (Fast)

An experienced physician sees a patient clutching their chest, sweating, with jaw pain — and immediately thinks "heart attack." They didn't analyze each symptom. The pattern triggered an instant recognition. This is System 1: fast, automatic, pattern-based. It's what makes experienced physicians appear to "just know."

System 2: Analytical Reasoning (Slow)

A medical student sees the same patient and systematically works through: "Chest pain → cardiac vs pulmonary vs GI vs MSK. Risk factors: age, smoking, diabetes. EKG shows ST elevation → STEMI protocol." This is System 2: slow, deliberate, step-by-step. It's how students learn before building the pattern library that enables System 1.

Key insight: Expertise isn't about being smarter — it's about having a larger library of patterns from experience. Every patient you see adds to your pattern library. That's why clinical exposure matters.

🤔 Reflect

Think about a time you recognized something instantly (a friend's voice, a song from the first note, a type of car). That's System 1. Now think about the first time you encountered that thing — you had to learn it deliberately (System 2) before it became automatic.

2. The Differential Diagnosis

Physicians don't guess. They generate a list of possibilities — ranked by likelihood and danger — then systematically narrow it down. This list is the differential diagnosis.

🏥 Try It Yourself

A 25-year-old woman comes to the ED with acute right lower abdominal pain. Before reading further, what could this be?

✏️ Write your differential

The Physician's Differential (ranked):

Must rule out (dangerous): Appendicitis, ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion

Common: Ruptured ovarian cyst, UTI, kidney stone, gastroenteritis

Less likely but possible: Inflammatory bowel disease, PID, mesenteric lymphadenitis

Notice the hierarchy: dangerous causes first, then common causes, then rare. The first pregnancy test isn't because pregnancy is the most likely — it's because ectopic pregnancy can kill in hours. Danger drives the workup, not probability alone.

The framework: For any chief complaint, physicians ask: (1) What could kill this patient? (2) What's most common? (3) What fits this specific patient's risk factors? This hierarchy — danger → probability → fit — is the engine of clinical reasoning.

3. Bayesian Thinking

Every piece of data — a symptom, a test result, an exam finding — changes the probability of each diagnosis on your differential. This is Bayesian reasoning: updating your belief with each new piece of evidence.

🏥 Watch It In Action

Before history: 25-year-old woman, RLQ pain → Appendicitis ~30%, Ectopic ~15%, Ovarian cyst ~20%, UTI ~15%, Other ~20%

After history: Last period was 6 weeks ago, sexually active → Ectopic jumps to 40%, appendicitis drops

After pregnancy test: Positive → Ectopic now 60-70%. Appendicitis nearly eliminated.

After ultrasound: No intrauterine pregnancy + free fluid → Ectopic ~95%. Proceed to treatment.

Each test changes the probabilities. The physician didn't randomly order tests — each one was chosen because it would maximally shift the differential. This is the art: choosing the test that gives you the most information for your specific clinical question.

🤔 Why This Matters for You

4. When Thinking Goes Wrong: Cognitive Biases

The same System 1 that makes expert physicians fast also makes them vulnerable to errors. These are cognitive biases — systematic thinking mistakes that affect everyone, including the best physicians.

⚓ Anchoring Bias

Locking onto the first diagnosis and not updating when new information contradicts it.

"The ER doc diagnosed gastritis, so I didn't reconsider when the troponin came back elevated."

📊 Availability Bias

Overweighting diagnoses you've seen recently or that are vivid in memory.

"I just saw a PE last week, so now I'm ordering CT angiograms on every chest pain patient."

🏁 Premature Closure

Stopping the diagnostic process too early — accepting a diagnosis before ruling out alternatives.

"The patient has a UTI, which explains her confusion." (But the confusion was actually a stroke.)

🪞 Confirmation Bias

Seeking information that supports your working diagnosis while ignoring contradictory evidence.

"The X-ray is probably normal" — when you've already decided it's just a muscle strain.

🏷️ Diagnostic Momentum

Once a label is applied, it sticks. Each subsequent physician accepts the previous one's diagnosis without questioning it.

Patient labeled as "anxiety" in triage → every subsequent provider dismisses her tachycardia as anxiety → PE is missed.
The antidote to bias: Deliberately ask "What else could this be?" and "What would I expect to see if my diagnosis is WRONG?" These two questions are the most powerful debiasing tools in medicine.

5. Your Clinical Reasoning Starter Kit

You now understand the foundations of how physicians think. Here's how to apply this during your clinical experiences:

Before the encounter: For the chief complaint, what are the dangerous diagnoses I should know about? What's common? (Use the Encounter Companion to prep.)
During the encounter: Watch the physician update their thinking. What questions changed the differential? What tests were chosen and why? Can you track the probability shifts?
After the encounter: Could I explain the reasoning? What would I have missed? Did I notice any cognitive biases? What question should I ask next time?

🎯 Your Challenge

For your next 5 clinical encounters, try this: before the physician gives their assessment, write down YOUR top 3 diagnoses. Then compare to theirs. Track your accuracy. You'll be surprised how quickly you improve.
