MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

GERD

Transient lower esophageal sphincter relaxation → gastric acid reflux → esophageal mucosal injury. Risk factors: obesity, hiatal hernia, pregnancy, smoking, fatty foods, caffeine, alcohol, medications (CCBs, anticholinergics).

CLINICAL
Diagnosis & Complications

GERD Diagnosis

Clinical diagnosis (heartburn + regurgitation). Trial of PPI × 8 weeks. EGD if: alarm symptoms (dysphagia, weight loss, anemia, age >60), refractory symptoms, >5 years for Barrett screening.

Barrett Esophagus

⚠️ Intestinal metaplasia (squamous → columnar with goblet cells). Premalignant → dysplasia → esophageal adenocarcinoma. Surveillance EGD with biopsy per dysplasia grade. No dysplasia: every 3-5 years. Low-grade: every 6-12 months or ablation. High-grade: endoscopic eradication (RFA/EMR).

TREATMENT
Management

H. pylori Eradication

Triple therapy: PPI + clarithromycin + amoxicillin × 14 days (if local clarithromycin resistance <15%). Bismuth quadruple: PPI + bismuth + metronidazole + tetracycline × 14 days (if allergy or resistance). Always confirm eradication (urea breath test or stool antigen ≥4 weeks post-treatment).

PPI Considerations

🔑 Long-term PPI Risks: C. difficile infection, hypomagnesemia, B12 deficiency, calcium malabsorption → fractures, fundic gland polyps, CKD (rare), pneumonia. Use lowest effective dose. Step-down to H2RA if possible. PPIs are still safe and effective when indicated — benefits usually outweigh risks.

NSAID Ulcer Prevention

If NSAID needed: co-prescribe PPI. Misoprostol alternative (prostaglandin analog — contraindicated in pregnancy). Switch to COX-2 selective (celecoxib) + PPI for highest-risk patients.
