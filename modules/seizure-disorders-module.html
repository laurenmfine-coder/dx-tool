Seizure Disorders & Epilepsy

Seizure classification, status epilepticus management, AED selection, and first seizure evaluation.

📊 Classification & First Seizure

ILAE Seizure Classification (2017)

CategoryTypesKey Features
Focal (partial)Aware (simple) vs impaired awareness (complex)Starts in one hemisphere. Aware = consciousness preserved. Impaired awareness = altered consciousness, automatisms. Can be motor or non-motor.
Focal to bilateral tonic-clonicPreviously 'secondary generalized'Starts focal → spreads to bilateral convulsive. Witness may only see GTC.
GeneralizedAbsence, tonic-clonic, myoclonic, atonic, tonic, clonicBoth hemispheres from onset. Absence = staring + abrupt onset/offset, 3 Hz spike-wave on EEG.
Unknown onsetWhen beginning is not witnessedClassify when possible after further information/testing

First Seizure Evaluation

  • History: Witnesses, preceding events, aura, semiology, duration, post-ictal state, tongue biting, incontinence
  • Exam: Focal deficits (Todd paralysis), signs of head trauma, meningismus, papilledema
  • Labs: BMP (glucose, Na, Ca, Mg), CBC, toxicology screen, pregnancy test, prolactin (elevated 10-20 min post-GTC)
  • Imaging: MRI brain preferred (CT acutely if MRI unavailable or emergent)
  • EEG: Within 24-48 hours ideally. Epileptiform discharges increase recurrence risk.
  • When to treat after first seizure: If abnormal EEG, structural lesion, focal seizure, or family history of epilepsy → higher recurrence (~60-70%), favor starting AED.

🚨 Status Epilepticus

Definition & Timeline

  • Definition: Seizure lasting >5 minutes OR ≥2 seizures without return to baseline between them
  • Refractory: Continues despite two appropriate AEDs (usually after benzodiazepine + second-line agent)
  • Super-refractory: Continues ≥24 hours despite anesthetic agents
  • Mortality: 20-30% if untreated. Neuronal injury begins after ~30 minutes of continuous seizure activity.

Stepwise Management Protocol

TimeStageTreatment
0-5 minStabilizeABCs, O2, IV access, fingerstick glucose. Give thiamine + dextrose if hypoglycemic.
5-20 minFirst-line: BenzodiazepineIV lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg (max 4 mg, repeat once) OR IM midazolam 10 mg (if no IV). Can also use rectal diazepam.
20-40 minSecond-line: AED loadIV fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg OR IV levetiracetam 60 mg/kg OR IV valproate 40 mg/kg. Choose ONE.
40-60 minRefractory → RSI + anestheticIntubate. Propofol or midazolam drip. Continuous EEG monitoring. ICU admission.

⚠️ Do NOT Wait

Every minute of status epilepticus increases morbidity and resistance to treatment. Give benzodiazepines at 5 minutes — do not wait for IV access if unavailable (use IM midazolam, rectal diazepam, or intranasal midazolam). The RAMPART trial showed IM midazolam is noninferior to IV lorazepam.

AED Selection for Epilepsy

Seizure TypeFirst-Line AEDsImportant Notes
Focal ± bilateralLamotrigine, levetiracetam, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepineMost have focal seizures. Carbamazepine can worsen absence/myoclonic.
Generalized tonic-clonicLamotrigine, levetiracetam, valproateValproate most effective but teratogenic — avoid in women of childbearing age.
AbsenceEthosuximide (#1), valproate, lamotrigineEthosuximide ONLY works for absence. Carbamazepine/phenytoin WORSEN absence.
Juvenile myoclonic epilepsyValproate, levetiracetam, lamotrigineLifelong treatment usually required. Carbamazepine/phenytoin WORSEN JME.
