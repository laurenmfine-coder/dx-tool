MECHANISM
Classification

Personality disorders are enduring, inflexible, pervasive patterns of inner experience and behavior that deviate from cultural expectations, are stable from adolescence/early adulthood, and cause significant distress or impairment. Organized into three clusters:

ClusterDescriptorDisordersCommon Feature
A — "Weird"Odd/eccentricParanoid, Schizoid, SchizotypalSocial detachment, suspiciousness. Genetic link to schizophrenia (especially schizotypal)
B — "Wild"Dramatic/emotional/erraticAntisocial, Borderline, Histrionic, NarcissisticEmotional dysregulation, impulsivity, interpersonal drama. Most clinically encountered
C — "Worried"Anxious/fearfulAvoidant, Dependent, Obsessive-Compulsive (OCPD)Anxiety-driven behaviors. Cluster C is the most common cluster overall
🔑 Key Distinction: Personality disorders are ego-SYNTONIC (the person sees their behavior as normal — "this is just who I am"), unlike most Axis I disorders which are ego-DYSTONIC (OCD, depression = recognized as problematic by the patient). This makes personality disorders harder to treat because patients often don't see the problem.
CLINICAL
High-Yield Disorders

Cluster A Details

DisorderKey FeatureDistinction
ParanoidPervasive distrust and suspiciousness of othersInterprets motives as malevolent. Unlike psychosis: no frank hallucinations or delusions
SchizoidVoluntary social detachment, restricted emotional rangePrefers isolation but NOT anxious about it (vs. avoidant PD who WANTS connections but fears rejection)
SchizotypalOdd beliefs, magical thinking, perceptual disturbances, eccentric behaviorQuasi-psychotic features. Genetic link to schizophrenia. May progress to schizophrenia

Cluster B Details

DisorderKey Features
Borderline (BPD)Unstable relationships + self-image + mood. Fear of abandonment, splitting (idealization/devaluation), chronic emptiness, impulsive self-harm, identity disturbance. 75% female. High suicide rate (8-10%)
Antisocial (ASPD)Disregard for rights of others, deceit, impulsivity, aggression, lack of remorse. Must be ≥18 with evidence of conduct disorder before 15. 75% male
Narcissistic (NPD)Grandiosity, need for admiration, lack of empathy. Fragile self-esteem beneath grandiose exterior. Vulnerable to "narcissistic injury" (criticism) → rage
Histrionic (HPD)Excessive emotionality, attention-seeking. Uncomfortable when not center of attention. Seductive behavior, dramatic, impressionistic speech
⚠️ Borderline PD — Highest-Yield: Defense mechanism: SPLITTING (viewing people as all good or all bad). Relationships are intense but unstable. Chronic suicidality and self-harm are common. Treatment of choice: Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT). Medications target symptoms, not the disorder itself. Frequently comorbid with MDD, PTSD, substance use, eating disorders.

Cluster C Details

DisorderKey FeatureImportant Distinction
AvoidantSocial inhibition, feelings of inadequacy, hypersensitivity to criticismDESIRES relationships but avoids due to fear of rejection (unlike schizoid who doesn't want them)
DependentExcessive need to be taken care of, submissive, clinging, fear of separationUrgently seeks new relationship when one ends. Cannot make decisions alone
OCPDPreoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, controlEgo-SYNTONIC (unlike OCD which is ego-dystonic). No obsessions/compulsions. Values rules/order

Management
DisorderPrimary TreatmentMedications
Borderline PDDBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy) — gold standard. Teaches mindfulness, distress tolerance, emotion regulation, interpersonal effectivenessSymptom-targeted only: SSRIs (mood), mood stabilizers (impulsivity), low-dose antipsychotics (transient psychosis). No FDA-approved medication for BPD
Antisocial PDMost treatment-resistant PD. CBT may help. Limit-setting importantNo proven pharmacotherapy
Avoidant PDCBT + exposure therapy (similar to social anxiety)SSRIs may help (overlap with social anxiety)
OCPDCBTSSRIs if comorbid anxiety/depression
Cluster ASupportive therapy. Low-dose antipsychotics for schizotypalTarget perceptual disturbances if present
🔑 Countertransference Clues: If you feel: angry/frustrated → consider BPD or ASPD. Heroic/rescuing → consider dependent PD. Special/unique with the patient → consider NPD or HPD. Suspicious of the patient → consider paranoid PD. These emotional reactions can help identify the personality pattern.
