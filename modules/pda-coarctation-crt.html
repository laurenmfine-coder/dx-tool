Teaching Point

Ductus arteriosus: Derived from the LEFT 6th aortic arch artery. In fetal life, it diverts ~90% of RV output away from the high-resistance pulmonary circulation → descending aorta. Maintained patent by PGE₂ (from placenta) and low O₂. After birth: ↑O₂ → ductal smooth muscle constriction (functional closure at 12-24h). ↓PGE₂ (cord clamping) → removes dilating stimulus. Anatomic closure (fibrosis → ligamentum arteriosum) by 2-3 weeks.

Teaching Point

PDA pharmacology: TO CLOSE → indomethacin or ibuprofen (COX inhibitors → ↓PGE₂). TO KEEP OPEN → PGE₁ (alprostadil) — used for duct-dependent lesions. Duct-dependent lesions include: critical coarctation, interrupted aortic arch, HLHS, pulmonary atresia, critical aortic stenosis, TGA. Mnemonic: 'IndoCLOSE' (indomethacin closes), 'ProstOPEN' (prostaglandin opens).

Teaching Point

Recurrent laryngeal nerve anatomy (high yield!): The LEFT recurrent laryngeal loops under the ligamentum arteriosum (left 6th arch derivative) → back up to the larynx. The RIGHT recurrent laryngeal loops under the right subclavian artery (right 4th arch derivative). With an aberrant right subclavian (from distal aorta), the RIGHT recurrent laryngeal nerve doesn't recur — it goes directly to the larynx (non-recurrent), which is a surgical hazard during thyroid surgery.

Teaching Point

Duct-dependent lesions — TWO categories: (1) DUCT-DEPENDENT SYSTEMIC CIRCULATION: coarctation, interrupted aortic arch, HLHS, critical aortic stenosis → PDA provides systemic blood flow via PA → ductus → descending aorta. Collapse when ductus closes. (2) DUCT-DEPENDENT PULMONARY CIRCULATION: pulmonary atresia, critical pulmonary stenosis, tricuspid atresia → PDA provides pulmonary blood flow via aorta → ductus → PA. Severe cyanosis when ductus closes.

Teaching Point

Coarctation associations: (1) Bicuspid aortic valve (50-85%); (2) Turner syndrome (45,X) — 10-20% of Turner patients have coarctation; (3) Berry aneurysms of the circle of Willis (risk of subarachnoid hemorrhage); (4) VSD; (5) PDA. Physical exam: upper extremity hypertension + radiofemoral delay or absent femoral pulses + BP gradient >20 mmHg between arms and legs. CXR: rib notching (from intercostal collateral arteries — NOT seen in neonates, develops over years), '3 sign' (pre-stenotic dilation + coarctation + post-stenotic dilation).

Teaching Point

Two coarctation theories: (1) DUCTUS TISSUE THEORY — ectopic ductal smooth muscle in the aortic wall constricts when the ductus closes. Explains juxtaductal location and postnatal presentation. (2) HEMODYNAMIC THEORY — decreased fetal blood flow through the aortic isthmus (from left-sided obstructive lesions like aortic stenosis or VSD diverting flow) → poor growth of the isthmus → coarctation. Explains association with other left-sided obstructive lesions. Both theories likely contribute.

Stage 7 of 7
🎓 Integration & Key Takeaways
The premature infant's PDA closes with indomethacin. The coarctation baby undergoes successful surgical repair (resection with end-to-end anastomosis). Both do well at follow-up.

Key Takeaways

✓Ductus arteriosus = distal LEFT 6th aortic arch; closes by ↑O₂ + ↓PGE₂
✓PDA common in prematurity: immature smooth muscle + high prostaglandins
✓Close PDA: indomethacin/ibuprofen (COX inhibitors → ↓PGE₂)
✓Keep PDA open: PGE₁ (alprostadil) for duct-dependent lesions
✓Duct-dependent systemic: coarctation, interrupted arch, HLHS, critical AS
✓Duct-dependent pulmonary: pulmonary atresia, critical PS, tricuspid atresia
✓Coarctation: juxtaductal, bicuspid AV (50-85%), Turner syndrome, rib notching
✓Left recurrent laryngeal nerve loops under ligamentum arteriosum (left 6th arch)
