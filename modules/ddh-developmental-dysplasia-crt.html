📖 Teaching Point

DDH embryology: The acetabulum and femoral head develop from the same mesenchymal condensation. The acetabulum forms around the femoral head — it needs the head's presence to develop properly (like a cup molding around a ball). If the head is displaced during the critical window of development, the acetabulum becomes shallow (dysplastic). Risk factors: female (6:1), breech, firstborn, family history, left hip (left occiput anterior position compresses the left hip against the sacrum).

📖 Teaching Point

DDH screening: Ultrasound is the gold standard until 4-6 months (cartilaginous femoral head). After 6 months, the ossification center appears and X-ray becomes useful. On X-ray, Hilgenreiner's line (horizontal through triradiate cartilage) and Perkin's line (vertical from lateral acetabulum) create quadrants — the femoral head ossification center should be in the inferomedial quadrant. The acetabular index measures acetabular slope (normal <30° at birth).

📖 Teaching Point

Pavlik harness success rate: ~90-95% for Barlow-positive (dislocatable) hips and ~80% for Ortolani-positive (dislocated but reducible) hips when started before 6 months. The harness fails if: the hip is irreducible, treatment is started after 6 months, or there is incorrect harness application. Key complication to avoid: forced abduction → femoral head AVN. Success is confirmed by ultrasound at 6 weeks showing alpha angle improvement.

📖 Teaching Point

DDH treatment ladder: (1) Pavlik harness (0-6 months); (2) Closed reduction + spica cast (6-18 months); (3) Open reduction ± femoral/pelvic osteotomy (>18 months or failed closed reduction). The older the child at treatment, the more invasive the intervention needed and the worse the outcomes. This is why screening matters. AVN risk is minimized by avoiding forced abduction, using the 'safe zone' of Ramsey (range between re-dislocation and forced abduction).

📖 Teaching Point

Late-presenting DDH signs: painless waddling gait (bilateral) or Trendelenburg gait (unilateral), leg length discrepancy, limited abduction (<60°), asymmetric skin folds. Galeazzi sign: with hips and knees flexed, the knee on the affected side is lower. Late diagnosis requires open reduction ± osteotomy and has worse outcomes than early detection. This is why universal screening with newborn hip exams is critical.

Stage 6 of 6
🎓 Integration & Key Takeaways
The late-presenting child undergoes open reduction with femoral shortening osteotomy. At age 5, she has good hip range of motion but will need long-term monitoring for AVN and early osteoarthritis.

Key Takeaways

✓The acetabulum needs the femoral head present to develop — the ball molds the cup
✓Risk factors: Female (6:1), breech, firstborn, family history, left hip
✓Ortolani = 'entry' (reduces dislocated hip); Barlow = 'exit' (dislocates reducible hip)
✓Ultrasound before 4-6 months; X-ray after (cartilaginous → ossified femoral head)
✓Pavlik harness works by maintaining concentric reduction → acetabular remodeling
✓AVN of the femoral head is the most feared complication
✓Treatment ladder: Pavlik → closed reduction + cast → open reduction + osteotomy
