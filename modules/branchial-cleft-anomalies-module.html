🔬 Branchial Cleft Anomalies

Cysts, Sinuses & Fistulae | Location-Based Diagnosis

📚 Overview

Branchial Cleft Anomalies result from incomplete obliteration of pharyngeal clefts or pouches. They present as cysts, sinuses, or fistulae at characteristic neck locations.

Terminology

TermDefinition
CystEnclosed cavity, no external opening
SinusBlind-ending tract, ONE opening
FistulaComplete tract, TWO openings (skin to pharynx)

Frequency

  • 2nd cleft: 90-95% (most common!)
  • 1st cleft: 5-8%
  • 3rd & 4th cleft: <2% combined

1️⃣ First Branchial Cleft

Location: Periauricular, parotid region
Internal opening: External auditory canal (EAC)
Key concern: Facial nerve relationship

Type I (Ectodermal only)

Duplication of EAC, pre/post-auricular, usually superficial to facial nerve

Type II (Ecto + Mesodermal)

Contains cartilage, passes through parotid, INTIMATE with facial nerve

⚠️ Surgery requires facial nerve dissection! Type II tracts may pass superficial, deep, or through nerve branches.

2️⃣ Second Branchial Cleft (90-95%)

Location: Anterior border of SCM (junction upper/middle 1/3)
Internal opening: Tonsillar fossa
Tract course: Between ICA and ECA

Classic Tract Path

  1. External: Anterior border of SCM
  2. Deep to platysma
  3. Between ICA and ECA
  4. Over CN XII and CN IX
  5. Internal: Tonsillar fossa
Presentation: Painless lateral neck mass, may enlarge with URI. History of "recurrent neck abscess" in same location.

3️⃣ Third Branchial Cleft

Location: Lower neck, anterior to SCM
Internal opening: Pyriform sinus
Key relationship: ABOVE superior laryngeal nerve
  • Presents with recurrent suppurative thyroiditis
  • Left side predominant (>90%)
  • Passes posterior to ICA

4️⃣ Fourth Branchial Cleft

Location: Lower neck, thyroid area
Internal opening: Apex of pyriform sinus
Key relationship: BELOW superior laryngeal nerve
  • Very rare
  • Left side: loops under aortic arch
  • Presents as recurrent thyroid abscess in children
3rd vs 4th: Both → pyriform sinus. 3rd = ABOVE SLN. 4th = BELOW SLN.

🔪 Management

Complete surgical excision is treatment of choice.
  • Wait until acute infection resolves
  • Excise entire tract to prevent recurrence
  • 3rd/4th: Consider endoscopic cauterization of internal opening
⚠️ Adults >40: New "branchial cyst" may be cystic SCC metastasis. Evaluate oropharynx!

🧠 Quick Quiz

A child has a lateral neck mass at the anterior border of SCM. Most likely diagnosis?

A. Second branchial cleft cyst
B. Thyroglossal duct cyst
C. First branchial cleft cyst
D. Dermoid cyst
Correct: A - Second branchial cleft cysts are 90-95% of branchial anomalies and present at the anterior border of SCM. Thyroglossal cysts are midline.
