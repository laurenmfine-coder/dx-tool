⬆️ Vesicoureteral Reflux

Diagnosis, Grading & Evidence-Based Management

📚 Overview

Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR) is the retrograde flow of urine from the bladder into the ureter and potentially the kidney. It is the most common urological anomaly in children.

🧬 Embryology & Pathophysiology

Normal Ureterovesical Junction (UVJ)

The ureter enters the bladder at an oblique angle and passes through the bladder wall for a distance (intramural segment). During bladder filling/voiding, the intramural ureter is compressed, creating a one-way flap valve that prevents reflux.

Embryologic Basis of Primary VUR

URETERIC BUD originates from Wolffian duct ↓ Position of origin determines final ureteral orifice location ↓ ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ NORMAL: Bud arises at correct position │ │ → Long intramural tunnel → Competent valve │ │ │ │ ABNORMAL: Bud arises too caudal (close to bladder) │ │ → Short intramural tunnel → SHORT = REFLUX │ │ → Lateral, gaping orifice position │ └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Types of VUR

TypeCauseResolution
Primary VURCongenital short intramural ureterMay resolve as bladder grows
Secondary VURElevated bladder pressure (PUV, neurogenic bladder)Requires treating underlying cause
Why VUR May Resolve: As the child grows, the bladder enlarges and the intramural ureteral tunnel lengthens, improving the valve mechanism. This is more likely with lower grades and younger age.

📊 Grading System (International Classification)

VUR is graded I-V based on VCUG findings. Grade correlates with prognosis and guides management.

Grade I

Reflux into non-dilated ureter only (doesn't reach renal pelvis)

Spontaneous resolution: >90%

Grade II

Reflux reaches renal pelvis; NO dilation of collecting system

Spontaneous resolution: ~80%

Grade III

Mild to moderate dilation of ureter and renal pelvis; minimal blunting of fornices

Spontaneous resolution: ~50%

Grade IV

Moderate dilation; tortuous ureter; blunted fornices but papillary impressions visible

Spontaneous resolution: ~30%

Grade V

Severe dilation; tortuous ureter; loss of papillary impressions; intrarenal reflux

Spontaneous resolution: Rare; usually requires surgery

🔍 Diagnosis

When to Evaluate for VUR

  • Febrile UTI in children (especially <2 years)
  • Prenatal hydronephrosis
  • Recurrent UTIs
  • Sibling of child with VUR (consider screening)
  • Renal scarring on imaging

Imaging Studies

StudyPurposeNotes
VCUGGold standard for diagnosis & gradingFluoroscopic; catheterization required
Radionuclide cystogram (RNC)Follow-up; lower radiationLess anatomic detail
Renal ultrasoundScreen for hydronephrosis, scarringDoes NOT diagnose VUR
DMSA scanDetect renal scarringGold standard for scarring
⚠️ Timing of VCUG: Wait until UTI is treated (4-6 weeks) to avoid false elevation of grade from inflammation. Exception: Obtain sooner if clinical concern for obstruction.

⚠️ Why VUR Matters

Reflux Nephropathy

VUR + UTI (bacteria) → Pyelonephritis ↓ Renal parenchymal infection ↓ RENAL SCARRING ↓ Complications: • Hypertension • Proteinuria • Chronic kidney disease • ESRD (in severe bilateral cases)
Key Concept: VUR alone (sterile reflux) rarely causes damage. The combination of VUR + infected urine leads to renal scarring. This is why preventing UTIs is a major treatment goal.

🔪 Management

Goals of Treatment

  1. Prevent febrile UTIs (pyelonephritis)
  2. Prevent renal scarring
  3. Allow time for spontaneous resolution (in appropriate cases)
  4. Preserve renal function

Management Options

ApproachDescriptionIndication
ObservationWatchful waiting, prompt UTI treatmentLow grade, no scarring, older child
CAPContinuous antibiotic prophylaxisYoung children, high grade, breakthrough UTIs
Endoscopic injectionDeflux (dextranomer/hyaluronic acid) injectionModerate VUR, alternative to surgery
Surgical reimplantationUreteral reimplantation (open or robotic)High grade, failed medical management, scarring

Continuous Antibiotic Prophylaxis (CAP)

Common agents:
  • TMP-SMX (after 2 months of age)
  • Nitrofurantoin (after 1 month)
  • Amoxicillin (neonates)

Given at 1/4 to 1/3 of therapeutic dose, once daily at bedtime

Surgical Options

Ureteral Reimplantation: 95-98% success rate
  • Open: Cohen cross-trigonal, Politano-Leadbetter
  • Robotic/laparoscopic: Growing use
Endoscopic (Deflux): 70-85% success (may need repeat)
  • Outpatient procedure
  • Lower success for high grades

Management by Grade (General Approach)

GradeTypical Approach
I-IIObservation or CAP; high spontaneous resolution
IIICAP; consider intervention if breakthrough UTIs
IV-VCAP initially; surgery if persistent or breakthrough UTIs; consider early surgery

📋 Key Clinical Pearls

RIVUR Trial (2014): Showed CAP reduced UTI recurrence by 50% in children with VUR after first febrile UTI. BUT no significant difference in renal scarring rates.
⚠️ Screen siblings? AAP does NOT recommend routine VCUG screening of asymptomatic siblings. Consider renal ultrasound; VCUG if abnormal or concerns.
Bottom Line:
  • VUR is common; most low grades resolve spontaneously
  • VUR + UTI → scarring risk
  • Treatment goal: Prevent febrile UTIs
  • Multiple management strategies; individualize

🧠 Quick Quiz

A 2-year-old girl has her first febrile UTI. VCUG shows reflux reaching a mildly dilated renal pelvis with minimal calyceal blunting. What grade is this?

A. Grade I
B. Grade II
C. Grade III
D. Grade IV
Correct: C - Grade III
Grade III VUR shows mild to moderate dilation of the ureter and renal pelvis with minimal blunting of the fornices. Grade II would show no dilation. Grade IV would show moderate dilation with clearly blunted fornices.
