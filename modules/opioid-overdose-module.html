MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

Opioids bind mu (μ) receptors → CNS depression, respiratory depression, miosis. Overdose = respiratory failure → hypoxic cardiac arrest. Fentanyl now #1 cause of opioid overdose deaths (50-100× more potent than morphine).

FeatureDetails
Classic triadCNS depression + respiratory depression + miosis (pinpoint pupils)
Mechanism of deathRespiratory arrest (μ-receptors in brainstem respiratory center)
High-risk agentsFentanyl (rapid onset, potent), heroin, methadone (long half-life → delayed toxicity), extended-release opioids
🔑 Exceptions to miosis: Meperidine (normeperidine causes mydriasis + seizures), tramadol (seizures + serotonin syndrome), mixed overdose (co-ingested sympathomimetics), severe hypoxia/brain death (fixed dilated pupils).
CLINICAL
Diagnosis & Complications

Complications of Opioid Overdose

Aspiration pneumonia/pneumonitis, non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema (NCPE — especially heroin/methadone), rhabdomyolysis (prolonged immobilization), compartment syndrome, anoxic brain injury, death.

⚠️ Methadone: Long half-life (24-36h) → overdose effects may outlast naloxone. Also prolongs QTc → torsades de pointes risk. Requires prolonged observation/repeated naloxone dosing or infusion.
TREATMENT
Naloxone (Narcan)
FeatureDetails
MechanismCompetitive mu-receptor antagonist
Dose0.04-0.4 mg IV initial (titrate up). 2-4 mg IN (intranasal). Goal: restore respiration, NOT full consciousness
Onset/DurationIV: 1-2 min. Duration: 30-90 min (shorter than most opioids → risk of re-sedation)
Repeat dosingMay repeat q2-3 min up to 10 mg total. If no response after 10 mg → reconsider diagnosis
🔑 Titrate naloxone to respiratory effort, NOT consciousness. Goal: RR >12, adequate SpO2. Full reversal in opioid-dependent patients → acute withdrawal (agitation, vomiting, diarrhea, piloerection, tachycardia). While uncomfortable, opioid withdrawal is NOT life-threatening (unlike alcohol/benzo withdrawal).
⚠️ Re-sedation risk: Naloxone duration (30-90 min) < most opioid durations. Observe ≥4h after last naloxone dose for short-acting opioids. Extended observation (≥24h) for methadone, extended-release formulations, fentanyl patches. Consider naloxone infusion (2/3 of effective bolus dose per hour).
