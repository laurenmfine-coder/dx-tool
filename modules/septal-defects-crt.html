Teaching Point

VSD is the MOST COMMON congenital heart defect, occurring in ~2-5/1000 live births. The interventricular septum has two components: the large muscular septum (grows up from the floor) and the small membranous septum (closes the remaining gap by fusion of endocardial cushions + muscular septum + aorticopulmonary septum). Membranous VSDs (~80%) are most common because they require three components to fuse correctly.

Teaching Point

VSD classification by location: PERIMEMBRANOUS (80%) — at the membranous septum, adjacent to the aortic valve. MUSCULAR (5-20%) — within the muscular septum, highest spontaneous closure rate. INLET (5-8%) — beneath the AV valves, part of the endocardial cushion defect spectrum. OUTLET/SUPRACRISTAL (5-7%) — below the semilunar valves, risk of aortic regurgitation from lack of support.

Teaching Point

VSD hemodynamics by size: SMALL (restrictive): high velocity jet, loud murmur (paradoxically louder than large VSDs), minimal hemodynamic significance. MODERATE: some volume overload, may cause symptoms. LARGE (nonrestrictive): RV pressure = LV pressure, shunt determined by PVR:SVR ratio. Complications of large L→R shunts: CHF (volume overload), failure to thrive, recurrent respiratory infections, and ultimately Eisenmenger syndrome if uncorrected.

Teaching Point

Eisenmenger syndrome timeline: L→R shunt → years of pulmonary overcirculation → pulmonary vascular remodeling (Heath-Edwards classification: Grade 1 = medial hypertrophy → Grade 6 = necrotizing arteritis) → PVR ↑↑ → shunt reverses to R→L → cyanosis. Management: avoid pregnancy (30-50% maternal mortality), avoid dehydration/anesthesia risks. Heart-lung transplant is the only definitive option. Prevention: early surgical repair of large VSDs before irreversible vascular changes develop (usually before age 2).

Teaching Point

Atrial septation review: (1) Septum primum grows DOWN toward endocardial cushions; the shrinking gap below = ostium primum. (2) Before ostium primum closes, perforations in septum primum coalesce → ostium secundum. (3) Septum secundum grows DOWN to the RIGHT of septum primum, covering most of ostium secundum but leaving the foramen ovale. (4) Septum primum acts as a flap valve. OSTIUM SECUNDUM ASD (~70% of ASDs) = excessive resorption of septum primum OR inadequate growth of septum secundum → the flap is too small to cover the foramen ovale.

Teaching Point

AVSD spectrum: PARTIAL (ostium primum ASD + cleft mitral valve, no VSD) → TRANSITIONAL → COMPLETE (primum ASD + inlet VSD + single common AV valve). T21 association: ~40-50% have CHD; AVSD accounts for ~40% of these. Non-Down AVSD often associated with heterotaxy syndromes. Surgical repair at 3-6 months before irreversible pulmonary vascular changes. Complete repair involves patching ASD + VSD and creating two separate AV valves from the common valve.

Key Takeaways

✓VSD = most common CHD; membranous type most common (80%)
✓Membranous septum requires fusion of: endocardial cushions + muscular IVS + AP septum
✓VSD shunt direction determined by PVR vs SVR (normally L→R)
✓Eisenmenger = irreversible pulmonary vascular remodeling → shunt reversal → cyanosis
✓ASD: fixed split S2 is pathognomonic; ostium secundum most common type
✓AVSD = endocardial cushion failure → primum ASD + inlet VSD + common AV valve
✓Down syndrome (T21): AVSD is the most characteristic cardiac defect
