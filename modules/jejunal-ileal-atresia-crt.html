📖 Teaching Point

Intestinal atresia X-ray patterns: Duodenal = double bubble (2 air collections, no distal gas). Jejunal = triple bubble or few dilated loops. Ileal = many dilated loops. The number of dilated loops increases with more distal obstruction. A critical distinction: duodenal atresia often has calcifications or microcolon on contrast enema, whereas jejunal/ileal atresia shows a microcolon (unused distal colon).

📖 Teaching Point

Two fundamentally different mechanisms: DUODENAL atresia = intrinsic (failed recanalization, weeks 8-10) → associated with T21, cardiac, annular pancreas. JEJUNAL/ILEAL atresia = extrinsic (mesenteric vascular accident, any gestational age) → isolated finding, no chromosomal association. Causes of in utero vascular accident: volvulus, intussusception, internal hernia, mesenteric vascular thrombosis. The evidence: experimental ligation of mesenteric vessels in animal models reproduces the exact types of atresia seen clinically.

📖 Teaching Point

Jejunal/ileal atresia types: Type 1 = intraluminal membrane (bowel in continuity). Type 2 = blind ends connected by fibrous cord (mesentery intact). Type 3a = complete separation with mesenteric V-shaped gap. Type 3b = apple peel (proximal jejunal atresia, absent distal SMA, bowel spirals around marginal vessel). Type 4 = multiple atresias (sausage-link pattern). Type 3b has worst prognosis — short bowel syndrome risk, marginal blood supply to remaining bowel, often familial (autosomal recessive).

📖 Teaching Point

Surgical principles for intestinal atresia: 1) Resect or taper the dilated proximal segment (dysmotile). 2) Assess total remaining bowel length (normal neonate: ~250 cm small bowel). 3) Size-discrepant anastomosis techniques: end-to-oblique, end-to-back, or Bishop-Koop. 4) Check for additional atresias distally (inject saline through distal lumen). 5) Preserve every centimeter of bowel — especially ileocecal valve (critical for preventing bacterial overgrowth).

📖 Teaching Point

Short bowel syndrome (SBS): Defined functionally as inability to maintain nutrition/hydration without parenteral support. Neonatal SBS threshold: <75 cm small bowel without ICV, or <40 cm with ICV. The ileocecal valve prevents colonic bacteria from refluxing into the small bowel (bacterial overgrowth) and slows transit. Intestinal adaptation: The remaining bowel undergoes villous hypertrophy over months-years, increasing absorptive surface. Microcolon: Not pathological — simply reflects disuse. It grows to normal caliber once enteral feeding begins.
