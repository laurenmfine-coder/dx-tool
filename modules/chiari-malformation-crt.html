📖 Teaching Point

Chiari malformation spectrum: Type I = tonsillar herniation ≥5mm, often acquired/developmental, presents in adolescence/adulthood. Type II = tonsillar + vermian + brainstem herniation, ALWAYS with myelomeningocele, presents in infancy. Type III = encephalocele with cerebellar herniation (rare, severe). Type IV = cerebellar hypoplasia (some question whether this truly exists as a separate entity).

📖 Teaching Point

Chiari I vs II embryology: CHIARI I — primary bone defect (small posterior fossa from underdeveloped occipital somites). The cerebellum has nowhere to go but down. CHIARI II — the open myelomeningocele allows CSF to drain from the ventricles through the spinal defect during fetal development → ventricles don't expand normally → posterior fossa doesn't get the hydraulic stimulus to grow → small fossa + hindbrain herniation. This is why fetal MMC repair (MOMS trial) reduces the severity of Chiari II.

📖 Teaching Point

Syringomyelia in Chiari I: occurs in 25-75% of cases. Mechanism: tonsillar herniation obstructs CSF flow at the foramen magnum → pressure differentials drive CSF into the central canal → syrinx formation. The expanding syrinx damages structures from inside out: (1) First: anterior commissure → dissociated sensory loss (pain/temp); (2) Next: anterior horn cells → LMN weakness, atrophy; (3) Later: lateral columns → UMN signs below the lesion.

📖 Teaching Point

Surgical indications for Chiari I: symptomatic (headaches, neurological deficits) or progressive syringomyelia. Asymptomatic Chiari I found incidentally does NOT require surgery — just monitoring. Post-op outcomes: headache improves in 80-90%, syrinx stabilizes/decreases in 70-80%, sensory deficits may or may not recover (depends on duration). Complications: CSF leak, pseudomeningocele, aseptic meningitis.

📖 Teaching Point

Chiari II brainstem crisis in neonates: This is the leading cause of death in myelomeningocele patients in the first 2 years. Signs: stridor (bilateral vocal cord paralysis), apnea (central or obstructive), dysphagia/aspiration, opisthotonus. Management: ensure VP shunt is working first → if shunt is functional, posterior fossa decompression is needed. Even with surgery, outcomes are guarded — some brainstem damage may be irreversible.

Stage 6 of 6
🎓 Integration & Key Takeaways
The 12-year-old recovers well from PF decompression. At 6 months, her headaches have resolved and the syrinx has decreased by 50%. The neonate with Chiari II undergoes urgent decompression with partial improvement.

Key Takeaways

✓Chiari I = tonsillar herniation ≥5mm, small posterior fossa (occipital somite underdevelopment)
✓Chiari II = vermis + brainstem herniation, ALWAYS with myelomeningocele
✓Classic Chiari I: occipital headache worsened by Valsalva + syringomyelia
✓Syringomyelia causes dissociated sensory loss (crossing spinothalamic fibers disrupted)
✓Surgery goal: restore CSF flow, not drain the syrinx directly
✓Chiari II brainstem crisis: stridor + apnea + dysphagia = neurosurgical emergency
✓Asymptomatic Chiari I = observation, NOT surgery
