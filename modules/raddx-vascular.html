Compression UltrasoundDeep Vein Thrombosis
Clinical Vignette
A 42-year-old woman presents with 3 days of progressive left calf swelling, warmth, and pain. She returned from a 14-hour flight from Tokyo 5 days ago. She takes combined oral contraceptives and smokes half a pack per day. BMI 34. On exam: left calf circumference 3 cm greater than right, positive Homan's sign, pitting edema to mid-calf.
A.Long flights rarely cause DVT
B.OCPs are the only relevant risk factor
C.Smoking does not affect clotting risk
D.Multiplicative risk factors: OCPs (3-4× DVT risk, especially with estrogen), prolonged air travel >4 hours (2-3× risk from immobility + dehydration + cabin pressure), smoking (activates coagulation cascade + endothelial damage), obesity (BMI >30 = 2-3× risk from venous stasis and chronic inflammation). These risks are MULTIPLICATIVE, not additive — her combined risk is dramatically higher than any single factor. Wells score should be calculated to guide workup
VTE Risk Factor Epidemiology

VTE affects ~1-2 per 1,000 adults annually in the US, with incidence rising exponentially with age. Risk factors are multiplicative: estrogen-containing contraceptives (3-4×, higher with third-generation progestins and drospirenone), immobility/travel >4 hours (economy class syndrome), surgery within 3 months, active cancer (4-7×), pregnancy/postpartum (5×), obesity, smoking, thrombophilia (Factor V Leiden in 5% of Caucasians, prothrombin gene mutation). The Wells DVT score integrates clinical probability: active cancer (+1), paralysis/immobilization (+1), bedridden >3 days (+1), localized tenderness (+1), entire leg swollen (+1), calf >3cm larger (+1), pitting edema (+1), collateral veins (+1), prior DVT (+1), alternative diagnosis as likely (−2).

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: The intersection of OCPs + smoking + obesity is one of the most common preventable VTE scenarios in young women. Combined OCPs are CONTRAINDICATED with BMI >35 or in smokers >35 years old per CDC Medical Eligibility Criteria. Progestin-only methods (IUD, implant) do not increase VTE risk and are the appropriate alternative. Counseling about VTE risk should be standard at every OCP prescription.
A.Compression ultrasound of the left lower extremity — the first-line diagnostic study for suspected DVT (sensitivity >95% for proximal DVT)
B.CT venography
C.D-dimer first in all patients
D.MRI venography
Wells Score Guides the Algorithm

The diagnostic algorithm depends on the Wells DVT score: Score ≥2 (DVT likely): proceed directly to compression US (D-dimer is unnecessary — it will be positive and wastes time). Score <2 (DVT unlikely): D-dimer first — if negative, DVT is effectively excluded; if positive, proceed to compression US. This patient has multiple positive Wells criteria (calf >3cm, pitting edema, localized tenderness, recent travel) → Wells ≥2 → go directly to US. Compression US evaluates common femoral, femoral, popliteal, and calf veins — a vein that does not fully compress with probe pressure contains thrombus.

A.Normal compressibility of all veins — DVT excluded
B.Non-compressible left popliteal vein or femoral vein with intraluminal echogenic material — confirming acute proximal DVT. Augmentation flow may be absent or diminished
C.Superficial thrombophlebitis only
D.Baker's cyst mimicking DVT
Acute DVT Ultrasound Findings

Acute DVT on compression US: the vein does not compress fully when pressure is applied with the probe (the hallmark finding). Additional signs: visible intraluminal thrombus (echogenic material), distended vein, absent augmentation (no flow increase with distal compression), and loss of normal phasicity with respiration. Acute vs chronic DVT: acute thrombus is hypoechoic (dark) and the vein is distended; chronic thrombus is echogenic (bright), the vein is contracted, and there may be collateral flow.

A.Aspirin and compression stockings only
B.Warfarin alone starting immediately
C.Proximal DVT confirmed → start anticoagulation immediately (DOAC preferred: rivaroxaban or apixaban as monotherapy, or LMWH bridged to warfarin). Assess for PE symptoms (dyspnea, tachycardia, pleuritic pain). Consider provoked vs unprovoked classification for treatment duration planning
D.IVC filter placement for all DVTs
Anticoagulation — Immediate and Duration-Dependent

Treatment initiation should not wait for specialist consultation. First-line: DOACs (rivaroxaban 15mg BID × 21 days then 20mg daily, or apixaban 10mg BID × 7 days then 5mg BID). Duration: provoked DVT (identifiable transient risk factor like travel, surgery, OCP) → 3 months. Unprovoked or recurrent → consider indefinite anticoagulation. IVC filter: only if anticoagulation is absolutely contraindicated (active hemorrhage, recent neurosurgery).

A.Anticoagulation for 3 months then stop
B.Anticoagulation and no lifestyle changes
C.Heparin drip and admission for all DVTs
D.Start DOAC + STOP combined OCPs immediately (switch to progestin-only or non-hormonal contraception) + smoking cessation counseling + weight management referral + assess for PE symptoms + provoked DVT (travel + OCPs) → plan for 3-month anticoagulation + thrombophilia testing after anticoagulation complete if considering future estrogen use + travel prophylaxis education (compression stockings, hydration, mobility for future flights)
Treat + Remove Modifiable Risk Factors

The US finding triggered anticoagulation, but the comprehensive plan removes every modifiable risk factor: OCPs stopped (the single most impactful intervention for recurrence prevention), smoking cessation, weight management, and travel prophylaxis education. Thrombophilia testing is NOT recommended during acute treatment (anticoagulation affects results) but may be considered later if results would change management (e.g., deciding about future estrogen use). This is a classic 'provoked' DVT with excellent prognosis if risk factors are addressed.

Ultrasound Screening → CTAAbdominal Aortic Aneurysm
Clinical Vignette
A 68-year-old male with a 45-pack-year smoking history presents for a routine visit. He has hypertension and mild COPD. He has never been screened for abdominal aortic aneurysm. During the visit, you palpate a pulsatile, non-tender abdominal mass. He is hemodynamically stable with no pain.
A.AAA only affects patients with known vascular disease
B.Screening is recommended for all adults over 50
C.USPSTF recommends one-time AAA screening ultrasound for men aged 65-75 who have EVER smoked. This patient is 68 with 45 pack-years — he should have been screened at 65. Smoking is by far the strongest risk factor (5-8× risk), followed by male sex (6:1 M:F), age >65, family history (first-degree relative with AAA doubles risk), hypertension, and Caucasian race. Ruptured AAA has 80-90% mortality — screening prevents death
D.A palpable mass always indicates rupture
AAA Screening Epidemiology

AAA (≥3.0 cm aortic diameter) is present in 4-8% of men >65 who have ever smoked. USPSTF screening recommendation (Grade B): one-time abdominal US for men aged 65-75 who have ever smoked. Screening reduces AAA-related mortality by ~40%. Risk factors: smoking (most powerful — duration matters more than intensity), male sex, age, family history, connective tissue disorders (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos), hypertension. Rupture risk by size: <4cm = <1%/year; 4-5cm = 1-3%/year; 5-5.5cm = 3-15%/year; >5.5cm = 20-40%/year → repair threshold.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: AAA screening is one of the most cost-effective screening programs in medicine — the NNS (number needed to screen) is ~300 to prevent one AAA death over 10 years. However, screening in women is NOT routinely recommended (lower prevalence, smaller absolute benefit) except for those with family history + smoking. The USPSTF gives screening an 'I' (insufficient evidence) rating for women, creating an evidence gap for high-risk female smokers.
A.Abdominal ultrasound to measure aortic diameter — the initial diagnostic and screening modality (sensitivity >95% for AAA, no radiation, low cost)
B.CTA abdomen immediately
C.Abdominal X-ray (lateral view)
D.MRI abdomen
Ultrasound First

Abdominal US is the ideal initial study: fast, accurate (≥95% sensitivity), no radiation, and well-suited for serial surveillance. US measures the maximal anteroposterior diameter of the infrarenal aorta. If AAA is confirmed and approaching surgical threshold: CTA abdomen/pelvis provides detailed anatomy for surgical planning (neck length, iliac involvement, renal artery relationship, branch vessels). CTA is the preoperative standard for both open and endovascular repair (EVAR) planning.

A.Normal aortic diameter (<3.0 cm)
B.Small AAA (3.0-3.9 cm) requiring surveillance only
C.Given the palpable pulsatile mass in a 68-year-old smoker, expect a significant AAA (≥4.5-5.5 cm). Mural thrombus is commonly seen lining the aneurysm wall. The US will measure the maximal AP diameter and identify if the aneurysm extends to the iliac arteries
D.Aortic dissection
AAA Size Classification

AAA classification by maximal diameter: Small (3.0-4.4 cm): surveillance US every 6-12 months. Medium (4.5-5.4 cm): surveillance US every 6 months, consider referral to vascular surgery. Large (≥5.5 cm in men, ≥5.0 cm in women): repair indicated (rupture risk exceeds surgical risk). Rapidly expanding (>0.5 cm/6 months): repair indicated regardless of size. Mural thrombus lines most AAAs but does NOT reduce rupture risk — the outer wall diameter is what matters.

A.All AAAs need immediate surgery
B.Management based on US diameter: <5.5 cm → surveillance + aggressive risk factor modification. ≥5.5 cm (or ≥5.0 in women, or rapid expansion, or symptomatic) → vascular surgery referral for elective repair. CTA for preoperative anatomy if repair indicated. EVAR (endovascular) vs open repair based on anatomy and patient factors
C.Observation regardless of size
D.Immediate open surgical repair for all aneurysms
Size-Based Management Algorithm

The US measurement drives the entire management algorithm. Below repair threshold: surveillance imaging + smoking cessation + blood pressure control + statin therapy. At repair threshold: CTA for anatomy → EVAR if anatomy suitable (short recovery, lower perioperative mortality) vs open repair (more durable, necessary for hostile neck anatomy). Elective repair mortality: EVAR ~1-2%, open ~4-5%. Ruptured AAA mortality: 80-90% overall, 40-50% even if reaching the OR. The entire rationale for screening is preventing rupture through elective repair.

A.US surveillance only
B.Surgery and no lifestyle changes
C.Watchful waiting with no modifications
D.Management per AAA size + AGGRESSIVE smoking cessation (the ONLY modifiable factor proven to slow AAA growth) + blood pressure optimization (target <130/80) + statin therapy (reduces cardiovascular events and may slow growth) + screen for other aneurysms (popliteal, thoracic — 15-25% of AAA patients have concomitant aneurysms elsewhere) + cardiovascular risk assessment (AAA patients have high concurrent CAD and cerebrovascular disease) + family screening discussion (first-degree relatives should be screened)
Surveillance + Risk Factor Elimination + Vascular Screening

AAA is a marker of systemic vascular disease. The imaging finding triggers not just aneurysm management but comprehensive cardiovascular risk reduction. Smoking cessation is the ONLY intervention proven to slow AAA growth rate — this cannot be overstated. Concomitant aneurysm screening is important: 15-25% of AAA patients have popliteal aneurysms, and thoracic aneurysms are also more common. First-degree male relatives should be offered screening. The US finding initiated a cascade from local aneurysm management to systemic vascular health optimization.

Carotid Duplex Ultrasound → CTACarotid Artery Stenosis — Stroke Prevention
Clinical Vignette
A 71-year-old male with hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and hyperlipidemia presents after a 20-minute episode of right-hand weakness and slurred speech yesterday that resolved completely. He has a left carotid bruit on exam. He smokes 1 pack per day. He did not seek care yesterday because 'it went away.'
A.Resolved symptoms mean no further workup is needed
B.A carotid bruit is a normal finding in elderly patients
C.This was a TIA (transient ischemic attack) — a WARNING STROKE. The 90-day stroke risk after TIA is 10-20%, with highest risk in the first 48 hours. His ABCD2 score is high: Age ≥60 (+1), BP elevation (+1), Clinical features (unilateral weakness +2), Duration 10-59 min (+1), Diabetes (+1) = 6/7 (high risk). The left carotid bruit + right-sided symptoms localizes to left carotid territory. Carotid stenosis is the cause in ~15-20% of ischemic strokes — and is surgically preventable
D.TIAs only occur in patients over 80
TIA as a Stroke Warning

TIA is a neurological emergency, not a benign event. Stroke risk after TIA: 5% at 48 hours, 10-20% at 90 days. The ABCD2 score stratifies risk: scores ≥4 warrant urgent evaluation (ideally within 24 hours). Carotid stenosis causes ~15-20% of ischemic strokes through artery-to-artery embolism (plaque rupture → thromboembolism to brain). Risk factors for carotid disease mirror atherosclerosis risk: smoking (#1 modifiable), hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia, age, male sex. The carotid bruit is a clinical clue but has limited sensitivity/specificity — imaging is definitive.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: Carotid endarterectomy (CEA) for symptomatic stenosis ≥70% is one of the most evidence-based surgical interventions in medicine (NASCET, ECST trials). The NNT is only 6 to prevent one stroke over 2 years. However, the benefit depends on surgical complication rate being <6% — high-volume centers with experienced surgeons are essential. For asymptomatic stenosis, the benefit is much smaller (NNT ~20 over 5 years), and medical therapy alone is increasingly competitive.
A.Carotid duplex ultrasound — the first-line imaging study for carotid stenosis. Non-invasive, no radiation, measures stenosis severity by peak systolic velocity
B.CT head only
C.Conventional cerebral angiography
D.MRI brain without vascular imaging
Carotid Duplex + Brain Imaging

The TIA workup includes both brain imaging and vascular imaging: CT head or MRI brain (rule out hemorrhage, detect acute infarction on DWI-MRI), carotid duplex US (first-line for extracranial carotid stenosis), and cardiac evaluation (ECG ± echocardiogram ± telemetry for AFib). Carotid US measures stenosis by peak systolic velocity (PSV): PSV >125 cm/s suggests ≥50% stenosis; PSV >230 cm/s suggests ≥70% stenosis. CTA or MRA confirms the US findings before surgical planning.

A.Normal carotid velocities bilaterally
B.Elevated peak systolic velocity (>230 cm/s) in the left internal carotid artery with visible echogenic plaque, consistent with ≥70% stenosis — correlating with the left-sided bruit and right-sided neurological symptoms
C.Complete left ICA occlusion
D.Vertebral artery stenosis
Hemodynamically Significant Stenosis

Carotid US grading: Normal: PSV <125 cm/s, no plaque. 50-69% stenosis: PSV 125-230 cm/s. ≥70% stenosis: PSV >230 cm/s, end-diastolic velocity >100 cm/s. Near-occlusion: very high or paradoxically low velocities with string sign. Complete occlusion: no flow detected. Plaque characterization matters too: echolucent (lipid-rich, unstable) plaques are higher risk than calcified (stable) plaques. The combination of ≥70% stenosis + symptomatic TIA puts this patient in the highest-benefit category for intervention.

A.Symptomatic ≥70% carotid stenosis = strongest indication for carotid endarterectomy (CEA). Confirm with CTA or MRA. Surgery should be performed within 2 weeks of the TIA for maximum benefit (stroke risk is front-loaded in the first days/weeks)
B.Medical management alone is sufficient for all carotid stenosis
C.Repeat imaging in 6 months before deciding
D.Antiplatelet therapy only
Symptomatic ≥70% = Surgery

NASCET trial evidence: CEA for symptomatic stenosis ≥70% reduces 2-year stroke risk from 26% to 9% (NNT 6). For 50-69% symptomatic stenosis, benefit is moderate (NNT 15). For <50%, no surgical benefit. Timing matters: CEA within 2 weeks of TIA/minor stroke provides maximum benefit — the risk of recurrent stroke is highest early. Carotid artery stenting (CAS) is an alternative for patients with hostile neck anatomy, prior neck radiation, or high surgical risk, but CEA remains preferred for most patients per current guidelines.

A.CEA and discharge with no further management
B.Aspirin only and follow-up in 3 months
C.Medical management without surgery
D.Urgent CEA within 2 weeks + dual antiplatelet therapy (aspirin + clopidogrel for 21 days per POINT trial) + high-intensity statin (atorvastatin 80mg) + blood pressure optimization + diabetes management (HbA1c target <7%) + SMOKING CESSATION (the most critical modifiable factor) + screen for contralateral carotid stenosis and other vascular beds (coronary, peripheral, AAA) + long-term antiplatelet and statin therapy + driving restrictions per guidelines
Urgent CEA + Comprehensive Vascular Risk Reduction

The carotid US finding directed the most time-sensitive intervention (CEA within 2 weeks). But carotid stenosis is a marker of systemic atherosclerosis: these patients have high rates of concurrent CAD (50%+), peripheral arterial disease, and AAA. Comprehensive vascular risk reduction is as important as the surgery itself. Smoking cessation reduces recurrent stroke risk more than any medication. The imaging cascade (duplex US → CTA confirmation → CEA) is one of the best-validated pathways from screening to life-saving intervention in all of medicine.

CTA → Conventional AngiographyAcute Mesenteric Ischemia
Clinical Vignette
A 74-year-old woman with atrial fibrillation (not on anticoagulation due to 'fall risk concerns') and heart failure presents with sudden-onset severe diffuse abdominal pain. She rates the pain 10/10 but her abdomen is surprisingly soft and non-tender on exam (“pain out of proportion to exam”). Vitals: HR 110 (irregular), BP 100/60. Labs: lactate 5.8 mmol/L (elevated), WBC 19,000. She has been taking warfarin intermittently.
A.Abdominal pain in elderly patients is usually benign
B.Atrial fibrillation is unrelated to abdominal emergencies
C.A soft abdomen rules out serious pathology
D.Acute mesenteric ischemia (AMI) is the 'abdominal stroke': non-anticoagulated AFib is the #1 risk factor for SMA embolism (accounting for 50% of AMI cases). The classic presentation is 'pain out of proportion to physical exam' — severe pain with minimal tenderness (early ischemia affects visceral afferents before peritoneal signs develop). Mortality is 60-80% overall and approaches 100% if bowel necrosis occurs before intervention. Her subtherapeutic anticoagulation was the preventable upstream cause
Acute Mesenteric Ischemia — The Abdominal Stroke

AMI causes: SMA embolism (50% — usually from AFib or LV thrombus), SMA thrombosis (25% — pre-existing atherosclerosis with acute occlusion), non-occlusive mesenteric ischemia (NOMI, 20% — low-flow states in heart failure, shock, vasopressors), and mesenteric venous thrombosis (5% — hypercoagulable states). The critical teaching point: 'pain out of proportion to exam' in an AFib patient should trigger immediate AMI consideration. Lactate elevation confirms tissue ischemia. Peritoneal signs (rebound, guarding) are LATE findings indicating bowel necrosis — the goal is diagnosis BEFORE necrosis.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: The decision not to anticoagulate AFib patients due to 'fall risk' is one of the most common and consequential errors in geriatric medicine. Studies show that the stroke/embolism risk from untreated AFib almost always exceeds the bleeding risk from anticoagulation, even in fall-prone elderly patients. DOACs have made this equation even more favorable. This patient's mesenteric embolism was a predictable consequence of undertreated AFib.
A.CTA abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast — the gold standard for AMI diagnosis (sensitivity >95%). Arterial and venous phase imaging identifies the site and type of occlusion and assesses bowel viability
B.Abdominal X-ray
C.Mesenteric Doppler ultrasound
D.Conventional angiography as the first study
CTA — Fast and Definitive

CTA is the first-line study: sensitivity >95% for SMA occlusion, shows the exact location and type of obstruction (embolism typically lodges 3-8 cm from the SMA origin, at the take-off of the middle colic artery), identifies bowel wall changes (non-enhancement = necrosis, pneumatosis = advanced ischemia), and evaluates for other causes of acute abdomen. Do not delay for US or X-ray — these are insensitive early in AMI. Conventional angiography is reserved for therapeutic intervention (catheter-directed thrombolysis, thrombectomy) after CTA diagnosis.

A.Normal mesenteric vasculature
B.Aortic dissection extending to the SMA
C.Filling defect (thrombus) in the SMA consistent with acute embolism, with non-enhancement of affected small bowel loops (suggesting ischemia). The embolus typically lodges at the first major branch point. Look for pneumatosis intestinalis and portal venous gas as signs of bowel necrosis
D.SMA stenosis without acute occlusion
CTA Findings in AMI

CTA findings: Arterial phase: filling defect in SMA (embolism) or abrupt cutoff (thrombosis), SMA calcification (chronic atherosclerosis suggesting thrombotic cause). Bowel findings: non-enhancing bowel wall (ischemia), bowel wall thickening (edema), pneumatosis intestinalis (gas in bowel wall = necrosis), portal venous gas (very late, poor prognosis). Embolism vs thrombosis differentiation: embolism → occlusion 3-8 cm from origin (past first jejunal branches), proximal SMA normal. Thrombosis → occlusion at SMA origin with proximal atherosclerosis.

A.CTA confirms SMA embolism = SURGICAL EMERGENCY. If no signs of bowel necrosis: emergent catheter-directed therapy (thrombectomy/thrombolysis) or surgical embolectomy + anticoagulation. If signs of necrosis (pneumatosis, non-enhancement): emergent laparotomy for resection of nonviable bowel + SMA revascularization
B.Heparin drip and observation
C.Outpatient vascular surgery referral
D.Broad-spectrum antibiotics and NPO only
Emergent Revascularization ± Bowel Resection

AMI treatment is time-critical: No necrosis on CTA → endovascular first (catheter-directed aspiration thrombectomy or thrombolysis via SMA catheter) — less invasive, can be followed by surgical exploration if needed. Signs of necrosis → emergent laparotomy (resect nonviable bowel, SMA embolectomy, assess remaining bowel viability, second-look laparotomy at 24-48h). Anticoagulation (IV heparin) is started immediately to prevent clot propagation. The window between reversible ischemia and irreversible necrosis may be as short as 6-12 hours.

B.Surgery and no anticoagulation changes
C.Emergent revascularization (endovascular or surgical) + second-look laparotomy at 24-48h if bowel viability uncertain + START ANTICOAGULATION for AFib (DOAC long-term — the failure to anticoagulate caused this event) + evaluate for short bowel syndrome if extensive resection + nutritional support + address the 'fall risk' anticoagulation decision (structured fall risk assessment, DOACs have lower ICH risk than warfarin, benefits vs risks documentation) + cardiology consultation for rate/rhythm control

The CTA finding directed emergent life-saving intervention. But the root cause — untreated AFib — must be addressed to prevent recurrence. The 'fall risk' justification for withholding anticoagulation requires re-evaluation: structured fall risk assessment tools (Timed Up and Go, STEADI) should replace subjective 'fall risk' labels, and DOACs (particularly apixaban) have lower intracranial hemorrhage rates than warfarin, tipping the benefit-risk balance further toward treatment. This catastrophic event was a predictable consequence of undertreated AFib.

Ankle-Brachial Index → CTA → AngiographyPeripheral Arterial Disease — Critical Limb Ischemia
Clinical Vignette
A 66-year-old male with type 2 diabetes (HbA1c 9.1%), 40-pack-year smoking history (quit 2 years ago), and hypertension presents with a painful non-healing ulcer on his left great toe for 6 weeks. He also reports left calf pain after walking one block that resolves with rest (claudication that has progressed). On exam: left foot is cool, pallor with elevation, dependent rubor, absent dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial pulses, and a 1.5 cm punched-out ulcer on the great toe.
A.A non-healing toe ulcer in a diabetic is always neuropathic
B.He quit smoking 2 years ago, so vascular risk is normalized
C.Claudication is a benign condition that doesn't require intervention
D.PAD affects 12-20% of adults >65. This patient has CLI — the most severe manifestation (rest pain, non-healing ulcers, gangrene). His risk factors are synergistic: smoking (strongest risk factor, residual risk persists years after cessation), diabetes (accelerates atherosclerosis and causes coexisting neuropathy, masking ischemic symptoms), and hypertension. CLI carries a 25% major amputation rate and 25% mortality at 1 year without revascularization
PAD and Critical Limb Ischemia Epidemiology

PAD is dramatically underdiagnosed: ~50% of patients are asymptomatic. Fontaine classification: Stage I (asymptomatic), Stage II (claudication — IIa: >200m, IIb: <200m), Stage III (rest pain), Stage IV (tissue loss/gangrene). Stages III-IV = CLI. Risk factors: smoking (2-6× risk, dose-dependent), diabetes (2-4×, accelerates progression), age >65, hypertension, dyslipidemia, CKD. Diabetic PAD is uniquely dangerous: coexisting neuropathy masks ischemic pain, causing late presentation; atherosclerosis is more distal (tibial vessels, harder to revascularize); and impaired wound healing creates a vicious cycle. CLI prognosis without treatment: 25% major amputation, 25% death at 1 year.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: PAD is the strongest marker of systemic atherosclerosis — patients with PAD have 3-6× higher risk of MI and stroke compared to age-matched controls. The 5-year mortality of CLI is WORSE than most cancers (~50%). Yet PAD remains undertreated: fewer than 50% of PAD patients receive appropriate antiplatelet therapy, statins, and smoking cessation support. Every PAD diagnosis should trigger comprehensive cardiovascular risk management.
A.MRI of the foot to evaluate the ulcer
B.Ankle-brachial index (ABI) first as the bedside screening test, then CTA of the lower extremity arterial system for revascularization planning if CLI is confirmed
C.Venous duplex ultrasound
D.Bone scan to rule out osteomyelitis first
ABI → CTA Pathway

ABI (ankle systolic pressure / brachial systolic pressure) is the first-line diagnostic test: Normal 1.0-1.4; mild PAD 0.7-0.9; moderate 0.4-0.69; severe/CLI <0.4. Caveat in diabetics: calcified tibial arteries can falsely elevate ABI (>1.4 = non-compressible, unreliable) — use toe-brachial index (TBI) or transcutaneous oxygen measurement (TcPO2) instead. CTA lower extremity provides the roadmap for revascularization: identifies the level and extent of stenosis/occlusion, runoff vessels, and anatomy for surgical bypass or endovascular intervention.

A.Normal ABI (>0.9) and patent arteries on CTA
B.DVT causing the ulcer
C.ABI <0.4 (severe ischemia/CLI). CTA shows multilevel atherosclerotic disease: superficial femoral artery occlusion + tibial vessel stenosis with poor distal runoff to the foot — a pattern typical of diabetic PAD (distal predominance)
D.Popliteal artery aneurysm
Diabetic PAD Pattern on CTA

Diabetic PAD has a characteristic distal pattern: tibial and peroneal arteries are preferentially affected (compared to non-diabetic PAD which tends to affect aortoiliac and femoropopliteal segments). CTA shows: SFA occlusion with reconstitution via collaterals, severely diseased tibial vessels, and limited pedal runoff. Runoff assessment is critical: at least one patent tibial vessel to the foot is needed for bypass graft success. The CTA identifies both the inflow lesion (for angioplasty/stenting) and the target vessel for distal bypass.

A.CLI confirmed by ABI + CTA = urgent vascular surgery/interventional radiology referral for revascularization. Endovascular (angioplasty/stenting) is first-line for many lesions; surgical bypass (femoral-distal) for complex disease or endovascular failure. Amputation is the last resort, not the first option
B.Amputation is the standard treatment for CLI
C.Conservative wound care and observation
D.Cilostazol for claudication and follow-up in 3 months
Revascularization — Limb Salvage Priority

CLI treatment prioritizes limb salvage through revascularization. Endovascular approach: angioplasty ± stenting of stenotic/occluded segments (SFA, tibial) — less invasive, suitable for focal lesions. Surgical bypass: femoral-popliteal or femoral-tibial bypass using autologous saphenous vein (gold standard conduit) — for long-segment occlusions or failed endovascular attempts. Hybrid approaches combining both are increasingly common. Primary amputation is reserved for extensive necrosis, non-reconstructable anatomy, or non-ambulatory patients with limited life expectancy.

Limb Salvage + Systemic Vascular Risk Reduction

The CTA roadmap enabled targeted revascularization for limb salvage. But PAD management extends far beyond the affected leg: these patients have the highest cardiovascular mortality of any atherosclerotic disease subgroup. The comprehensive plan addresses local (revascularization, wound care), metabolic (diabetes optimization), and systemic (antiplatelet, statin, BP control, smoking maintenance, cardiovascular screening) domains. Supervised exercise programs (structured walking to claudication threshold) are evidence-based and improve walking distance by 50-200% — yet are dramatically underutilized.
