ECG + Coronary AngiographyAcute Chest Pain with ECG Changes
Clinical Vignette
A 58-year-old male presents to the ED with crushing substernal chest pain radiating to his left arm for 45 minutes. Diaphoretic, pale, nauseated. PMH: hypertension, hyperlipidemia, type 2 diabetes, 30 pack-year smoking history. Vitals: HR 98, BP 154/92, RR 22, SpO₂ 96%.

Which of the following best describes the epidemiological and clinical significance of this presentation?

A.His age alone — ACS is rare under age 65
B.His smoking history is the only relevant risk factor
C.The clustering of diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and smoking creates
Epidemiological Context Matters

Framingham risk factors are multiplicative, not additive. A 58-year-old male with diabetes, HTN, HLD, and heavy smoking has dramatically higher pre-test probability than any single factor suggests. Sex matters: men have higher ACS rates at this age, though women's risk is frequently underestimated (atypical presentations in women are a major diagnostic pitfall). Cocaine use should also be screened — it can cause ACS in young patients with zero traditional risk factors. Always ask about stimulant use, especially in patients under 45.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: In South Florida, the large Caribbean and Latin American populations have high rates of metabolic syndrome and often delayed presentation due to health access barriers. Consider that patients presenting with 'first MI' may have had years of uncontrolled risk factors without primary care.
A.12-lead ECG immediately (within 10 minutes of arrival)
B.Stat troponin first, then ECG based on results
C.CXR to rule out aortic dissection first
D.Echocardiogram at bedside
ECG Within 10 Minutes

ACC/AHA guidelines mandate ECG within 10 minutes of first medical contact. Troponin takes 1-3 hours to rise — it cannot rule out STEMI. ECG identifies ST-elevation that triggers cath lab activation. CXR is important but secondary. Echo is useful for wall motion abnormalities but shouldn't delay ECG.

🌍 Modality Reasoning: ECG is the most time-sensitive test in medicine. Door-to-ECG time predicts door-to-balloon time. Every minute of delay = more myocardial death.
A.Normal sinus rhythm with nonspecific T-wave changes
B.ST elevation in leads II, III, aVF with reciprocal ST depression in I, aVL
C.Diffuse ST elevation with PR depression
D.New left bundle branch block
Territory-Based ECG Patterns

The clinical picture (crushing substernal, radiation, diaphoresis, risk factors) with inferior leads (II, III, aVF) = RCA territory. Reciprocal changes in lateral leads (I, aVL) increase specificity for true STEMI. Option C describes pericarditis (diffuse + PR depression). New LBBB can indicate STEMI but is less common.

A.Activate cath lab for PCI within 90 minutes
B.Give thrombolytics immediately since this is clearly STEMI
C.Activate cath lab AND check right-sided leads (V4R) —
The Right Ventricle Question

Inferior STEMI (RCA territory) frequently involves the right ventricle. V4R showing ST elevation confirms RV involvement, which has critical management implications: avoid nitrates and aggressive diuresis (RV is preload-dependent). IV fluids may be needed instead. This is a common board question and a real clinical trap.

🌍 Clinical Pearl: RV infarct complicates ~30-50% of inferior STEMIs. The triad of hypotension + clear lungs + elevated JVP in inferior STEMI = RV involvement until proven otherwise.
A.PCI + dual antiplatelet therapy + aggressive IV furosemide
B.PCI + dual antiplatelet therapy + cautious IV fluids
C.Thrombolytics + heparin + morphine
D.Medical management with heparin and serial troponins
RV-Aware Management

With confirmed RV involvement, management shifts: fluids instead of diuretics, avoid nitrates (drops preload), PCI to open the culprit vessel ASAP. This is where imaging interpretation directly changes the treatment plan — the same STEMI requires completely different supportive care based on the V4R finding.

CXR + EchocardiogramProgressive Dyspnea with Lower Extremity Edema
Clinical Vignette
A 72-year-old woman with known HFrEF (EF 25%) presents with 3 days of worsening dyspnea, orthopnea (sleeps on 4 pillows), and 15-lb weight gain over 2 weeks. She ran out of her furosemide 10 days ago. Vitals: HR 108, BP 162/94, RR 28, SpO₂ 88% on room air. JVP elevated to ear, bilateral crackles to mid-lung fields, 3+ pitting edema to knees.

Which of the following best describes the epidemiological and clinical significance of this presentation?

A.Family history of cardiomyopathy
B.Dietary sodium intake over the past month
C.Whether she has been using NSAIDs for any reason
D.All of the above — medication non-adherence, dietary indiscretion, NSAID use
Social Determinants Drive HF Exacerbations

The most common cause of HF decompensation is medication non-adherence, often driven by cost, access, or health literacy barriers. This patient ran out of furosemide — was it cost? Transportation? Understanding of the medication's importance? NSAIDs (available OTC) cause fluid retention and are a hidden trigger. Dietary sodium excess is another major contributor. Understanding the 'why' behind the exacerbation is as important as treating the current episode.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: HF readmission rates are highest in low-income ZIP codes with limited pharmacy access. Identifying and addressing the social determinant (medication assistance programs, 90-day prescriptions, community health worker follow-up) reduces 30-day readmission more than any single medication change.
A.Portable AP chest X-ray + bedside echocardiogram
B.CT chest with contrast
C.Right heart catheterization
D.Cardiac MRI for EF assessment
CXR + Echo: The HF Workup

Portable CXR assesses pulmonary edema severity, cardiomegaly, pleural effusions, and rules out pneumonia as a trigger. Bedside echo confirms EF, wall motion, valvular function, and pericardial effusion. This guides whether the patient needs IV diuretics alone vs. inotropes vs. mechanical support. CT adds radiation and contrast without changing management. RHC is for refractory cases.

A.Clear lung fields with normal cardiac silhouette
B.Unilateral opacity suggesting pneumonia
C.Cardiomegaly, cephalization, Kerley B lines, bilateral pleural effusions
D.Widened mediastinum suggesting dissection
The CXR Tells the Hemodynamic Story

Classic CHF CXR progresses: (1) Cephalization (upper lobe vessel distension, PCWP 12-18) → (2) Interstitial edema/Kerley B lines (PCWP 18-25) → (3) Alveolar edema (bat-wing pattern) (PCWP >25) → (4) Pleural effusions (bilateral, R>L typical). Cardiomegaly (CTR >0.5) present in chronic HF.

A.Start IV nitroglycerin for afterload reduction
B.Assess the CXR in context of exam: bilateral effusions + edema = volume overload → IV diuresis is the priority;
C.Order CT pulmonary angiography to rule out PE as the cause of dyspnea
CXR Guides Diuretic Strategy

The CXR confirms what the exam shows: severe volume overload. IV furosemide (bolus or drip) is first-line. Echo determines whether the heart needs inotropic support (severely depressed EF with cardiogenic shock signs) or if diuresis alone will suffice. Bilateral effusions in HF = transudative; thoracentesis only if asymmetric, febrile, or refractory to diuresis.

A.Discharge once oxygen improved with plan for outpatient follow-up
B.IV diuresis alone, discharge in 24 hours
C.Add digoxin and metolazone, discharge in 48 hours
D.IV diuresis to euvolemia, restart GDMT, address medication access barriers
Integration: Imaging + Social Context = Plan

The imaging confirms severe decompensation. But the root cause was medication non-adherence due to running out. The discharge plan must address the precipitant: medication assistance programs, 90-day prescriptions, pharmacy delivery, health literacy about daily weights. EF 25% qualifies for ICD evaluation (primary prevention if EF ≤35% despite 3 months optimal medical therapy). The imaging findings trigger both acute management and long-term device consideration.

EchocardiogramHypotension with Distended Neck Veins
Clinical Vignette
A 45-year-old woman with recently diagnosed metastatic breast cancer presents with progressive dyspnea, light-headedness when standing, and muffled heart sounds. Vitals: HR 118, BP 92/70 (narrowed pulse pressure), RR 24, SpO₂ 93%. Pulsus paradoxus measured at 18 mmHg. Neck veins distended.

Which of the following best describes the epidemiological and clinical significance of this presentation?

A.Her sex — women are more likely to have takotsubo cardiomyopathy
B.Metastatic cancer is the most common cause of malignant pericardial effusion, and breast, lung
C.She likely has PE from cancer-related hypercoagulability
Cancer Changes the Pericardial Differential

In a patient with known malignancy, pericardial effusion has a different epidemiological profile than in the general population. Malignant effusions (breast, lung, lymphoma, melanoma) account for most symptomatic pericardial effusions in oncology patients. They tend to accumulate gradually, becoming hemodynamically significant at larger volumes. Radiation pericarditis (if prior chest radiation), drug-induced pericarditis (immune checkpoint inhibitors), and infectious causes (immunosuppression) must also be considered. The cancer history completely reframes the diagnostic approach.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: Immune checkpoint inhibitors (pembrolizumab, nivolumab) cause myocarditis and pericarditis in 1-2% of patients. Always ask about recent immunotherapy. Also consider that cancer patients on anticoagulation for VTE prophylaxis can develop hemorrhagic pericardial effusions.
A.Bedside echocardiogram (point-of-care)
B.CT chest with contrast
C.Chest X-ray followed by formal transthoracic echo
D.Cardiac MRI
Bedside Echo Is Emergent

In suspected tamponade with hemodynamic compromise, bedside echo is diagnostic within minutes. You're looking for: (1) pericardial effusion, (2) RV diastolic collapse, (3) RA systolic collapse, (4) IVC plethora without respiratory variation, (5) respiratory variation in mitral/tricuspid inflow. CXR can show 'water bottle' heart but doesn't assess hemodynamic significance. CT delays care in an unstable patient.

A.Small pericardial effusion with normal RV function
B.Dilated RV with septal bowing (PE pattern)
C.Large circumferential effusion with RV diastolic collapse and IVC plethora
D.Severe aortic stenosis with LVH
Tamponade Echo Findings

Malignant effusions are typically large and circumferential. Hemodynamic significance is determined by chamber collapse (RV diastolic collapse is most specific, RA systolic collapse is most sensitive) and IVC plethora (dilated IVC without respiratory variation = elevated RA pressure). The 'swinging heart' sign (electrical alternans on ECG) correlates with large effusions.

A.Emergent pericardiocentesis — the echo confirms tamponade physiology with hemodynamic compromise
B.Start IV fluids and observe — this could resolve spontaneously
C.Consult cardiothoracic surgery for pericardial window — but this can wait until morning
D.Order CT chest to characterize the effusion before draining
Echo Drives Immediate Intervention

Echo-guided pericardiocentesis is both diagnostic and therapeutic. In malignant tamponade, fluid analysis (cytology, tumor markers) helps confirm the cause. A pericardial drain is typically left in place until output drops below 25-50 mL/day. Pericardial window is definitive for recurrent malignant effusions but is a surgical procedure — not first-line in acute tamponade.

A.Pericardiocentesis and discharge with oncology follow-up
B.Pericardiocentesis and radiation therapy to the pericardium
C.Pericardiocentesis with drain placement, pericardial fluid cytology
D.IV fluids and vasopressors as a bridge to surgery
Imaging Triggers the Full Clinical Picture

The echo finding of malignant tamponade triggers a cascade: acute intervention (pericardiocentesis), diagnostic workup (cytology to confirm malignant etiology), oncologic reassessment (is systemic therapy controlling disease?), and palliative care integration (this represents disease progression). For recurrent effusions, options include pericardial window, intrapericardial sclerotherapy, or indwelling catheter. The imaging doesn't just diagnose — it reframes the goals of care conversation.

CTA ChestTearing Chest Pain Radiating to the Back
Clinical Vignette
A 62-year-old male with poorly controlled hypertension (takes medications 'sometimes') presents with sudden-onset 'tearing' chest pain radiating to his back. BP 210/110 in right arm, 170/90 in left arm. A new diastolic murmur is heard at the right sternal border. He appears acutely uncomfortable.

Which of the following best describes the epidemiological and clinical significance of this presentation?

A.Only chronic hypertension matters — it accounts for 70% of dissections
B.Age over 60 increases the risk, but sex is not relevant
C.Cocaine use should be screened — it causes acute hypertensive surges that trigger dissection
D.Chronic uncontrolled hypertension
Dissection Risk Profiling

Aortic dissection has a distinct epidemiological profile. Hypertension is present in 70-80% of cases and is the dominant modifiable risk factor. In patients under 40, consider connective tissue disease (Marfan, Loeys-Dietz, vascular EDS), bicuspid aortic valve, and cocaine/methamphetamine use. Pregnancy (third trimester and postpartum) is a risk factor due to aortic wall changes. Iatrogenic dissection occurs after cardiac surgery or catheterization. The BP differential between arms (>20 mmHg systolic) is itself an epidemiological red flag.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: Men are affected 21 over women, but women with dissection have higher mortality — partly because they present atypically and are diagnosed later. In patients with cocaine-related chest pain, dissection must be considered alongside ACS. Always ask about stimulant use.
A.CXR first to look for widened mediastinum
B.CT angiography of the chest, abdomen, and pelvis
C.Transthoracic echocardiogram to assess the aortic root
D.MRI of the aorta
CTA Is the Gold Standard

CTA has >95% sensitivity and specificity for dissection and can be obtained rapidly. It shows the intimal flap, true and false lumens, extent of dissection, branch vessel involvement, and complications (pericardial effusion, malperfusion). CXR is insensitive (~60% show widened mediastinum). TEE is an alternative in unstable patients who can't go to CT. MRI is most accurate but impractical in acute settings.

🌍 Scan must include abdomen/pelvis — dissection can extend to iliac arteries, and branch vessel malperfusion (renal, mesenteric, limb) must be identified.:
A.Intimal flap in the ascending aorta with true and false lumens —
B.Intimal flap limited to the descending aorta distal to the left subclavian
C.Ascending aortic aneurysm without dissection flap
Stanford Classification Drives Surgery

The new diastolic murmur (aortic regurgitation) + BP differential = ascending aortic involvement (Stanford Type A). The flap disrupts the aortic valve apparatus. Type A (involves ascending) = surgical emergency. Type B (descending only) = medical management unless complicated (malperfusion, rupture, refractory pain).

A.Emergent cardiothoracic surgery consultation —
B.Start IV esmolol and nitroprusside, admit to ICU for medical management
C.Endovascular stent-graft placement
Type A = Emergent Surgery

Type A dissection is a surgical emergency with ~1-2% mortality per hour if untreated. While awaiting surgery: IV beta-blocker first (esmolol or labetalol to HR <60), then add nitroprusside if BP remains elevated. Target SBP 100-120. Avoid isolated nitroprusside (increases aortic wall shear stress without HR control). Pain control reduces sympathetic drive.

Imaging → Surgery → Lifelong Surveillance

Post-repair, patients need lifelong aortic imaging surveillance (CTA or MRA annually) because the residual false lumen can expand. BP control is critical (target <130/80). If the patient is under 50, genetic testing for connective tissue disorders should be pursued. The underlying cause — medication non-adherence with uncontrolled hypertension — must be addressed to prevent recurrence.

Echocardiogram (TTE/TEE)Persistent Fever with New Heart Murmur
Clinical Vignette
A 32-year-old male with a history of IV drug use presents with 2 weeks of fever, chills, night sweats, and new-onset pleuritic chest pain. Track marks on bilateral arms. Vitals: Temp 39.4°C, HR 112, BP 110/60. A new holosystolic murmur is best heard at the left lower sternal border, louder with inspiration. Splinter hemorrhages noted on exam.

Which of the following best describes the epidemiological and clinical significance of this presentation?

A.IV drug use only affects the left-sided valves
B.The patient's age makes endocarditis unlikely — it primarily affects the elderly
C.IVDU-associated endocarditis typically involves the TRICUSPID valve
IVDU Endocarditis Epidemiology

The epidemiology of endocarditis varies dramatically by population. IVDU-associated: 50-70% tricuspid valve, S. aureus (including MRSA) in >60% of cases, presents with septic pulmonary emboli (pulmonary infiltrates, chest pain, hemoptysis). Non-IVDU native valve: mitral > aortic, viridans streptococci traditionally most common (though S. aureus is rising). Prosthetic valve: early (<60 days) = S. aureus, S. epidermidis; late = similar to native valve. Geography matters too: Coxiella burnetii (Q fever) is common in rural settings with livestock contact. Bartonella in homeless populations with louse exposure.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: The opioid epidemic has driven a surge in right-sided endocarditis in young adults. In communities with high IV drug use prevalence, any febrile IVDU with new murmur = endocarditis until proven otherwise. MRSA rates in IVDU endocarditis exceed 50% in many US regions — empiric vancomycin is essential.
A.CT chest to evaluate for septic emboli first
B.Cardiac MRI for vegetation detection
C.Blood cultures × 3 from separate sites PLUS transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE)
D.Go directly to TEE — TTE misses most vegetations
Modified Duke Criteria Imaging

Blood cultures (3 sets, 30 min apart, before antibiotics) + TTE is the initial approach. TTE detects vegetations >2mm with ~70% sensitivity for native valves. If TTE is negative but clinical suspicion remains high, TEE increases sensitivity to >90%. For IVDU with suspected right-sided disease, TTE is actually quite good for tricuspid valve visualization. TEE is essential for prosthetic valves and left-sided disease.

A.Mitral valve vegetation with severe regurgitation
B.Tricuspid valve vegetation with moderate-to-severe tricuspid
C.Aortic root abscess
Right-Sided Endocarditis Pattern

In IVDU endocarditis, the injected organisms hit the tricuspid valve first (venous return → right heart). The murmur increasing with inspiration (Carvallo sign) localizes to the tricuspid valve. Septic emboli from tricuspid vegetations seed the lungs → septic pulmonary infarcts → the patient's pleuritic chest pain.

A.Vegetation size doesn't matter — start antibiotics regardless
B.Any vegetation means immediate surgery
C.Only vegetation mobility predicts embolic risk
D.Vegetation size (>10mm = higher embolic risk), mobility, and valve function all guide the surgical vs. medical decision
Echo Findings Drive Surgery Decisions

Echo provides three critical pieces: (1) Vegetation size — >10mm = increased embolic risk, >20mm = strong surgical indication; (2) Valve function — severe regurgitation with heart failure = surgery; (3) Complications — abscess, fistula, pseudoaneurysm = surgery. For isolated right-sided endocarditis, surgery is generally reserved for failure of medical therapy, large vegetations (>20mm), or recurrent septic PE despite antibiotics.

B.Immediate surgery given the vegetation
C.IV vancomycin + gentamicin empirically

Comprehensive Treatment Plan

Empiric vancomycin covers MRSA (most likely organism in IVDU endocarditis). The CXR will likely show septic emboli (multiple peripheral nodules ± cavitation). Treatment duration: right-sided = 4 weeks minimum, left-sided = 6 weeks. Addiction medicine consultation is essential — without addressing the underlying substance use disorder, reinfection rates are very high. Harm reduction strategies (naloxone, needle exchange referral, buprenorphine) are part of comprehensive care.

Clinical Vignette
A 78-year-old male presents after a syncopal episode while climbing stairs. Over the past 6 months, he has noticed progressive exertional dyspnea and one prior episode of exertional chest pressure. A crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur radiating to the carotids is heard with diminished S2 and pulsus parvus et tardus. He is a retired construction worker.

Which of the following best describes the epidemiological and clinical significance of this presentation?

A.At age 78, rheumatic heart disease is the most common cause
B.Degenerative calcific aortic stenosis is the most common cause over age 65, driven by the same
C.Bicuspid aortic valve is the most likely cause at any age
Age-Dependent Etiology of AS

Aortic stenosis etiology shifts with age: <40: congenital bicuspid valve (1-2% of population); 40-65: bicuspid valve with superimposed calcification; >65: degenerative calcific disease of a trileaflet valve. Rheumatic AS (commissural fusion) is now uncommon in developed countries but remains a major cause globally. Occupational exposures that promote chronic systemic inflammation (silica, asbestos, diesel exhaust) may accelerate atherosclerotic-type valve calcification — an emerging area of research.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: Prior chest radiation (e.g., for Hodgkin lymphoma) causes radiation-associated valvular disease 10-20 years later, often affecting the aortic valve. Always ask about prior cancer treatment — 'survivor' patients are now presenting with late cardiac complications.
A.CT calcium scoring to quantify valve calcification
B.Transthoracic echocardiogram —
C.Cardiac catheterization
Echo Is the Valvular Gold Standard

TTE provides valve area (by continuity equation), peak/mean gradient, jet velocity, LV function, wall thickness, and associated findings. Severe AS: valve area <1.0 cm², mean gradient >40 mmHg, peak velocity >4 m/s. Catheterization is reserved for discrepant echo/clinical findings or pre-surgical coronary assessment.

A.Heavily calcified aortic valve with restricted leaflet
B.Mitral valve prolapse with moderate regurgitation
C.Dilated cardiomyopathy with global hypokinesis
D.Normal valve with dynamic LVOT obstruction (HCM)
Severe Calcific AS Pattern

Syncope + exertional dyspnea + exertional angina = the classic 'SAD' triad of severe AS. The murmur character (late peaking, diminished A2, parvus et tardus pulse) correlates with severity. Echo will confirm: calcified trileaflet valve with restricted opening, high gradient, and compensatory concentric LVH.

A.Medical management — aortic stenosis responds well to vasodilators
B.Balloon valvuloplasty as definitive treatment
C.Refer for aortic valve replacement (SAVR or TAVR) —
Severe Symptomatic AS = Valve Replacement

Severe symptomatic AS is a class I indication for valve replacement. Prognosis without intervention: average survival 5 years after angina onset, 3 years after syncope, 2 years after heart failure. TAVR vs SAVR decision depends on surgical risk score (STS), anatomy, age, and patient preference. At 78, TAVR is often preferred (lower procedural risk).

The 'heart team' approach is standard for severe AS. Coronary angiography is performed pre-operatively (PCI concurrent or staged with SAVR, or done before TAVR). TAVR may be preferred if pulmonary function testing shows significant impairment.
