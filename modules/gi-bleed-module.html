MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

GI bleeding is classified as upper (proximal to ligament of Treitz) or lower (distal to ligament of Treitz). Upper GIB (UGIB) accounts for ~75% of cases.

Common Etiologies

Upper GI BleedingFrequencyKey Features
Peptic Ulcer Disease35-50%Most common cause. H. pylori (70%), NSAIDs (25%). Duodenal > gastric. Posterior duodenal ulcer erodes into gastroduodenal artery.
Esophageal Varices10-20%Portal hypertension (cirrhosis). High mortality (15-20% per episode). Massive hematemesis.
Mallory-Weiss Tear5-10%Mucosal tear at GE junction from retching/vomiting. Usually self-limited (90%).
Erosive Gastritis/Esophagitis10-15%NSAIDs, stress ulcers (ICU), alcohol. Diffuse mucosal injury.
Dieulafoy Lesion1-2%Aberrant submucosal artery bleeds through tiny mucosal defect. Massive, intermittent bleeding. Hard to find endoscopically.
Lower GI BleedingFrequencyKey Features
Diverticulosis30-40%Most common cause. Painless, large-volume hematochezia. Usually right-sided (vasa recta). Self-limited 80%.
Angiodysplasia (AVMs)10-20%Cecum/right colon. Associated with aortic stenosis (Heyde syndrome), CKD, vWD. Chronic/recurrent.
Colorectal Cancer/Polyps10-15%Occult or overt bleeding. Iron deficiency anemia. Requires colonoscopy.
Ischemic Colitis5-10%Watershed areas (splenic flexure, rectosigmoid). Bloody diarrhea + cramping in elderly/hypotension.
🔑 Key Concept: Heyde syndrome = aortic stenosis + GI bleeding from angiodysplasia. Mechanism: high shear stress through stenotic valve → acquired von Willebrand factor type 2A deficiency → impaired hemostasis at fragile AVMs. Resolves with aortic valve replacement.
CLINICAL
Assessment & Risk Stratification

Presentation Clues

FindingSuggests
Hematemesis (bright red or coffee-ground)Upper GIB
Melena (black, tarry stool)Upper GIB (or proximal small bowel). Requires only ~50 mL of blood.
Hematochezia (bright red blood per rectum)Lower GIB — but can be brisk UPPER GIB (10% of hematochezia is UGIB)
BUN:Creatinine ratio >20:1Upper GIB (digested blood = urea nitrogen load)

Risk Stratification

Glasgow-Blatchford Score (GBS) at presentation: BUN, hemoglobin, systolic BP, heart rate, melena, syncope, liver disease, heart failure. GBS = 0 identifies very low-risk patients who may be managed as outpatients. Rockall Score: post-endoscopy, incorporates endoscopic findings for rebleeding/mortality risk.

⚠️ Critical: Hemoglobin may be NORMAL early in acute bleeding because both RBCs and plasma are lost proportionally. Hemodilution occurs over 24-72 hours as interstitial fluid shifts intravascularly. Tachycardia and orthostatic hypotension are better early indicators of significant hemorrhage than Hgb.
TREATMENT
Management

Initial Resuscitation

PriorityInterventionDetails
1. Access/Labs2 large-bore IVs, type & crossCBC, BMP, coags, LFTs, lactate. Order 2-4 units PRBCs.
2. ResuscitateCrystalloid, blood productsTransfuse PRBCs for Hgb <7 (or <8 with cardiac disease). Restrictive transfusion strategy in non-variceal UGIB improves outcomes. Massive transfusion protocol if hemodynamically unstable.
3. PPIIV PPI for suspected UGIBPantoprazole 80 mg bolus then 8 mg/hr infusion. Reduces rebleeding after endoscopic therapy but does NOT improve pre-endoscopy outcomes.
4. EndoscopyWithin 24 hours (urgent if high-risk)Within 12 hours for hemodynamic instability, ongoing bleeding, or suspected variceal bleed.

Variceal Bleeding — Specific Management

Octreotide 50 μg bolus then 50 μg/hr (reduces splanchnic blood flow), antibiotics (ceftriaxone 1g IV × 7 days — reduces mortality and rebleeding), endoscopic band ligation (preferred over sclerotherapy), TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt) for refractory bleeding.

⚠️ Massive UGIB: If patient is hemodynamically unstable despite resuscitation → endoscopy ASAP. If endoscopy fails → interventional radiology (angiographic embolization) or surgery. For variceal bleed: Sengstaken-Blakemore tube as temporizing bridge to definitive therapy (max 24 hours — risk of esophageal necrosis).
