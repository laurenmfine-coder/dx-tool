Tear in the aortic intima → blood enters media → creates false lumen. #1 risk factor: hypertension. Others: Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos, bicuspid aortic valve, Turner syndrome, cocaine, 3rd trimester pregnancy.

CLINICAL
Presentation & Diagnosis

Classic Presentation

Sudden severe "tearing/ripping" chest pain radiating to the back (interscapular). Maximal at onset (unlike MI which crescendos). May have BP differential between arms (>20 mmHg), pulse deficits, new aortic regurgitation murmur, or neurologic deficits (stroke from carotid involvement).

🔑 vs. ACS Distinction: Dissection = sudden, maximal from onset, tearing, radiates to back. MI = gradual onset, pressure/squeezing, radiates to jaw/arm. CRITICAL: Do NOT give fibrinolytics for suspected ACS if dissection is possible — catastrophic hemorrhage. Dissection can cause MI (coronary ostia involvement, usually RCA → inferior STEMI).

TREATMENT
Management

Anti-Impulse Therapy (ALL Dissections)

Goal: reduce aortic wall stress by lowering HR AND BP.

🔑 IV beta-blocker FIRST (esmolol or labetalol). Target HR <60 bpm, SBP 100-120 mmHg. Beta-blocker BEFORE vasodilator — vasodilators alone cause reflex tachycardia → increased aortic shear stress → worsened dissection. If BP still elevated after BB: add IV nicardipine or nitroprusside. If BB contraindicated: IV diltiazem or verapamil.
