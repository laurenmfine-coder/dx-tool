MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

Malignant hyperthermia (MH) is a pharmacogenetic disorder of skeletal muscle caused by mutations in the ryanodine receptor (RYR1) on the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Triggering agents cause uncontrolled calcium release into the myoplasm, resulting in sustained muscle contraction, hypermetabolism, and potentially lethal hyperthermia.

Molecular Mechanism

Volatile anesthetics and succinylcholine bind to mutant RYR1 channels → channels remain open → massive Ca²⁺ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum → sustained actin-myosin cross-bridging → skeletal muscle rigidity. The hypercontractile state consumes ATP and generates heat, CO₂, lactate, and K⁺. Membrane breakdown releases myoglobin and CK.

Triggering Agents

Triggers (AVOID)Safe Agents
All volatile anesthetics: sevoflurane, desflurane, isoflurane, halothanePropofol, etomidate, ketamine
Succinylcholine (depolarizing NMJ blocker)Non-depolarizing NMJ blockers (rocuronium, vecuronium, cisatracurium)
All local anesthetics, opioids, benzodiazepines, nitrous oxide
🔑 Genetics: MH susceptibility is autosomal dominant with variable penetrance. RYR1 mutations account for ~70% of cases. CACNA1S (dihydropyridine receptor) mutations account for ~1%. A susceptible individual may undergo multiple uneventful anesthetics before triggering an episode — a negative history does NOT exclude susceptibility.
CLINICAL
Presentation & Diagnosis

Clinical Signs (Order of Appearance)

TimingSignMechanism
EarliestUnexplained rise in end-tidal CO₂ (ETCO₂)Hypermetabolism producing excess CO₂
EarlyMasseter muscle rigidity (after succinylcholine)Abnormal Ca²⁺ release in jaw muscles
EarlyTachycardia, tachypneaSympathetic response to hypermetabolism
ProgressiveGeneralized muscle rigiditySustained contraction from Ca²⁺ overload
LateHyperthermia (can exceed 43°C/109°F)Heat from sustained contraction/hypermetabolism
LateRhabdomyolysis, hyperkalemia, DICMuscle breakdown, membrane damage
⚠️ ETCO₂ is the earliest sign: An unexplained, rapidly rising ETCO₂ that does not respond to increasing ventilation is the FIRST indicator of MH during anesthesia. Temperature rise is a LATE finding — do not wait for fever to act.

Diagnostic Testing

The caffeine-halothane contracture test (CHCT) is the gold standard for MH susceptibility testing. A muscle biopsy is exposed to caffeine and halothane — susceptible muscle contracts at lower thresholds. Genetic testing for RYR1 mutations can confirm diagnosis if positive but cannot exclude MH if negative (30% have unknown mutations).

Differential Diagnosis

ConditionKey Distinguishing Feature
Neuroleptic malignant syndromeDevelops over days (not minutes); caused by dopamine antagonists, NOT anesthetics
Serotonin syndromeClonus, hyperreflexia, agitation; caused by serotonergic drugs
Thyroid stormHistory of hyperthyroidism; no muscle rigidity
TREATMENT
Management

Acute MH Crisis Protocol

StepAction
1. Stop triggersDiscontinue volatile agents and succinylcholine immediately
2. DantroleneIV dantrolene 2.5 mg/kg bolus; repeat every 5 min until resolution (max ~10 mg/kg)
3. Hyperventilate100% O₂ at high fresh gas flows to wash out volatile agents
4. Cool patientIV cold saline, ice packs to axillae/groin, gastric/bladder lavage
5. Treat hyperkalemiaCalcium chloride/gluconate, insulin + glucose, bicarbonate
6. MonitorABG, CK, myoglobin, electrolytes, coagulation; ICU admission
🔑 Dantrolene: Acts directly on RYR1 to block calcium release from the sarcoplasmic reticulum. It is the ONLY specific treatment for MH. Dantrolene must be available wherever triggering agents are used (OR suites). Ryanodex is a newer formulation requiring less reconstitution volume.
⚠️ Do NOT use calcium channel blockers with dantrolene: The combination of dantrolene + verapamil/diltiazem can cause life-threatening hyperkalemia and cardiovascular collapse.
