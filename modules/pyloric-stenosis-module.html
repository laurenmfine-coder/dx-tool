🍼 Pyloric Stenosis

The "Hungry Vomiter" - Hypertrophic Pylorus → Gastric Outlet Obstruction

📚 Core Definition

Infantile Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis (IHPS) = Progressive hypertrophy and hyperplasia of the pyloric muscle causing gastric outlet obstruction

Epidemiology:

  • Incidence: 2-4 per 1,000 live births
  • Male:Female ratio = 4:1 (much more common in males)
  • Classic age: 2-8 weeks (rarely presents before 2 weeks or after 12 weeks)
  • More common in firstborn children
  • Associated with macrolide antibiotics (erythromycin) in first 2 weeks of life

⚙️ Pathophysiology

The Mechanism Chain:

Pyloric muscle hypertrophy
→
Narrowed pyloric channel
→
Gastric outlet obstruction
→
Forceful projectile vomiting
→
Loss of gastric contents (HCl)
Key Point: The obstruction is ABOVE the ampulla of Vater, so the vomit is NON-BILIOUS (stomach contents only, no bile). This distinguishes pyloric stenosis from malrotation/volvulus.

The Classic Electrolyte Pattern:

Loss of gastric HCl
→
Hypochloremia
→
Metabolic alkalosis
→
Hypokalemia (renal K+ wasting)
FindingMechanism
HypochloremicDirect loss of HCl in vomitus
HypokalemicKidney exchanges K+ for H+ to conserve acid in alkalosis
Metabolic alkalosisLoss of H+ in vomitus, HCO3- retention
Paradoxical aciduriaKidney excretes H+ to conserve K+ when severely depleted
Memorize: Hypochloremic, Hypokalemic, Metabolic Alkalosis = Classic "pyloric stenosis electrolytes"

🩺 Clinical Presentation

The "Hungry Vomiter":

Classic Triad:
  1. Projectile, non-bilious vomiting - "Shoots across the room"
  2. Palpable "olive" - Firm, mobile, 1-2cm mass in RUQ/epigastrium
  3. Visible peristaltic waves - Left-to-right gastric contractions

Typical History:

  • Age: 2-8 weeks old
  • Progressive vomiting over days to weeks
  • Vomiting typically immediately after feeds
  • Baby appears hungry after vomiting (wants to feed again)
  • Weight loss or failure to thrive
  • Decreased wet diapers (dehydration)
  • No diarrhea, no fever

Physical Exam Findings:

  • "Olive" - Best felt after vomiting, with relaxed abdomen, in RUQ
  • Visible gastric peristalsis (left-to-right waves)
  • Signs of dehydration (sunken fontanelle, dry mucous membranes)
  • May appear well initially despite severe disease
Clinical Pearl: The "olive" is palpable in only 70-80% of cases. Ultrasound is the diagnostic test of choice when physical exam is inconclusive.

🔬 Diagnosis

Ultrasound (Gold Standard):

Diagnostic Criteria:
  • Pyloric muscle thickness ≥3 mm (wall, not total diameter)
  • Pyloric channel length ≥15-17 mm
  • Total pyloric diameter ≥13 mm

Sensitivity: >95%

Upper GI Series (if ultrasound unavailable):

  • "String sign" - Narrow, elongated pyloric channel
  • "Shoulder sign" - Bulging of muscle into antrum
  • "Beak sign" - Tapered distal antrum
  • Delayed gastric emptying

Laboratory Findings:

TestExpected Finding
ChlorideLow (<95 mEq/L)
PotassiumLow (<3.5 mEq/L)
BicarbonateHigh (>30 mEq/L)
pHElevated (alkalosis)
BUNMay be elevated (dehydration)

💊 Management

⚠️ Pyloric stenosis is NOT a surgical emergency!
Resuscitate FIRST, operate SECOND. Correct fluid and electrolyte abnormalities before surgery.

Step 1: Fluid Resuscitation

  • NPO status
  • IV fluid bolus: NS 20 mL/kg
  • Maintenance fluids: D5 ½NS with KCl (once urinating)
  • Goal: Correct dehydration AND electrolyte abnormalities
  • Target before surgery: Cl >100, K+ >3.5, HCO3 <30

Step 2: Pyloromyotomy (Ramstedt Procedure)

Surgical Treatment:
  • Longitudinal incision through hypertrophied muscle
  • Muscle is split, NOT removed
  • Mucosa is NOT entered
  • Can be done open or laparoscopically
  • Curative - does not recur

Post-operative Care:

  • Feeding typically resumes within 4-8 hours
  • Some post-op vomiting is common (self-limited)
  • Most discharged within 24-48 hours
  • Excellent prognosis
Why wait for surgery? Anesthetizing a baby with severe metabolic alkalosis is dangerous. Alkalosis shifts the oxygen-hemoglobin dissociation curve left, causes hypoventilation (respiratory compensation), and can cause arrhythmias from hypokalemia.

🔄 Differential Diagnosis

Pyloric Stenosis

  • Age 2-8 weeks
  • NON-bilious vomiting
  • Progressive
  • "Hungry vomiter"
  • Metabolic alkalosis

Malrotation/Volvulus

  • Any age (often first weeks)
  • BILIOUS vomiting
  • Acute onset
  • Abdominal distension
  • Surgical EMERGENCY
Red Flag: BILIOUS vomiting in a neonate = Malrotation with volvulus until proven otherwise. This IS a surgical emergency (risk of bowel necrosis).

Other Differentials:

  • GERD - Non-projectile, no weight loss, no electrolyte abnormalities
  • Overfeeding - Normal weight gain, normal electrolytes
  • Milk protein allergy - May have bloody stools, eczema
  • Adrenal insufficiency - Hyponatremia, hyperkalemia (opposite electrolytes!)

🧠 Test Your Understanding

A 4-week-old male infant presents with 5 days of progressively worsening vomiting after feeds. The vomit is non-bilious. Labs show: Na 138, K 2.9, Cl 88, HCO3 34. What is the expected arterial blood pH?

A. 7.25 (acidemia)
B. 7.52 (alkalemia)
C. 7.40 (normal)
D. 7.32 (mild acidemia)
Correct Answer: B

This infant has the classic presentation and electrolyte pattern of pyloric stenosis: non-bilious vomiting, hypokalemia (K 2.9), hypochloremia (Cl 88), and elevated bicarbonate (34). The loss of gastric acid (HCl) causes metabolic alkalosis, which would manifest as an elevated pH (alkalemia). The expected ABG would show pH >7.45 with elevated bicarbonate and potentially some respiratory compensation (elevated pCO2).
