Clinical Vignette
A 62-year-old man with COPD presents with 3 days of productive cough, fever (39.1°C), and right-sided pleuritic chest pain. SpO₂ is 91% on room air. Decreased breath sounds and dullness to percussion at the right base.

Pneumonia Epidemiology

Pneumonia etiology varies dramatically by setting and host: Community-acquired: S. pneumoniae (#1), H. influenzae, atypicals (Mycoplasma in young adults, Legionella in smokers/travelers). COPD exacerbation: H. influenzae, Moraxella, Pseudomonas if severe COPD. Immunocompromised: add PCP, fungi, TB. Post-hurricane/flood: Aspergillus, Legionella from contaminated water systems. Geographic: Coccidioides (Southwest US), Histoplasma (Ohio/Mississippi valley). Always ask about travel, exposures, and immune status.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: In South Florida, consider endemic fungi (Histoplasma, Coccidioides in travelers from the West) and Legionella (cruise ships, hot tubs, industrial water systems). Post-hurricane flooding increases Aspergillus and mold-related pneumonias — ask about water damage and remediation exposure.
A. PA and lateral chest X-ray
B. CT chest with IV contrast
C. CT pulmonary angiography
D. No imaging — treat empirically
Why CXR?

PA and lateral CXR is first-line for suspected CAP. It confirms the diagnosis, characterizes the pattern (lobar vs interstitial vs multifocal), and identifies complications like effusion or abscess. CT is reserved for non-responders or immunocompromised patients.

A. Bilateral diffuse ground-glass opacities
B. Right lower lobe consolidation with possible parapneumonic effusion
C. Right upper lobe cavitary lesion
D. Normal chest X-ray
Hypothesis Formation

Focal decreased breath sounds + dullness at the right base → right lower lobe consolidation. Dullness may also indicate parapneumonic effusion. Form this hypothesis BEFORE viewing the film.

Clinical Pearl: Air bronchograms within consolidation suggest alveolar filling (pneumonia) rather than atelectasis. The silhouette sign localizes — loss of right heart border = RML; loss of hemidiaphragm = RLL.
ABCDEFGHI Approach: Airway (trachea midline?), Bones (fractures?), Cardiac (size/silhouette), Diaphragm (flat? free air?), Effusions (costophrenic angles), Fields (lung parenchyma), Gastric bubble, Hilum (LAD?), Instruments/lines

This case: trachea midline, cardiac normal, right hemidiaphragm obscured (→ RLL), right costophrenic blunting (→ effusion), dense RLL opacity with air bronchograms (→ consolidation).

Management Integration
  • Lobar pattern → typical bacterial (S. pneumoniae) → beta-lactam + macrolide or respiratory fluoroquinolone
  • Parapneumonic effusion >10mm → thoracentesis to rule out empyema (pH, glucose, LDH, Gram stain)
  • COPD + hypoxia + effusion → admit (CURB-65/PSI scoring)
  • Follow-up CXR 6-8 weeks in >50yo/smokers to rule out underlying malignancy
Red Flag: Effusion with pH <7.2, glucose <60, or positive Gram stain → complicated effusion/empyema → chest tube drainage

A.Recent surgery is the only relevant risk factor
B.Wells score integrates multiple epidemiological risk factors: recent surgery +
C.PE can only occur in hospitalized patients
VTE Risk Stratification

VTE risk assessment is epidemiology in action. The Wells score quantifies clinical probability: clinical signs of DVT (+3), PE most likely diagnosis (+3), HR >100 (+1.5), immobilization/surgery (+1.5), prior VTE (+1.5), hemoptysis (+1), malignancy (+1). Score >4 = PE likely → CTPA. This patient has surgery + immobilization + DVT signs + tachycardia = very high probability. D-dimer is only useful in LOW probability patients — it should not be ordered here.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: Cancer-associated VTE is increasingly common as patients live longer with malignancy. Occult cancer is found in 4-10% of patients with unprovoked VTE — age-appropriate cancer screening is recommended. In young women, always ask about hormonal contraceptives and pregnancy status.
A. Chest X-ray
B. CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA)
C. V/Q scan
D. Lower extremity duplex ultrasound
Why CTPA?

High pretest probability (recent surgery + immobilization + leg swelling + tachycardia + hypoxia) → CTPA is gold standard. V/Q alternative if contrast contraindicated. LE duplex confirms DVT but doesn't assess PE severity or RV strain.

Modality Reasoning: CTPA gives direct clot visualization, RV/LV ratio, and alternative diagnoses. In pregnancy, V/Q sometimes preferred (lower breast dose), though CTPA delivers lower total fetal dose.
A. Bilateral ground-glass opacities
B. Aortic intimal flap
C. Filling defects in pulmonary arteries + possible RV enlargement
D. Normal CTPA with pleural effusion
Expected Findings

Intraluminal filling defects — contrast wrapping around thrombus. With this degree of hypoxia/tachycardia, expect significant clot burden. RV/LV ratio >1.0 indicates right heart strain (submassive PE).

CTPA Systematic Approach: Trace from main PA → right/left main → lobar → segmental → subsegmental. Assess: filling defects (acute = central with obtuse angles; chronic = eccentric/calcified), RV/LV ratio (>1.0 = strain), septal bowing, IVC contrast reflux, pleural effusion, lung infarct (wedge-shaped peripheral opacity).
📚 Radiopaedia: PE Cases →
PE Severity → Treatment
  • Massive (hypotensive, SBP<90) → systemic thrombolytics or catheter-directed therapy
  • Submassive (stable + RV strain + elevated troponin/BNP) → anticoagulation + monitoring ± escalation
  • Low-risk (no RV strain, normal biomarkers) → anticoagulation, may qualify for outpatient (sPESI=0)
Critical: If hypotensive before CTPA obtained → empiric thrombolytics or cath lab based on clinical diagnosis + bedside echo showing RV dilation. Don't delay for imaging.

Clinical Vignette
A 23-year-old man after a motorcycle accident. Intubated, hypotensive (BP 78/50), HR 138. Absent breath sounds on the left, tracheal deviation to the right, distended neck veins.

Which of the following best describes the epidemiological and clinical significance of this presentation?

A.Motorcycle accidents have the highest fatality rate of any motor vehicle type.
B.Pneumothorax only occurs spontaneously in tall, thin men
C.Trauma patients don't need imaging before treatment
Traumatic vs Spontaneous PTX

Pneumothorax has distinct epidemiological profiles: Primary spontaneous: tall, thin males age 20-40, smokers, apical blebs. Secondary spontaneous: underlying lung disease (COPD, cystic fibrosis, Marfan, catamenial in women). Traumatic: blunt/penetrating chest trauma — motorcycle accidents have 3× the thoracic injury rate vs car accidents. Iatrogenic: central line placement (subclavian > IJ), thoracentesis, lung biopsy, positive pressure ventilation. The mechanism determines the treatment approach and associated injuries to evaluate.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: Spontaneous pneumothorax recurrence is 30% after first episode and 50% after second — pleurodesis is recommended after second occurrence or first bilateral occurrence. In catamenial pneumothorax (menstrual-related, right-sided), thoracic endometriosis is the cause — gynecology referral is essential.
A. Portable chest X-ray
B. CT chest
C. Bedside ultrasound (E-FAST)
D. No imaging — immediate needle decompression
This Is a Clinical Diagnosis

Tension pneumothorax in trauma with the classic triad (hypotension + absent breath sounds + tracheal deviation) demands IMMEDIATE needle decompression. Imaging delays can be fatal.

Teaching Point: Needle decompression (2nd ICS midclavicular or 5th ICS anterior axillary) is both diagnostic and therapeutic. CXR AFTER decompression confirms chest tube placement.
E-FAST Exception: In hemodynamically stable patients with equivocal exam, bedside US detects absent lung sliding in seconds — faster than CXR. But in classic tension physiology, decompress first.

ARDS Triggers and Phenotypes

Pulmonary ARDS (direct lung injury): pneumonia, aspiration, inhalation injury, pulmonary contusion. Extrapulmonary ARDS (systemic inflammation → lung injury): sepsis, pancreatitis, massive transfusion (TRALI), trauma, burns. The distinction matters: extrapulmonary ARDS may have more recruitable lung tissue and respond better to PEEP. Pancreatitis-associated ARDS carries ~20-40% mortality depending on the severity of pancreatic necrosis.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: ARDS mortality has decreased from ~70% to ~30-40% primarily through low tidal volume ventilation and prone positioning — highlighting that clinical practice changes (not new drugs) have driven the improvement. COVID-19 ARDS highlighted phenotypic differences (L-type vs H-type) that may require different ventilation approaches.
A. Portable AP chest X-ray
B. CT pulmonary angiography
C. Chest CT with contrast
D. Echocardiogram
CXR First

Berlin criteria require bilateral opacities on CXR/CT not explained by effusions or atelectasis. Portable CXR avoids transporting an unstable patient. Echo is reasonable to rule out cardiogenic edema but isn't first-line imaging.

Management by P/F Ratio
  • Mild (200-300): Low tidal volume (6 mL/kg IBW), PEEP per ARDSNet
  • Moderate (100-200): Add prone positioning ≥16 h/day (PROSEVA trial)
  • Severe (<100): All above + NMB blockade 48h, ECMO referral
  • This patient (P/F 142): Moderate → intubation + lung-protective vent + prone

A 70-year-old woman with heart failure and 10-lb weight gain presents with progressive dyspnea. Elevated JVP, diminished basilar breath sounds bilaterally. BNP 1,400 pg/mL.

B.Known heart failure + bilateral effusions + 10-lb weight gain
C.BNP levels are unreliable in elderly patients

Effusion Etiology by Context

Pleural effusion etiology varies by population: In the US/developed world: HF (#1), pneumonia (#2), malignancy (#3). Globally: tuberculosis is the most common cause of exudative effusion. In cancer patients: malignant effusion (lung, breast, lymphoma). In ICU patients: parapneumonic, volume overload, atelectasis. In liver disease: hepatic hydrothorax (transudative, usually right-sided). Knowing the patient's context allows you to predict the cause before tapping the fluid.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: In areas with high TB prevalence or large immigrant populations, tuberculous pleural effusion must be on the differential for any lymphocyte-predominant exudative effusion — especially in young patients. ADA (adenosine deaminase) >40 U/L in pleural fluid has high sensitivity for TB pleuritis.
A. PA/lateral CXR + bedside ultrasound
B. CT chest with contrast
C. Echocardiogram only
D. Left lateral decubitus X-ray
Multi-Modality Approach

CXR confirms effusion and assesses cardiac size. US guides thoracentesis (reduces pneumothorax risk from ~12% to <2%). In known HF with bilateral effusions and elevated BNP, diurese first — thoracentesis if asymmetric, febrile, or refractory.

Management
  • Bilateral + HF + elevated BNP: Diurese. Tap only if asymmetric/febrile/refractory
  • Unilateral or unexplained: Diagnostic thoracentesis → Light's criteria
  • Exudate: Cell count, culture, Gram stain, cytology, ± amylase/triglycerides/ADA
  • Complicated/empyema: pH<7.2, glucose<60, positive Gram stain → chest tube

TB Epidemiology in the US

TB in the US is primarily a disease of foreign-born individuals (>70% of US TB cases). High-prevalence countries: India, China, Philippines, Vietnam, Mexico. Risk factors for progression from LTBI to active TB: HIV (100× risk), TNF-alpha inhibitors, organ transplant immunosuppression, diabetes (3× risk), silicosis, chronic renal failure, and recent infection (<2 years). MDR-TB risk increases with prior treatment, origin from Eastern Europe, Central Asia, India, or China. Always assess drug resistance risk before starting empiric RIPE therapy.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: In US healthcare settings, TB exposure is an occupational hazard — annual PPD/IGRA testing for healthcare workers is standard. Failure to isolate a TB patient exposes other patients and staff, creating a public health investigation. The medicolegal implications of missed TB are significant.
A. PA and lateral CXR
B. CT chest with contrast
C. CT-guided biopsy
D. No imaging — start RIPE empirically
CXR for TB

CXR is initial imaging. Determines active vs latent, reactivation vs primary pattern, extent of disease, and need for isolation. Sputum AFB × 3 confirms the diagnosis — imaging alone doesn't.

A. Bilateral lower lobe infiltrates
B. Upper lobe infiltrates with cavitation
C. Miliary pattern (diffuse tiny nodules)
D. Hilar LAD with Ghon complex
TB Patterns

Reactivation (this case): upper lobe cavitary disease — TB bacilli thrive in high O₂ (apices).
Primary (children/first exposure): mid/lower lobe + hilar LAD → Ghon focus → Ranke complex.
Miliary (hematogenous): diffuse 1-3mm nodules = "millet seeds."

Cavitation = High Infectivity: Cavitary lesions have highest bacillary burden. Strict airborne isolation until 3 negative AFB smears.
Management
  • Cavitary → airborne isolation, longer treatment if culture+ at 2 months
  • Miliary → check extrapulmonary (LP for TB meningitis, liver, bone marrow)
  • Normal CXR + positive IGRA → LTBI → treat to prevent reactivation (3HP, 4R, or 9H)
  • All active TB → RIPE × 2 months, then RI × 4 months

Low-Dose CT (LDCT)Long-Term Smoker — Screening Protocol
Clinical Vignette
A 62-year-old male presents for his annual wellness visit. He smoked 1.5 packs/day for 30 years and quit 8 years ago. He has COPD and mild emphysema. No respiratory symptoms beyond baseline. His wife recently died of lung cancer, prompting him to ask about screening. He works in construction with prior asbestos exposure.

Which of the following best describes the epidemiological and clinical significance of this presentation?

A.Only current smokers benefit from lung cancer screening
B.Quitting smoking 8 years ago eliminates his lung cancer risk
C.Lung cancer screening is recommended for all adults over 50
D.USPSTF 2021 criteria: age 50-80, ≥20 pack-year smoking history, currently smoke or quit within 15 years.
Lung Cancer Screening Eligibility and Epidemiology

Lung cancer is the #1 cause of cancer death in both men and women. USPSTF 2021 expanded criteria (vs 2013): lowered age from 55→50 and pack-years from 30→20, adding ~6.4 million eligible Americans. The NLST trial (53,000+ participants): LDCT vs CXR screening showed 20% lung cancer mortality reduction. Risk factors beyond smoking: asbestos (synergistic — smoking × asbestos = 50-90× risk), radon (2nd leading cause of lung cancer), occupational carcinogens (silica, diesel exhaust, chromium), family history, COPD/emphysema, prior radiation therapy. Health disparities: Black men have the highest lung cancer incidence and mortality, yet screening uptake is lowest in Black and rural populations.

🌍 Epidemiologic Pearl: Despite strong evidence, LDCT screening uptake in the US is only ~5-15% of eligible individuals — one of the largest gaps between evidence and implementation in cancer screening. Barriers include lack of awareness, primary care physician screening behavior, insurance navigation, and the stigma of smoking ('I deserve it'). Integrating screening with smoking cessation counseling doubles quit rates — every screening visit is a cessation opportunity.
A.Low-dose CT chest (LDCT) — the only validated screening modality for lung cancer.
B.Standard chest CT with contrast
C.Chest X-ray annually (the prior standard)
LDCT — The Only Validated Screening Modality

LDCT uses ultra-low radiation dose (~1.5 mSv vs ~7 mSv for standard chest CT) without IV contrast. CXR is NOT adequate for lung cancer screening — the NLST and PLCO trials showed no mortality benefit from CXR screening. Results are reported using Lung-RADS (Lung CT Screening Reporting and Data System): standardizes nodule management based on size, morphology, and growth. Annual LDCT continues until the patient no longer meets criteria (age >80, quit >15 years ago, or develops a health condition limiting life expectancy or willingness to pursue treatment).

A.Lung-RADS 1 (negative) — completely normal scan
B.Large central mass (advanced cancer)
C.Given his emphysema, expect background changes
Lung-RADS Classification and False Positive Rates

Lung-RADS categories: 1 (negative, no nodules): continue annual screening. 2 (benign appearance): continue annual screening. 3 (probably benign, <1% malignancy): 6-month LDCT follow-up. 4A (suspicious, 5-15% malignancy): 3-month LDCT or PET-CT. 4B (very suspicious, >15%): chest CT with contrast, PET-CT, and/or tissue sampling. 4X (additional features raising suspicion). False positive management is the biggest challenge: ~25% of screens are positive, but >95% of those are benign. This creates anxiety, additional imaging, and occasional unnecessary procedures.

A.Biopsy all nodules found on screening
B.Management per Lung-RADS category: Lung-RADS 1-2 → annual LDCT.
C.PET-CT for all positive screens
Lung-RADS Drives Structured Follow-Up

The Lung-RADS system prevents both overreaction (unnecessary biopsies for benign nodules) and underreaction (missing early cancers). PET-CT is useful for nodules >8mm to assess metabolic activity (SUV >2.5 suggests malignancy), but has limitations: false positives from inflammation/infection, false negatives for slow-growing adenocarcinomas and carcinoid. Tissue diagnosis can be obtained via: CT-guided percutaneous biopsy (peripheral nodules), navigational bronchoscopy (central or difficult-to-access), or surgical resection (VATS lobectomy for highly suspicious lesions). Multidisciplinary tumor board review is recommended for all Lung-RADS 4 findings.

Screening + Cessation + Incidental Finding Management

LDCT screening is not just cancer detection — it's a comprehensive lung health intervention. Incidental findings on LDCT are extremely common: coronary artery calcification (predicts cardiovascular events), emphysema severity (may change COPD management), thyroid nodules, adrenal incidentalomas, and vertebral compression fractures. Each requires appropriate follow-up. His asbestos exposure history may qualify him for additional surveillance programs (VA, workers' compensation). The screening visit is also the ideal time to reinforce smoking cessation, update vaccinations (pneumococcal, influenza), and address other preventive care gaps.
