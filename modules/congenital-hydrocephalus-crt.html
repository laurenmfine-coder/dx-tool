📖 Teaching Point

Congenital hydrocephalus signs in infants: (1) Rapidly increasing HC (crossing percentiles); (2) Bulging, tense anterior fontanelle; (3) Splayed sutures (palpable gaps); (4) Prominent scalp veins (due to venous congestion); (5) Sunset sign (Parinaud — impaired upgaze); (6) Irritability or lethargy; (7) Poor feeding; (8) 'Cracked pot' sound on percussion (Macewen sign). The open fontanelles and unfused sutures allow head expansion, which initially compensates for increased pressure.

📖 Teaching Point

CSF pathway: Choroid plexus (lateral ventricles) → foramen of Monro → 3rd ventricle → cerebral aqueduct (of Sylvius) → 4th ventricle → foramina of Luschka (lateral) and Magendie (midline) → subarachnoid space → arachnoid granulations → venous sinuses. Obstruction at ANY point causes hydrocephalus. The location determines which ventricles dilate: aqueductal stenosis = lateral + 3rd dilated, 4th normal.

📖 Teaching Point

Causes of congenital hydrocephalus: AQUEDUCTAL STENOSIS (most common congenital cause) — can be genetic (L1CAM/HSAS), post-inflammatory (TORCH infections causing gliosis), or idiopathic. DANDY-WALKER MALFORMATION — cystic 4th ventricle obstructing outflow. CHIARI II — with myelomeningocele. VEIN OF GALEN MALFORMATION — AV malformation compressing the aqueduct. CONGENITAL TUMORS — posterior fossa tumors. TORCH INFECTIONS — CMV, toxo causing inflammation → scarring → obstruction.

📖 Teaching Point

VP shunt complications: (1) OBSTRUCTION (30-40% in year 1) — most common overall; (2) INFECTION (5-10%) — Staph epidermidis > S. aureus, treat with shunt removal + external drainage + antibiotics → new shunt; (3) OVER-DRAINAGE — headache when upright, slit ventricles, subdural hematomas; (4) SHUNT MIGRATION — catheter tip moves; (5) PERITONEAL COMPLICATIONS — pseudocyst, CSF ascites. Children with shunts are 'shunt-dependent' — any acute illness with vomiting/lethargy must include shunt evaluation.

📖 Teaching Point

ETV vs VP Shunt: ETV advantage = no hardware, no shunt-dependent patient, no risk of shunt infection/malfunction. ETV disadvantage = lower success rate in young infants, not suitable for communicating hydrocephalus, risk of basilar artery injury during procedure. ETV SUCCESS SCORE: predicts outcome based on age, etiology, and previous shunts. Highest success: age >1 year + aqueductal stenosis + no prior shunt. ETV + choroid plexus cauterization (CPC) is used in some centers for infants.

🎓 Integration & Key Takeaways
The VP shunt functions well. At 2 years, the child's head circumference has normalized and he's meeting developmental milestones with early intervention services. The family plans genetic counseling for future pregnancies given the L1CAM mutation.
