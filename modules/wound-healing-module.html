Phases of Wound Healing
PhaseTimingKey EventsKey Cells
HemostasisImmediateVasoconstriction, platelet plug, fibrin clotPlatelets
InflammationDays 1-4Neutrophils (first 24-48h, clear debris/bacteria), then macrophages (MOST IMPORTANT cell in wound healing — coordinate all phases)Neutrophils → Macrophages
ProliferationDays 4-21Granulation tissue, angiogenesis, collagen deposition (type III initially), wound contraction (myofibroblasts), epithelializationFibroblasts (collagen), Myofibroblasts
RemodelingDay 21 - 1 year+Type III collagen replaced by Type I collagen. Cross-linking increases strength. Maximum tensile strength = 80% of original (never 100%)Fibroblasts
🔑 Maximum Wound Strength: Wounds reach only 80% of original tensile strength, never 100%. Type I collagen (mature) replaces Type III (immature). Collagen cross-linking continues for up to 1 year.

Wound Types & Complications

Wound Closure Types

TypeDescriptionExample
Primary intentionEdges approximated, minimal granulationClean surgical incision closed with sutures
Secondary intentionWound left open, heals by granulation + contractionAbscess cavity after I&D, pressure ulcers
Tertiary (delayed primary)Wound left open initially, closed later (3-5 days)Contaminated wounds cleaned, then closed once infection risk passes

Wound Complications

ComplicationKey Features
DehiscenceWound separation. Risk: malnutrition, steroids, obesity, infection, excessive coughing. Salmon-colored peritoneal fluid leaking = impending evisceration
EviscerationProtrusion of abdominal contents through wound. EMERGENCY: moist sterile dressing, NPO, OR
KeloidScar extends BEYOND wound borders. More common in dark skin, earlobes, sternum. Treat: steroid injection, excision + radiation, silicone sheets
Hypertrophic scarRaised scar WITHIN wound borders. Often improves with time. Treat: pressure garments, silicone, steroid injection
SeromaSerous fluid collection. Aspiration, compression
HematomaBlood collection. Evacuate if expanding/infected
TREATMENT
Nutrition & Factors Affecting Healing

Nutritional Requirements for Wound Healing

NutrientRole in Wound HealingDeficiency Effect
Vitamin CCollagen synthesis (hydroxylation of proline and lysine)Scurvy: impaired collagen cross-linking, wound breakdown, bleeding gums
ZincDNA synthesis, cell division, immune functionImpaired epithelialization and immune response
Vitamin AEpithelial growth, immune function. REVERSES steroid impairment of healingPoor epithelialization
ProteinCollagen synthesis, immune functionAlbumin <3: impaired healing. Pre-albumin better marker of acute nutrition
IronOxygen delivery via hemoglobinAnemia → poor tissue oxygenation
🔑 Factors That Impair Healing: Diabetes (microvascular disease, impaired neutrophil function), smoking (#1 modifiable risk factor — nicotine vasoconstriction), steroids (anti-inflammatory → impaired all phases; Vitamin A can partially reverse), malnutrition, radiation, infection, ischemia/hypoxia, advanced age.
⚠️ Vitamin A reverses steroid-impaired wound healing. This is high-yield. Mechanism: Vitamin A increases inflammation and collagen synthesis, counteracting steroid effects. Does NOT reverse the impairment from other causes (diabetes, radiation, etc.).
