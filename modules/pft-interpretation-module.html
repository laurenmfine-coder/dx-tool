📏 Lung Volumes & Capacities

Understanding lung volumes and capacities is fundamental to PFT interpretation. Volumes are single measurements that cannot be subdivided, while capacities are combinations of two or more volumes.

The Four Primary Lung Volumes

VolumeDefinitionNormal ValueMeasured By
Tidal Volume (TV)Volume inhaled/exhaled during normal breathing~500 mLSpirometry
Inspiratory Reserve (IRV)Additional volume inhaled after normal inspiration~3000 mLSpirometry
Expiratory Reserve (ERV)Additional volume exhaled after normal expiration~1200 mLSpirometry
Residual Volume (RV)Volume remaining after maximal expiration~1200 mLPlethysmography
🔑 Clinical Pearl

RV cannot be measured by spirometry because you cannot exhale it. This is why spirometry alone cannot diagnose restriction - you need body plethysmography or gas dilution to measure TLC.

Lung Capacities

CapacityComponentsNormal ValueClinical Significance
Total Lung Capacity (TLC)TV + IRV + ERV + RV~6000 mL↓ in restriction, ↑ in hyperinflation
Vital Capacity (VC)TV + IRV + ERV~4800 mL↓ in both obstruction and restriction
Functional Residual Capacity (FRC)ERV + RV~2400 mL↑ in air trapping/hyperinflation
Inspiratory Capacity (IC)TV + IRV~3500 mL↓ in hyperinflation

🌬️ Spirometry: The Cornerstone of PFT Interpretation

Key Spirometric Parameters

#ParameterDefinitionClinical Use
1FVCTotal volume exhaled during maximal forced expirationReduced in both obstruction and restriction
2FEV₁Volume exhaled in the first second of FVCPrimary measure of airflow obstruction severity
3FEV₁/FVC RatioPercentage of FVC exhaled in first secondTHE key discriminator: obstruction vs restriction
4FEF₂₅₋₇₅Forced expiratory flow at 25-75% of FVCReflects small airway function (effort-independent)
💡 The Golden Rule

The FEV₁/FVC ratio is the FIRST number to evaluate. It determines whether the pattern is obstructive, restrictive, or mixed. Everything else follows from this.

Interpreting the FEV₁/FVC Ratio

PatternFEV₁/FVCFEV₁FVCTLC
Normal≥0.70 (or LLN)NormalNormalNormal
Obstructive<0.70↓↓Normal or ↓Normal or ↑
Restrictive≥0.70 (often ↑)↓↓↓↓ (required)
Mixed<0.70↓↓↓↓↓
⚠️ Critical Pitfall: Pseudorestriction

A low FVC alone does NOT confirm restriction. In severe obstruction, air trapping can reduce FVC. TLC measurement is required to confirm true restrictive disease. If TLC is normal or elevated, the low FVC is due to air trapping, not restriction.

🔄 Systematic PFT Interpretation Algorithm

4-Step Interpretation Approach

1
Is the FEV₁/FVC ratio reduced (<0.70 or below LLN)?
→ YES: Obstructive pattern - proceed to Step 2
→ NO: Proceed to Step 3
2
Grade obstruction severity by FEV₁ (% predicted):
Mild: ≥70% | Moderate: 60-69% | Mod-Severe: 50-59% | Severe: 35-49% | Very Severe: <35%
3
If ratio normal, is FVC reduced (<80%)?
→ YES: Possible restriction - need TLC to confirm
→ NO: Normal spirometry
4
Assess bronchodilator response:
Positive = ≥12% AND ≥200 mL improvement in FEV₁ or FVC → suggests reversible obstruction

📈 Flow-Volume Loop Interpretation

The flow-volume loop provides visual representation of airflow during forced breathing. The expiratory limb (above x-axis) and inspiratory limb (below x-axis) reveal distinct patterns.

Normal

Features: Rapid rise to peak flow, linear descent. Symmetric inspiratory limb.

Obstructive (Asthma/COPD)

Features: "Scooped" concave expiratory limb due to airflow limitation.

Restrictive

Features: "Tall and thin" - normal shape but reduced volumes.

Fixed Upper Airway Obstruction

Features: BOTH limbs flattened. Think: tracheal stenosis, goiter.

Variable Extrathoracic

Features: INSPIRATORY limb flattened. Think: VCD, vocal cord paralysis.

Variable Intrathoracic

Features: EXPIRATORY limb flattened. Think: tracheomalacia.
💡 Understanding the Physics

Why does location matter? During inspiration, negative intrathoracic pressure pulls extrathoracic airways inward (worsening extrathoracic obstruction). During expiration, positive pressure compresses intrathoracic airways (worsening intrathoracic obstruction). Fixed obstructions are unaffected by pressure changes.

🫁 DLCO: Diffusing Capacity

The DLCO measures the lung's ability to transfer gas from alveoli to blood. It reflects alveolar-capillary membrane integrity and pulmonary capillary blood volume.

Spirometry PatternDLCOLikely Diagnosis
Obstructive↓↓Emphysema (alveolar destruction)
Normal or ↑Asthma, chronic bronchitis (airways only)
Restrictive↓↓Interstitial lung disease (IPF, sarcoidosis)
NormalExtrapulmonary restriction (chest wall, neuromuscular)
Normal spirometry↓Pulmonary vascular disease, early ILD, anemia
Normal spirometry↑Polycythemia, pulmonary hemorrhage, L→R shunt
🔑 High-Yield Clinical Application

DLCO distinguishes emphysema from chronic bronchitis: Both have obstructive spirometry, but emphysema has LOW DLCO (alveolar destruction) while chronic bronchitis has NORMAL DLCO (airway inflammation only).
