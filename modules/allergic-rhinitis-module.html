🤧 Allergic Rhinitis

MechanismDx Module - Understanding the Pathophysiology & Management

📚 Overview & Epidemiology

Allergic rhinitis (AR) is an IgE-mediated inflammatory disease of the nasal mucosa characterized by sneezing, rhinorrhea, nasal congestion, and pruritus.

Key Facts

  • Prevalence: 10-30% of adults, up to 40% of children
  • Economic burden: $3-4 billion annually in direct costs
  • Quality of life: Significant impact on sleep, work, school performance
  • Comorbidities: Asthma (20-40% comorbidity), sinusitis, otitis media, sleep apnea

Classification: ARIA Guidelines

Intermittent vs Persistent:

  • Intermittent Symptoms <4 days/week OR <4 consecutive weeks
  • Persistent Symptoms >4 days/week AND >4 consecutive weeks

Mild vs Moderate-Severe:

  • Mild No impairment of sleep, daily activities, work/school
  • Moderate-Severe ≥1 of: sleep disturbance, impaired activities, impaired work/school, troublesome symptoms
🔑 Clinical Pearl: Always ask about asthma! The "unified airway" concept recognizes that AR and asthma frequently coexist. Treating AR can improve asthma control.

🔬 Pathophysiology

Two-Phase Response

Early Phase (Minutes)

Allergen exposure → IgE cross-linking on mast cells → Mast cell degranulation → Histamine, tryptase, PGD2, leukotrienes

Clinical effect: Sneezing, itching, rhinorrhea, early congestion (within 5-30 minutes)

Late Phase (4-8 hours)

Cytokine release (IL-4, IL-5, IL-13) → Eosinophil recruitment → Tissue inflammation → Nasal congestion (predominant)

Clinical effect: Persistent congestion, mucosal swelling, continued symptoms

Key Mediators & Their Effects

Mediator Source Clinical Effect
Histamine Mast cells, basophils Itch, sneeze, rhinorrhea, early congestion
Leukotrienes (CysLTs) Mast cells, eosinophils Prolonged congestion, mucus secretion
Prostaglandin D2 Mast cells Congestion, Th2 cell recruitment
IL-4, IL-13 Th2 cells, ILC2s IgE class switching, goblet cell hyperplasia
IL-5 Th2 cells, ILC2s Eosinophil survival and recruitment
🔑 Clinical Pearl: This two-phase response explains why antihistamines help itching/sneezing (early phase) but are less effective for congestion (late phase) – use intranasal steroids for congestion!

🌿 Common Allergens & Patterns

Seasonal (Intermittent) Allergens

🌸 Tree Pollen

Season: Early spring (Feb-May)

Oak, birch, cedar, maple

🌾 Grass Pollen

Season: Late spring-summer (May-July)

Timothy, Bermuda, Kentucky blue

🍂 Weed Pollen

Season: Late summer-fall (Aug-Oct)

Ragweed (most common), sagebrush

🍄 Outdoor Mold

Season: Varies, peaks fall

Alternaria, Cladosporium

Perennial (Persistent) Allergens

🏠 Dust Mites

Der p 1, Der f 1 allergens

Thrive in humidity >50%

🐱 Cat Dander

Fel d 1 (saliva protein)

Highly persistent in environment

🐕 Dog Dander

Can f 1

No truly "hypoallergenic" breeds

🪳 Cockroach

Bla g 1, Bla g 2

Major urban allergen

Pattern Timing Likely Allergen

🔑 Clinical Pearl: "Hypoallergenic" pets don't exist! All cats produce Fel d 1 (in saliva/skin), and all dogs produce Can f 1. Some breeds produce LESS, but none produce zero.

🔍 Diagnosis

Clinical Features

Cardinal Symptoms (The "SOAP" of AR)

  • Sneezing (paroxysmal, often in clusters)
  • Obstruction (nasal congestion)
  • Aqueous rhinorrhea (clear, watery discharge)
  • Pruritus (nasal, ocular, palatal)

Physical Exam Findings

Finding Description Significance
Allergic shiners Dark circles under eyes Venous congestion from nasal obstruction
Allergic salute Upward rubbing of nose Common in children
Transverse nasal crease Horizontal line across nose From repeated "allergic salute"
Pale, boggy turbinates Bluish-gray nasal mucosa Classic for allergic (vs. red in infectious)
Cobblestoning Bumpy posterior pharynx Lymphoid hyperplasia from postnasal drip
Dennie-Morgan lines Extra eyelid creases Associated with atopy

Diagnostic Testing

Skin Prick Testing (SPT)

  • Gold standard for identifying specific IgE sensitization
  • Results in 15-20 minutes
  • Must hold antihistamines 3-7 days prior
  • Positive = wheal ≥3mm larger than negative control

Serum Specific IgE (ImmunoCAP)

  • Alternative when SPT not feasible
  • No need to hold antihistamines
  • Useful in dermatographism, severe eczema, or antihistamine dependence
  • More expensive than SPT
🔑 Clinical Pearl: A positive test shows SENSITIZATION, not clinical allergy! Always correlate with history. Up to 50% of people with positive tests have no clinical symptoms.

Differential Diagnosis

Condition Key Distinguishing Features
Vasomotor (non-allergic) rhinitis Triggered by irritants, temperature, no IgE sensitization
Infectious rhinitis Purulent discharge, fever, short duration
Rhinitis medicamentosa Rebound congestion from decongestant overuse (>3-5 days)
NARES Non-allergic rhinitis with eosinophilia, negative allergy testing
Anatomic (septal deviation, polyps) Unilateral symptoms, persistent obstruction

💊 Treatment

Treatment Ladder (ARIA-Based)

1
Allergen Avoidance - Foundation for all patients

Dust mite encasings, HEPA filters, pet avoidance if relevant

2
Mild Intermittent: Oral or intranasal antihistamine PRN

2nd-gen oral (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine) or intranasal (azelastine)

3
Moderate-Severe or Persistent: Intranasal corticosteroid (INCS)

Most effective single agent! Fluticasone, mometasone, budesonide

4
Inadequate response: Combination INCS + intranasal antihistamine

Dymista (azelastine/fluticasone) - synergistic effect

5
Refractory: Consider allergen immunotherapy (AIT)

SCIT or SLIT - disease-modifying, prevents asthma progression

Medication Comparison

Medication Class Sneezing/Itch Rhinorrhea Congestion Eye Symptoms
Oral antihistamine +++ ++ + ++
Intranasal antihistamine +++ ++ ++ +
Intranasal corticosteroid +++ +++ +++ ++
Leukotriene antagonist + + ++ +
Decongestant 0 0 +++ 0
🔑 Clinical Pearl: INCS is the most effective single therapy for moderate-severe AR! It takes 1-2 weeks for full effect, so counsel patients on consistent daily use, not PRN.

Allergen Immunotherapy (AIT)

When to Consider

  • Inadequate response to pharmacotherapy
  • Significant side effects from medications
  • Patient preference to avoid long-term medications
  • Comorbid asthma (may prevent progression)

SCIT vs SLIT

Feature SCIT (Shots) SLIT (Tablets/Drops)
Administration Office-based injections Home-based sublingual
Frequency Weekly → monthly Daily
Efficacy +++ ++ (FDA tablets for grass, ragweed, dust mite)
Systemic reaction risk Higher (rare) Lower
Duration 3-5 years 3-5 years
🔑 Clinical Pearl: AIT is DISEASE-MODIFYING! Unlike medications that only control symptoms, AIT can induce long-term tolerance and may prevent allergic rhinitis from progressing to asthma in children.

📝 Knowledge Check

1. A 25-year-old woman has year-round nasal congestion worse in the morning. Skin testing shows sensitivity to dust mites. What is the MOST effective pharmacotherapy?

A. Oral cetirizine daily
B. Oral pseudoephedrine PRN
C. Intranasal fluticasone daily
D. Oral montelukast daily
Correct! Intranasal corticosteroids are the MOST effective single agent for moderate-severe and persistent allergic rhinitis. They address all symptoms including congestion (which antihistamines do poorly). For perennial symptoms with dust mite sensitivity, daily INCS is first-line.

2. Which mediator is primarily responsible for the LATE-PHASE nasal congestion in allergic rhinitis?

A. Histamine
B. Leukotrienes and eosinophil recruitment
C. Bradykinin
D. Substance P
Correct! The late phase (4-8 hours) is driven by cytokines (IL-4, IL-5, IL-13), leukotriene release, and eosinophil recruitment causing tissue inflammation and persistent congestion. Histamine is the key early-phase mediator (sneezing, itching). This explains why antihistamines help itch/sneeze but not congestion.

3. A patient has a positive skin test to cat dander but reports no symptoms around cats. What is the correct interpretation?

A. The test is a false positive and should be repeated
B. The patient has cat allergy and should avoid all cats
C. The patient is sensitized but not clinically allergic; no avoidance needed
D. The patient needs immunotherapy to prevent future reactions
Correct! A positive skin test indicates SENSITIZATION (presence of specific IgE), not necessarily clinical allergy. Up to 50% of sensitized individuals have no symptoms on exposure. Always correlate test results with clinical history. No avoidance or treatment is needed for asymptomatic sensitization.

4. What is the PRIMARY advantage of allergen immunotherapy over pharmacotherapy?

A. Faster symptom relief
B. Disease modification with potential long-term tolerance
C. Lower cost
D. Fewer office visits
Correct! AIT is the only DISEASE-MODIFYING treatment for allergic rhinitis. It induces immunologic tolerance that can persist after treatment ends (3-5 years). It may also prevent new sensitizations and progression from rhinitis to asthma in children. Medications only control symptoms while being taken.

5. Which physical exam finding is MOST specific for allergic rhinitis?

A. Pale, boggy, bluish turbinates
B. Purulent nasal discharge
C. Deviated nasal septum
D. Erythematous turbinates
Correct! Pale, boggy, bluish-gray turbinates are classic for allergic rhinitis. Erythematous (red) turbinates suggest infectious or inflammatory rhinitis. Purulent discharge suggests infection. The pale/boggy appearance results from edema and venous congestion from the allergic inflammatory response.

🎯 Key Takeaways

  • AR has EARLY (histamine → itch/sneeze) and LATE (leukotrienes/eosinophils → congestion) phases
  • INCS is the MOST effective single agent for moderate-severe AR
  • Positive allergy test = sensitization, NOT necessarily clinical allergy
  • AIT is DISEASE-MODIFYING and may prevent asthma progression
  • Always assess for comorbid asthma (unified airway)
