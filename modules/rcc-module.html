🫘 Renal Cell Carcinoma

The "Internist's Tumor" - Classic Triad & Paraneoplastic Syndromes

📚 Overview

Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC) is the most common primary malignancy of the kidney in adults, arising from the renal tubular epithelium. It's known as the "internist's tumor" due to its diverse paraneoplastic manifestations.

Epidemiology

ParameterValue
Rank~3% of adult malignancies; 6th most common
Male:Female2:1
Peak age60-70 years
Incidental detection>50% now found incidentally on imaging
5-year survival (localized)~93%
5-year survival (metastatic)~12%

Risk Factors

  • Smoking (2x risk)
  • Obesity
  • Hypertension
  • Chronic kidney disease/dialysis
  • Acquired cystic kidney disease
  • Family history
  • Genetic syndromes (VHL, hereditary papillary RCC)

🧬 Genetics & Subtypes

Clear Cell RCC (70-80%)

  • Most common subtype
  • VHL gene inactivation (chromosome 3p)
  • Clear cytoplasm (lipid/glycogen)
  • Highly vascular
  • Responds to targeted therapy (TKIs, immunotherapy)

Papillary RCC (10-15%)

  • Type 1: MET mutations; better prognosis
  • Type 2: More aggressive
  • Papillary architecture
  • Less vascular than clear cell

Chromophobe RCC (5%)

  • Birt-Hogg-Dubé syndrome association
  • Best prognosis of main subtypes
  • Large cells with pale cytoplasm

Collecting Duct (Bellini Duct) Carcinoma (<1%)

  • Very aggressive
  • Poor prognosis
  • Central/medullary location

🔍 Clinical Presentation

Classic Triad (Now Uncommon)

Classic Triad (seen in only 10% of patients):
  1. Hematuria (gross or microscopic)
  2. Flank pain
  3. Palpable abdominal mass
This triad usually indicates advanced disease.

Modern Presentation

  • >50% incidental - found on imaging for other reasons
  • Constitutional symptoms (fever, weight loss, fatigue)
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes
  • Metastatic disease symptoms (bone pain, dyspnea)

Varicocele

New LEFT varicocele in an older male should raise concern for RCC with renal vein involvement blocking the left gonadal vein. Right varicocele is concerning for IVC involvement.

💊 Treatment

Localized Disease

LOCALIZED RCC (T1-T3, N0, M0) ↓ ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ T1a (≤4 cm) │ T1b-T3 │ │ Options: │ RADICAL NEPHRECTOMY │ │ • Partial Nx │ (or Partial if │ │ • Active surveil. │ technically feasible)│ │ (small, elderly) │ │ └─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘ Partial (nephron-sparing) preferred when feasible Preserves renal function, equivalent oncologic outcomes for small tumors

Metastatic Disease

Systemic Therapy for Metastatic RCC:
  • Immunotherapy: Checkpoint inhibitors (nivolumab, ipilimumab, pembrolizumab)
  • TKIs: Sunitinib, pazopanib, cabozantinib, axitinib
  • mTOR inhibitors: Temsirolimus, everolimus
  • Combination therapy: Now standard first-line (IO/IO or IO/TKI)
RCC is generally resistant to conventional chemotherapy and radiation.

Cytoreductive Nephrectomy

  • Removing primary tumor even with metastases
  • May improve response to systemic therapy
  • Reduces tumor burden
  • Patient selection important (good performance status)

📋 Key Clinical Pearls

High-Yield Points:
  • Most RCC now found incidentally on imaging
  • Clear cell (70-80%) is most common; VHL pathway
  • Paraneoplastic syndromes in up to 40%
  • New varicocele in older man → suspect RCC
  • Partial nephrectomy preferred for small tumors
  • Immunotherapy + TKIs are mainstay for metastatic disease
  • Can metastasize to unusual sites (skin, thyroid)

🧠 Quick Quiz

A 55-year-old man presents with hematuria and is found to have elevated calcium. CT shows a 6 cm renal mass. The hypercalcemia is most likely due to:

A. Bone metastases
B. PTHrP secretion by the tumor
C. Primary hyperparathyroidism
D. Vitamin D excess
Correct: B - PTHrP secretion
Hypercalcemia is the most common paraneoplastic syndrome in RCC, caused by tumor secretion of PTHrP (parathyroid hormone-related peptide). This is humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy, not bone metastases.
