📖 Teaching Point

Pulmonary sequestration = non-functioning lung tissue that has lost its connection to the normal bronchial tree and receives systemic arterial blood supply. It's an accessory lung bud that separates from the foregut during embryonic development. Two types: INTRALOBAR (75%) — within the visceral pleura, presents with recurrent infection in older children/adults; EXTRALOBAR (25%) — has its own pleural envelope, presents in infancy, associated with other anomalies.

📖 Teaching Point

Lung development: Week 4 — lung bud appears from ventral foregut (endoderm). Weeks 5-17 — pseudoglandular stage (branching morphogenesis). Weeks 16-26 — canalicular stage (vascularization). Weeks 24-38 — saccular stage. Week 36-8 years — alveolar stage. The pulmonary arteries develop alongside the bronchial tree during branching morphogenesis. An accessory bud that separates early misses this co-development and retains embryonic systemic supply.

Key differences: INTRALOBAR (75%): shared pleura, pulmonary venous drainage, presents age 4-40 with recurrent LLL pneumonia, usually isolated, LEFT lower lobe 60%. EXTRALOBAR (25%): own pleura, systemic venous drainage (azygos/IVC), presents in infancy (often incidental on prenatal US), 50-65% associated anomalies, 90% LEFT sided, 10-15% subdiaphragmatic.

📖 Teaching Point

Surgical pearls: (1) Always get CTA preoperatively to identify ALL feeding arteries (some have 2-3); (2) Ligate the systemic artery FIRST before any parenchymal dissection; (3) For ILS: lobectomy (since it shares pleura with normal lung, segmentectomy is difficult); (4) For ELS: simple excision (it has its own pleural envelope). Increasingly, VATS (video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery) is used for both types.

📖 Teaching Point

CPAM types: Type 1 (65%) = large cysts (>2cm), best prognosis; Type 2 (15-20%) = small cysts (<2cm), associated with other anomalies; Type 3 (5-10%) = microcystic/solid, worst prognosis. Prenatal complications: hydrops fetalis from mediastinal shift. Most CPAMs that don't cause hydrops can be observed prenatally — many regress or shrink. Postnatal resection is recommended even for asymptomatic CPAMs due to small (~2-4%) malignancy risk (pleuropulmonary blastoma).

Stage 6 of 6
🎓 Integration & Key Takeaways
The sequestration patient recovers uneventfully from lobectomy. No further pneumonias occur. The fetal CPAM is monitored and remains stable; postnatal resection is planned at 6 months.

Key Takeaways

✓Sequestration = accessory lung bud that separates from foregut early, retains aortic supply
✓Intralobar (75%): shared pleura, pulmonary venous drainage, recurrent LLL pneumonia in older kids
✓Extralobar (25%): own pleura, systemic venous drainage, 50-65% associated anomalies (especially CDH)
✓Systemic arterial supply from aorta — identify and ligate FIRST at surgery
✓CPAM = hamartomatous bronchiolar overgrowth, pulmonary artery supply, communicates with airways
✓CPAM Types 1-3: large cyst (best prognosis) → microcystic (worst prognosis)
✓Both conditions: LEFT lower lobe predominance
