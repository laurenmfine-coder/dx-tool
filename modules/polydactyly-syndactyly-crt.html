📖 Teaching Point

Polydactyly classification: PREAXIAL = thumb/great toe side (radial/tibial). Associated with SHH regulatory mutations. More likely syndromic (Holt-Oram, Fanconi anemia). POSTAXIAL = pinky/5th toe side (ulnar/fibular). Type A (well-formed digit with bone) vs Type B (rudimentary pedunculated nubbin). Most common form overall. CENTRAL = middle digits (rare, often syndromic). Embryology: digits form during weeks 6-8 through condensation of mesenchyme in the hand/foot plate, with interdigital apoptosis separating them.

📖 Teaching Point

Digit formation molecular players: (1) SHH from ZPA → specifies digit identity (which digit forms where); (2) FGFs from AER → maintain proliferation of progress zone, keep digits growing; (3) BMPs in interdigital zones → trigger apoptosis to free the digits; (4) Noggin/Gremlin → BMP antagonists that PROTECT digit mesenchyme from apoptosis. If BMPs are blocked → syndactyly. If BMPs are overexpressed → excessive apoptosis → brachydactyly/missing digits.

📖 Teaching Point

Syndactyly classification: Simple (cutaneous) vs Complex (osseous). Complete (to fingertip) vs Incomplete (partial). Syndromic associations for COMPLEX syndactyly: APERT SYNDROME (mitten hand — complex syndactyly of digits 2-5 + craniosynostosis, FGFR2 mutation); POLAND SYNDROME (unilateral syndactyly + absent pectoralis major + ipsilateral breast hypoplasia); Carpenter syndrome; Pfeiffer syndrome. Simple incomplete toe syndactyly (2nd-3rd toes) is almost always isolated and benign.

📖 Teaching Point

Preaxial polydactyly red flags requiring syndromic workup: (1) Bilateral involvement; (2) Associated cardiac defect → Holt-Oram (TBX5); (3) Associated radial aplasia → Fanconi anemia (FA DNA repair genes), thrombocytopenia-absent radius (TAR); (4) Short limbs + rib anomalies → short-rib polydactyly syndromes; (5) Cleft lip + polydactyly → Trisomy 13 (Patau). Always: echo + blood count + chromosomes/genetics for preaxial polydactyly.

📖 Teaching Point

Surgical timing: Postaxial Type B (pedunculated) — suture ligation in nursery (simple, safe, no anesthesia needed). Postaxial Type A (well-formed) — formal excision at 6-12 months. Preaxial (thumb) — reconstruction at 9-15 months (before pincer grasp development). Syndactyly release — 12-18 months for fingers (before abnormal growth from tethering); border digits (thumb or 5th finger) released earlier to prevent angular deformity.

Stage 6 of 6
🎓 Integration & Key Takeaways
All three babies do well. The postaxial nubbin sites heal cleanly. The reconstructed thumb has excellent function. The syndactyly toes require no intervention.

Key Takeaways

✓Digits form weeks 6-8: digital ray condensation → interdigital apoptosis (BMP-driven)
✓Polydactyly: preaxial (thumb side, SHH excess) vs postaxial (pinky side, most common)
✓Syndactyly: failure of interdigital apoptosis; simple (skin) vs complex (bone)
✓Postaxial Type B (nubbin): suture ligation in nursery — benign, often familial
✓Preaxial polydactyly: always evaluate for syndromes (Holt-Oram, Fanconi, T13)
✓Apert syndrome: complex syndactyly + craniosynostosis (FGFR2 mutation)
✓Molecular: SHH (digit identity), BMPs (apoptosis), FGFs (growth), Noggin (BMP inhibitor)
