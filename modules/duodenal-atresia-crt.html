📖 Teaching Point

Duodenal atresia occurs from failure of recanalization of the duodenal lumen. During weeks 5-6, the duodenal epithelium proliferates and occludes the lumen (solid cord stage). By weeks 8-10, vacuolization restores the lumen. Failure of recanalization → atresia. The obstruction is most commonly at or distal to the ampulla of Vater (hence bilious vomiting). 30% of duodenal atresia is associated with Down syndrome.

📖 Teaching Point

Atresia mechanisms: DUODENAL — failure to recanalize (intrinsic, weeks 8-10); JEJUNAL/ILEAL — mesenteric vascular accident (extrinsic, can occur anytime). This explains why duodenal atresia has associated anomalies (T21, cardiac, annular pancreas) while jejunal/ileal atresias are typically isolated. Apple peel atresia = jejunal atresia with the distal bowel spiraling around a single mesenteric vessel.

📖 Teaching Point

Pancreatic embryology: TWO buds — ventral (from hepatic diverticulum, becomes uncinate process + main duct) and dorsal (from duodenum, becomes body/tail + accessory duct). The ventral bud rotates clockwise behind the duodenum to fuse with the dorsal bud. Failure → annular pancreas. The main pancreatic duct (Wirsung) is formed by the distal dorsal duct + entire ventral duct. Accessory duct (Santorini) = proximal dorsal duct.

📖 Teaching Point

Duodenal atresia repair: The standard is duodenoduodenostomy (Kimura diamond anastomosis). Gastrojejunostomy is an alternative but less preferred. Key surgical pearl: NEVER divide annular pancreas. Post-op recovery is often prolonged — the proximal dilated duodenum has poor motility and may take 1-2 weeks to function. A transanastomotic feeding tube allows early enteral nutrition.

📖 Teaching Point

Duodenal obstruction spectrum: Complete atresia (no distal gas on X-ray) vs stenosis (some distal gas). Type 1 (web/membrane) is most common and can cause either complete atresia or partial stenosis depending on whether the web has a perforation. Important: the double bubble with SOME distal gas still needs surgical evaluation — it may represent stenosis, malrotation, or a perforated web.

The baby recovers well. Full feeds are established by day 10. At 6 months, she's growing on her T21 growth curve and feeding normally.

Key Takeaways

✓Duodenal atresia = failure of recanalization (solid cord stage, weeks 8-10)
✓Double bubble sign on X-ray is pathognomonic
✓30% associated with Down syndrome (Trisomy 21)
✓Duodenal atresia is INTRINSIC; jejunal/ileal atresia is VASCULAR (extrinsic)
✓Annular pancreas = failed ventral bud rotation — NEVER divide it
✓Repair: duodenoduodenostomy (diamond/Kimura anastomosis)
✓Type 1 (web) can create 'windsock' deformity — may present as stenosis
