Antipsychotic Pharmacology

First- and second-generation antipsychotics: mechanisms, metabolic effects, EPS, NMS, and clozapine monitoring.

📚 2 Sections ❓ 4 MCQs ⏱ ~30 min

🧪 Antipsychotic Classes

First-Generation (Typical) Antipsychotics

  • Mechanism: D2 receptor blockade (primarily mesolimbic pathway for efficacy; nigrostriatal blockade causes EPS)
  • High-potency: Haloperidol, fluphenazine — MORE EPS, LESS sedation/anticholinergic effects
  • Low-potency: Chlorpromazine, thioridazine — LESS EPS, MORE sedation/anticholinergic/orthostatic hypotension
  • Primary use: Acute psychosis, agitation, Tourette syndrome, intractable hiccups (chlorpromazine)

💎 The Dopamine Pathways

Mesolimbic: blockade → treats positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions). Mesocortical: blockade → may worsen negative symptoms. Nigrostriatal: blockade → EPS. Tuberoinfundibular: blockade → hyperprolactinemia (galactorrhea, amenorrhea).

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)

  • Cause: Idiosyncratic reaction to antipsychotics (or abrupt withdrawal of dopaminergic drugs in Parkinson's)
  • Classic tetrad: Fever + Lead-pipe rigidity + Autonomic instability + Mental status changes (mnemonic: FARM)
  • Labs: Massively elevated CK (>1000), leukocytosis, metabolic acidosis, elevated LFTs
  • Treatment: STOP antipsychotic, aggressive cooling, IV fluids, dantrolene (muscle relaxant) ± bromocriptine (dopamine agonist)
  • Key distinction from serotonin syndrome: NMS = RIGIDITY (lead-pipe); SS = CLONUS (hyperreflexia). NMS develops over days; SS develops over hours.

⚠️ Clozapine = Powerful but Dangerous

Reserved for treatment-resistant schizophrenia (failed 2+ other antipsychotics). Requires REMS program with mandatory CBC monitoring: weekly for 6 months → biweekly for 6 months → monthly thereafter. ANC <1000 = hold drug. ANC <500 = discontinue permanently. Also monitor for: myocarditis (first month), seizures (dose-related), metabolic syndrome, sialorrhea.

Metabolic Syndrome Risk

  • Highest risk: Clozapine, olanzapine (worst weight gain, hyperglycemia, dyslipidemia)
  • Moderate risk: Quetiapine, risperidone
  • Lowest risk: Aripiprazole, ziprasidone, lurasidone (consider for patients with metabolic concerns)
  • Monitoring: Baseline and periodic fasting glucose, lipids, weight, waist circumference, BP

A patient on haloperidol develops fever (40°C), lead-pipe rigidity, altered mental status, and CK of 15,000. What is the diagnosis and what distinguishes it from serotonin syndrome?

Serotonin syndrome — distinguished by rigidity
NMS — distinguished by rigidity (vs. clonus in SS)
Malignant hyperthermia — triggered by anesthetics
