❤️ Chest Pain: 5 Diagnoses You Must Know

Why Chest Pain Matters

Chest pain is the second most common reason for emergency department visits in the United States. It accounts for 8 million ED visits per year. And here's the terrifying part: the differential ranges from "completely benign" to "dead in minutes."

A physician evaluating chest pain must rapidly distinguish between five critical diagnoses that demand different — and sometimes opposite — treatments. Give the wrong treatment for the wrong diagnosis, and you can kill the patient.

The teaching goal: By the end of this module, you'll understand how physicians think through chest pain — not as a list of diseases to memorize, but as a reasoning framework you can apply to any chief complaint.

The Big Five: Chest Pain Emergencies

When a patient says "my chest hurts," an emergency physician immediately considers five diagnoses — not because they're the most common, but because they're the most deadly if missed:

1. Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) — Heart attack. A coronary artery is blocked, and heart muscle is dying. Minutes matter. Treatment: open the artery (catheterization) or dissolve the clot.
2. Pulmonary Embolism (PE) — Blood clot in the lung. A clot from the leg (DVT) travels to the pulmonary arteries and blocks blood flow. Treatment: blood thinners or clot retrieval.
3. Aortic Dissection — The aorta tears. The inner lining of the body's largest artery rips, and blood flows into the wall. Treatment: emergency surgery (Type A) or blood pressure control (Type B).
4. Tension Pneumothorax — Collapsed lung under pressure. Air leaks into the chest cavity and compresses the heart and opposite lung. Treatment: needle decompression — stick a needle in the chest.
5. Cardiac Tamponade — Fluid compresses the heart. Blood or fluid fills the pericardial sac and prevents the heart from filling. Treatment: pericardiocentesis — drain the fluid.
Clinical Pearl: Notice that treatments are OPPOSITE for some of these. ACS gets blood thinners; aortic dissection is KILLED by blood thinners. PE gets anticoagulation; tamponade needs a needle. The wrong diagnosis → the wrong treatment → a dead patient. This is why the differential matters.

Your Prereqs Made Clinical

Every one of these diagnoses connects directly to something you're learning in your prerequisite classes right now. This is where medicine stops being abstract:

Biology: Coagulation Cascade → ACS & PE

Remember learning about clotting factors, fibrin cross-linking, and platelet aggregation? That's exactly what's happening inside a coronary artery during a heart attack. The atherosclerotic plaque ruptures → platelets aggregate → fibrin stabilizes the clot → the artery occludes → myocardium distal to the blockage starts dying. Every drug used in ACS targets a specific step in this cascade: aspirin (platelet aggregation), heparin (thrombin), tPA (plasminogen → plasmin → fibrin lysis).

Physics: Boyle's Law → Tension Pneumothorax

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂. When air enters the pleural space, the lung volume decreases and intrapleural pressure increases. In tension pneumothorax, a one-way valve forms — air enters on inspiration but can't escape on expiration. Pressure builds progressively, compressing the heart and great vessels. The treatment (needle decompression) is literally applying Boyle's law: create an opening to equalize pressure.

Anatomy: The Pericardial Sac → Tamponade

The pericardium is a tough, fibrous sac with minimal elasticity. When fluid accumulates slowly (pericardial effusion), the sac can stretch to hold 1-2 liters. When fluid accumulates rapidly (trauma, aortic rupture), as little as 100-200 mL can cause tamponade because the sac can't stretch fast enough. This is why the RATE of accumulation matters more than the VOLUME.

Chemistry: Troponin Biochemistry → Diagnosing ACS

Troponin is a structural protein in the cardiac sarcomere (troponin I and T regulate calcium-mediated actin-myosin interaction). When myocardial cells die, their membranes rupture and intracellular troponin leaks into the bloodstream. This is why troponin is both highly sensitive and highly specific for cardiac injury — it's only found in cardiac muscle cells.

Study tip: When you're in biology learning about the coagulation cascade, mentally add this note: "This is what goes wrong in a heart attack, and these are the drugs that target each step." That mental link will serve you in MCAT, medical school, and clinical practice.

Apply It: Quick Cases

Read each case and think about which of the Big Five it represents before revealing the answer.

Case A

A 62-year-old male smoker with diabetes presents with substernal pressure "like someone sitting on my chest" radiating to his left jaw. He is diaphoretic and nauseated. EKG shows ST elevations in leads V1-V4.
A.Pulmonary embolism with acute right heart strain and ST changes
B.Cardiac tamponade from malignant pericardial effusion
C.Type A aortic dissection with coronary artery malperfusion
D.Anterior wall STEMI from left anterior descending artery occlusion
E.Esophageal spasm mimicking acute coronary syndrome presentation
✅ Anterior STEMI. Classic presentation: pressure-type chest pain, radiation to jaw, diaphoresis, ST elevation in V1-V4 (anterior leads = LAD territory). Multiple risk factors: age, male, smoker, diabetic. Treatment: emergent catheterization. Time is myocardium — door-to-balloon goal under 90 minutes.

Case B

A 28-year-old woman on oral contraceptives presents with sudden-onset sharp right-sided chest pain that worsens when she breathes in. She flew from London 3 days ago. HR 118, O2 sat 92%, chest X-ray is normal.
A.Right-sided spontaneous pneumothorax with pleuritic chest pain
B.Acute pericarditis with positional pleuritic pain and tachycardia
C.Musculoskeletal chest wall strain from prolonged travel positioning
D.Community-acquired pneumonia presenting before radiographic infiltrate
E.Pulmonary embolism with risk factors, pleuritic pain, and hypoxia
✅ Pulmonary embolism. Risk factors: OCPs + long-haul flight (Virchow's triad: hypercoagulable + stasis). Pleuritic pain, tachycardia, hypoxia, AND a normal chest X-ray — classic PE presentation. Normal CXR is actually a clue: most causes of hypoxia show SOMETHING on X-ray. PE often shows nothing. Next step: CTA chest.

Case C

A 72-year-old male with longstanding hypertension presents with sudden severe "tearing" pain in his chest radiating to his back. BP in the right arm is 178/96 and in the left arm is 140/82. He appears anxious and diaphoretic.
A.Acute coronary syndrome with atypical pain radiation to the back
B.Tension pneumothorax causing hemodynamic asymmetry between arms
C.Aortic dissection with classic tearing pain and blood pressure differential
D.Hypertensive emergency with end-organ damage to the thoracic aorta
E.Esophageal perforation from forceful emesis causing mediastinal pain
✅ Aortic dissection. The classic triad: (1) tearing/ripping pain to the back, (2) BP differential >20 mmHg between arms, (3) history of uncontrolled hypertension. CRITICAL: do NOT give anticoagulants — if you misdiagnose this as ACS and give heparin/tPA, you can cause fatal hemorrhage. The first question a physician should always ask with chest pain: "Could this be a dissection?" before giving blood thinners.

Integrative Question 1: The Reasoning Framework

A 50-year-old presents to the ED with acute chest pain. Before any test results are available, what is the FIRST reasoning step the emergency physician should take?
A.Determine which of the five deadly diagnoses to rule out first
B.Order a comprehensive metabolic panel and complete blood count
C.Consult cardiology for an emergent cardiac catheterization workup
D.Administer aspirin and nitroglycerin per standard chest pain protocol
E.Obtain a detailed past medical and family history before proceeding
✅ Rule out deadly diagnoses first. The first step is always: "What can kill this patient?" before "What's most likely?" This is the core of emergency medicine reasoning. You can't give aspirin (ACS treatment) until you've considered whether it could be a dissection (where blood thinners are harmful). The history and exam guide which of the Big Five to pursue.

Integrative Question 2: The Prereq Connection

In cardiac tamponade, as little as 100 mL of rapidly accumulating fluid can be fatal, while chronic effusions may accumulate over 1 liter before causing symptoms. Which scientific principle best explains this difference?
A.Starling's law relating ventricular preload to stroke volume output
B.Laplace's law relating wall tension to radius and transmural pressure
C.Stress-strain relationship of the pericardium as a viscoelastic material
D.Ohm's law applied to vascular resistance and cardiac output relationship
E.Poiseuille's law relating flow rate to vessel radius and fluid viscosity
✅ Stress-strain (viscoelastic) properties. The pericardium is a viscoelastic tissue — it can stretch gradually over time (stress relaxation) but resists rapid stretching. Slow accumulation allows tissue remodeling; rapid accumulation exceeds the elastic limit. This is the same physics concept from your materials science unit: rate of loading determines whether a material deforms plastically (accommodates) or fails acutely (tamponade).
