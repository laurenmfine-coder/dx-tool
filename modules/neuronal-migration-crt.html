📖 Teaching Point

Normal cortical development (weeks 8-16): 1) Proliferation in ventricular zone. 2) Migration along radial glial fibers. 3) Inside-out layering — later neurons migrate PAST earlier ones to form superficial layers. Result: 6-layered neocortex with complex gyration. Lissencephaly: Type I (classical) = 4 thick disorganized layers, smooth surface. LIS1 gene (chromosome 17p13.3) → posterior predominant. DCX gene (X-linked) → anterior predominant in males, band heterotopia in females.

📖 Teaching Point

Migration disorder spectrum (mild to severe): Periventricular nodular heterotopia (neurons stuck at ventricular surface, form nodules — often FLNA gene, X-linked dominant in females). Band heterotopia (neurons form a subcortical band — DCX in females). Lissencephaly (complete migration failure — LIS1 or DCX in males). This spectrum illustrates that the DEGREE of migration failure determines the phenotype. Periventricular heterotopia is the mildest (normal intelligence possible, seizures); lissencephaly is the most severe.

📖 Teaching Point

Cortical development timeline: Proliferation (weeks 8-16) → Migration (weeks 12-24) → Organization/lamination (weeks 24-postnatal). Disorders at each stage: Proliferation defects: microcephaly (too few neurons), megalencephaly (too many). Migration defects: lissencephaly, heterotopia. Organization defects: polymicrogyria, focal cortical dysplasia. Polymicrogyria types: bilateral perisylvian (most common — pseudobulbar palsy), bilateral frontal, bilateral generalized. Focal cortical dysplasia (FCD) is the most common cause of medication-refractory focal epilepsy requiring surgery.

📖 Teaching Point

Schizencephaly vs porencephaly: Schizencephaly = full-thickness cleft lined by GRAY MATTER (developmental). Porencephaly = CSF-filled cavity lined by WHITE MATTER/gliosis (destructive — result of ischemia, infection, or hemorrhage). This distinction is critical because schizencephaly is a malformation (migration/organization defect) while porencephaly is acquired destruction of previously normal brain. Schizencephaly is associated with EMX2 transcription factor mutations and septo-optic dysplasia.

📖 Teaching Point

Migration disorder clinical pearls: 1) Lissencephaly → severe ID, refractory epilepsy, poor prognosis (most die in childhood). 2) Heterotopia → seizures, variable cognition (periventricular nodular = often normal IQ). 3) PMG → seizures, variable deficits based on location (perisylvian = pseudobulbar). 4) FCD → focal seizures, surgically treatable if localized. 5) Schizencephaly → seizures, motor deficits (open-lip worse). The clinical severity correlates with the extent and timing of the developmental disruption.
