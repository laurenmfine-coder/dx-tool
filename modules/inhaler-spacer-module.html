📊 Evidence: Spacers Improve Outcomes

  • Lung deposition increases 3-5x with proper spacer use (Roller et al.)
  • Oropharyngeal deposition decreases by 90% → less thrush, less dysphonia with ICS
  • Coordination becomes less critical → spacer "holds" the aerosol cloud
  • Particle size optimized: Large particles (>5μm) deposit in spacer walls instead of throat
  • In acute asthma: MDI + spacer is as effective as nebulizer (Cochrane review)
Key Message: ALL patients using an MDI for inhaled corticosteroids should use a spacer. This reduces local side effects (thrush, hoarseness) and improves drug delivery to the lungs. Spacers are not just for children or those with poor coordination.

2. MDI + Spacer Technique (Recommended)

1

Remove cap and shake well

Shake the MDI vigorously for 5 seconds to mix the medication and propellant evenly.

💡 If not used for >2 weeks, prime by spraying 2-4 puffs into the air
2

Attach MDI to spacer

Insert the MDI mouthpiece firmly into the spacer's rubber opening. Ensure a tight seal.

3

Breathe out completely

Exhale fully away from the spacer to empty your lungs before inhaling the medication.

💡 Don't breathe into the spacer—this can disperse the medication
4

Seal lips around mouthpiece

Place the spacer mouthpiece between your teeth and close your lips tightly around it.

5

Press canister and inhale slowly

Press down on the canister once while breathing in SLOWLY and DEEPLY over 3-5 seconds.

💡 Slow is key! Fast inhalation deposits drug in throat, not lungs
6

Hold breath for 10 seconds

Remove spacer from mouth and hold your breath for up to 10 seconds (or as long as comfortable).

7

Wait and repeat if needed

Wait 30-60 seconds between puffs. Shake MDI again before next puff.

8

Rinse mouth (for ICS)

After inhaled corticosteroids, rinse mouth with water and spit out. This prevents thrush and hoarseness.

💡 Even with a spacer, rinsing further reduces local side effects

3. Dry Powder Inhaler (DPI) Technique

DPIs are breath-actuated—they release medication when you inhale forcefully. No spacer is used with DPIs.

1

Prepare the device

Diskus: Open and slide lever until it clicks
Turbuhaler: Twist base back and forth until click
Ellipta: Open cover fully until click

2

Hold device level (horizontal)

Keep the DPI level while loading. Tilting can spill the powder dose.

⚠️ Never exhale INTO the device—moisture clumps the powder
3

Breathe out AWAY from device

Exhale fully, but turn your head away from the mouthpiece first.

4

Seal lips and inhale FAST and DEEP

Unlike MDIs, DPIs require a QUICK, FORCEFUL inhalation to break up the powder and carry it to the lungs.

💡 Think "sipping a thick milkshake through a straw"
5

Hold breath for 10 seconds

Remove device and hold breath as long as comfortable.

6

Rinse mouth (for ICS)

Rinse and spit after using inhaled corticosteroids.

Clinical Pearl - DPI Limitation: Patients with severe airflow obstruction during an exacerbation may not generate sufficient inspiratory flow for effective DPI delivery. Use MDI + spacer or nebulizer in acute asthma/COPD exacerbations instead.

4. Device Selection Guide

💨 MDI + Spacer

Metered Dose Inhaler
  • ✓ Works at any inspiratory flow
  • ✓ Best for acute exacerbations
  • ✓ Excellent lung deposition with spacer
  • ✗ Requires coordination (less with spacer)
  • ✗ Spacer is bulky to carry

Best for: Children, elderly, acute attacks, ICS (reduced side effects)

🌀 DPI (Diskus, Turbuhaler, Ellipta)

Dry Powder Inhaler
  • ✓ Breath-actuated (no coordination needed)
  • ✓ Portable, no spacer required
  • ✓ Dose counter on most devices
  • ✗ Requires fast, forceful inhalation
  • ✗ Not suitable during severe exacerbations
  • ✗ Humidity can affect powder

Best for: Maintenance therapy, patients with good inspiratory flow

🌫️ Soft Mist Inhaler (Respimat)

Slow-Moving Aerosol
  • ✓ Slow mist, easy to coordinate
  • ✓ High lung deposition (~50%)
  • ✓ No propellant gases
  • ✗ Requires priming
  • ✗ More complex loading

Best for: COPD maintenance (tiotropium, olodaterol)

💧 Nebulizer

Aerosolized Solution
  • ✓ Minimal coordination (tidal breathing)
  • ✓ Can deliver high doses
  • ✓ Good for severe distress
  • ✗ Time-consuming (10-15 min)
  • ✗ Equipment required
  • ✗ NOT superior to MDI+spacer for most patients

Best for: Infants, severe attacks, patients unable to use inhalers

5. Common Technique Errors

❌ Forgetting to shake MDI

Medication and propellant separate when sitting. Unshaken = inconsistent dosing.

✓ Shake for 5 seconds before EVERY puff
❌ Inhaling too fast (MDI)

Fast inhalation deposits most medication in the throat, not lungs.

✓ Breathe in SLOWLY over 3-5 seconds with MDI
❌ Inhaling too slowly (DPI)

DPIs need forceful inhalation to disperse powder properly.

✓ Breathe in FAST and DEEP with DPIs
❌ Actuating before/after inhaling

Poor timing means drug sprays into air or throat instead of lungs.

✓ Use spacer to eliminate timing issues
❌ Not holding breath

Immediate exhalation blows out medication before it can deposit.

✓ Hold breath 10 seconds after inhalation
❌ Exhaling into DPI

Moisture from breath clumps powder and blocks device.

✓ Turn head AWAY before exhaling
❌ Firing multiple puffs at once

Only one puff can be inhaled effectively at a time.

✓ One puff per inhalation; wait 30-60 sec between
❌ Not rinsing after ICS

Medication deposited in mouth causes thrush and hoarseness.

✓ Rinse mouth and SPIT after every ICS dose

6. Patient Teaching Strategies

Demonstrate, Don't Just Tell

  • Show technique with placebo device
  • Have patient demonstrate back ("teach-back" method)
  • Correct errors in real-time
  • Re-assess technique at EVERY visit—skills deteriorate over time

Key Messages for Patients

🗣️ How to Explain to Patients

  • "The spacer is like a holding chamber" — It holds the medicine in a cloud so you have time to breathe it in, and catches the big droplets that would stick in your throat.
  • "Slow and low for the puffer" — MDIs work best with slow, deep breaths.
  • "Fast and hard for the powder" — DPIs need a quick, strong breath to pick up the powder.
  • "Always rinse after the steroid inhaler" — This prevents the white patches (thrush) and scratchy voice.
  • "Your rescue inhaler should last 2-3 months" — Using it more often means your asthma isn't controlled.
Clinical Pearl: Studies show that <90% of patients use inhalers incorrectly, and errors persist unless actively corrected. Check technique at every visit—don't assume they remember. A 2-minute technique check can make the difference between controlled and uncontrolled disease.
