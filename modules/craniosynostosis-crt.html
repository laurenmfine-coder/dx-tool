📖 Teaching Point

Skull embryology: The calvarium (skull vault) forms by intramembranous ossification — mesenchyme directly becomes bone without a cartilage intermediate. Sutures are fibrous joints between calvarial bones that allow brain growth. Sutures normally close in adulthood (20s-30s). Premature closure restricts growth perpendicular to the suture. Virchow's law: the skull compensates by growing parallel to the fused suture. Neural crest cells form frontal bone and part of parietal; mesoderm forms the rest of the parietal and occipital bones.

📖 Teaching Point

Suture-shape correlation (Virchow's law): Sagittal → scaphocephaly (long/narrow, 40-55%). Bilateral coronal → brachycephaly (short/wide, tower skull). Unilateral coronal → anterior plagiocephaly (asymmetric). Metopic → trigonocephaly (triangular forehead, 15-25%). Lambdoid → posterior plagiocephaly (rare, <5%). KEY: The metopic suture is the ONLY suture that normally fuses in infancy (by age 2). All other sutures remain open into adulthood.

📖 Teaching Point

Syndromic craniosynostosis syndromes: Apert (FGFR2) = bilateral coronal + symmetric syndactyly (mitten hands), midface hypoplasia. Crouzon (FGFR2) = bilateral coronal + midface hypoplasia, NO hand anomalies. Pfeiffer (FGFR1/2) = coronal/sagittal + broad thumbs and great toes. Muenke (FGFR3) = uni/bilateral coronal, variable expressivity. Saethre-Chotzen (TWIST1) = unilateral/bilateral coronal + low frontal hairline, ptosis. Most involve FGFR mutations (fibroblast growth factor receptor) — autosomal dominant.

📖 Teaching Point

Surgical options: 1) Endoscopic strip craniectomy: minimally invasive, done at 2-6 months, removes fused suture through small incisions, requires post-op helmet therapy (12-18 months) to mold the growing skull. Less blood loss, shorter hospital stay. 2) Open cranial vault remodeling (CVR): done at 6-12 months, larger incision, bone removed and reshaped, immediate correction. More blood loss but no helmet needed. Timing matters — earlier surgery allows more natural brain-directed growth.

📖 Teaching Point

Positional plagiocephaly vs lambdoid synostosis: Positional is 100x more common. Key differentiators: 1) Ear position (anterior shift in positional, posterior in lambdoid). 2) Head shape from above: parallelogram (positional) vs trapezoid (lambdoid). 3) Palpation: open suture in positional, ridged/absent suture in lambdoid. 4) Mastoid: normal in positional, bulging in lambdoid. Prevention: tummy time when awake, alternating head position during sleep. Treatment of positional: repositioning, physical therapy, helmet if persistent after 4-6 months.

🎓 Integration & Key Takeaways
✓Craniosynostosis = premature fusion of cranial sutures, restricting brain growth
✓Virchow's law: growth restricted perpendicular to fused suture, compensates parallel
✓Sagittal (scaphocephaly) is most common; Metopic is the only suture that normally closes in infancy
✓Syndromic: multi-suture + limb anomalies (Apert = FGFR2 + syndactyly; Crouzon = FGFR2, no limbs)
✓Surgery: endoscopic (<6mo + helmet) or open CVR (6-12mo) — primary goal is allowing brain growth
✓Positional plagiocephaly: ear shifts ANTERIOR, all sutures open, managed conservatively
