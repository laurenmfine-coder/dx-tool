MECHANISM
Definitions & Classification

Status epilepticus (SE): continuous seizure activity ≥5 minutes OR ≥2 seizures without returning to baseline between them. A neurologic emergency — prolonged seizures cause excitotoxic neuronal death, cerebral edema, rhabdomyolysis, hyperthermia, and death.

TypeFeatures
Convulsive SETonic-clonic movements — clinically obvious. Most dangerous
Non-convulsive SE (NCSE)Altered consciousness WITHOUT motor manifestations. Requires EEG to diagnose. Suspect in unexplained AMS, especially post-ictal patients who don't wake up
Refractory SEContinues despite 2 appropriately dosed medications. Requires continuous IV anesthetics
Super-refractory SEContinues ≥24h of anesthetic treatment or recurs during weaning
🔑 Most common cause of SE in adults: Anti-epileptic drug (AED) non-compliance/subtherapeutic levels. Other causes: CNS infection, stroke, metabolic (hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, hypocalcemia), alcohol withdrawal, drug toxicity, TBI, brain tumor.
CLINICAL
Evaluation

Immediate Assessment

ABCs → glucose (hypoglycemia causes seizures!) → IV access → labs (BMP, CBC, AED levels, toxicology, lactate) → thiamine before glucose if suspected alcohol use.

⚠️ Complications of Prolonged SE:
• Excitotoxic neuronal death (glutamate-mediated, NMDA receptor overactivation)
• Rhabdomyolysis → AKI (from tonic-clonic activity)
• Aspiration pneumonia
• Hyperthermia → multi-organ failure
• Lactic acidosis
• Cerebral edema → herniation
• Mortality: 20% for convulsive SE, higher for refractory

When to Get Imaging

CT head for: new-onset seizure, focal features, trauma, fever (abscess?), cancer history (metastases), persistent altered mental status. MRI for: first unprovoked seizure workup, suspected encephalitis, tumor evaluation.

TREATMENT
Stepwise Protocol
StageTimeTreatment
1st line0-5 minBenzodiazepines: IV lorazepam 4 mg (can repeat × 1) OR IV diazepam 10 mg OR IM midazolam 10 mg (if no IV access — RAMPART trial: IM midazolam non-inferior to IV lorazepam)
2nd line5-20 minIV AED loading: fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg (preferred — less hypotension/tissue necrosis than phenytoin), OR levetiracetam 60 mg/kg, OR valproate 40 mg/kg
3rd line (Refractory)>20 minContinuous IV anesthetics: midazolam, propofol, or pentobarbital infusion. Requires intubation + continuous EEG monitoring. Goal: burst suppression on EEG
🔑 Key Points:
• Lorazepam is preferred over diazepam IV (longer duration of action in brain)
• IM midazolam if no IV access (RAMPART trial — faster to administer, non-inferior outcomes)
• Fosphenytoin: monitor for hypotension and cardiac arrhythmias (Purple Glove Syndrome with phenytoin extravasation — fosphenytoin avoids this)
• Levetiracetam (Keppra): gaining favor as 2nd-line — fewer drug interactions, no cardiac toxicity, safe in hepatic disease
• ESETT trial: fosphenytoin = levetiracetam = valproate for 2nd-line (all ~47% effective)
