CLINICAL
Diagnosis & Differentiation
🔑 Key Distinction: Painless = Previa. Painful = Abruption. This is the single most important clinical pearl for antepartum hemorrhage.
⚠️ NEVER perform digital cervical exam with suspected placenta previa → can cause torrential hemorrhage. Always confirm placental location by ultrasound first. Transvaginal US is safe and more accurate than transabdominal for previa.

Abruption Severity

GradeFeatures
Mild (I)Small separation, no fetal distress, stable mother
Moderate (II)Fetal distress, uterine tenderness, mother stable
Severe (III)Fetal demise, DIC, maternal shock. Board-form uterus (rigid, woody)

Placenta Accreta Spectrum

Accreta (attached to myometrium, 75%), Increta (invades into myometrium, 18%), Percreta (penetrates through myometrium to serosa/adjacent organs, 7%). Risk: prior C-section + placenta previa. Planned cesarean hysterectomy at 34-36 weeks at experienced center.

TREATMENT
Management
ConditionManagement
Placenta Previa<37 wks + stable: pelvic rest, monitor. Cesarean delivery at 36-37 wks (planned). Earlier if heavy bleeding. No vaginal delivery if previa overlaps os
AbruptionMild + preterm: expectant management with close monitoring. Moderate-severe: emergent delivery (vaginal if imminent, C-section if not). Correct coagulopathy (DIC). Fetal death → vaginal delivery preferred (unless contraindicated)
Vasa PreviaIf diagnosed prenatally: planned C-section at 34-36 wks. If rupture → emergent C-section (minutes count — fetal exsanguination). Apt test: confirms fetal blood if bleeding at ROM
🔑 Apt Test: Differentiates fetal from maternal blood. Mix blood with NaOH: fetal hemoglobin (HbF) resists denaturation → stays PINK. Adult hemoglobin denatures → turns BROWN. Fetal blood at ROM = vasa previa.
