MECHANISM
Definition & Categories

Classic FUO is defined as: temperature >38.3\u00b0C (101\u00b0F) on several occasions, duration >3 weeks, and no diagnosis after 1 week of inpatient evaluation (or 3 outpatient visits). The differential is organized into four major categories.

The Four Categories of FUO

CategoryFrequencyKey Examples
Infection~25-30%Endocarditis, TB, abscess (intra-abdominal, dental), osteomyelitis, CMV/EBV, HIV
Malignancy~20-25%Lymphoma (especially NHL), leukemia, renal cell carcinoma, hepatocellular carcinoma, atrial myxoma
Autoimmune/Inflammatory~15-20%Adult-onset Still disease, SLE, giant cell arteritis, polyarteritis nodosa, sarcoidosis
Undiagnosed~20-25%Often resolve spontaneously; generally good prognosis
\U0001f511 Age-based clues: Young adults: infection, autoimmune. Elderly (>65): giant cell arteritis, lymphoma, abscess, TB. The distribution has shifted over decades\u2014infections are less common as the cause of FUO now due to improved diagnostics, while undiagnosed cases have increased.

Drug Fever

Often overlooked cause of FUO. Can occur days to weeks after starting a medication. Classic offenders: antibiotics (beta-lactams, sulfonamides), anticonvulsants (phenytoin), allopurinol, heparin. Patient may appear "relatively well" despite fever. Diagnosis: fever resolves within 48-72 hours of drug discontinuation. Eosinophilia may be present but is not required.

Systematic Workup

Tiered Evaluation Approach

TierStudiesTargets
Tier 1 (Initial)CBC with diff, CMP, LFTs, ESR/CRP, blood cultures (x3), UA/UCx, CXR, HIV, ANA, RFCommon infections, renal/hepatic causes, autoimmune
Tier 2 (Directed)CT chest/abdomen/pelvis, echocardiography, PPD/IGRA, peripheral smear, ferritin, LDH, procalcitoninAbscess, endocarditis, TB, lymphoma, Still disease
Tier 3 (Advanced)FDG-PET/CT, temporal artery biopsy, bone marrow biopsy, liver biopsyOccult malignancy, vasculitis, granulomatous disease
\U0001f511 FDG-PET/CT: Increasingly used early in FUO workup. Can identify occult infections, vasculitis (large-vessel), and malignancies that CT misses. Studies show it changes management in 40-60% of FUO cases when conventional workup is negative.

TREATMENT
Management Principles

General Approach

The cornerstone of FUO management is systematic diagnosis before empiric treatment. Empiric antibiotics should be avoided unless the patient is critically ill or neutropenic, as they obscure diagnostic clues.

\U0001f511 Naproxen test: In cases where malignancy-related fever is suspected, the "Naproxen test" can help. Neoplastic fevers often respond dramatically to naproxen (defervescence within 24-48h), while infectious fevers generally do not. Not definitive but can guide workup direction.

Empiric Therapy Indications

ScenarioEmpiric Treatment
Suspected TB (granulomas, endemic exposure)Anti-TB therapy trial
Suspected GCA (elderly + headache + elevated ESR)High-dose corticosteroids (do not delay for biopsy)
Neutropenic feverBroad-spectrum antibiotics (piperacillin-tazobactam or cefepime)
Culture-negative endocarditisEmpiric antibiotics per guidelines
