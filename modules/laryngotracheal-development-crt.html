📖 Teaching Point

Laryngeal embryology: The larynx develops from pharyngeal arches 4 and 6. Arch 4 → thyroid cartilage, superior laryngeal nerve (internal branch = sensory above cords). Arch 6 → cricoid, arytenoid, corniculate, cuneiform cartilages, recurrent laryngeal nerve (motor to all intrinsic muscles except cricothyroid, sensory below cords). The epiglottis develops from hypobranchial eminence (arches 3-4). Laryngeal lumen is temporarily occluded by epithelial proliferation at week 10, then recanalized — failure causes laryngeal atresia or web.

📖 Teaching Point

Recurrent laryngeal nerve embryology: The RLN is originally a branch of the vagus to the 6th pharyngeal arch. As the heart descends, the nerve gets 'caught' under the vascular derivatives of the 6th arch. Left: 6th arch becomes ductus arteriosus → nerve loops under aortic arch/ligamentum arteriosum. Right: 6th arch regresses → nerve loops under the right subclavian (4th arch derivative). Non-recurrent right laryngeal nerve (0.5%) occurs with aberrant right subclavian artery (arteria lusoria) — the nerve goes directly to the larynx without looping.

📖 Teaching Point

Subglottic stenosis: The cricoid ring (6th arch) is the only complete cartilage ring in the airway — it defines the narrowest point of the pediatric airway (adults' narrowest point is the glottis). Grading (Cotton-Myer): Grade I (<50% obstruction), II (51-70%), III (71-99%), IV (complete). Management: mild → observation/dilation. Moderate → balloon dilation, topical mitomycin C. Severe → laryngotracheal reconstruction (LTR) with cartilage grafting (rib cartilage). ET tube sizing matters: use leak test, cuffed tubes with low cuff pressure.

📖 Teaching Point

Tracheal embryology: The trachea develops from the ventral foregut (separated from esophagus by tracheoesophageal septum). It's lined by endoderm (respiratory epithelium) with mesoderm-derived cartilage, smooth muscle, and connective tissue. Normal: 16-20 C-shaped cartilage rings. Complete tracheal rings: developmental error where the posterior membranous wall fails to form → rigid fixed-diameter airway. Strong association with pulmonary artery sling. Ring-sling complex: complete rings + PA sling = classic surgical entity requiring both airway and vascular repair.

📖 Teaching Point

Pharyngeal arch derivatives — airway focus: Arch 1: muscles of mastication (V3). Arch 2: muscles of facial expression (VII). Arch 3: stylopharyngeus (IX). Arch 4: cricothyroid + pharyngeal constrictors + levator veli palatini (SLN/pharyngeal branch of X). Arch 6: all OTHER intrinsic laryngeal muscles (RLN). Clinical pearl: Bilateral RLN injury → both cords paralyzed in median position → stridor/airway obstruction (may need tracheostomy). Unilateral RLN → hoarseness, breathy voice, aspiration risk.

🎓 Integration & Key Takeaways
✓Laryngomalacia = most common neonatal stridor (60-70%), immature arch 4/6 cartilage, resolves by 18-24 months
✓Left RLN loops under aortic arch → longer course → more vulnerable to injury
✓Cricoid = only complete cartilage ring → narrowest point of pediatric airway → stenosis site
✓Cricothyroid is the ONLY intrinsic laryngeal muscle innervated by SLN (not RLN) — arch 4 muscle
✓Complete tracheal rings: O-shaped, associated with PA sling, treated with slide tracheoplasty
✓Lung development stages: Embryonic → Pseudoglandular → Canalicular → Saccular → Alveolar
