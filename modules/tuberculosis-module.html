MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

Mycobacterium tuberculosis is an acid-fast, obligate aerobe (Ziehl-Neelsen stain, auramine-rhodamine). Transmitted by aerosol droplets. Preferentially infects the upper lobes (highest pO2 = optimal for this aerobe).

Primary TB vs Reactivation

Primary TBLatent TB (LTBI)Reactivation (Active) TB
Initial infection. Ghon focus (lower/middle lobe) + hilar lymphadenopathy = Ghon complex. Ranke complex = calcified Ghon complex.Immune containment in granulomas. PPD/IGRA positive. CXR normal. NOT infectious. 5-10% lifetime risk of reactivation.Active disease, usually UPPER LOBES (apical). Cavitary lesions. Cough >2-3 weeks, hemoptysis, night sweats, weight loss. INFECTIOUS.
🔑 Granuloma formation: Macrophages ingest TB → present to CD4 T cells → Th1 response → IFN-γ activates macrophages → epithelioid cells + Langhans giant cells → caseating granuloma (caseous necrosis is characteristic of TB). This immune response is what PPD/IGRA detect.
CLINICAL
Diagnosis

Screening Tests (detect IMMUNE RESPONSE, not active disease)

Intradermal injection, read at 48-72hBlood test — measures IFN-γ release by T cells
Positive cutoffs: ≥5mm (HIV, close contacts, immunosuppressed, CXR fibrotic); ≥10mm (immigrants, IVDU, healthcare workers); ≥15mm (no risk factors)Single cutoff, more specific
False positive: BCG vaccine, NTMNOT affected by BCG — preferred in BCG-vaccinated patients

Active TB Diagnosis

Gold standard: Sputum culture on Löwenstein-Jensen medium (takes 4-8 weeks). Rapid: Sputum AFB smear (3 samples), GeneXpert MTB/RIF (PCR, detects TB + rifampin resistance in 2 hours — WHO recommended). CXR: upper lobe cavitation, infiltrates, fibrosis.

⚠️ Airborne precautions (negative pressure room + N95 respirator) for any suspected active pulmonary TB. Continue until 3 negative sputum AFB smears collected on separate days.
TREATMENT
Treatment

Latent TB (LTBI)

Preferred regimens: 3HP (isoniazid + rifapentine weekly × 12 weeks — directly observed), 4R (rifampin daily × 4 months), or 9H (isoniazid daily × 9 months — oldest regimen, most side effects).

Active TB: RIPE Therapy

🔑 RIPE × 2 months → RI × 4 months (total 6 months).
Rifampin: turns body fluids orange, P450 inducer (reduces warfarin, OCPs, many drugs), hepatotoxic.
Isoniazid: peripheral neuropathy (supplement with pyridoxine/B6!), hepatotoxic, lupus-like syndrome, sideroblastic anemia.
Pyrazinamide: hyperuricemia (gout), hepatotoxic (most hepatotoxic of the four in the first 2 months).
Ethanbutol: optic neuritis (red-green color blindness) — check visual acuity monthly.

TB meningitis: Extend to 12 months. Add dexamethasone (reduces mortality). MDR-TB: Resistant to at least isoniazid + rifampin. Use fluoroquinolone-based regimen (18-20 months).

Directly Observed Therapy (DOT)

Healthcare worker watches patient take each dose. Standard of care for active TB to ensure compliance and prevent resistance.
