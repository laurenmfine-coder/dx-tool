🔑 Why no ketosis in HHS? Patients with HHS have ENOUGH insulin to suppress lipolysis and ketogenesis, but NOT enough to allow glucose uptake. So glucose rises dramatically without ketone production. In DKA, there's essentially NO insulin → unrestrained lipolysis → fatty acids → hepatic ketogenesis → metabolic acidosis.
TREATMENT
DKA Management

The DKA Protocol (in order)

1. Fluids FIRST: Normal saline (0.9% NS) 1-1.5 L/hr for the first hour, then 250-500 mL/hr. Switch to 0.45% NS when Na normalizes or is high. Total deficit: 5-8 liters.

2. Potassium: Check K before insulin! If K <3.3 → hold insulin, replace K first (cardiac arrest risk). If K 3.3-5.3 → give K with insulin. If K >5.3 → hold K, recheck in 2h. Insulin drives K intracellular → all DKA patients are total-body K depleted even if serum K appears normal.

⚠️ NEVER give insulin if K <3.3. Insulin drives K into cells → severe hypokalemia → fatal arrhythmia. Replace K first.

3. Insulin: Regular insulin drip 0.1 U/kg/hr (or 0.14 U/kg/hr without bolus). Goal: reduce glucose by 50-75 mg/dL/hr. When glucose reaches 200 mg/dL → add D5 to fluids and reduce insulin rate (prevent hypoglycemia while continuing to close the anion gap).

4. Bicarbonate: Only if pH <6.9 (controversial, rare). Do NOT routinely give bicarb — acidosis corrects with insulin + fluids.

Resolution Criteria

DKA is resolved when: glucose <200 AND at least 2 of: pH >7.3, bicarb ≥15, anion gap ≤12. Transition to subcutaneous insulin (give SQ 1-2 hours BEFORE stopping drip — overlap is critical).

COMPLICATIONS
HHS Management & Cerebral Edema

HHS Treatment

Similar to DKA but with MORE aggressive fluid resuscitation (total deficit 8-12 liters). Insulin is less critical initially — focus on fluids. Lower insulin rate (0.025-0.05 U/kg/hr). Correct slowly — rapid osmolality changes → cerebral edema.

Cerebral Edema in DKA

⚠️ Most common cause of death in pediatric DKA. Occurs in 0.5-1% of pediatric DKA, mortality 20-25%. Usually 4-12 hours after treatment begins. Mechanism: rapid fluid administration + rapid glucose/osmolality correction → water shifts into brain cells. Signs: headache, altered mental status, Cushing triad (bradycardia, hypertension, irregular respirations). Treatment: MANNITOL 0.5-1 g/kg IV or 3% hypertonic saline. Slow IV fluid rate.

Other DKA Complications

Hypokalemia (from insulin + fluid resuscitation), hypoglycemia (from insulin), cerebral edema (pediatric), rhabdomyolysis, pulmonary edema (fluid overload), mucormycosis (rhinocerebral — diabetic ketoacidosis + iron overload creates perfect environment for Mucor).
