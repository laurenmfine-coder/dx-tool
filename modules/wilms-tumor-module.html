🎗️ Wilms Tumor

Nephroblastoma: The Most Common Pediatric Renal Malignancy

📚 Overview

Wilms tumor (nephroblastoma) is the most common primary renal malignancy in children, arising from persistent metanephric blastema (embryonic kidney tissue). It typically presents as an asymptomatic abdominal mass in a child under 5.

Epidemiology

ParameterValue
Incidence~500 cases/year in US
Peak age3-4 years (90% diagnosed <7 years)
Bilateral5-10%
5-year survival (favorable histology)>90%
Associated syndromes10-15%

🧬 Embryology & Pathogenesis

Nephrogenic Rests: Wilms tumor arises from nephrogenic rests - persistent foci of embryonic metanephric blastema that failed to differentiate into normal nephrons. These are precursor lesions found in 30-40% of Wilms tumors.

Associated Syndromes

WAGR Syndrome (WT1 deletion):
  • Wilms tumor (50% risk)
  • Aniridia
  • Genitourinary anomalies
  • Retardation (intellectual disability)
Beckwith-Wiedemann Syndrome (WT2/11p15):
  • Macroglossia, macrosomia, hemihypertrophy
  • Omphalocele
  • Visceromegaly
  • Ear creases/pits
  • Hypoglycemia (neonatal)
  • Wilms tumor risk ~5-10%
Denys-Drash Syndrome (WT1 missense):
  • Wilms tumor (90% risk)
  • Diffuse mesangial sclerosis → early renal failure
  • Ambiguous genitalia (46,XY DSD)

🔍 Clinical Presentation

Classic Presentation

  • Asymptomatic abdominal mass - most common (parent notices during bath)
  • Usually smooth, firm, does not cross midline
  • Abdominal pain (30%)
  • Hematuria (20%) - gross or microscopic
  • Hypertension (25%) - renin secretion from tumor
  • Fever (sometimes)
⚠️ DO NOT aggressively palpate! Wilms tumor is friable - vigorous palpation risks rupture and tumor spillage, which upstages the disease and worsens prognosis.

🔬 Histology

Classic "Triphasic" Pattern

Wilms tumor classically shows three components:
  1. Blastemal - primitive small blue cells
  2. Epithelial - tubules, glomeruloid structures
  3. Stromal - mesenchymal elements (muscle, cartilage, bone)

Histologic Classification

CategoryFeaturesPrognosis
Favorable histologyNo anaplasia (~90%)Excellent
Unfavorable (Anaplastic)Focal or diffuse anaplasia (~10%)Worse (esp. diffuse)
Anaplasia = Poor Prognosis
  • Marked nuclear enlargement (3x)
  • Hyperchromasia
  • Abnormal mitoses
  • Associated with TP53 mutations
  • More common in older children

💊 Treatment

North American Approach (COG)

SUSPECTED WILMS TUMOR ↓ Imaging (US → CT chest/abd/pelvis) ↓ UPFRONT NEPHRECTOMY (if feasible) ↓ Staging based on surgical/pathologic findings ↓ Adjuvant chemotherapy ± radiation based on stage/histology

Treatment by Stage (Favorable Histology)

StageTreatment
I & IINephrectomy + Vincristine/Actinomycin D (VA) x18-24 weeks
III & IVNephrectomy + VAD (add Doxorubicin) + XRT to flank ± lung
V (bilateral)Neoadjuvant chemo → nephron-sparing surgery if possible
Excellent Outcomes: Overall survival >90% with modern multimodal therapy. Even stage IV has ~80% survival with favorable histology.

Surgical Principles

  • Radical nephrectomy with lymph node sampling
  • Inspect contralateral kidney
  • Avoid tumor rupture (upstages to III)
  • Nephron-sparing for bilateral disease

📈 Surveillance

Screening for At-Risk Patients

Renal ultrasound screening every 3-4 months until age 7:
  • Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome
  • WAGR syndrome
  • Hemihypertrophy
  • Denys-Drash syndrome
  • Family history of Wilms

Post-Treatment Surveillance

  • Physical exam, imaging per protocol
  • Monitor for late effects (cardiac from doxorubicin, secondary malignancy from radiation)
  • Renal function monitoring (especially if nephron-sparing)

🧠 Quick Quiz

A 3-year-old with aniridia is found to have an abdominal mass. Genetic testing would most likely show a deletion at which locus?

A. 11p13 (WT1)
B. 11p15 (WT2)
C. 13q14 (RB1)
D. 17p13 (TP53)
Correct: A - 11p13 (WT1)
Aniridia + Wilms tumor suggests WAGR syndrome, caused by contiguous gene deletion at 11p13 affecting WT1 (Wilms tumor) and PAX6 (aniridia). The "R" in WAGR refers to intellectual disability from adjacent gene involvement.
