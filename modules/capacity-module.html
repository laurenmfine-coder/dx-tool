MECHANISM
Capacity vs Competency

Decision-making capacity is a clinical determination made by any physician. Competency is a legal determination made by a court. All adult patients are presumed to have capacity unless demonstrated otherwise. Capacity is decision-specific and can fluctuate.

The Four Elements of Capacity

ElementAssessmentExample Question
UnderstandingCan the patient comprehend the information presented?"Tell me in your own words what I explained about your condition."
AppreciationCan the patient recognize how the information applies to their situation?"How do you think this illness will affect you personally?"
ReasoningCan the patient weigh risks and benefits logically?"Why did you choose this option over the others?"
Expressing a ChoiceCan the patient clearly communicate a consistent decision?"What have you decided about the recommended treatment?"
\U0001f511 Key distinction: Capacity is assessed by the treating physician at the bedside. Competency requires a judge. A patient found to lack capacity is not automatically "incompetent"\u2014that requires a court proceeding. However, a physician finding lack of capacity has immediate clinical implications for treatment decisions.

Capacity Is Decision-Specific

A patient may have capacity for some decisions but not others. A patient with mild dementia may have capacity to consent to a blood draw but lack capacity for complex surgical decisions. Capacity can also fluctuate\u2014a delirious patient may regain capacity once the delirium resolves. Always reassess when clinical status changes.

Assessment Framework

TriggerExamples
Patient refuses recommended treatmentRefusing life-saving surgery, leaving AMA
Abrupt change in decisionPreviously agreeable patient suddenly refuses

Cognitive impairmentDementia, intoxication, TBI

\u26a0\ufe0f Common misconception: A patient who REFUSES treatment does NOT automatically lack capacity. The decision to refuse treatment\u2014even life-saving treatment\u2014may be a fully capacitated choice. You must assess the PROCESS of decision-making, not the OUTCOME of the decision.

Sliding Scale of Capacity

The rigor of capacity assessment should match the risk of the decision. Low-risk decisions (routine blood work) require minimal assessment. High-risk decisions with significant consequences (refusing cancer treatment, AMA discharge) require more thorough evaluation of all four elements.

Special Populations

PopulationKey Considerations
MinorsGenerally cannot consent; exceptions: emancipated minors, emergencies, specific conditions (STIs, contraception, substance abuse vary by state)
Intoxicated patientsIntoxication alone does not equal incapacity. Assess the four elements. Reassess when sober if decision can safely wait
Psychiatric patientsPsychiatric diagnosis alone does not equal incapacity. Many patients with schizophrenia, depression, etc. retain full capacity
Elderly/DementiaDementia diagnosis alone does not equal incapacity. Assess specific decision-making ability
TREATMENT
When Patient Lacks Capacity

Decision-Making Hierarchy

PriorityDecision MakerDetails
1stAdvance directive / Living willPatient's previously documented wishes when they had capacity
2ndHealthcare proxy / Durable power of attorneyPerson designated by patient to make decisions
3rdSurrogate (next of kin)Spouse \u2192 adult children \u2192 parents \u2192 siblings (hierarchy varies by state)
4thEthics committee / Court-appointed guardianWhen no surrogate available or disagreement exists
\U0001f511 Surrogate decision standard: Surrogates should use substituted judgment (what the patient would want) if patient's wishes are known. If unknown, use best interest standard (what a reasonable person would want). Substituted judgment takes priority over surrogate's personal preferences.

Emergency Exception

In a life-threatening emergency where the patient lacks capacity AND no surrogate is available AND delay would result in death or serious harm, physicians may treat under implied consent. The assumption is that a reasonable person would consent to life-saving treatment. Document the emergency circumstances thoroughly.

\u26a0\ufe0f AMA discharge: A patient with capacity has the absolute right to leave AMA. Document: (1) capacity assessment, (2) risks explained, (3) patient's understanding of risks, (4) patient's decision. Do NOT withhold prescriptions or follow-up as "punishment" for leaving AMA.
