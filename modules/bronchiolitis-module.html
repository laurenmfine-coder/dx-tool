⚙️ Bronchiolitis

Pathophysiology & Clinical Correlations | Pediatrics

🎯 Learning Objectives

  • RSV is the most common cause; peak age 2-6 months
  • Supportive care is mainstay - no proven benefit from bronchodilators or steroids
  • Airway diameter is critical - small changes cause large resistance increases
  • Red flags: apnea, poor feeding, respiratory fatigue, hypoxia

🔬 Core Pathophysiology

Viral Injury Cascade

RSV and other viruses cause small airway inflammation and obstruction in infants.

Pathway

Viral infection (RSV most common)→ Bronchiolar epithelial necrosis→ Inflammatory infiltrate + edema→ Mucus plugging of small airways→ Air trapping + V/Q mismatch

Why Infants Are Vulnerable

Anatomic and immunologic factors make infants susceptible to severe disease.

Pathway

Small airway diameter (resistance ∝ 1/r⁴)→ Immature immune response→ Less collateral ventilation→ Compliant chest wall→ Prone to respiratory fatigue

🏥 Clinical Correlations

Wheezing and crackles

Mechanism: Air moving through narrowed, mucus-filled bronchioles

Clinical Significance: Classic auscultatory finding; may have both inspiratory and expiratory components

Hyperinflation on CXR

Mechanism: Air trapping distal to obstructed bronchioles

Clinical Significance: Flattened diaphragms, increased AP diameter

Apnea in young infants

Mechanism: Immature respiratory control + increased work of breathing

Clinical Significance: Red flag - indicates need for admission/monitoring
