MECHANISM
Classification
TypeFeaturesDuration
TensionMost common primary headache. Bilateral, band-like pressure, mild-moderate. No aura, no N/V, no photophobia. Pericranial tenderness30 min - 7 days
MigraineUnilateral, pulsating/throbbing, moderate-severe. Associated: N/V, photophobia, phonophobia. ± Aura (visual scintillations, scotoma, paresthesias — 25%)4-72 hours
ClusterSevere unilateral orbital/periorbital pain. Ipsilateral autonomic features: lacrimation, rhinorrhea, conjunctival injection, ptosis/miosis (partial Horner). Male predominance (5:1). Restless/agitated (pacing — unlike migraine patients who lie still)15-180 min, 1-8× daily for weeks ("clusters")
Trigeminal neuralgiaSevere, brief, lancinating "electric shock" pain in V2/V3 distribution. Triggered by touch, chewing, wind. Most common in >50, women. MRI to rule out compressionSeconds-2 min
🔑 Behavioral Clue: Migraine → lies still in dark room. Cluster → paces/rocks/agitated (restlessness). This distinction is high-yield on boards.
CLINICAL
Red Flags & Secondary Headaches
⚠️ SNOOP Red Flags (Require Imaging/Workup):
Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, cancer, HIV, pregnancy)
Neurologic signs (focal deficits, papilledema, altered mental status, seizure)
Onset sudden ("thunderclap" — worst headache of my life → SAH until proven otherwise)
Older age (new headache >50 → temporal arteritis, mass)
Pattern change (progressive worsening, different from prior headaches, positional)

Key Secondary Headaches

CauseClues
SAHThunderclap headache. CT → LP if negative
Temporal arteritis (GCA)Age >50, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, visual loss (ophthalmic artery). Elevated ESR/CRP. Biopsy confirms (skip lesions). Start steroids BEFORE biopsy (prevent blindness)
Idiopathic intracranial HTN (pseudotumor)Obese young women, headache, papilledema, vision changes, pulsatile tinnitus. LP: elevated opening pressure >25 cmH2O. Normal MRI
Brain tumorProgressive, worse in AM, worsens with Valsalva. Focal deficits. Papilledema
TREATMENT
Management
TypeAcute TreatmentProphylaxis (if frequent)
TensionNSAIDs, acetaminophenAmitriptyline (TCA)
MigraineTriptans (sumatriptan — 5-HT1B/1D agonist, first-line). NSAIDs. Anti-emetics. Avoid opioidsTopiramate, propranolol, amitriptyline, valproate. CGRP monoclonal antibodies (erenumab, galcanezumab). Botox for chronic migraine (≥15 days/month)
ClusterHigh-flow O2 (100% NRB — first-line), sumatriptan SCVerapamil (first-line prophylaxis). Short-term: prednisone bridge. Galcanezumab (CGRP)
Trigeminal neuralgiaCarbamazepine (first-line). Oxcarbazepine alternativeMicrovascular decompression (surgery) for refractory
🔑 Triptan Contraindications: Coronary artery disease, uncontrolled HTN, hemiplegic/basilar migraine, concurrent use of ergotamines or MAOIs, history of stroke. Triptans cause vasoconstriction → risk of coronary vasospasm/MI.
