MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

Urticaria and angioedema result from two distinct pathways of vascular permeability: mast cell/histamine-mediated and bradykinin-mediated. Distinguishing these pathways is critical because their treatments differ fundamentally.

Mast Cell-Mediated Pathway

Mast cell degranulation releases histamine, leukotrienes, and prostaglandins causing vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, and dermal edema (urticaria) or deeper subcutaneous/submucosal edema (angioedema).

Bradykinin-Mediated Pathway

C1-inhibitor (C1-INH) deficiency or ACE inhibitor use leads to uncontrolled contact system activation, excess bradykinin, B2 receptor activation, and vasodilation/edema WITHOUT mast cell involvement.

🔑 Key Distinction: Histamine-mediated = urticaria WITH pruritus, responds to antihistamines/epinephrine. Bradykinin-mediated = angioedema WITHOUT urticaria, does NOT respond to antihistamines, steroids, or epinephrine. This is the single most tested concept.

Clinical Presentation & Workup

Red Flags

⚠️ Anaphylaxis signs: Urticaria/angioedema + hypotension, bronchospasm, stridor, or GI symptoms = treat as anaphylaxis (IM epinephrine first). Urticaria alone without systemic symptoms is NOT anaphylaxis.

ACE Inhibitor Angioedema

Occurs in ~0.5% of ACE-I users. Can present weeks to YEARS after starting the medication. Classically affects lips, tongue, and face. More common in Black patients (4-5x risk). Management: discontinue ACE-I permanently; acute treatment with icatibant (bradykinin B2 antagonist) or C1-INH concentrate.

Urticarial Vasculitis

Suspect when individual lesions last >24 hours, are painful (not pruritic), and resolve with bruise-like discoloration. Diagnosis by skin biopsy showing leukocytoclastic vasculitis. Hypocomplementemic urticarial vasculitis carries worse prognosis with SLE association.

TREATMENT
Management

Chronic Urticaria Step Therapy

StepTreatmentNotes
Step 12nd-gen H1-antihistamine (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine)Standard dosing; first-line for all
Step 2Up-dose antihistamine (up to 4x standard)Off-label but guideline-recommended
Step 3Add omalizumab (anti-IgE)FDA-approved for chronic spontaneous urticaria
Step 4Cyclosporine or other immunosuppressantReserved for severe refractory cases
🔑 Omalizumab: Anti-IgE monoclonal antibody that reduces free IgE and downregulates FcεRI on mast cells/basophils. Response can be dramatic within days. Also FDA-approved for allergic asthma.
