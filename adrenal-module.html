🌙 Cushing's Syndrome (Cortisol Excess)

Clinical Features

  • • Central obesity, moon facies, buffalo hump
  • • Purple striae (>1cm width)
  • • Thin skin, easy bruising
  • • Proximal muscle weakness
  • • Hypertension, hyperglycemia
  • • Osteoporosis
  • • Hirsutism, acne (androgen excess)
  • • Depression, psychosis

Causes

ACTH-Independent (↓ACTH)
• Exogenous steroids (most common!)
• Adrenal adenoma/carcinoma
ACTH-Dependent (↑ACTH)
• Pituitary adenoma (Cushing's Disease - 70%)
• Ectopic ACTH (small cell lung cancer)

Diagnostic Workup

Step 1 - Confirm hypercortisolism: 24hr urine cortisol, late-night salivary cortisol, or 1mg dexamethasone suppression test (failure to suppress = Cushing's)
Step 2 - Check ACTH: Low = adrenal source. High = pituitary or ectopic
Step 3 - If high ACTH: High-dose dex suppression or CRH stimulation to differentiate pituitary vs ectopic. MRI pituitary.

🥀 Adrenal Insufficiency

Primary (Addison's Disease)

Problem: Adrenal gland destruction
Labs: ↓Cortisol, ↑ACTH, ↓Aldosterone
Unique: Hyperpigmentation (↑ACTH = ↑MSH)
Causes: Autoimmune (developed), TB (developing), hemorrhage, mets

Secondary/Tertiary

Problem: Pituitary/hypothalamus failure OR chronic steroid use
Labs: ↓Cortisol, ↓or N ACTH, Normal aldosterone
NO hyperpigmentation (ACTH not elevated)
Aldosterone OK (regulated by RAAS, not ACTH)

Symptoms

  • • Fatigue, weakness, weight loss
  • • Hypotension (especially orthostatic)
  • • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • • Salt craving (aldosterone deficiency)
  • • Hyperpigmentation (primary only)

Labs

  • • Hyponatremia (↓aldosterone + ↓cortisol)
  • • Hyperkalemia (↓aldosterone)
  • • Hypoglycemia (↓cortisol)
  • • Metabolic acidosis (↓aldosterone → ↓H⁺ excretion)
  • • Eosinophilia (cortisol normally suppresses)

Diagnosis: ACTH Stimulation (Cosyntropin) Test

Give synthetic ACTH → Measure cortisol at 30-60 min
Normal: Cortisol rises to >18-20 mcg/dL
Primary AI: No cortisol response (adrenals destroyed)
Secondary AI: Blunted response (adrenals atrophied from lack of ACTH)

⚠️ Adrenal Crisis (Emergency!)

Trigger: Stress (infection, surgery, trauma) in patient with AI, or sudden steroid withdrawal
Features: Severe hypotension/shock, fever, altered mental status, severe abdominal pain, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia
Treatment: Don't wait for labs!
  • • IV Hydrocortisone 100mg bolus, then 50-100mg q6-8h
  • • IV fluids (NS - they're volume depleted)
  • • Correct hypoglycemia (D50 if needed)
  • • Treat precipitating cause

💥 Pheochromocytoma

The 5 P's

  • • Pressure (hypertension - paroxysmal or sustained)
  • • Pain (headache)
  • • Palpitations
  • • Perspiration (diaphoresis)
  • • Pallor
Classic triad: Headache + Palpitations + Sweating

Rule of 10's

  • • 10% bilateral
  • • 10% extra-adrenal (paraganglioma)
  • • 10% malignant
  • • 10% familial (MEN2, VHL, NF1)
  • • 10% in children
Diagnosis: Plasma free metanephrines (most sensitive) or 24hr urine catecholamines/metanephrines
Imaging: CT or MRI adrenals → MIBG scan if extra-adrenal suspected
Treatment: Alpha-blockade FIRST (phenoxybenzamine) → Then beta-blockade → Then surgery
⚠️ Never give beta-blocker first! Unopposed alpha = hypertensive crisis

🧂 Primary Hyperaldosteronism (Conn's Syndrome)

Features

  • • Hypertension (often resistant)
  • • Hypokalemia (not always present)
  • • Metabolic alkalosis
  • • Low renin (suppressed by high aldo)

Causes

• Aldosterone-producing adenoma (Conn's) - 35%
• Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia - 60%
• Adrenal carcinoma - rare
Screening: Aldosterone:Renin ratio (ARR) >30 with aldo >15
Confirm: Salt loading test (aldo fails to suppress)
Localize: CT adrenals → Adrenal vein sampling if bilateral lesions
Treat: Adenoma → surgery. Hyperplasia → Spironolactone/Eplerenone
