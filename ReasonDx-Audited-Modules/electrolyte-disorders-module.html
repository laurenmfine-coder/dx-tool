🧂 Sodium Disorders

Hyponatremia (Na <135)

Step 1: Check serum osmolality
  • Low (<280): True hypotonic hyponatremia → Go to Step 2
  • Normal (280-295): Pseudohyponatremia (lipids, proteins)
  • High (>295): Hyperglycemia, mannitol (dilutional)
Step 2: Assess volume status
Volume StatusCausesUrine Na
HypovolemicVomiting, diarrhea, diuretics, adrenal insufficiency<20 (renal losses: >20)
EuvolemicSIADH, hypothyroidism, psychogenic polydipsia>20 (SIADH)
HypervolemicCHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome<20 (usually)

SIADH Criteria

  • Serum osmolality <275
  • Urine osmolality >100 (inappropriately concentrated)
  • Urine Na >40
  • Euvolemic
  • Normal thyroid, adrenal function

Treatment

Correction rate: ≤8-10 mEq/L per 24 hours
Rapid correction → Osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS)
ScenarioTreatment
Symptomatic (seizures)3% saline 100mL bolus over 10 min (repeat ×2 if needed)
HypovolemicNormal saline
SIADHFluid restriction, salt tabs, vasopressin antagonists (tolvaptan)
HypervolemicFluid restriction, diuretics, treat underlying cause

Hypernatremia (Na >145)

Almost always due to water deficit (free water loss > intake)

Causes:
  • Inadequate water intake (altered mental status, no access)
  • Diabetes insipidus (central or nephrogenic)
  • GI losses, skin losses, renal losses
Treatment: Free water replacement (D5W or oral water)
Correction rate: ≤10-12 mEq/L per 24 hours (risk of cerebral edema)

🔋 Potassium Disorders

Hypokalemia (K <3.5)

MechanismCauses
Transcellular shiftInsulin, β-agonists, alkalosis, refeeding
GI lossesVomiting (metabolic alkalosis), diarrhea (metabolic acidosis)
Renal lossesDiuretics, hyperaldosteronism, RTA types 1&2, Mg deficiency
ECG Changes (progressive):
  • Flattened T waves
  • U waves
  • ST depression
  • Prolonged QT → arrhythmias
Treatment:
  • Oral preferred if mild and patient can take PO
  • IV KCl: max 10-20 mEq/hr peripherally; 40 mEq/hr centrally
  • Replace magnesium (K repletion difficult if Mg low)
  • Roughly: 10 mEq KCl raises K by 0.1 mEq/L

Hyperkalemia (K >5.0)

MechanismCauses
PseudohyperkalemiaHemolysis, thrombocytosis, difficult blood draw
Transcellular shiftAcidosis, insulin deficiency, β-blockers, cell lysis
Decreased excretionAKI/CKD, hypoaldosteronism, K-sparing diuretics, ACEi/ARB
Increased intakeSupplements, diet (rare alone)
ECG Changes (progressive):
  • Peaked T waves (earliest)
  • Prolonged PR, flattened P waves
  • Widened QRS
  • Sine wave → VF/asystole

🦴 Calcium Disorders

Always correct for albumin:
Corrected Ca = Measured Ca + 0.8 × (4 - Albumin)
Or check ionized calcium (more accurate)

Hypercalcemia (Ca >10.5)

"Stones, Bones, Groans, Psychiatric Overtones"
Kidney stones, bone pain, abdominal pain/constipation, confusion/depression
PTH LevelCauses
High PTHPrimary hyperparathyroidism (most common outpatient)
Low PTHMalignancy (most common inpatient), granulomatous disease, vitamin D toxicity

Treatment of Hypercalcemia

  1. IV fluids: NS 200-300 mL/hr (restore volume, ↑ Ca excretion)
  2. Loop diuretics: After volume repletion (furosemide)
  3. Bisphosphonates: Zoledronic acid or pamidronate (takes 2-4 days)
  4. Calcitonin: Rapid but transient effect
  5. Steroids: For granulomatous disease, myeloma
  6. Dialysis: If severe or refractory

Treatment: IV calcium gluconate if symptomatic; oral calcium + vitamin D for maintenance

✨ Magnesium Disorders

Hypomagnesemia (Mg <1.8)

Causes:
  • GI losses: Diarrhea, malabsorption, PPIs
  • Renal losses: Diuretics, alcoholism, aminoglycosides
  • Poor intake: Alcoholism, malnutrition
Important associations:
  • Refractory hypokalemia - can't correct K without fixing Mg!
  • Refractory hypocalcemia
  • Cardiac arrhythmias (Torsades de pointes)

Treatment: IV magnesium sulfate (1-2g) or oral supplements

Hypermagnesemia (Mg >2.5)

  • Almost always iatrogenic (Mg-containing antacids/laxatives) + renal impairment
  • Symptoms: Hyporeflexia, muscle weakness, bradycardia, hypotension
  • Treatment: IV calcium gluconate, loop diuretics, dialysis

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A patient has hyponatremia with urine Na >40 and urine osmolality >100. What's the most likely diagnosis?

A. SIADH
B. Heart failure
C. Cirrhosis
Correct: A. SIADH has inappropriately concentrated urine (>100) with high urine Na (>40) in a euvolemic patient. CHF and cirrhosis are hypervolemic with low urine Na.

Q2: What is the maximum safe rate of hyponatremia correction?

A. 8-10 mEq/L per 24 hours
B. 20 mEq/L per 24 hours
C. As fast as possible
Correct: A. Limit correction to 8-10 mEq/L per 24 hours to prevent osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS).

Q3: First ECG change in hyperkalemia?

A. Peaked T waves
B. Widened QRS
C. Absent P waves
Correct: A. Peaked T waves are the earliest ECG sign. Progression: peaked T → prolonged PR → widened QRS → sine wave → VF.

Q4: Why replace magnesium before potassium in hypokalemia?

A. Low Mg makes K repletion refractory
B. Mg is more dangerous than K
C. K causes Mg loss
Correct: A. Hypomagnesemia impairs renal potassium retention. K repletion will be ineffective until Mg is corrected.

Q5: Hypercalcemia with LOW PTH suggests:

A. Primary hyperparathyroidism
B. Malignancy
C. Familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia
Correct: B. Low/suppressed PTH with hypercalcemia = PTH-independent cause (malignancy, granulomatous disease, vitamin D toxicity). Primary hyperparathyroidism has HIGH PTH.
