📚 Overview

Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH) is the leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide. Defined as blood loss ≥1000 mL OR blood loss with signs/symptoms of hypovolemia within 24 hours of delivery (regardless of delivery route).

Definitions

TermDefinition
Primary PPHWithin 24 hours of delivery
Secondary PPH24 hours to 12 weeks postpartum
Severe PPH≥1500 mL blood loss

Risk Factors

CategoryRisk Factors
UterineOverdistension (twins, polyhydramnios, macrosomia), prolonged labor, chorioamnionitis, high parity
PlacentalPlacenta previa, accreta spectrum, abruption, retained placenta
TraumaOperative vaginal delivery, episiotomy, cesarean, precipitous labor
CoagulationPreexisting disorders, anticoagulation, DIC, massive transfusion
HistoryPrior PPH (recurrence risk 10-15%)
🎯 Key Point: Visual estimation of blood loss is notoriously inaccurate (underestimates by 30-50%). Use quantitative blood loss (QBL) measurement - weigh blood-soaked materials: 1 gram = 1 mL blood.

🔍 Causes: The 4 T's

💡 "4 T's" of PPH

Tone (70%) → Trauma (20%) → Tissue (10%) → Thrombin (<1%)

🔴 TONE - Uterine Atony (70%)

Most common cause! Failure of uterus to contract after placental delivery.

Risk factors:

  • Uterine overdistension (multiples, polyhydramnios, macrosomia)
  • Prolonged labor or very rapid labor
  • Chorioamnionitis
  • High parity (≥5)
  • Magnesium sulfate, tocolytics
  • General anesthesia (halogenated agents)

Clinical finding: Soft, "boggy" uterus on palpation

🟠 TRAUMA - Genital Tract Injury (20%)

  • Lacerations: Vaginal, cervical, perineal (1st-4th degree)
  • Hematomas: Vulvar, vaginal, retroperitoneal
  • Uterine rupture: Prior cesarean, obstructed labor
  • Uterine inversion: Rare but dramatic

Clinical finding: Firm uterus but continued bleeding → suspect laceration

🟣 TISSUE - Retained Products (10%)

  • Retained placental fragments
  • Retained membranes
  • Placenta accreta spectrum (abnormal adherence)
  • Succenturiate lobe (accessory lobe)

Clinical finding: Incomplete placenta on inspection, uterus doesn't fully contract

🔵 THROMBIN - Coagulopathy (<1%)

  • Pre-existing: von Willebrand disease, hemophilia carrier, thrombocytopenia
  • Acquired: DIC, massive transfusion, amniotic fluid embolism, severe preeclampsia/HELLP, placental abruption
  • Iatrogenic: Anticoagulation

Clinical finding: Diffuse oozing, bleeding from IV sites, no clot formation

⚕️ Stepwise Management

🚨 IMMEDIATE ACTIONS - Do Simultaneously:
  1. Call for help (activate hemorrhage protocol)
  2. Massage the uterus
  3. Establish large-bore IV access (2 x 16-18 gauge)
  4. Type and crossmatch, CBC, coags, fibrinogen
  5. Administer uterotonics
  6. Empty bladder (Foley catheter)

Systematic Approach

Step 1: Assess & Resuscitate

  • Quantify blood loss
  • Crystalloid bolus (warm fluids)
  • Activate massive transfusion protocol if needed
  • Target: MAP >65, HR <100, UOP >0.5 mL/kg/hr

Step 2: Identify Cause (4 T's)

  • Tone: Bimanual uterine massage
  • Trauma: Inspect cervix and vagina systematically
  • Tissue: Examine placenta, consider manual exploration/ultrasound
  • Thrombin: Check labs, observe for oozing

Step 3: Uterotonics (for Atony)

  • Oxytocin (first-line)
  • Methylergonovine (Methergine)
  • Carboprost (Hemabate/PGF2α)
  • Misoprostol

Step 4: Tamponade

  • Intrauterine balloon (Bakri, Ebb, Foley)
  • Uterine packing

Step 5: Surgical Intervention

  • Compression sutures (B-Lynch)
  • Uterine artery ligation
  • Hypogastric artery ligation
  • Uterine artery embolization
  • Hysterectomy (definitive)
🔵 Tranexamic Acid (TXA)
  • 1 g IV over 10 minutes
  • Give within 3 hours of delivery
  • Can repeat once after 30 minutes if bleeding continues
  • Antifibrinolytic - stabilizes clots

💊 Uterotonics

OXYTOCIN (Pitocin) - First Line
Dose10-40 units in 1L NS, run wide open; or 10 units IM
OnsetImmediate IV, 3-5 min IM
Side effectsWater intoxication (high doses), hypotension (bolus)
ContraindicationsNone absolute
METHYLERGONOVINE (Methergine)
Dose0.2 mg IM q2-4h (can give IV slowly in emergency)
Onset2-5 minutes
Side effectsHypertension, nausea, vasoconstriction
ContraindicationsHYPERTENSION, preeclampsia
CARBOPROST (Hemabate, 15-methyl PGF2α)
Dose250 mcg IM q15-90 min (max 8 doses / 2 mg)
OnsetMinutes
Side effectsBronchospasm, diarrhea, fever, hypertension
ContraindicationsASTHMA, active pulmonary/cardiac/hepatic disease
MISOPROSTOL (Cytotec, PGE1)
Dose800-1000 mcg PR or SL
Onset~20 minutes
Side effectsFever, chills, diarrhea
ContraindicationsNone absolute; useful when others contraindicated
💡 Uterotonic Contraindications Memory Aid:

Methergine = No Mountain-high BP (hypertension)
Hemabate = No Hacking/wheezing (asthma)

🔪 Surgical & Interventional Options

Intrauterine Balloon Tamponade

  • Bakri balloon: Specifically designed for PPH
  • Foley catheter: Inflate bulb with 60-80 mL
  • Technique: Insert into uterine cavity, inflate with saline until bleeding stops
  • Positive tamponade test: Bleeding stops with inflation
  • Can leave 12-24 hours; give antibiotics

Compression Sutures

B-Lynch Suture

Compresses the uterus like a brace. Requires laparotomy.

  • Preserves fertility in most cases
  • Can combine with balloon tamponade
  • Complications: Uterine necrosis (rare), pyometra

Vascular Ligation

Uterine Artery Ligation

  • O'Leary stitch - bilateral ligation
  • Reduces pulse pressure to uterus
  • 90% success rate

Internal Iliac (Hypogastric) Artery Ligation

  • Reduces pulse pressure by 85%
  • More difficult, risk of ureteral injury
  • Collateral flow usually preserves fertility

Uterine Artery Embolization

  • Performed by interventional radiology
  • 90% success rate
  • Requires hemodynamic stability for transport
  • Preserves fertility

Hysterectomy

Definitive treatment - do not delay if bleeding uncontrolled!
  • Indications: Failed conservative measures, placenta accreta spectrum, uterine rupture
  • Usually subtotal (supracervical) - faster, less blood loss
  • Total if cervical bleeding or accreta involving cervix
🎯 Key Principle: Do not delay hysterectomy while waiting for other interventions to work. The decision to proceed with hysterectomy should be made early rather than after multiple failed attempts and worsening coagulopathy.

📝 Test Your Knowledge

Question 1

The most common cause of primary postpartum hemorrhage is:

A. Uterine atony
B. Genital tract laceration
C. Retained placenta
D. Coagulopathy
Answer: A. Uterine atony accounts for ~70% of PPH cases. The first clinical finding is a soft, "boggy" uterus.

Question 2

Which uterotonic is contraindicated in a patient with asthma?

A. Oxytocin
B. Methylergonovine
C. Carboprost (Hemabate)
D. Misoprostol
Answer: C. Carboprost (Hemabate/PGF2α) causes bronchospasm and is contraindicated in asthma. Methylergonovine is contraindicated in hypertension.

Question 3

A patient has a firm uterus but continues to bleed heavily after vaginal delivery. The most likely cause is:

A. Uterine atony
B. Genital tract laceration
C. Uterine inversion
D. Placenta accreta
Answer: B. A firm uterus with ongoing bleeding suggests the source is NOT the uterus - inspect for cervical, vaginal, and perineal lacerations.

Question 4

Tranexamic acid for PPH should be given within what time frame?

A. 1 hour
B. 3 hours
C. 6 hours
D. 12 hours
Answer: B. TXA 1g IV should be given within 3 hours of delivery for maximum benefit. Can repeat once after 30 minutes if bleeding continues.

Question 5

The "positive tamponade test" refers to:

A. Bleeding that worsens with balloon inflation
B. Bleeding that stops with balloon inflation
C. Uterine rupture with balloon placement
D. Balloon expulsion from uterus
Answer: B. A positive tamponade test = bleeding stops when the balloon is inflated, suggesting tamponade will be effective for controlling hemorrhage.
