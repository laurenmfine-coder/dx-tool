🎭 Extrapyramidal Symptoms (EPS)

Mechanism: D2 receptor blockade in nigrostriatal pathway
Risk factors: High-potency FGAs, high doses, rapid titration, young age, prior EPS
TypeOnsetFeaturesTreatment
Acute Dystonia Hours to days Sustained muscle contractions: torticollis, oculogyric crisis, opisthotonus, laryngospasm Anticholinergics (benztropine, diphenhydramine)
Akathisia Days to weeks Inner restlessness, inability to sit still, pacing; subjective distress Beta-blockers (propranolol), benzodiazepines, reduce dose
Parkinsonism Weeks to months Tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, shuffling gait, masked facies Anticholinergics, amantadine, reduce dose
Tardive Dyskinesia Months to years Involuntary choreoathetoid movements, especially orofacial (lip smacking, tongue protrusion) Stop/switch agent; VMAT2 inhibitors (valbenazine, deutetrabenazine)
Tardive Dyskinesia (TD):
  • May be irreversible even after stopping medication
  • Risk increases with duration of treatment and older age
  • Screen with AIMS exam regularly
  • FDA-approved treatments: valbenazine (Ingrezza), deutetrabenazine (Austedo)

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)

Life-threatening emergency! ~10% mortality

Classic tetrad (FARM):
  • Fever (hyperthermia)
  • Autonomic instability (tachycardia, labile BP, diaphoresis)
  • Rigidity ("lead pipe")
  • Mental status changes
Labs: Elevated CK (often >1000), leukocytosis, elevated LFTs, myoglobinuria

Treatment:
  1. STOP antipsychotic immediately
  2. Supportive care (cooling, IV fluids, monitoring)
  3. Dantrolene (muscle relaxant) and/or bromocriptine (dopamine agonist)
  4. Monitor for rhabdomyolysis, renal failure, DIC

⚖️ Metabolic Side Effects

SGAs (especially olanzapine, clozapine) have significant metabolic effects:
  • Weight gain
  • Hyperglycemia / new-onset diabetes
  • Dyslipidemia
  • Metabolic syndrome

Monitoring Requirements

Before starting and during treatment:
  • Weight/BMI: Baseline, then monthly × 3, then quarterly
  • Waist circumference: Baseline, then annually
  • Fasting glucose/HbA1c: Baseline, at 12 weeks, then annually
  • Fasting lipids: Baseline, at 12 weeks, then every 5 years (or annually if abnormal)
  • Blood pressure: Baseline, then at each visit

🔍 Other Side Effects

Hyperprolactinemia

Mechanism: D2 blockade in tuberoinfundibular pathway
Highest risk: Risperidone, paliperidone, haloperidol
Symptoms: Galactorrhea, amenorrhea, sexual dysfunction, gynecomastia, osteoporosis
Management: Switch to prolactin-sparing agent (aripiprazole, quetiapine) or add aripiprazole

QTc Prolongation

Highest risk: Ziprasidone, thioridazine, IV haloperidol
Risk factors: Electrolyte abnormalities (K, Mg), other QT-prolonging drugs, cardiac disease
Monitoring: ECG at baseline and periodically; correct electrolytes
QTc >500ms: Consider discontinuation

Clozapine-Specific Side Effects

Agranulocytosis (1-2% risk):
  • Potentially fatal neutropenia
  • Requires REMS program enrollment
  • Mandatory ANC monitoring: weekly × 6 months → biweekly × 6 months → monthly
  • Stop clozapine if ANC <1000/μL
Other clozapine effects: Seizures (dose-related), myocarditis, sedation, hypersalivation (drooling), constipation (can be severe)

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A patient on haloperidol develops sustained neck twisting to one side hours after starting the medication. What is this, and how do you treat it?

A. Acute dystonia - benztropine or diphenhydramine
B. Tardive dyskinesia - valbenazine
C. Akathisia - propranolol
D. NMS - dantrolene
Correct: A. Acute dystonia presents within hours to days as sustained muscle contractions (torticollis, oculogyric crisis). Treat with anticholinergics (benztropine, diphenhydramine).

Q2: A patient on risperidone develops fever, muscle rigidity, altered mental status, and CK of 15,000. What is the diagnosis?

A. Serotonin syndrome
B. Neuroleptic malignant syndrome
C. Malignant hyperthermia
D. Acute dystonia
Correct: B. NMS presents with the classic tetrad: Fever, Autonomic instability, Rigidity, and Mental status changes (FARM). Markedly elevated CK is characteristic. Treatment: stop antipsychotic, supportive care, dantrolene/bromocriptine.

Q3: Which antipsychotic requires regular blood monitoring due to agranulocytosis risk?

A. Risperidone
B. Aripiprazole
C. Clozapine
D. Quetiapine
Correct: C. Clozapine carries a 1-2% risk of agranulocytosis, requiring enrollment in a REMS program with mandatory ANC monitoring: weekly × 6 months, then biweekly × 6 months, then monthly.

Q4: Which antipsychotics have the HIGHEST metabolic side effects (weight gain, diabetes, dyslipidemia)?

A. Aripiprazole and ziprasidone
B. Olanzapine and clozapine
C. Haloperidol and risperidone
D. Lurasidone and ziprasidone
Correct: B. "COLA" - Clozapine and Olanzapine have the highest metabolic side effects. Aripiprazole, ziprasidone, and lurasidone are relatively metabolically neutral.

Q5: A patient on an antipsychotic develops involuntary lip smacking and tongue movements after 2 years of treatment. What is this condition?

A. Acute dystonia
B. Akathisia
C. Tardive dyskinesia
D. Drug-induced parkinsonism
Correct: C. Tardive dyskinesia develops after months to years of antipsychotic use, presenting with involuntary choreoathetoid movements, especially orofacial (lip smacking, tongue protrusion). It may be irreversible. Treatment: VMAT2 inhibitors (valbenazine, deutetrabenazine).
