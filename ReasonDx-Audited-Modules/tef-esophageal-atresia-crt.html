🔬 Understanding the Embryology

💡 Tracheoesophageal Septum

During week 4, the foregut divides into ventral (respiratory) and dorsal (esophageal) portions via the tracheoesophageal septum. Abnormal septation causes TEF/EA.

The MOST COMMON type of TEF/EA (85%) has which configuration?

1️⃣
Pure EA without fistula
No connection between esophagus and trachea
2️⃣
Proximal EA + distal TEF
Blind proximal pouch; distal esophagus connects to trachea
3️⃣
H-type TEF (no atresia)
Both esophagus and trachea are patent with connecting fistula
4️⃣
Proximal TEF + distal EA
Proximal esophagus connects to trachea

💡 VACTERL Association

TEF/EA is associated with other midline defects. Must evaluate for:

  • Vertebral anomalies
  • Anorectal malformations
  • Cardiac defects
  • TracheoEsophageal fistula (index finding)

  • Limb defects (radial ray)

🔑 Key Learning Points

  • Embryology: Failure of tracheoesophageal septum formation (week 4)
  • Most common type (85%): Proximal EA + distal TEF
  • Prenatal clue: Polyhydramnios (can't swallow amniotic fluid)
  • Clinical triad: Drooling, choking/coughing with feeds, cyanosis
  • Diagnosis: Unable to pass NG tube → coiled on X-ray
  • Gas in stomach: Confirms distal TEF (air enters via fistula)
  • VACTERL workup: Echo most important (30-40% cardiac defects)
  • Management: NPO, head up, continuous suction of upper pouch
  • Surgery: Fistula ligation + esophageal anastomosis

⚠️ Complications to Know

  • Anastomotic leak: Most serious early complication
  • Stricture: May require dilation
  • Recurrent fistula: Rare but serious
  • Tracheomalacia: Common; usually improves
  • GERD: Very common; dysmotility persists
