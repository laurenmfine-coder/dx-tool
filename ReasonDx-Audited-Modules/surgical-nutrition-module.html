🎯 Nutrition Assessment

The Golden Rule: "If the gut works, use it!"
Enteral nutrition is ALWAYS preferred over parenteral when possible.

Why Enteral is Preferred

Enteral AdvantageExplanation
Maintains gut integrityPrevents bacterial translocation
Fewer infectious complicationsNo central line needed
Less expensiveLower cost of formulas and delivery
More physiologicFirst-pass hepatic metabolism, gut hormones
Preserves gut immune functionMaintains IgA, GALT

Nutrition Screening

Markers suggesting malnutrition:
  • Unintentional weight loss >10% in 6 months or >5% in 1 month
  • BMI <18.5 kg/m²
  • Albumin <3.0 g/dL (reflects chronic status, not acute)
  • Prealbumin <15 mg/dL (better for acute changes, t½ ~2 days)
  • Poor oral intake for ≥5-7 days

Caloric Requirements

Quick Estimate: 25-30 kcal/kg/day

Harris-Benedict Equation (BEE):
Men: 66 + (13.7 × wt kg) + (5 × ht cm) - (6.8 × age)
Women: 655 + (9.6 × wt kg) + (1.8 × ht cm) - (4.7 × age)

Total Energy = BEE × Activity Factor × Stress Factor
Stress FactorMultiplier
Minor surgery1.1-1.2
Major surgery1.2-1.4
Sepsis1.3-1.5
Burns (per % TBSA)1.5-2.0+
Trauma1.3-1.5

Initiation and Advancement

Starting Tube Feeds:
  1. Confirm tube position (X-ray for post-pyloric; pH or auscultation for NG)
  2. Elevate HOB ≥30-45°
  3. Start at 20-25 mL/hr (or full rate if hemodynamically stable)
  4. Advance by 10-25 mL/hr every 4-8 hours as tolerated
  5. Goal: full rate within 24-48 hours

Gastric vs Post-pyloric

Gastric (NG, PEG)

  • Can bolus feed
  • More physiologic
  • Easier access
  • Higher aspiration risk
  • Check residuals (controversial)

Post-pyloric (NJ, PEJ)

  • MUST be continuous
  • Lower aspiration risk
  • Use if gastroparesis
  • More difficult placement
  • Can feed with ileus (small bowel works)
Clinical Pearl: "The small bowel always works!" Even with gastric ileus, post-pyloric feeding is often tolerated because small bowel motility recovers first (usually within 24 hours).

💉 Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)

TPN Indications (when gut cannot be used):
  • Bowel obstruction
  • Severe ileus
  • High-output fistula (>500 mL/day)
  • Short bowel syndrome
  • Severe malabsorption
  • Bowel ischemia
  • Severe mucositis/GI toxicity

TPN Components

ComponentCaloric ValueNotes
Dextrose3.4 kcal/gPrimary carb source; hyperglycemia risk
Amino acids4 kcal/gUsually 10-15% of TPN; protein equivalent
Lipids9 kcal/g (10 kcal/mL for 20%)Essential fatty acids; limit if triglycerides elevated

TPN Formulation

Quick TPN Calculation:

Goal: ~25-30 kcal/kg/day, 1.5 g protein/kg/day

Example: 70 kg patient
• Calories: 70 × 25 = 1750 kcal/day
• Protein: 70 × 1.5 = 105 g amino acids/day
• Dextrose: ~60% of non-protein calories
• Lipids: ~30-40% of total calories (max 1g/kg/day)
• + electrolytes, vitamins, trace elements

Lipid Emulsions

FormulationConcentrationNotes
10% lipid1.1 kcal/mLMore volume
20% lipid2 kcal/mLMost common; less volume
30% lipid3 kcal/mLOnly for 3-in-1 admixtures
Lipid Restrictions:
  • Hold if triglycerides >400 mg/dL
  • Limit to 1 g/kg/day (2.5 g/kg/day max)
  • Essential fatty acid deficiency if withheld >2 weeks

Central vs Peripheral

Central TPN

  • Via central line (PICC, subclavian, IJ)
  • Can use hypertonic solutions (>900 mOsm)
  • Higher dextrose concentrations (15-25%)
  • Preferred for long-term use
  • Line infection risk

Peripheral Parenteral (PPN)

  • Via peripheral IV
  • Must be ≤900 mOsm (avoid phlebitis)
  • Limited dextrose (≤10%)
  • Short-term only (<2 weeks)
  • Cannot meet full caloric needs

Glucose Management

Target glucose: 140-180 mg/dL in critically ill

Managing hyperglycemia:
  • First: Add insulin to TPN bag (start ~0.1 units/g dextrose)
  • If severe: Separate insulin drip
  • If persistent: Reduce dextrose concentration

Nitrogen Balance

Nitrogen Balance = Nitrogen In - Nitrogen Out

Nitrogen In = Protein intake (g) ÷ 6.25
Nitrogen Out = 24-hour urine urea nitrogen + 4 (insensible losses)

Goal: Positive balance (+2 to +4 g/day) for anabolism

Markers of Nutritional Status

MarkerHalf-lifeInterpretation
Albumin~20 daysChronic status; affected by inflammation, hydration
Prealbumin (Transthyretin)~2 daysBetter for acute changes; affected by inflammation, renal function

Clinical Pearl: Prealbumin and albumin are "negative acute phase reactants" - they decrease with inflammation/infection regardless of nutrition. Don't rely on them alone in acute illness.

⚠️ Complications of Nutrition Support

Refeeding Syndrome

Definition: Metabolic derangements when nutrition is restarted after prolonged starvation

High-risk patients:
  • BMI <16
  • >10% weight loss in 3-6 months
  • Little/no intake for >10 days
  • Alcoholism, anorexia nervosa
  • Low K, Mg, or Phos before feeding
Refeeding Syndrome Key Features:
↓ Phosphorus (most important) - cardiac dysfunction, respiratory failure
↓ Potassium - arrhythmias
↓ Magnesium - arrhythmias, seizures
Fluid retention - edema, CHF
Prevention:
  • Start LOW, go SLOW (10-20 kcal/kg/day)
  • Replete electrolytes BEFORE starting feeds
  • Supplement thiamine (prevent Wernicke's)
  • Monitor electrolytes closely (daily)
  • Advance slowly over 4-7 days

TPN-Associated Liver Disease

Pattern: Elevated LFTs (AST, ALT, Alk phos, bilirubin)

Prevention:
  • Use enteral nutrition when possible (even trophic feeds help)
  • Cycle TPN (run over 12-18 hours, off overnight)
  • Avoid overfeeding (especially dextrose)
  • Include lipids (essential fatty acids)
  • Fish oil-based lipids (Omegaven) in severe cases

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A patient has a complete small bowel obstruction and needs nutrition support. What is the most appropriate route?

A. Nasogastric tube feeding
B. Nasojejunal tube feeding
C. Total parenteral nutrition
D. PEG tube
Correct: C. Complete bowel obstruction is an absolute contraindication to enteral nutrition. The gut cannot be used, so TPN is required until the obstruction is resolved.

Q2: Which electrolyte abnormality is MOST characteristic of refeeding syndrome?

A. Hyperkalemia
B. Hypophosphatemia
C. Hypernatremia
D. Hypercalcemia
Correct: B. Hypophosphatemia is the hallmark of refeeding syndrome. When carbohydrates are reintroduced, insulin drives glucose (and phosphorus) into cells for ATP production, causing severe hypophosphatemia. This can cause cardiac dysfunction, respiratory failure, and death.

Q3: A patient on TPN develops elevated triglycerides to 550 mg/dL. What should you do with the lipid emulsion?

A. Hold lipids until triglycerides decrease
B. Double the lipid dose
C. Continue lipids unchanged
D. Switch to 10% lipid emulsion
Correct: A. Hold IV lipids when triglycerides exceed 400 mg/dL to avoid pancreatitis and fat overload syndrome. Resume at lower dose once triglycerides normalize. Can supplement essential fatty acids topically if needed.

Q4: Why is enteral nutrition preferred over TPN?

A. Enteral provides more calories
B. Enteral maintains gut integrity and reduces infections
C. Enteral causes less diarrhea
D. Enteral is easier to calculate
Correct: B. Enteral nutrition maintains gut mucosal integrity, prevents bacterial translocation, preserves gut immune function (GALT), and has fewer infectious complications (no central line). "If the gut works, use it!"

Q5: Which serum protein is BEST for monitoring short-term response to nutrition therapy?

A. Albumin (half-life ~20 days)
B. Prealbumin (half-life ~2 days)
C. Transferrin (half-life ~8 days)
D. Hemoglobin
Correct: B. Prealbumin (transthyretin) has a short half-life of ~2 days, making it responsive to recent nutritional changes. Albumin's 20-day half-life makes it better for chronic status. Note: Both are negative acute phase reactants and decrease with inflammation.

Q6: A patient with gastroparesis needs long-term tube feeding. What is the best access?

A. Nasogastric tube
B. PEG (gastrostomy)
C. PEJ (jejunostomy)
D. Peripheral TPN
Correct: C. Post-pyloric feeding (PEJ/J-tube) bypasses the poorly functioning stomach and reduces aspiration risk. For long-term use (>4-6 weeks), a surgical/endoscopic tube is preferred over nasal tubes. Gastric feeding would pool in the atonic stomach.
