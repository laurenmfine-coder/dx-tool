📚 Labor & Delivery Overview

Labor is the process of regular uterine contractions leading to cervical change (dilation and effacement) and ultimately delivery of the fetus. Normal labor at term (37-42 weeks) involves three distinct stages.

Cardinal Movements of Labor

💡 "Every Darn Fool In Egypt Eats Eggs"
Engagement → Descent → Flexion → Internal rotation → Extension → External rotation → Expulsion

📍 Cardinal Movements Explained

  • Engagement: Biparietal diameter passes pelvic inlet
  • Descent: Fetus moves through pelvis
  • Flexion: Chin to chest (smallest diameter presents)
  • Internal rotation: Occiput rotates to anterior (OA position)
  • Extension: Head extends under symphysis
  • External rotation (restitution): Head realigns with shoulders
  • Expulsion: Delivery of shoulders and body

🎯 Clinical Pearl: The most common (and optimal) presentation is vertex with occiput anterior (OA). Occiput posterior (OP) or "sunny side up" is associated with prolonged labor and back labor.

📊 Stages of Labor

Labor is divided into three stages: First stage (cervical dilation), Second stage (pushing/delivery), and Third stage (placental delivery). Each has expected durations that help identify abnormal labor.

🔴 First Stage: Onset of Labor → Complete Dilation (10 cm)

Latent Phase:

  • Onset of regular contractions to 6 cm dilation
  • Slow cervical change
  • Duration: Highly variable (hours to days)
  • Prolonged latent phase: >20 hours (nulliparous), >14 hours (multiparous)

Active Phase:

  • 6 cm to complete dilation (10 cm)
  • Rapid cervical change
  • Expected: ≥1 cm/hour dilation
  • Arrest of dilation: No change for ≥4 hours with adequate contractions (or ≥6 hours without)

🟠 Second Stage: Complete Dilation → Delivery of Baby

  • Begins when cervix is fully dilated (10 cm)
  • Active pushing phase
  • Duration limits (with epidural):
    • Nulliparous: <4 hours
    • Multiparous: <3 hours
  • Without epidural: Subtract 1 hour from each

🟢 Third Stage: Delivery of Baby → Delivery of Placenta

  • Usually 5-30 minutes
  • Prolonged: >30 minutes → increased PPH risk
  • Active management: Oxytocin, cord traction, uterine massage

⚠️ Arrest Disorders
  • Arrest of dilation: ≥4 hours at ≥6 cm with adequate contractions
  • Arrest of descent: No descent for ≥1 hour in second stage with pushing
  • Failed induction: Unable to achieve active labor after adequate oxytocin and ROM
Consider: Amniotomy, oxytocin augmentation, or cesarean delivery

Cervical examination assesses dilation, effacement, station, consistency, and position. The Bishop Score predicts likelihood of successful induction.

🟢 Bishop Score Interpretation
  • ≥8: Favorable cervix - high success rate for induction
  • 6-7: Moderate - consider cervical ripening
  • <6: Unfavorable - cervical ripening recommended before oxytocin

Cervical Ripening Methods

📍 Mechanical Methods

  • Foley catheter balloon: 30-60 mL inflated above internal os
  • Laminaria: Hygroscopic dilators (rarely used)
  • Membrane stripping: Separating membranes from lower uterine segment

📍 Pharmacologic Methods

  • Misoprostol (Cytotec): PGE1 analog, vaginal or oral
  • Dinoprostone (Cervidil/Prepidil): PGE2, vaginal insert or gel
  • ⚠️ Contraindication: Prior cesarean or uterine surgery (rupture risk with prostaglandins)
🎯 Clinical Pearl: After prostaglandin ripening, wait appropriate interval before starting oxytocin (usually 4+ hours after misoprostol, remove Cervidil 30 min before) to reduce hyperstimulation risk.

⚕️ Labor Management

Labor management includes monitoring (maternal and fetal), pain control, and intervention when needed (augmentation, operative delivery).

Admission Criteria

  • Regular painful contractions with cervical change
  • Active labor: ≥6 cm dilation with regular contractions
  • Ruptured membranes
  • Significant bleeding
  • Decreased fetal movement or non-reassuring FHR

Routine Labor Orders

📍 Monitoring

  • Continuous fetal heart rate monitoring (or intermittent auscultation in low-risk)
  • Contraction monitoring (tocometry)
  • Vital signs q4h (q1h with epidural)
  • Cervical exams q2-4h (minimize if PROM)

📍 IV Access & Labs

  • IV access (18-20 gauge)
  • Type and screen
  • CBC if not recent
  • GBS status (treat if positive or unknown)

Labor Augmentation

🔵 Oxytocin (Pitocin)
  • Indicated for: Inadequate contractions, arrest of labor
  • Goal: 3-5 contractions per 10 minutes
  • Start low, increase gradually (institution-specific protocols)
  • Monitor for tachysystole (>5 contractions/10 min)
🔵 Amniotomy (AROM)
  • Artificial rupture of membranes
  • Can augment labor, allows internal monitoring
  • Confirm vertex presentation first (cord prolapse risk)
  • Note: Color (clear, meconium), amount, time

Operative Vaginal Delivery

📍 Indications

  • Prolonged second stage
  • Maternal exhaustion
  • Non-reassuring FHR in second stage
  • Maternal conditions limiting pushing (cardiac disease, etc.)

📍 Prerequisites

  • Fully dilated cervix
  • Ruptured membranes
  • Known fetal position
  • Adequate anesthesia
  • Station ≥+2 (outlet/low)

⚠️ Labor Complications

🔴 Umbilical Cord Prolapse
  • Definition: Cord descends ahead of presenting part after ROM
  • Risk factors: Malpresentation, polyhydramnios, preterm, multiparity
  • Management:
    • Call for help - EMERGENCY
    • Elevate presenting part (hand in vagina)
    • Knee-chest or Trendelenburg position
    • Fill bladder (500 mL saline) to elevate presenting part
    • Immediate cesarean delivery
    • Do NOT attempt to replace cord
🔴 Shoulder Dystocia
  • Definition: Head delivers but shoulders impacted
  • Risk factors: Macrosomia, diabetes, obesity, prolonged 2nd stage
  • Management (HELPERR mnemonic):
    • Help - call for assistance
    • Evaluate for episiotomy
    • Legs - McRoberts maneuver (hyperflexion)
    • Pressure - suprapubic (NOT fundal)
    • Enter - rotational maneuvers (Woods, Rubin)
    • Remove posterior arm
    • Roll to all-fours (Gaskin maneuver)
🔴 Uterine Rupture
  • Risk factors: Prior cesarean (especially classical), uterine surgery, oxytocin use
  • Signs:
    • Sudden severe abdominal pain
    • FHR abnormalities (prolonged bradycardia)
    • Loss of fetal station
    • Vaginal bleeding
    • Cessation of contractions
  • Management: Emergent cesarean, possible hysterectomy
🔴 Uterine Atony (Leading cause of PPH)
  • Risk factors: Prolonged labor, overdistension (twins, polyhydramnios), high parity, chorioamnionitis
  • Management:
    • Bimanual uterine massage
    • Oxytocin IV infusion
    • Methylergonovine (Methergine) - avoid in HTN
    • Carboprost (Hemabate) - avoid in asthma
    • Misoprostol PR
    • Tranexamic acid
    • Bakri balloon, B-Lynch suture, embolization, hysterectomy
💡 Causes of PPH: "4 T's"
Tone (atony - most common)
Trauma (lacerations, hematoma)
Tissue (retained placenta)
Thrombin (coagulopathy)

📝 Test Your Knowledge

Question 1

Active labor is defined as beginning at what cervical dilation?

A. 3 cm
B. 4 cm
C. 6 cm
D. 8 cm
Answer: C. Current guidelines define active labor as beginning at 6 cm dilation. This is when rapid cervical change is expected (≥1 cm/hour).

Question 2

A Bishop score of 4 indicates:

A. Unfavorable cervix - ripening recommended
B. Favorable cervix - proceed with oxytocin
C. Cesarean delivery indicated
D. Patient is in active labor
Answer: A. Bishop score <6 indicates an unfavorable cervix. Cervical ripening (mechanical or prostaglandins) is recommended before oxytocin induction.

Question 3

During shoulder dystocia, the FIRST maneuver after calling for help should be:

A. Fundal pressure
B. McRoberts maneuver
C. Zavanelli maneuver
D. Cesarean delivery
Answer: B. McRoberts (maternal hip hyperflexion) is the first-line maneuver for shoulder dystocia. It straightens the lumbosacral angle and increases pelvic diameter. NEVER apply fundal pressure (worsens impaction).

Question 4

The most common cause of postpartum hemorrhage is:

A. Uterine atony
B. Genital tract laceration
C. Retained placenta
D. Coagulopathy
Answer: A. Uterine atony accounts for 70-80% of PPH cases. Risk factors include prolonged labor, overdistension, and high parity. Treatment starts with massage and uterotonics.

Question 5

In cord prolapse, the immediate management includes all EXCEPT:

A. Elevating the presenting part manually
B. Placing patient in Trendelenburg
C. Attempting to replace the cord
D. Filling the bladder with saline
Answer: C. NEVER attempt to replace a prolapsed cord - this can cause vasospasm. Management is elevating the presenting part and emergent cesarean delivery.
