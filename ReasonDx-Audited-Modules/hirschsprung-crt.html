The contrast enema shows a narrow rectosigmoid segment with proximal dilation. A clear "transition zone" is visible where the caliber changes.

💡 Transition Zone

The transition zone marks the junction between ganglionic (dilated, normal) and aganglionic (narrow, abnormal) bowel. This is the key finding on contrast enema. NOTE: Do NOT prep the bowel before the study - it can obscure the transition zone!

🔑 Key Learning Points

  • Embryology: Neural crest cells fail to complete cranial-to-caudal migration → distal aganglionic segment
  • Always includes rectum: Migration failure means the most distal bowel is always affected
  • Pathophysiology: Absent ganglion cells → unopposed sympathetic tone → functional obstruction
  • "Squirt sign": Explosive decompression on rectal exam is classic
  • Contrast enema: Transition zone (narrow distal → dilated proximal). DON'T PREP!
  • Gold standard: Rectal suction biopsy - absent ganglion cells, hypertrophied nerves, ↑AChE
  • Treatment: Surgical resection + pull-through (Swenson, Duhamel, or Soave procedure)

⚠️ Complications to Know

  • Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis (HAEC): Life-threatening! Fever, diarrhea, abdominal distension. Treat with rectal irrigations + antibiotics.
  • Total colonic aganglionosis (TCA): ~5% of cases. Entire colon + variable small bowel affected.
  • Associations: Down syndrome (2-15% of Hirschsprung patients), MEN2B, Waardenburg syndrome

📚 Embryology Connection

Neural crest cells are incredibly versatile - they also form melanocytes, craniofacial bones, cardiac outflow tract, adrenal medulla, and more. Hirschsprung disease is a "neurocristopathy." The RET proto-oncogene is critical for neural crest migration and survival.
