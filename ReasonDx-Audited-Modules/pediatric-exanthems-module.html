🦠 Viral Exanthems

Measles (Rubeola) - 1st Disease

CauseMeasles virus (Paramyxovirus)
Prodrome3 C's: Cough, Coryza, Conjunctivitis + high fever
PathognomonicKoplik spots (white spots on buccal mucosa) - appear BEFORE rash
RashMaculopapular, starts at hairline/face → spreads downward (cephalocaudal)
ComplicationsOtitis media, pneumonia, encephalitis, SSPE (late)
TreatmentSupportive, Vitamin A (reduces mortality)
Highly contagious! Airborne transmission. Reportable disease. Isolate patient.

Rubella (German Measles) - 3rd Disease

CauseRubella virus (Togavirus)
ProdromeMild - low-grade fever, malaise
Key findingPosterior auricular & suboccipital lymphadenopathy
RashPink maculopapular, face → body (faster spread than measles, 3 days)
ComplicationCongenital Rubella Syndrome if in pregnancy (cataracts, deafness, heart defects)

Erythema Infectiosum (Fifth Disease)

CauseParvovirus B19
ProdromeMild URI symptoms, then afebrile when rash appears
Classic rash"Slapped cheek" appearance on face, then lacy/reticular rash on trunk and extremities
CourseRash may wax/wane with sun, heat, bathing for weeks
ComplicationsAplastic crisis (sickle cell), hydrops fetalis (pregnancy)
Not contagious once rash appears. Most infectious during prodrome.

Roseola (Exanthem Subitum) - 6th Disease

CauseHHV-6 (Human Herpesvirus 6)
Age6 months - 2 years (classic)
Key patternHIGH fever (up to 40°C) for 3-5 days → fever breaks → rash appears
RashRose-pink macules on trunk → spreads outward, fades in 1-2 days
ComplicationFebrile seizures (during fever phase)
"Roseola = Rash ON the way Out of fever"

Varicella (Chickenpox)

CauseVaricella-Zoster Virus (VZV)
ProdromeFever, malaise 1-2 days before rash
Rash"Dew drops on rose petal" - vesicles on erythematous base; starts on trunk
Key featureLesions in all stages (macules, papules, vesicles, crusts simultaneously)
ContagiousUntil all lesions crusted
ComplicationsSecondary bacterial infection, pneumonia, encephalitis, Reye syndrome (if aspirin)

Hand-Foot-Mouth Disease

CauseCoxsackievirus A16 (most common), Enterovirus 71
Age<5 years typically
FindingsOral ulcers (painful) + vesicles on palms, soles, buttocks
CourseSelf-limited, 7-10 days
TreatmentSupportive - pain control, hydration

🔬 Bacterial Rashes

Scarlet Fever - 2nd Disease

CauseGroup A Streptococcus (erythrogenic toxin)
PresentationPharyngitis + fever + rash
Rash"Sandpaper" texture, starts neck/axilla → generalizes, spares face
Key findingsStrawberry tongue, circumoral pallor, Pastia lines (creases)
DesquamationPeeling of fingertips/toes 1-2 weeks later
TreatmentPenicillin or amoxicillin × 10 days
Treat to prevent: Rheumatic fever, post-strep glomerulonephritis

Impetigo

CauseStaph aureus (bullous), Group A Strep (non-bullous)
Non-bullousVesicles → "honey-crusted" lesions, often perioral/nasal
BullousFlaccid bullae with clear fluid → rupture leaving thin brown crust
TreatmentTopical mupirocin (localized) or oral antibiotics (extensive)

Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS)

CauseStaph aureus exfoliative toxin
AgeNeonates and young children
PresentationFever, irritability → diffuse erythema → superficial skin sloughing
Key findingPositive Nikolsky sign (skin sloughs with gentle pressure)
vs TENSSSS = superficial split (granular layer), mucous membranes SPARED
TreatmentIV anti-staphylococcal antibiotics, wound care

Meningococcemia

Medical Emergency!
CauseNeisseria meningitidis
RashPetechiae/purpura - NON-BLANCHING
FeaturesRapid progression, fever, toxic appearance, hypotension
ComplicationPurpura fulminans, DIC, Waterhouse-Friderichsen (adrenal hemorrhage)
TreatmentImmediate IV ceftriaxone, supportive care

🔍 Other Important Rashes

Kawasaki Disease

Must diagnose to prevent coronary artery aneurysms!
CriteriaFever ≥5 days + 4 of 5 criteria:
"CRASH and Burn"
Conjunctivitis (bilateral, non-purulent)
Rash (polymorphous, trunk/extremities)
Adenopathy (cervical, >1.5cm, usually unilateral)
Strawberry tongue / lip changes (red, cracked lips)
Hand/foot changes (edema, erythema → desquamation)
TreatmentIVIG + high-dose aspirin → low-dose aspirin
WorkupEchocardiogram for coronary aneurysms

Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (IgA Vasculitis)

Age3-15 years, often after URI
Classic tetradPalpable purpura (buttocks/legs) + arthritis + abdominal pain + renal involvement
RashPalpable purpura on buttocks, lower extremities (gravity-dependent)
ComplicationsIntussusception, nephritis
TreatmentSupportive; steroids for severe GI/renal involvement

Erythema Multiforme

TriggersHSV (most common), Mycoplasma, drugs
RashTarget lesions (3 zones) - acral distribution (hands, feet)
vs SJS/TENEM minor = limited skin, no mucosa; EM major = mucosal involvement but less severe
TreatmentTreat underlying cause; supportive

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A child has high fever for 4 days, then the fever breaks and a rose-pink rash appears on the trunk. What is the diagnosis?

A. Measles
B. Roseola
C. Rubella
D. Fifth disease
Correct: B. Roseola (HHV-6) has the classic pattern of HIGH fever for 3-5 days, then fever breaks and rash appears. "Rash ON the way Out of fever."

Q2: What are Koplik spots pathognomonic for?

A. Measles
B. Rubella
C. Scarlet fever
D. Kawasaki disease
Correct: A. Koplik spots (white spots on buccal mucosa) are pathognomonic for measles and appear BEFORE the rash.

Q3: A child with pharyngitis develops a sandpaper-textured rash with circumoral pallor. What is the treatment?

A. Acyclovir
B. Penicillin
C. Supportive care only
D. IVIG
Correct: B. This is scarlet fever (GAS). Treatment with penicillin or amoxicillin prevents rheumatic fever and post-strep glomerulonephritis.

Q4: A child with fever for 6 days has bilateral conjunctivitis, cracked lips, and a polymorphous rash. What complication must you screen for?

A. Renal failure
B. Coronary artery aneurysms
C. Encephalitis
D. Hepatitis
Correct: B. This is Kawasaki disease. The major complication is coronary artery aneurysms. All patients need echocardiogram. Treatment is IVIG + aspirin.

Q5: A febrile child develops petechiae that don't blanch with pressure and appears toxic. What is your immediate action?

A. Observe and recheck in 24 hours
B. Oral antibiotics and discharge
C. Immediate IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone)
D. Dermatology consult
Correct: C. Non-blanching petechiae/purpura + fever + toxic appearance = meningococcemia until proven otherwise. This is a medical emergency requiring immediate IV antibiotics.
