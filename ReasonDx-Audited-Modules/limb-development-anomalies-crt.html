🔬 Normal Limb Development

Timeline:
  • Day 24-26: Upper limb buds appear
  • Day 28-30: Lower limb buds appear (4-5 days after upper)
  • Week 4-8: Limb patterning and differentiation
  • Week 8: Digits separated, limb rotation complete

Three Axes of Limb Development

AxisDirectionSignaling CenterKey Molecule
Proximal-Distal Shoulder → Fingers Apical Ectodermal Ridge (AER) FGFs
Anterior-Posterior Thumb → Pinky (radial-ulnar) Zone of Polarizing Activity (ZPA) Sonic Hedgehog (SHH)
Dorsal-Ventral Back of hand → Palm Dorsal ectoderm Wnt7a
Limb Development Centers:
AER (ridge) = proximal-distal (FGF)
ZPA (zone) = anterior-posterior (SHH)
Remember: "AER makes limbs longer, ZPA says which finger"

Digit Formation

Digits form by programmed cell death (apoptosis):
  • Hand starts as paddle-shaped plate
  • Interdigital mesenchyme undergoes apoptosis (BMP signaling)
  • Failure of apoptosis → syndactyly (webbed digits)

Limb Rotation

  • Upper limb: Rotates 90° laterally (extensors posterior)
  • Lower limb: Rotates 90° medially (extensors anterior)
  • Explains why elbow bends opposite direction to knee

✂️ Limb Reduction Defects

Terminology:
  • Amelia: Complete absence of limb
  • Meromelia: Partial absence of limb
  • Phocomelia: "Seal limb" - hands/feet attached to trunk
  • Hemimelia: Absence of part of limb segment

Types of Reduction Defects

TypeDescriptionExample
Transverse Amputation-like; all structures distal to level missing Congenital below-elbow amputation
Longitudinal Absence of one bone of a pair (radius/ulna or tibia/fibula) Radial ray deficiency

Radial Ray Deficiency (Radial Club Hand)

Spectrum: Absent/hypoplastic radius ± thumb
Clinical: Shortened forearm with radial deviation of hand
Associated syndromes:
  • TAR syndrome (Thrombocytopenia-Absent Radius)
  • VACTERL association
  • Fanconi anemia (check CBC!)
  • Holt-Oram syndrome (cardiac defects)
Thalidomide Embryopathy:
Classic cause of phocomelia (historical). Thalidomide disrupted limb bud development when taken days 20-36. Caused absence of proximal limb segments with hands/feet attached near trunk.

Amniotic Band Syndrome

Mechanism: Fibrous bands from ruptured amnion constrict fetal parts
Results: Limb constriction rings, amputation, syndactyly (atypical distribution)
Key feature: Asymmetric, random distribution (not following embryologic patterns)

✋ Digit Anomalies

Polydactyly (Extra Digits)

TypeLocationInheritanceAssociations
Preaxial Thumb/great toe side Often sporadic More likely associated with syndromes
Postaxial Little finger/toe side Autosomal dominant (common in African descent) Often isolated; Ellis-van Creveld, trisomy 13
Central Middle digits Rare Usually syndromic
Postaxial polydactyly = most common type, often benign
Preaxial polydactyly = think syndromes!

Syndactyly (Fused Digits)

TypeDescription
SimpleSkin/soft tissue fusion only
ComplexBone fusion
CompleteExtends to fingertips
IncompletePartial webbing

Mechanism: Failure of interdigital apoptosis (programmed cell death)

Most common: 3rd web space (middle-ring fingers)

Thumb Anomalies

  • Triphalangeal thumb: Three phalanges (normally 2); associated with Holt-Oram, VACTERL
  • Thumb hypoplasia/aplasia: Part of radial ray deficiency spectrum
  • Trigger thumb: Stenosing tenosynovitis (acquired, common in toddlers)

🧬 Syndromes with Limb Anomalies

SyndromeLimb FindingsOther Features
Holt-Oram Radial ray defects (thumb to absent radius), triphalangeal thumb Congenital heart defects (ASD, VSD); TBX5 gene
TAR Bilateral absent radii WITH thumbs present Thrombocytopenia; bleeding in infancy
VACTERL Radial/thumb anomalies Vertebral, Anal, Cardiac, TE fistula, Renal, Limb
Fanconi Anemia Radial ray defects, short stature Bone marrow failure, cancer predisposition
Poland Syndrome Unilateral symbrachydactyly, hypoplastic hand Absent pectoralis major (same side)
Apert Syndrome Complex syndactyly ("mitten hands") Craniosynostosis; FGFR2 mutation
Ellis-van Creveld Postaxial polydactyly, short limbs Cardiac defects, nail/teeth abnormalities
Trisomy 13 Postaxial polydactyly Holoprosencephaly, cleft lip, cardiac defects
TAR vs Fanconi vs Holt-Oram:
TAR: Absent radius BUT thumb PRESENT + thrombocytopenia
Fanconi: Radial defects + bone marrow failure (later onset)
Holt-Oram: Radial defects + congenital heart disease

Workup for Radial Ray Deficiency

  • CBC with platelet count (TAR, Fanconi)
  • Echocardiogram (Holt-Oram, VACTERL)
  • Renal ultrasound (VACTERL)
  • Spinal X-rays (VACTERL)
  • Chromosomal breakage study (Fanconi anemia)
  • Genetic testing as indicated

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: Which signaling center controls proximal-distal limb development?

A. Apical Ectodermal Ridge (AER)
B. Zone of Polarizing Activity (ZPA)
C. Dorsal ectoderm
D. Notochord
Correct: A. The AER (apical ectodermal ridge) at the tip of the limb bud controls proximal-distal growth through FGF signaling. The ZPA controls anterior-posterior patterning (thumb vs pinky) through Sonic Hedgehog.

Q2: What embryologic process, when disrupted, causes syndactyly?

A. AER signaling
B. Interdigital apoptosis
C. Limb rotation
D. ZPA signaling
Correct: B. Digits are separated by programmed cell death (apoptosis) of the interdigital mesenchyme. Failure of this apoptosis results in syndactyly (fused/webbed digits).

Q3: A newborn has bilateral absent radii but BOTH thumbs are present, along with severe thrombocytopenia. What is the diagnosis?

A. Holt-Oram syndrome
B. TAR syndrome
C. Fanconi anemia
D. VACTERL association
Correct: B. TAR (Thrombocytopenia-Absent Radius) syndrome has the unique feature of absent radii WITH thumbs present, plus thrombocytopenia. In other radial ray deficiencies, the thumb is typically absent or hypoplastic.

Q4: A baby has radial ray deficiency. What workup should be done to rule out Fanconi anemia?

A. Echocardiogram
B. Renal ultrasound
C. Chromosomal breakage study
D. Spinal X-ray
Correct: C. Fanconi anemia is diagnosed with chromosomal breakage studies (DEB or MMC). Cells from FA patients show increased chromosome breaks. This is important as FA leads to bone marrow failure and cancer predisposition.

Q5: Which type of polydactyly is most commonly an isolated, benign finding?

A. Preaxial (thumb side)
B. Postaxial (little finger side)
C. Central
D. All types equally
Correct: B. Postaxial polydactyly is the most common type and is often isolated, especially in individuals of African descent (autosomal dominant inheritance). Preaxial polydactyly is more commonly associated with syndromes.
