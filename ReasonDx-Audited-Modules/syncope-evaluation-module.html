🎯 Definition & Approach

Syncope = Transient loss of consciousness (TLOC) due to cerebral hypoperfusion, with rapid onset, short duration, and spontaneous complete recovery.

Key question: Is this cardiac or non-cardiac?

Orthostatic Hypotension

Definition: Drop in SBP ≥20 mmHg or DBP ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing

Causes:
  • Volume depletion (bleeding, dehydration)
  • Medications (antihypertensives, diuretics, nitrates, α-blockers, antidepressants)
  • Autonomic dysfunction (diabetes, Parkinson's, MSA)
  • Prolonged bed rest

Red Flags for Cardiac Syncope

High-risk features suggesting cardiac cause:
  • Syncope during exertion
  • Syncope while supine or sitting
  • Preceded by palpitations
  • Family history of sudden cardiac death or inherited cardiac disease
  • Known structural heart disease or heart failure
  • Abnormal ECG
  • No prodrome (sudden onset)
  • Age >60 with no clear trigger

🔬 Syncope Workup

Initial Evaluation (All Patients)

Core evaluation:
  1. History - triggers, prodrome, position, witnesses, recovery
  2. Physical exam - vitals, orthostatic BP, cardiac exam, neuro exam
  3. ECG - ALL patients (arrhythmia, ischemia, conduction, intervals)
This alone establishes diagnosis in ~50% of cases!

What NOT to Order Routinely

Low yield tests:
  • CT head (unless head trauma or focal neuro signs)
  • EEG (unless seizure suspected)
  • Carotid ultrasound (carotid stenosis doesn't cause syncope)
  • Extensive lab panel (CBC, BMP usually sufficient)

📊 Risk Stratification

San Francisco Syncope Rule (CHESS)

Predicts 7-day serious outcomes. If ANY positive → higher risk.
  • CHF history
  • Hematocrit <30%
  • ECG abnormal
  • Shortness of breath
  • Systolic BP <90 at triage

Disposition Guidelines

Safe for Discharge:
  • Classic vasovagal with clear trigger and prodrome
  • Situational syncope
  • Orthostatic hypotension (correctable cause)
  • Normal ECG, no cardiac history, young patient
Consider Admission:
  • Abnormal ECG
  • Known or suspected cardiac disease
  • Exertional syncope
  • Syncope causing injury
  • Family history of sudden death
  • Elderly with unexplained syncope
  • Frequent recurrent episodes

🎯 Clinical Reasoning Trainer

Case: 22-year-old with Syncope in Church

A 22-year-old woman fainted while standing in church. She reports feeling hot, lightheaded, and nauseated before losing consciousness. She was out for ~20 seconds and recovered quickly with no confusion. No palpitations, chest pain, or exertional symptoms. No family history of sudden death. Vitals normal, exam normal, ECG normal.

Based on the history and presentation, what is the most likely diagnosis?

Cardiac arrhythmia
Vasovagal syncope
Seizure
Orthostatic hypotension

What additional workup is needed?

Echocardiogram, Holter monitor, EP study
CT head and EEG
No additional workup needed at this time
Cardiac catheterization

What is the appropriate disposition?

Admit for cardiac monitoring
Discharge with education and reassurance
Observation unit for 23 hours
Summary: This is classic vasovagal syncope: young patient, clear trigger (prolonged standing in warm environment), prodrome (warmth, lightheadedness, nausea), brief episode with rapid recovery, no concerning features, normal ECG. No additional workup needed. Educate on triggers, avoidance, and counterpressure maneuvers. Safe for discharge.

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: Which feature is MOST concerning for cardiac syncope?

A. Preceded by nausea and warmth
B. Occurred during exercise
C. Triggered by prolonged standing
D. Followed by rapid complete recovery
Correct: B. Exertional syncope is a red flag for cardiac causes (HOCM, aortic stenosis, arrhythmia). Prodromal symptoms (nausea, warmth) and clear triggers suggest benign vasovagal syncope.

Q2: What test should ALL patients with syncope receive?

A. ECG
B. CT head
C. Echocardiogram
D. Tilt table test
Correct: A. An ECG is indicated for ALL patients with syncope to screen for arrhythmias, conduction abnormalities, and inherited conditions. History + exam + ECG establishes diagnosis in ~50% of cases.

Q3: What defines orthostatic hypotension?

A. SBP drop ≥10 mmHg on standing
B. SBP drop ≥20 mmHg or DBP drop ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing
C. Any symptomatic BP drop
D. SBP <90 mmHg on standing
Correct: B. Orthostatic hypotension is defined as a drop in SBP ≥20 mmHg OR DBP ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing from supine position.

Q4: A 70-year-old man has syncope while shaving his neck. What test would be most helpful?

A. Tilt table test
B. Carotid sinus massage
C. CT head
D. EEG
Correct: B. Syncope triggered by neck manipulation (shaving, tight collar, head turning) in an elderly patient suggests carotid sinus hypersensitivity. Carotid sinus massage (with continuous ECG monitoring) can confirm this.

Q5: Which of the following is NOT a typical feature of vasovagal syncope?

A. Prodrome of nausea and diaphoresis
B. Triggered by prolonged standing
C. Occurring while seated at rest
D. Rapid recovery without confusion
Correct: C. Vasovagal syncope typically has triggers (standing, heat, stress, pain) and prodrome. Syncope occurring while seated at rest without trigger or prodrome should raise concern for cardiac causes.
