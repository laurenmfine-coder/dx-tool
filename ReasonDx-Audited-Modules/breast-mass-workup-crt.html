🎯 Triple Assessment

The gold standard for breast mass evaluation requires THREE components:
  1. Clinical examination - History + physical exam
  2. Imaging - Mammography and/or ultrasound
  3. Tissue sampling - FNA, core biopsy, or excisional biopsy

All three must be concordant. If any component is discordant or suspicious, further workup is needed.

⚠️ Never assume a mass is benign based on clinical features alone!
Up to 10% of breast cancers feel "benign" on exam. Triple assessment is required for ALL palpable masses.

📋 Age-Based Workup Algorithm

Key Principle: Age determines first-line imaging
<30: Ultrasound first (dense breast tissue, low cancer risk)
≥30: Mammogram first (better for fatty tissue, higher cancer risk)

Age <30 Years

  1. Clinical exam
  2. Ultrasound - First-line imaging
    • Simple cyst → Reassure, follow
    • Complex cyst → Aspirate or biopsy
    • Solid mass → Core biopsy
  3. Add mammogram if US suspicious
  4. Biopsy any concerning finding
Why US first? Young patients have dense breast tissue that limits mammogram sensitivity. US also avoids radiation exposure.

Age ≥30 Years

  1. Clinical exam
  2. Diagnostic mammogram - First-line imaging
    • BI-RADS 1-2 → Low concern, may add US
    • BI-RADS 3 → Short-interval follow-up or biopsy
    • BI-RADS 4-5 → Biopsy required
  3. Ultrasound - Adjunct/targeted
  4. Biopsy per BI-RADS category
Why mammogram first? Better sensitivity in less-dense tissue, can detect microcalcifications, and higher cancer prevalence in this age group.

Biopsy Options

MethodBest ForLimitations
FNA (Fine Needle Aspiration)Cyst aspiration, cytology if massCannot distinguish invasive from in situ; high inadequate rate
Core Needle BiopsySolid masses, image-guidedGold standard; provides architecture for histology
Vacuum-Assisted BiopsyCalcifications, larger sample neededMore tissue; good for small lesions
Excisional BiopsyDiscordant results, high-risk lesionsSurgical procedure; may be diagnostic + therapeutic

Special Considerations

Palpable mass with negative imaging:
  • A palpable mass requires biopsy even if mammogram/US are negative
  • Imaging has false negatives (especially in dense breasts)
  • Consider MRI if clinical concern persists

📊 BI-RADS Classification

BI-RADS (Breast Imaging Reporting and Data System) standardizes mammography reporting and guides management based on malignancy risk.
Category 0: Incomplete
Malignancy risk: N/A
Need additional imaging (spot compression, US, prior films)
Action: Additional workup before assigning final category
Category 1: Negative
Malignancy risk: ~0%
No abnormality detected
Action: Routine screening
Category 2: Benign
Malignancy risk: ~0%
Definitely benign finding (calcified fibroadenoma, oil cyst, lipoma)
Action: Routine screening
Category 3: Probably Benign
Malignancy risk: ≤2%
Expected benign (non-calcified circumscribed solid mass, focal asymmetry, cluster of punctate calcifications)
Action: Short-interval follow-up (6 months) OR biopsy if patient prefers
Category 4: Suspicious
Malignancy risk: 2-95%
Subdivided into:
  • 4A: Low suspicion (2-10%) - e.g., palpable solid mass with some benign features
  • 4B: Moderate suspicion (10-50%) - e.g., indistinct margins
  • 4C: High suspicion (50-95%) - e.g., irregular spiculated mass
Action: Tissue diagnosis recommended (biopsy)
Category 5: Highly Suggestive of Malignancy
Malignancy risk: ≥95%
Classic malignant features (spiculated mass, fine linear branching calcifications)
Action: Biopsy required; may proceed with definitive treatment planning
Category 6: Known Malignancy
Malignancy risk: 100%
Biopsy-proven cancer, imaging done for extent of disease or treatment monitoring
Action: Appropriate treatment

📚 Breast Mass Differentials

Benign Conditions

ConditionAgeFeaturesManagement
Fibroadenoma 15-35 Rubbery, mobile ("breast mouse"), well-circumscribed, painless Observe if <2cm and classic features; excise if growing or >2cm
Fibrocystic changes 30-50 Bilateral, lumpy, cyclic pain, improves after menses Reassurance, supportive bra, evening primrose oil
Simple cyst 35-50 Round, mobile, may be tender, anechoic on US Aspiration if symptomatic; observe if not
Fat necrosis Any History of trauma/surgery, may have skin retraction Can mimic cancer; biopsy if uncertain
Intraductal papilloma 40-50 Bloody/serous nipple discharge, subareolar Excision (small cancer risk)
Breast abscess Lactating or smokers Painful, erythema, fever, fluctuant Antibiotics ± aspiration/I&D

High-Risk Lesions

These are not cancer but increase future cancer risk and usually require excision:
  • Atypical ductal hyperplasia (ADH) - 4-5x increased risk
  • Atypical lobular hyperplasia (ALH) - 4x increased risk
  • Lobular carcinoma in situ (LCIS) - Risk marker (8-10x), not precursor
  • Phyllodes tumor - Can be benign, borderline, or malignant; requires excision

Malignant Conditions

TypeFeaturesKey Points
Invasive ductal carcinoma (IDC) Hard, irregular, fixed, may have skin changes Most common (70-80%)
Invasive lobular carcinoma (ILC) Subtle thickening, often missed on mammogram Second most common (10-15%); often multifocal/bilateral
DCIS Usually non-palpable, detected by calcifications Pre-invasive; variable progression risk
Inflammatory breast cancer Erythema, peau d'orange, warm, no discrete mass Aggressive; dermal lymphatic invasion
Paget disease of nipple Eczematous nipple changes, scaling, erosion Associated with underlying DCIS or invasive cancer

Nipple Discharge

Concerning features (require workup):
  • Spontaneous (not expressed)
  • Unilateral
  • Single duct
  • Bloody or serous
Usually benign: Bilateral, milky, multiduct, with expression only

Case 1: 25-year-old with Breast Lump

A 25-year-old woman presents with a 2cm lump in her right breast that she noticed 1 month ago. It is mobile, rubbery, and non-tender. No nipple discharge, no skin changes. No family history of breast cancer.

Core biopsy confirms fibroadenoma. Patient asks about next steps. What do you recommend?

Observation with follow-up US in 6-12 months
Immediate surgical excision
Start tamoxifen
Radiation therapy
Case Summary: This is a classic presentation of fibroadenoma - the most common breast mass in young women. Key features: young age, mobile, rubbery, well-circumscribed on US. Despite reassuring features, triple assessment (exam + imaging + biopsy) is standard for palpable masses. Small fibroadenomas (<2cm) with benign biopsy can be observed; excision is offered if growing, symptomatic, or patient preference.

Case Summary: This is a classic presentation of breast cancer found on screening - spiculated mass + pleomorphic calcifications are highly suspicious (BI-RADS 5). Biopsy is required before treatment. Once IDC is confirmed, additional workup includes breast MRI (to assess extent of disease, look for multifocality), receptor testing (already done), genetic testing if indicated, and oncotype (for ER+ early-stage to guide chemotherapy decision). 🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A 28-year-old woman presents with a palpable breast mass. What is the most appropriate first imaging study?

A. Mammography
B. Breast ultrasound
C. Breast MRI
D. PET scan
Correct: B. In patients <30, ultrasound is first-line imaging. Young women have dense breast tissue that limits mammogram sensitivity, and they have lower breast cancer risk. US is also radiation-free.

Q2: BI-RADS 4 indicates:

A. Negative mammogram
B. Probably benign - recommend 6-month follow-up
C. Suspicious - biopsy recommended
D. Known malignancy
Correct: C. BI-RADS 4 = Suspicious abnormality with 2-95% malignancy risk (subdivided into 4A, 4B, 4C). Biopsy is indicated. BI-RADS 3 is probably benign (short-interval follow-up), and BI-RADS 5 is highly suspicious (≥95% risk).

Q3: Which finding on mammogram is most suspicious for malignancy?

A. Spiculated mass with fine linear branching calcifications
B. Well-circumscribed oval mass
C. Coarse "popcorn" calcifications
D. Oil cyst
Correct: A. Spiculated mass + fine linear branching calcifications (suggesting DCIS) are classic for malignancy. Circumscribed masses are usually benign. Popcorn calcifications are typical of fibroadenomas. Oil cysts are definitely benign.

Q4: A 45-year-old has a palpable breast mass but mammogram and ultrasound are negative. What should be done?

A. Reassurance - imaging rules out cancer
B. Proceed with biopsy despite negative imaging
C. Follow-up mammogram in 1 year
D. Breast MRI only if family history positive
Correct: B. A palpable mass requires biopsy regardless of imaging results. Imaging has false negatives, especially in dense breast tissue. Triple assessment (clinical + imaging + tissue) must be concordant; if any component is concerning (palpable mass), proceed with biopsy.

Q5: Which type of nipple discharge is most concerning for malignancy?

A. Bilateral milky discharge in a lactating woman
B. Unilateral spontaneous bloody discharge from a single duct
C. Bilateral green discharge with expression
D. Clear discharge from multiple ducts bilaterally
Correct: B. Concerning discharge is: Spontaneous, Unilateral, Single-duct, and Bloody or serous. This requires workup for intraductal papilloma or carcinoma. Bilateral, milky, multiduct discharge is usually physiologic or related to medications.
