📋 CDC Surgical Wound Classification

Purpose: Predicts surgical site infection (SSI) risk and guides antibiotic prophylaxis decisions.
Class I - Clean
SSI Risk: ~1-2%
  • Uninfected wound
  • No inflammation
  • Respiratory, GI, GU, biliary tracts NOT entered
  • Primary closure, no drains
Examples: Thyroidectomy, hernia repair (mesh), breast biopsy, carpal tunnel release
Antibiotics: Usually NOT required (except with implants)
Class II - Clean-Contaminated
SSI Risk: ~3-10%
  • Respiratory, GI, GU, or biliary tract entered UNDER CONTROLLED conditions
  • No unusual contamination
  • Minor break in sterile technique
Examples: Elective cholecystectomy, appendectomy (non-perforated), colon resection (with bowel prep), hysterectomy
Antibiotics: Single preoperative dose recommended
Class III - Contaminated
SSI Risk: ~10-20%
  • Open, fresh traumatic wounds
  • Major break in sterile technique
  • Gross GI spillage
  • Entry into GU/biliary tract with infected contents
Examples: Gunshot wound to abdomen, bile spillage during cholecystectomy, unprepared colon surgery
Antibiotics: Required (extended course)
Class IV - Dirty/Infected
SSI Risk: ~20-40%
  • Old traumatic wounds (>4-6 hours)
  • Clinical infection present preoperatively
  • Perforated viscera
  • Devitalized tissue, fecal contamination
Examples: Perforated appendicitis, abscess drainage, fecal peritonitis, necrotizing fasciitis
Antibiotics: Therapeutic course (treatment, not prophylaxis)

🔬 Wound Healing Phases

Phases of Wound Healing: "He Is Pretty Rude"
Hemostasis → Inflammation → Proliferation → Remodeling

Phase 1: Hemostasis (Minutes)

  • Vasoconstriction
  • Platelet aggregation and plug formation
  • Clotting cascade activation → fibrin clot
Key cells: Platelets
Key mediators: Thrombin, fibrin, TGF-β, PDGF

Phase 2: Inflammation (Days 1-4)

  • Vasodilation, increased permeability
  • Neutrophils arrive first (peak 24-48 hrs) - clear bacteria
  • Macrophages follow (48-72 hrs) - phagocytosis + orchestrate healing
  • Signs: rubor, calor, tumor, dolor
Key cells: Neutrophils → Macrophages
Key mediators: IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α, prostaglandins

Phase 3: Proliferation (Days 4-21)

  • Angiogenesis - new blood vessels
  • Granulation tissue - fibroblasts + new vessels
  • Collagen synthesis - Type III initially
  • Epithelialization - keratinocyte migration
  • Wound contraction - myofibroblasts
Key cells: Fibroblasts, endothelial cells, keratinocytes
Key mediators: VEGF, FGF, TGF-β

Phase 4: Remodeling (3 weeks - 1+ years)

  • Type III collagen → Type I collagen
  • Collagen crosslinking and reorganization
  • Wound strength increases (max ~80% of original at 3 months)
  • Scar maturation
Key cells: Fibroblasts
Key mediators: MMPs (matrix metalloproteinases)

Factors That Impair Wound Healing

Local Factors:
  • Infection
  • Ischemia/hypoxia
  • Foreign bodies
  • Radiation injury
  • Mechanical stress
  • Repeated trauma
Systemic Factors:
  • Diabetes (microvascular disease, neuropathy)
  • Malnutrition (especially protein, vitamin C, zinc)
  • Smoking (vasoconstriction, hypoxia)
  • Steroids (impair inflammation)
  • Chemotherapy
  • Advanced age
  • Immunodeficiency

🧵 Wound Closure Techniques

Closure Timing

TypeDefinitionWhen to Use
Primary (1°) Immediate closure Clean/clean-contaminated wounds, minimal tissue loss, good blood supply
Secondary (2°) Left open to heal by granulation Contaminated/infected wounds, abscess cavities, tissue loss
Tertiary (Delayed Primary) Left open, then closed in 3-5 days Contaminated wounds after infection controlled, war wounds

Suture Selection

Key Principles:
  • Match suture strength to tissue strength
  • Use absorbable for deep layers, permanent for skin (or absorbable for subcuticular)
  • Use monofilament in infected/contaminated wounds (braided harbors bacteria)
  • Fascia needs long-lasting suture (slow absorbing or permanent)
Suture TypeAbsorptionCommon Uses
Chromic gut~2-3 weeksMucosa, GI anastomoses
Vicryl (polyglactin)~8-10 weeksSubcutaneous, fascia (not best), general
PDS (polydioxanone)~6 monthsFascia (preferred), tendons
Monocryl~3-4 monthsSubcuticular skin closure
Prolene (polypropylene)Non-absorbableSkin, vascular, running subcuticular
Nylon (Ethilon)Non-absorbableSkin closure
SilkNon-absorbable*Vessel ligation, ties (*degrades slowly)

Fascia Closure

Critical Points:
  • Use slowly absorbing (PDS) or permanent suture
  • Mass closure (all layers except skin) is acceptable
  • 4:1 suture length to wound length ratio
  • Bites 1cm from edge, 1cm apart
  • Continuous > interrupted for midline laparotomy

Staples vs Sutures

Staples:
  • Faster placement
  • Good for scalp, trunk, extremities
  • Lower infection rate (less handling)
  • Worse cosmesis
Sutures:
  • Better cosmesis (face)
  • More precise approximation
  • Required for mucosa, tendons
  • More time-consuming

⚠️ Wound Complications

Surgical Site Infection (SSI)

Classification:
  • Superficial incisional: Skin/subcutaneous within 30 days
  • Deep incisional: Fascia/muscle within 30-90 days
  • Organ/space: Deep to fascia, any organ manipulated
Risk FactorMechanism
DiabetesImpaired neutrophil function, microvascular disease
ObesityPoor tissue perfusion, larger dead space
MalnutritionImpaired immune function, healing
SmokingTissue hypoxia, impaired macrophage function
ImmunosuppressionImpaired immune response
Prolonged surgeryTissue trauma, bacterial exposure time
Emergency surgeryUnable to optimize patient, contamination

Dehiscence & Evisceration

Dehiscence: Separation of wound layers (partial or complete)
Evisceration: Protrusion of bowel through dehisced wound

Risk factors: Malnutrition, steroids, infection, poor technique, increased intra-abdominal pressure (cough, vomiting), diabetes, obesity

Classic presentation: "Salmon-colored" (serosanguinous) drainage POD 5-8

Evisceration management:
  1. Cover bowel with saline-soaked sterile gauze
  2. Keep patient NPO
  3. IV fluids, antibiotics
  4. Emergent return to OR for closure

Seroma & Hematoma

Seroma:
  • Collection of serous fluid
  • Common after mastectomy, hernia repair
  • Management: Observation, serial aspiration
  • Prevention: Drains, compression
Hematoma:
  • Collection of blood
  • Increases infection risk
  • May compromise flaps/grafts
  • Management: Small - observe; Large - OR evacuation

Abnormal Scarring

TypeDefinitionTreatment
Hypertrophic scar Raised but WITHIN wound borders; may regress Silicone sheets, pressure, steroid injection, laser
Keloid EXTENDS BEYOND wound borders; does not regress Steroid injection, excision + radiation (high recurrence)

Bite Wounds

Dog Bites:
  • Pasteurella multocida (most common)
  • Strep, Staph, anaerobes
  • Can usually close (except hand/foot)
  • Augmentin prophylaxis for high-risk
Cat Bites:
  • Pasteurella (very common, ~80%)
  • Deep puncture wounds
  • Higher infection rate than dog bites
  • Do NOT close; Augmentin prophylaxis
Human Bites:
  • Eikenella corrodens + mixed oral flora
  • "Fight bite" - clenched fist vs teeth, often over MCP joint
  • Very high infection risk
  • Do NOT close; Augmentin; explore for tendon/joint involvement

Diabetic Foot Ulcers

Management Principles:
  1. Offloading - Total contact cast, special footwear
  2. Debridement - Sharp debridement of necrotic tissue
  3. Infection control - Antibiotics if infected, wound cultures
  4. Vascular assessment - ABI, consider revascularization
  5. Glycemic control - Optimize blood sugar
  6. Moist wound healing - Appropriate dressings

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: An elective cholecystectomy with no bile spillage is classified as which wound class?

A. Class I - Clean
B. Class II - Clean-contaminated
C. Class III - Contaminated
D. Class IV - Dirty
Correct: B. Clean-contaminated means a hollow viscus (biliary tract) was entered under controlled conditions without spillage. If there had been bile spillage, it would be Class III (contaminated).

Q2: Which cell type is most critical for orchestrating wound healing and arrives at the wound around 48-72 hours?

A. Neutrophils
B. Macrophages
C. Fibroblasts
D. Platelets
Correct: B. Macrophages are considered the "conductor" of wound healing. They arrive after neutrophils (which peak at 24-48 hrs), phagocytose debris, and release growth factors that coordinate the transition to proliferation.

Q3: A patient is POD 6 from laparotomy. The nurse notes "salmon-colored" drainage soaking the dressing. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Wound dehiscence
B. Superficial SSI
C. Seroma
D. Normal postoperative drainage
Correct: A. "Salmon-colored" (serosanguinous) drainage around POD 5-8 is the classic presentation of fascial dehiscence. The peritoneal fluid mixing with blood creates this characteristic appearance. This requires urgent assessment and likely return to OR.

Q4: Which suture is BEST for fascial closure?

A. Chromic gut
B. Vicryl
C. PDS (polydioxanone)
D. Plain gut
Correct: C. PDS is slowly absorbable (~6 months) and maintains strength long enough for fascia to heal adequately. Fascia achieves about 80% of its strength at 3 months, so it needs long-lasting suture support. Gut and Vicryl lose strength too quickly.

Q5: A cat bite to the hand occurred 6 hours ago. What is the MOST appropriate management?

A. Irrigation and primary closure
B. Irrigation, leave open, and prophylactic Augmentin
C. Irrigation, closure, no antibiotics needed
D. Debridement and skin grafting
Correct: B. Cat bites have high infection rates (~80% Pasteurella) due to deep puncture wounds. Management: thorough irrigation, do NOT close (especially hand/foot), prophylactic antibiotics (Augmentin covers Pasteurella + oral flora), and close follow-up.

Q6: What distinguishes a keloid from a hypertrophic scar?

A. Keloids extend beyond the original wound borders
B. Keloids regress over time
C. Hypertrophic scars are more common in darker skin
D. Keloids stay within wound borders
Correct: A. The key distinction: Keloids grow BEYOND the original wound margins and do not regress. Hypertrophic scars stay WITHIN wound borders and may regress over time. Both are more common in patients with darker skin tones.
