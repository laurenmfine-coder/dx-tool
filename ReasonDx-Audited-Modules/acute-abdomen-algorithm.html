🎯 What is an Acute Abdomen?

Definition: Sudden onset of severe abdominal pain requiring urgent evaluation for potential surgical intervention. Time-sensitive diagnosis is critical.

The Critical Question

Every acute abdomen evaluation asks: "Does this patient need surgery NOW?"

🔴 Surgical Emergencies - "PIVO"
Perforation (free air) • Ischemia (mesenteric, strangulated hernia)
Vascular catastrophe (AAA rupture) • Obstruction (complete, with signs of strangulation)

Step 1: Is the patient hemodynamically UNSTABLE?
(Hypotension, tachycardia, altered mental status, signs of shock)
🚨 UNSTABLE PATIENT - Immediate Action Required
  • Large bore IV access x2, fluid resuscitation
  • Type & crossmatch, activate massive transfusion if needed
  • Bedside FAST exam or portable imaging ONLY
  • Emergent surgical consultation
Most Likely Unstable Etiologies?
Top Diagnoses in Unstable Acute Abdomen:
  • Ruptured AAA - pulsatile mass, back/flank pain, elderly
  • Mesenteric ischemia - pain out of proportion, AF history
  • Perforated viscus - rigid abdomen, free air
  • Ruptured ectopic - female, positive pregnancy test
  • Splenic rupture - trauma, LUQ pain, Kehr sign

→ OR without delay for confirmed surgical emergency

↓ Stable Patient
Step 2: Signs of PERITONITIS?
(Rigid abdomen, involuntary guarding, rebound, patient lying still)
⚠️ Peritonitis Present - Urgent Surgical Evaluation
Get Upright CXR or CT: Free Air?
FREE AIR = Perforated Viscus
Most common: Perforated peptic ulcer, diverticulitis, appendicitis
→ NPO, IV fluids, antibiotics, emergent surgery
No Free Air but Peritonitis:
Consider: Appendicitis, cholecystitis, pancreatitis, strangulated hernia
→ CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast for diagnosis
↓ No Peritonitis

Step 4: LOCALIZE the Pain
Use quadrant-based differential (see Quadrants tab)
Proceed with focused workup based on location:
  • RUQ → RUQ ultrasound (biliary)
  • RLQ → CT A/P (appendicitis)
  • LLQ → CT A/P (diverticulitis)
  • Epigastric → Lipase, consider CT
  • Suprapubic → UA, pelvic exam, US

📍 Pain by Location

Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ)

  • Biliary colic - postprandial, colicky
  • Acute cholecystitis - Murphy sign +
  • Cholangitis - Charcot triad (fever, jaundice, RUQ pain)
  • Hepatitis - tender hepatomegaly
  • Hepatic abscess
  • Perforated duodenal ulcer
  • Right lower lobe pneumonia
  • Fitz-Hugh-Curtis - perihepatitis

First test: RUQ ultrasound

Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ)

  • Splenic pathology - rupture, infarct, abscess
  • Gastric ulcer
  • Pancreatitis - radiates to back
  • Left lower lobe pneumonia
  • MI - inferior wall

Kehr sign (referred shoulder pain) = splenic pathology

Right Lower Quadrant (RLQ)

  • Acute appendicitis - McBurney point, Rovsing, psoas, obturator signs
  • Mesenteric adenitis - children, post-viral
  • Meckel diverticulitis
  • Cecal diverticulitis
  • Crohn disease - terminal ileum
  • Ovarian pathology - torsion, cyst rupture, ectopic
  • Inguinal hernia

Classic: Periumbilical → RLQ migration = appendicitis

Left Lower Quadrant (LLQ)

  • Diverticulitis - "left-sided appendicitis"
  • Sigmoid volvulus
  • Ovarian pathology
  • Inguinal hernia
  • Constipation/fecal impaction
  • IBD flare

Diverticulitis: elderly, fever, leukocytosis

⚠️ Recognizing Peritonitis

Peritonitis = Inflammation of the peritoneal lining
Can be localized (early appendicitis) or generalized (perforated viscus)

Clinical Signs

SignDescriptionTechnique
Involuntary guardingReflex muscle contractionPalpate while patient distracted
Rigidity"Board-like" abdomenCannot indent abdominal wall
Rebound tendernessPain worse on releasePress slowly, release quickly
Percussion tendernessPain with light percussionGentle tapping causes pain
Cough testPain with coughingAsk patient to cough
The Still Patient: Patients with peritonitis lie very still because any movement worsens pain. They avoid coughing, breathing deeply, or being jostled (unlike renal colic patients who writhe in pain).

Causes of Free Air (Pneumoperitoneum)

Surgical causes of free air - most to least common:
Perforated peptic ulcer > Perforated diverticulitis > Perforated appendix > Trauma > Bowel ischemia
Non-surgical causes of free air (don't always need OR):
  • Recent abdominal surgery (normal up to 7 days post-op)
  • Recent endoscopy with perforation
  • Peritoneal dialysis
  • Pneumatosis intestinalis (may be benign)
  • Barotrauma from mechanical ventilation

When to Skip CT and Go to OR

  • Hemodynamically unstable with obvious surgical abdomen
  • Free air on upright CXR with peritonitis
  • Incarcerated/strangulated hernia
  • Ruptured AAA (confirmed by bedside US)
  • Gunshot wound to abdomen

CT Findings You Must Know

Bowel Obstruction

  • Dilated proximal loops (>3cm small bowel)
  • Decompressed distal loops
  • Transition point
  • "Small bowel feces" sign
  • Strangulation: poor enhancement, mesenteric haziness

Appendicitis

  • Appendix >6mm diameter
  • Wall enhancement
  • Periappendiceal fat stranding
  • Appendicolith (25%)
  • Perforation: abscess, extraluminal air

Mesenteric Ischemia

  • Bowel wall thickening or paper-thin
  • Pneumatosis intestinalis
  • Portal venous gas
  • SMA/SMV thrombus
  • Mesenteric fat stranding

Perforation

  • Free intraperitoneal air
  • Extraluminal contrast
  • Focal bowel wall defect
  • Adjacent inflammatory changes
  • Free fluid

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Case 1: A 68-year-old man with atrial fibrillation presents with severe diffuse abdominal pain for 6 hours. He rates it 10/10. On exam, his abdomen is soft with minimal tenderness. Labs show WBC 18,000 and lactate 4.2. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Mesenteric ischemia
B. Small bowel obstruction
C. Acute pancreatitis
D. Perforated peptic ulcer
Correct: A. Classic mesenteric ischemia presentation: "Pain out of proportion to physical exam" - severe pain but minimal findings. AF is a major risk factor for arterial embolism to SMA. Elevated lactate confirms tissue ischemia.

Case 2: A 45-year-old woman presents with sudden-onset severe epigastric pain radiating to her back. She is lying very still and has a rigid abdomen with diffuse guarding. Upright CXR shows air under the diaphragm. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Acute pancreatitis
B. Perforated peptic ulcer
C. Acute cholecystitis
D. Ruptured AAA
Correct: B. Free air under diaphragm = perforated viscus. Epigastric location + rigid abdomen + radiating to back is classic for perforated peptic ulcer. She needs emergent surgery. Pancreatitis does NOT cause free air.

Case 3: A 72-year-old man with history of diverticulosis presents with LLQ pain, fever, and leukocytosis. CT shows sigmoid wall thickening with pericolic fat stranding but no free air or abscess. What is the appropriate initial management?

A. Emergent sigmoid colectomy
B. IV antibiotics, bowel rest, serial exams
C. Colonoscopy to rule out malignancy
D. CT-guided drainage
Correct: B. This is uncomplicated diverticulitis (no abscess, perforation, or fistula). Initial management is conservative: NPO/clear liquids, IV antibiotics covering gram-negatives and anaerobes, and close monitoring. Surgery reserved for complications or recurrence. Colonoscopy done 6-8 weeks later to rule out malignancy.

Case 4: Which physical exam finding is MOST specific for peritonitis?

A. Voluntary guarding
B. Involuntary guarding
C. Hyperactive bowel sounds
D. Abdominal distension
Correct: B. Involuntary guarding (reflex muscle contraction that the patient cannot suppress) is the most specific sign of peritoneal irritation. Voluntary guarding can occur with anxiety or anticipation of pain. Hyperactive bowel sounds suggest early obstruction. Distension is nonspecific.

Case 5: A 25-year-old woman presents with RLQ pain, nausea, and anorexia. Her pain started periumbilically 12 hours ago and has now localized to the RLQ. Pregnancy test is negative. This pain migration pattern is most consistent with:

A. Acute appendicitis
B. Ovarian torsion
C. Ruptured ovarian cyst
D. Mesenteric adenitis
Correct: A. The classic pain migration from periumbilical (visceral pain from appendix distension) to RLQ (parietal pain from peritoneal irritation as inflammation progresses) is highly suggestive of appendicitis. Ovarian pathology typically presents with sudden-onset localized pain without migration.
