👂 Peripheral Vestibular Disorders

BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)

Most common cause of vertigo

Mechanism: Otoconia (crystals) displaced into semicircular canal
Location: Posterior canal most common (90%)

Features:
  • Brief episodes (seconds to <1 min)
  • Triggered by head position changes (rolling over, looking up)
  • Fatigable (lessens with repetition)
  • NO hearing loss or tinnitus

Dix-Hallpike Test

Diagnostic for posterior canal BPPV:
Patient sits, turn head 45° to one side, rapidly lay back with head hanging

Positive: Upbeat-torsional nystagmus after brief latency (2-5 sec), lasting <1 min, fatigable

Treatment: Epley Maneuver

Canalith repositioning procedure for posterior canal BPPV
~80% effective with single treatment; may need repetition

Vestibular Neuritis

Acute unilateral vestibular loss (viral or post-viral)

Features:
  • Sudden onset severe vertigo lasting days
  • Continuous (not episodic)
  • N/V, unsteady gait
  • NO hearing loss (if hearing loss present → labyrinthitis)
Treatment: Supportive, short-term vestibular suppressants, vestibular rehab

Meniere's Disease

Endolymphatic hydrops

Classic triad (+ aural fullness):
  • Episodic vertigo (20 min to hours)
  • Fluctuating sensorineural hearing loss (low frequency initially)
  • Tinnitus
  • Aural fullness
Treatment: Low-sodium diet, diuretics, vestibular rehab; severe cases may need intratympanic steroids/gentamicin or surgery

🧠 Central Causes

Always consider stroke in acute vertigo!
Posterior circulation strokes can present with isolated vertigo

Posterior Circulation Stroke

Risk factors: Age >50, vascular risk factors, sudden onset

Concerning features:
  • Severe imbalance (can't walk)
  • Vertical or direction-changing nystagmus
  • New headache
  • Any focal neurologic signs
  • Negative HINTS exam (see next tab)
Note: CT is often NEGATIVE for posterior fossa strokes early; MRI more sensitive but can still miss

Cerebellar Infarction/Hemorrhage

Neurosurgical emergency if large!
Risk of herniation and brainstem compression
Watch for: deteriorating consciousness, new cranial nerve signs

Vestibular Migraine

Common but underdiagnosed

Features:
  • Recurrent episodes of vertigo (minutes to days)
  • History of migraine
  • Migrainous features (headache, photophobia, aura) during some episodes
  • Exclusion of other causes
Treatment: Migraine prophylaxis (topiramate, propranolol, amitriptyline)

🔍 HINTS Exam

HINTS = Head Impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew

For acute continuous vertigo - distinguishes peripheral from central
More sensitive than early MRI for stroke!

Components

TestPeripheral (Benign)Central (Dangerous)
Head Impulse (HIT) ABNORMAL (corrective saccade) Normal (no saccade)
Nystagmus Unidirectional, horizontal-torsional Direction-changing or vertical
Test of Skew Negative (no skew) Positive (vertical misalignment)
"INFARCT" = Impulse Normal, Fast-phase Alternating, Refixation on Cover Test
Any ONE of these suggests central cause → emergent workup!

Head Impulse Test (HIT)

  • Patient focuses on examiner's nose
  • Rapidly turn patient's head to one side
  • Normal: Eyes stay fixed on target
  • Abnormal: Catch-up saccade (eyes move with head, then snap back)
Abnormal HIT = peripheral lesion (good news!)
Normal HIT in acute vertigo = concerning for central cause

Test of Skew (Alternate Cover Test)

  • Patient focuses on target
  • Cover one eye, then quickly move cover to other eye
  • Positive: Vertical correction movement when uncovered
Positive skew = concerning for brainstem lesion

When to Use HINTS

Use for: Acute vestibular syndrome (continuous vertigo, nystagmus, N/V, gait instability)

Do NOT use for: Episodic vertigo, BPPV, presyncope, or if patient is asymptomatic at time of exam

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A patient has vertigo lasting seconds triggered by rolling over in bed. Most likely diagnosis?

A. BPPV
B. Meniere's disease
C. Vestibular neuritis
Correct: A. BPPV causes brief (seconds) episodes triggered by position changes. Meniere's lasts minutes-hours; vestibular neuritis is continuous for days.

Q2: In acute vertigo, a NORMAL head impulse test suggests:

A. Benign peripheral cause
B. Possible central (dangerous) cause
C. BPPV
Correct: B. In acute vestibular syndrome, normal HIT suggests the peripheral vestibular system is intact, so the problem may be central (stroke). Abnormal HIT = peripheral = benign.

Q3: Meniere's disease classically includes all EXCEPT:

A. Episodic vertigo
B. Fluctuating hearing loss
C. Bilateral symptoms at onset
Correct: C. Meniere's is classically unilateral with vertigo, hearing loss, tinnitus, and aural fullness. Bilateral involvement can occur but is not typical at onset.

Q4: Which nystagmus pattern suggests a central cause?

A. Horizontal, unidirectional
B. Vertical or direction-changing
C. Suppressed by visual fixation
Correct: B. Vertical or direction-changing nystagmus suggests central pathology. Peripheral nystagmus is typically horizontal-torsional, unidirectional, and suppressed by fixation.

Q5: Treatment for posterior canal BPPV is:

A. Epley maneuver
B. Meclizine
C. Prednisone
Correct: A. The Epley maneuver (canalith repositioning) is first-line for posterior canal BPPV. Vestibular suppressants like meclizine only treat symptoms and may delay recovery.
