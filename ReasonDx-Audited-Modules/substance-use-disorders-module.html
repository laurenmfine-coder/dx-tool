🎯 DSM-5 Substance Use Disorder Criteria

SUD diagnosis requires ≥2 criteria in past 12 months:
Mild: 2-3 criteria | Moderate: 4-5 criteria | Severe: ≥6 criteria
SUD Criteria: "WITHDRAWALS"
Withdrawal • Impaired control (larger/longer than intended)
Tolerance • Hazardous use • Desire to cut down (unsuccessful)
Role obligations neglected • Activities given up
Work/time spent obtaining/using/recovering • Amplified craving
Legal problems* • Social/interpersonal problems

*Legal problems removed in DSM-5; craving added

CIWA-Ar Score

Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol - guides treatment
Scores: Nausea/vomiting, tremor, paroxysmal sweats, anxiety, agitation, tactile/auditory/visual disturbances, headache, orientation

<10: Mild, may not need meds
10-18: Moderate withdrawal
>18: Severe - high risk for DTs/seizures

Treatment

Acute Withdrawal:
  • Benzodiazepines - first-line (chlordiazepoxide, diazepam, lorazepam)
  • Symptom-triggered dosing (CIWA-guided) preferred over fixed-schedule
  • Lorazepam preferred in liver failure (no hepatic metabolism)
  • Thiamine - give BEFORE glucose (prevent Wernicke's)
  • Folate, multivitamins
  • Correct electrolytes (especially Mg, K, phos)

Medications for AUD (Maintenance)

Wernicke-Korsakoff

Wernicke encephalopathy (acute, reversible with treatment):
Classic triad: Confusion, Ataxia, Ophthalmoplegia (nystagmus, CN VI palsy)
Treatment: High-dose IV thiamine BEFORE glucose

Korsakoff syndrome (chronic, often irreversible):
Anterograde/retrograde amnesia, confabulation

💊 Opioid Use Disorder

Intoxication

Classic Triad: Miosis (pinpoint pupils), respiratory depression, CNS depression

Other signs: Euphoria, drowsiness, constipation, hypotension, bradycardia

Overdose treatment:
  • Naloxone (Narcan) - opioid antagonist; 0.4-2mg IV/IM/IN, repeat q2-3min
  • Titrate to respiratory status (not consciousness)
  • May precipitate withdrawal - start low
  • Half-life shorter than most opioids - may need repeat dosing or infusion

Withdrawal

NOT life-threatening but extremely uncomfortable ("flu-like")

Timeline: Starts 8-24 hours after last use (shorter-acting = faster onset)
Peak: 36-72 hours
Duration: 5-10 days

Symptoms: Anxiety, restlessness, myalgias, lacrimation, rhinorrhea, yawning, piloerection ("gooseflesh"), dilated pupils, nausea/vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal cramps
Opioid Withdrawal: "YAWNS"
Yawning • Aches (muscle) • Watery eyes/nose
Nausea/vomiting • Sweating, stomach cramps

Cocaine Chest Pain:
  • Causes coronary vasospasm and accelerated atherosclerosis
  • Avoid beta-blockers (unopposed alpha stimulation → worse vasospasm)
  • Treatment: Benzodiazepines, nitrates, aspirin, CCBs if needed
  • Consider phentolamine for refractory hypertension

Amphetamines/Methamphetamine

Similar to cocaine but longer duration (hours vs minutes)

Intoxication: Same as cocaine + bruxism, formication ("meth mites")
Chronic use: "Meth mouth" (dental decay), skin picking, weight loss, paranoia
Withdrawal: Same as cocaine - crash, depression, fatigue, hyperphagia
Treatment: Supportive; no FDA-approved medications for stimulant use disorder

Stimulant Intoxication Treatment

  • Benzodiazepines - first-line for agitation, seizures, cardiovascular symptoms
  • Cooling measures for hyperthermia
  • Antipsychotics for severe psychosis (careful - lower seizure threshold)
  • Avoid beta-blockers in cocaine intoxication

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A chronic heavy drinker presents 48 hours after his last drink with confusion, visual hallucinations, tremor, fever, and tachycardia. What is the diagnosis?

A. Alcoholic hallucinosis
B. Delirium tremens
C. Wernicke encephalopathy
D. Korsakoff syndrome
Correct: B. Delirium tremens occurs 48-96 hours after last drink and includes confusion (delirium), hallucinations, autonomic instability (fever, tachycardia), and agitation. This is a medical emergency with 5-15% mortality if untreated.

Q2: What should be given BEFORE glucose in a patient with suspected alcohol use disorder?

A. Thiamine
B. Folate
C. Naloxone
D. Flumazenil
Correct: A. Glucose can precipitate or worsen Wernicke encephalopathy in thiamine-deficient patients by depleting remaining thiamine stores. Always give thiamine before (or with) glucose.

Q3: A patient on cocaine presents with chest pain. What should you AVOID?

A. Benzodiazepines
B. Aspirin
C. Beta-blockers
D. Nitroglycerin
Correct: C. Beta-blockers are contraindicated in cocaine-induced chest pain because they cause unopposed alpha-adrenergic stimulation, worsening coronary vasoconstriction and hypertension.

Q4: Which medication for opioid use disorder is a partial agonist that can be prescribed in an office setting?

A. Methadone
B. Buprenorphine
C. Naltrexone
D. Naloxone
Correct: B. Buprenorphine is a partial opioid agonist that can be prescribed in office settings. Methadone (full agonist) requires daily dispensing at specialized clinics. Naltrexone is an antagonist.

Q5: Which withdrawal syndrome is NOT life-threatening?

A. Alcohol withdrawal
B. Benzodiazepine withdrawal
C. Opioid withdrawal
D. Barbiturate withdrawal
Correct: C. Opioid withdrawal is extremely uncomfortable but NOT life-threatening (unlike alcohol, benzodiazepine, and barbiturate withdrawal which can cause fatal seizures). However, opioid withdrawal can lead to relapse and overdose.
