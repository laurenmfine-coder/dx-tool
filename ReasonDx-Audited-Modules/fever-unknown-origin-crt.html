🎯 Definition

Classic FUO Definition (Petersdorf & Beeson):
  • Temperature >38.3°C (101°F) on several occasions
  • Duration >3 weeks
  • No diagnosis after 1 week of inpatient investigation
    OR 3+ outpatient visits OR 3 days in hospital

🔬 Systematic Workup

Step 1: Thorough History

Critical history elements:
  • Travel (endemic infections)
  • Animal/pet exposure (zoonoses)
  • Occupational exposures
  • Sexual history, HIV risk factors
  • Medication list (drug fever)
  • Family history (Mediterranean fever, inflammatory conditions)
  • Dental procedures (endocarditis)
  • Prosthetic devices, recent surgery
  • TB exposure, prior PPD status

Step 2: Physical Exam (Repeated!)

  • Lymph nodes (all stations)
  • Cardiac (new murmurs)
  • Abdominal (hepatosplenomegaly, masses)
  • Skin (rashes, embolic phenomena, nodules)
  • Joints
  • Temporal arteries (if >50)
  • Teeth/gums
  • Fundoscopic exam (Roth spots)

Step 5: Consider Biopsy

  • Lymph node biopsy (adenopathy)
  • Bone marrow biopsy (cytopenias, disseminated infection)
  • Liver biopsy (hepatomegaly, granulomatous disease)
  • Temporal artery biopsy (suspected GCA)
High-yield FUO tests:
  • PET-CT (localizes occult infection, malignancy, vasculitis)
  • TEE (endocarditis)
  • Temporal artery biopsy (GCA in elderly)
  • Bone marrow biopsy (hematologic malignancy, granulomatous disease)

🔍 Special FUO Types

Nosocomial FUO

Definition: Fever developing after ≥3 days in hospital

Common causes:
  • C. difficile colitis
  • Catheter-related infection (line, Foley)
  • Surgical site infection
  • Drug fever
  • DVT/PE
  • Sinusitis (intubated patients)
  • Transfusion reaction

Neutropenic FUO

Definition: Fever in patient with ANC <500

Key points:
  • Medical emergency - start empiric antibiotics IMMEDIATELY
  • Often no source identified (bacteria from GI translocation)
  • Gram-negative coverage essential (Pseudomonas)
  • Consider fungal infection if persistent fever >5-7 days on antibiotics

HIV-Associated FUO

Consider:
  • Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC) - CD4 <50
  • TB
  • Pneumocystis jirovecii (PJP)
  • CMV
  • Lymphoma
  • Cryptococcosis
  • Histoplasmosis

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: What is the classic definition of FUO?

A. Any unexplained fever
B. Fever >38.3°C for >3 weeks without diagnosis after adequate investigation
C. Fever lasting >1 week
D. Recurrent fevers of any duration
Correct: B. Classic FUO requires temperature >38.3°C (101°F), duration >3 weeks, and no diagnosis after adequate investigation (1 week inpatient or 3 outpatient visits/3 hospital days).

Q2: A 65-year-old woman with FUO has severe headache, jaw claudication, and ESR of 95. What diagnosis should you suspect?

A. Adult Still's disease
B. Giant cell arteritis
C. Lymphoma
D. Endocarditis
Correct: B. Age >50 + headache + jaw claudication + very elevated ESR = classic for giant cell arteritis. This requires urgent treatment to prevent blindness. Get temporal artery biopsy (don't delay treatment for biopsy).

Q3: What lab finding is characteristic of Adult Still's disease?

A. Positive ANA
B. Positive rheumatoid factor
C. Very elevated ferritin (often >10,000)
D. Elevated uric acid
Correct: C. Adult Still's disease is characterized by quotidian fever, salmon-colored rash, arthritis, and markedly elevated ferritin (often >10,000 ng/mL). ANA and RF are typically negative.

Q4: Which imaging modality is most helpful in localizing occult infection or malignancy in FUO?

A. Chest X-ray
B. PET-CT
C. Abdominal ultrasound
D. MRI brain
Correct: B. PET-CT is highly useful in FUO workup. It can identify occult infections (abscesses, osteomyelitis), malignancies (lymphoma), and vasculitis by detecting areas of increased metabolic activity.

Q5: A patient develops fever 5 days after starting a new antibiotic. They appear well despite temperature of 39°C. What should you suspect?

A. Drug fever
B. Treatment failure
C. Superinfection
D. C. difficile colitis
Correct: A. Drug fever classically appears 7-10 days after starting a medication. The patient often appears "too well" for the degree of fever. Common culprits include beta-lactams, sulfonamides, and anticonvulsants. May have eosinophilia.
