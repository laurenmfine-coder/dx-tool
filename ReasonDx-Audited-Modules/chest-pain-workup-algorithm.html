🚨 Immediately Life-Threatening Causes

The "Deadly 6" - Rule out FIRST:
  1. ACS (MI, unstable angina)
  2. Aortic dissection
  3. Pulmonary embolism
  4. Tension pneumothorax
  5. Cardiac tamponade
  6. Esophageal rupture (Boerhaave syndrome)
ConditionKey FeaturesInitial Workup
STEMI Crushing chest pressure, diaphoresis, radiation to arm/jaw, nausea ECG within 10 min, troponin, activate cath lab
Aortic dissection Tearing pain radiating to back, BP differential between arms, widened mediastinum CXR, CT angiography (or TEE if unstable)
Pulmonary embolism Pleuritic pain, dyspnea, tachycardia, hypoxia, risk factors (immobility, cancer, DVT) D-dimer (if low pretest prob), CTA chest
Tension pneumothorax Acute dyspnea, absent breath sounds, tracheal deviation, hypotension Clinical diagnosis → immediate needle decompression
Tamponade Beck's triad (hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds), pulsus paradoxus Bedside echo → pericardiocentesis
Esophageal rupture Severe chest pain after vomiting/retching, subcutaneous emphysema CXR (mediastinal air), CT with water-soluble contrast

Initial Assessment for ALL Chest Pain

Within first 10 minutes:
  • Vital signs including bilateral BP and SpO2
  • 12-lead ECG
  • Focused history and physical
  • IV access
  • Cardiac monitor

💔 Acute Coronary Syndrome Workup

ACS Spectrum:
Unstable Angina → NSTEMI → STEMI
(Troponin negative) → (Troponin positive, no ST elevation) → (ST elevation)

Troponin Interpretation

High-sensitivity troponin (hs-TnI/T):
  • Serial measurements at 0 and 3 hours (or 0/1 hour with validated protocol)
  • Rise and fall pattern suggests acute MI
  • Elevated but stable may indicate chronic elevation (renal failure, HF)
Causes of elevated troponin besides ACS: PE, myocarditis, HF, sepsis, renal failure, takotsubo, arrhythmia

Initial ACS Management (MONA-B)

Morphine (if pain not controlled - use cautiously)
Oxygen (only if SpO2 <90%)
Nitrates (sublingual NTG; avoid if RV infarct, hypotension, PDE5 inhibitor use)
Aspirin (162-325mg chewed)
Beta-blocker (if no contraindications)

+ Anticoagulation (heparin) and P2Y12 inhibitor (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, prasugrel)

STEMI Door-to-Balloon Goals

PCI-capable hospital: Door-to-balloon ≤90 minutes
Transfer for PCI: First medical contact to balloon ≤120 minutes
If PCI not available in timely manner: Fibrinolytics within 30 minutes

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A 55-year-old man presents with crushing chest pain and diaphoresis. ECG shows ST elevation in leads II, III, aVF. Which coronary artery is most likely occluded?

A. Left anterior descending (LAD)
B. Right coronary artery (RCA)
C. Left circumflex
D. Left main
Correct: B. ST elevation in II, III, aVF indicates inferior STEMI, most commonly caused by RCA occlusion (85% of cases). Check right-sided leads (V4R) to assess for RV involvement.

Q2: A patient has chest pain with ST elevation and you note a blood pressure difference of 30 mmHg between arms. What diagnosis must you consider?

A. STEMI - activate cath lab
B. Aortic dissection
C. Pulmonary embolism
D. Pericarditis
Correct: B. BP differential between arms (>20 mmHg) is a classic finding in aortic dissection. This is critical because thrombolytics/anticoagulation for presumed MI could be fatal in dissection. Get CTA before cath lab if dissection is suspected.

Q3: A patient with chest pain has diffuse ST elevation with PR depression on ECG. The pain is sharp and improves when leaning forward. Diagnosis?

A. Acute pericarditis
B. STEMI
C. Pulmonary embolism
D. Aortic dissection
Correct: A. Diffuse ST elevation + PR depression is classic for pericarditis. The positional improvement (better leaning forward) and sharp/pleuritic quality also support this diagnosis. STEMI would have territorial ST elevation.

Q4: When should you NOT give nitroglycerin for chest pain?

A. Anterior STEMI
B. Inferior STEMI with RV involvement and hypotension
C. Unstable angina
D. NSTEMI with stable vitals
Correct: B. RV infarcts are preload-dependent. Nitrates cause venodilation, reduce preload, and can precipitate severe hypotension. Also avoid NTG with recent PDE5 inhibitor use (sildenafil, etc.) or systolic BP <90.

Q5: A young patient presents after severe vomiting with chest pain and subcutaneous emphysema in the neck. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Pneumothorax
B. Myocardial infarction
C. Esophageal rupture (Boerhaave syndrome)
D. Pulmonary embolism
Correct: C. Boerhaave syndrome is esophageal rupture from forceful vomiting. Classic triad: vomiting, chest pain, subcutaneous emphysema. Mackler triad. Life-threatening emergency requiring surgery.
