🎯 5-Step ABG Interpretation

Normal Values:
pH: 7.35-7.45 | PaCO2: 35-45 mmHg | HCO3: 22-26 mEq/L
Step 1: Look at the pH
  • pH < 7.35 → Acidemia
  • pH > 7.45 → Alkalemia
Step 2: Identify Primary Disorder
Look at CO2 and HCO3 - which one explains the pH?
If pH is low (acidemia)Primary Disorder
Low HCO3Metabolic acidosis
High CO2Respiratory acidosis
If pH is high (alkalemia)Primary Disorder
High HCO3Metabolic alkalosis
Low CO2Respiratory alkalosis
Step 3: Calculate Anion Gap
AG = Na - (Cl + HCO3)
Normal: 8-12 (some labs 3-11)
If AG > 12 → Anion gap metabolic acidosis
Step 4: Check Compensation
Primary DisorderExpected Compensation
Metabolic acidosisPaCO2 = 1.5 × HCO3 + 8 (±2) (Winter's formula)
Metabolic alkalosisPaCO2 = 0.7 × HCO3 + 21 (±2)
Acute respiratory acidosisHCO3 ↑ 1 for every 10 ↑ CO2
Chronic respiratory acidosisHCO3 ↑ 3.5 for every 10 ↑ CO2
Acute respiratory alkalosisHCO3 ↓ 2 for every 10 ↓ CO2
Chronic respiratory alkalosisHCO3 ↓ 5 for every 10 ↓ CO2
Step 5: If AG Elevated, Calculate Delta-Delta
Delta gap = (AG - 12) - (24 - HCO3)
  • If ~0: Pure AG metabolic acidosis
  • If > +6: Additional metabolic alkalosis
  • If < -6: Additional non-AG metabolic acidosis

Acute: HCO3 increases 1 per 10 increase in CO2
Chronic: HCO3 increases 3.5 per 10 increase in CO2 (renal compensation takes 3-5 days)

🔀 Mixed Disorders

Clues to mixed disorder:
  • pH normal but CO2 and HCO3 both abnormal
  • Compensation "off" from expected
  • Anion gap present with normal or high HCO3

Common Mixed Disorders

CombinationClinical ExampleABG Pattern
Metabolic acidosis + Respiratory alkalosisSalicylate toxicity, sepsisLow pH, low HCO3, low CO2 (more than expected)
Metabolic acidosis + Respiratory acidosisCardiopulmonary arrestVery low pH, low HCO3, high CO2
Metabolic alkalosis + Respiratory alkalosisCirrhosis + diureticsVery high pH, high HCO3, low CO2
Metabolic acidosis + Metabolic alkalosisVomiting + DKAUse delta-delta to detect

Triple Disorders

Example: Alcoholic with AG acidosis (ketoacidosis) + metabolic alkalosis (vomiting) + respiratory alkalosis (withdrawal)

Look for: AG elevated, delta-delta positive, CO2 lower than expected

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: ABG shows pH 7.28, PaCO2 24, HCO3 11, Na 140, Cl 100. What is the anion gap?

A. 18
B. 29
C. 12
D. 8
Correct: B. AG = Na - (Cl + HCO3) = 140 - (100 + 11) = 29. This is elevated (normal 8-12), indicating an anion gap metabolic acidosis.

Q2: What formula is used to check compensation in metabolic acidosis?

A. Winter's formula: PaCO2 = 1.5 × HCO3 + 8
B. Henderson-Hasselbalch equation
C. Calculated osmolality
D. Anion gap formula
Correct: A. Winter's formula predicts expected PaCO2 in metabolic acidosis. If actual PaCO2 differs significantly, there's an additional respiratory disorder.

Q3: A patient has metabolic acidosis with normal anion gap, urine pH 6.5, and hyperkalemia. What type of RTA is this?

A. Type 1 (Distal)
B. Type 2 (Proximal)
C. Type 4
Correct: C. Type 4 RTA (hypoaldosteronism) is the only RTA with HYPERkalemia. Types 1 and 2 have hypokalemia. Common in diabetes on ACEi.

Q4: Which causes anion gap metabolic acidosis?

A. Diarrhea
B. RTA
C. DKA
D. Normal saline infusion
Correct: C. DKA causes AG acidosis (ketoacids). Diarrhea, RTA, and saline cause NON-AG (hyperchloremic) acidosis. Remember MUDPILES for AG causes.

Q5: Vomiting typically causes which acid-base disorder?

A. Metabolic acidosis
B. Metabolic alkalosis
C. Respiratory acidosis
D. Respiratory alkalosis
Correct: B. Vomiting causes loss of gastric HCl, leading to metabolic alkalosis. It's saline-responsive (urine Cl <20).
