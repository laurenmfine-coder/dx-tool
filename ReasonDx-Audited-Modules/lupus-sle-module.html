📋 Diagnosis

SLE = Chronic multisystem autoimmune disease
Most common in women of childbearing age (9:1 female:male)
Higher prevalence in African American, Hispanic, Asian populations

Complement Levels

Low C3 and C4: Suggests active disease (complement consumption)
Monitor for flares; rising complement = improvement

Drug-Induced Lupus

Classic drugs: Hydralazine, procainamide, isoniazid, minocycline
Features: Arthralgias, serositis, fever, rash
Labs: Anti-histone antibodies (>95%), ANA positive
Usually spares: Kidneys and CNS
Treatment: Stop offending drug; usually resolves

🎯 Organ Manifestations

Mucocutaneous

FindingFeatures
Malar (butterfly) rashErythema over cheeks and nose bridge; spares nasolabial folds
Discoid rashRaised, scarring plaques; can cause permanent alopecia
PhotosensitivityRash after sun exposure
Oral ulcersUsually painless; hard/soft palate
AlopeciaDiffuse or patchy; "lupus hair" at hairline

Musculoskeletal

  • Inflammatory polyarthritis (symmetric, small joints)
  • Non-erosive (unlike RA)
  • Jaccoud's arthropathy (reducible deformities)
  • Avascular necrosis (especially with steroid use)

Renal (Lupus Nephritis)

Major cause of morbidity and mortality!

Hematologic

  • Autoimmune hemolytic anemia (Coombs positive)
  • Leukopenia, lymphopenia
  • Thrombocytopenia

Cardiovascular

  • Pericarditis (most common cardiac manifestation)
  • Libman-Sacks endocarditis (sterile vegetations)
  • Accelerated atherosclerosis
  • Myocarditis (rare)

Neuropsychiatric

  • Headache, cognitive dysfunction
  • Seizures, psychosis
  • Stroke (especially with antiphospholipid antibodies)
  • Peripheral neuropathy

Pulmonary

  • Pleuritis/pleural effusion (most common)
  • Acute lupus pneumonitis
  • Chronic interstitial lung disease
  • Shrinking lung syndrome
  • Pulmonary hypertension

💊 Treatment

General Principles

  • Hydroxychloroquine: All patients (reduces flares, mortality, thrombosis)
  • Sun protection, smoking cessation
  • Monitor for disease activity and damage
  • Vaccinations (avoid live vaccines on immunosuppression)
  • Cardiovascular risk management

Treatment by Severity

ManifestationTreatment
Mild (skin, joints)NSAIDs, hydroxychloroquine, low-dose prednisone
Moderate (serositis, hematologic)Hydroxychloroquine + moderate-dose prednisone ± methotrexate/azathioprine
Severe (nephritis, CNS)High-dose steroids + mycophenolate or cyclophosphamide

Lupus Nephritis Treatment

Induction (Class III/IV):
  • Mycophenolate mofetil (preferred, especially in Black patients)
  • OR Cyclophosphamide (IV pulses)
  • PLUS high-dose glucocorticoids
Maintenance:
  • Mycophenolate or azathioprine
  • Low-dose prednisone
  • Continue hydroxychloroquine
Newer: Voclosporin, belimumab (add-on therapy)

Biologics

AgentTargetUse
BelimumabBLyS (B-cell survival)Active SLE despite standard therapy; lupus nephritis
AnifrolumabType I interferon receptorModerate-severe SLE
RituximabCD20 (B cells)Off-label for refractory disease

Pregnancy Considerations

  • Plan pregnancy during remission (≥6 months stable)
  • Continue hydroxychloroquine (safe, reduces flares)
  • Stop: Mycophenolate, cyclophosphamide, methotrexate (teratogenic)
  • Azathioprine is acceptable if needed
  • Anti-Ro antibodies → risk of neonatal lupus, congenital heart block
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome → aspirin ± heparin

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: Which antibody is most specific for SLE?

A. ANA
B. Anti-Smith
C. Anti-dsDNA
Correct: B. Anti-Smith is the most specific antibody for SLE (~99%), though less sensitive (~30%). ANA is sensitive but not specific.

Q2: Anti-histone antibodies are associated with:

A. Drug-induced lupus
B. Lupus nephritis
C. Neonatal lupus
Correct: A. Anti-histone antibodies are present in >95% of drug-induced lupus. Common culprits: hydralazine, procainamide, isoniazid.

Q3: Which lupus nephritis class is most common and severe?

A. Class IV (diffuse proliferative)
B. Class II (mesangial)
C. Class V (membranous)
Correct: A. Class IV diffuse proliferative nephritis is the most common and severe form, requiring aggressive immunosuppression.

Q4: Which medication should ALL SLE patients take?

A. Hydroxychloroquine
B. Prednisone
C. Cyclophosphamide
Correct: A. Hydroxychloroquine is recommended for all SLE patients - reduces flares, organ damage, thrombosis, and mortality. Safe in pregnancy.

Q5: Anti-Ro (SS-A) antibodies in a pregnant woman put the fetus at risk for:

A. Neonatal lupus and congenital heart block
B. Lupus nephritis
C. Antiphospholipid syndrome
Correct: A. Anti-Ro antibodies cross the placenta and can cause neonatal lupus (rash, cytopenias - usually transient) and congenital heart block (permanent).
