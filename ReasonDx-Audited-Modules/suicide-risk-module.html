📋 Assessment Approach

Every patient should be asked about suicidal ideation. Asking does NOT increase risk - it opens dialogue and can save lives.

Key Terminology

TermDefinition
Suicidal ideation (SI)Thoughts of ending one's life
Passive SI"I wish I were dead" without active planning
Active SIThinking about methods/planning suicide
PlanSpecific method identified
IntentDesire to act on thoughts
MeansAccess to method (guns, medications, etc.)
AttemptSelf-injurious behavior with intent to die
💡 Assessment Framework: "SAD PERSONS"
Sex (male) • Age (<19 or >45) • Depression • Previous attempt
Ethanol/drug use • Rational thinking loss • Social support lacking
Organized plan • No spouse • Sickness (chronic illness)

⚡ Risk & Protective Factors

🔴 High-Risk Factors

  • Prior suicide attempt (strongest predictor)
  • Access to lethal means (especially firearms)
  • Recent psychiatric hospitalization/discharge
  • Active psychosis or severe depression
  • Hopelessness
  • Recent significant loss (job, relationship, loved one)
  • Substance intoxication
  • Specific plan with intent

🟡 Demographic Risk Factors

  • Sex: Males complete suicide 4x more often (females attempt more)
  • Age: Highest rates in elderly white males; also elevated in adolescents
  • Race: White and Native American highest rates
  • Marital status: Single, divorced, widowed
  • LGBTQ+ youth: Elevated risk

🟡 Psychiatric Risk Factors

  • Major depressive disorder (especially with hopelessness)
  • Bipolar disorder (especially mixed states)
  • Schizophrenia (command hallucinations)
  • Borderline personality disorder
  • Substance use disorders
  • PTSD
  • Anxiety disorders

🟢 Protective Factors

  • Strong social support
  • Children in the home
  • Religious/spiritual beliefs
  • Engaged in treatment
  • Problem-solving skills
  • Reasons for living (future-oriented thinking)
  • Restricted access to means

❓ Questions to Ask

Approach with empathy: "I ask all my patients about this because it's important..."

Screening Questions

  • "Have you had thoughts that life isn't worth living?"
  • "Have you wished you were dead or could go to sleep and not wake up?"
  • "Have you had thoughts of hurting yourself?"
  • "Have you had thoughts of killing yourself?"

If Positive → Assess Further

DomainQuestions

Columbia-Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS)

Validated screening tool with 6 questions progressing in severity:
  1. Wish to be dead
  2. Non-specific suicidal thoughts
  3. Active SI with any method (no plan)
  4. Active SI with some intent to act
  5. Active SI with specific plan
  6. Active SI with plan and intent

⚕️ Management & Disposition

🚨 HIGH RISK - Consider Hospitalization

  • Active SI with plan AND intent
  • Recent serious suicide attempt
  • Command hallucinations to harm self
  • Severe agitation or psychosis
  • Unable to contract for safety / no protective factors
  • Intoxication preventing reliable assessment

Means Restriction

Reducing access to means saves lives.
  • Firearms: Ask about guns; encourage removal or secure storage
  • Medications: Limit supply, remove stockpiles
  • Document means restriction counseling

Safety Planning

Stanley-Brown Safety Plan (6 steps):
  1. Warning signs (thoughts, feelings, behaviors)
  2. Internal coping strategies
  3. Social contacts for distraction
  4. People to ask for help
  5. Professionals/agencies to contact
  6. Making environment safe (means restriction)

Resources

  • 988: Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text)
  • Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741
  • Veterans Crisis Line: 988, press 1

Disposition Decision

Risk LevelDisposition
High risk, unable to remain safePsychiatric hospitalization (voluntary or involuntary)
Moderate risk with protective factorsPossible partial hospitalization, intensive outpatient, close follow-up
Low risk, good supportsSafety plan, outpatient follow-up within 24-72 hours

📝 Quiz

Q1: The strongest predictor of completed suicide is:

A. Previous suicide attempt
B. Male sex
C. Major depression
D. Substance use
Prior suicide attempt is the strongest predictor of future completed suicide. All others are risk factors but not as strong.

Q2: Which demographic has the highest rate of completed suicide?

A. Young females
B. Elderly white males
C. Middle-aged females
D. Adolescent males
Elderly white males have the highest rate of completed suicide. Females attempt more often but males complete more often (especially with firearms).

Q3: The most important intervention for reducing suicide risk is:

A. Means restriction (reducing access to lethal methods)
B. Antidepressant medications
C. Cognitive behavioral therapy
D. No-suicide contracts
Means restriction is the most evidence-based intervention for reducing suicide. No-suicide contracts have NO evidence of effectiveness.

Q4: Asking patients about suicidal thoughts:

A. Plants the idea and increases risk
B. Does NOT increase risk and can be life-saving
C. Should only be done by psychiatrists
Asking about suicide does NOT increase risk. This is a harmful myth. Direct questioning opens dialogue and allows intervention.
