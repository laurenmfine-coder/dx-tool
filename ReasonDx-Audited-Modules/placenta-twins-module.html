🥚 Fetal Membranes

Two membranes surround the fetus: the inner amnion and outer chorion. The amnion contains amniotic fluid; the chorion develops into the fetal portion of the placenta.

Development Timeline

Week 1: Blastocyst Formation

  • Inner cell mass (embryoblast) → embryo proper
  • Outer cell mass (trophoblast) → placenta + chorion

Week 2: Bilaminar Disc & Cavities

  • Amniotic cavity forms within epiblast
  • Yolk sac (primary, then secondary) forms below hypoblast
  • Trophoblast differentiates: cytotrophoblast + syncytiotrophoblast

Weeks 3-4: Membrane Establishment

  • Amnion: Lines amniotic cavity (from epiblast/ectoderm)
  • Chorion: Outer membrane (trophoblast + extraembryonic mesoderm)
  • Chorionic villi develop → placenta

🎯 Clinical Pearl: The amnion and chorion normally fuse by 12-16 weeks. Persistence of the space between them can be seen on ultrasound and is associated with early gestation or certain complications.

🫀 Placental Development

The placenta has two components: fetal portion (chorion frondosum) and maternal portion (decidua basalis). It serves as the interface for gas, nutrient, and waste exchange.

Decidua Regions

The decidua is the specialized endometrium of pregnancy. It has three regions:
  • Decidua basalis: Under embryo → maternal part of placenta
  • Decidua capsularis: Over embryo (degenerates as fetus grows)
  • Decidua parietalis: Lines rest of uterus

Chorionic Villus Development

Primary villi (Week 2)

Cytotrophoblast core covered by syncytiotrophoblast

Secondary villi (Week 3)

Mesoderm grows into villi

Tertiary villi (Week 4+)

Blood vessels form within mesoderm → functional exchange unit

🎯 Placental Functions:
  • Gas exchange: O₂ to fetus, CO₂ to mother
  • Nutrition: Glucose, amino acids, fatty acids
  • Waste removal: Urea, bilirubin
  • Hormone production: hCG, hPL, estrogen, progesterone
  • Immunologic: Transfers IgG (passive immunity)

Umbilical Cord

Contains 2 umbilical arteries (carry deoxygenated blood FROM fetus) and 1 umbilical vein (carries oxygenated blood TO fetus). Surrounded by Wharton's jelly.

Single umbilical artery (~1% of births) - associated with renal anomalies and chromosomal abnormalities. Screen with ultrasound.

👯 Twin Types

Twin placentation depends on zygosity (identical vs fraternal) and timing of division (for identical twins). The earlier the split, the more separate the membranes.

Dizygotic (Fraternal) Twins

Two eggs + Two sperm = Two separate individuals

  • ALWAYS dichorionic-diamniotic (Di-Di)
  • Two separate placentas (may fuse if implanted close)
  • Can be different sexes
  • Genetically like any siblings (~50% shared DNA)
  • ~70% of all twins

Monozygotic (Identical) Twins

Timing of division determines membrane configuration:

Di-Di (Days 1-3)

Division before morula

2 chorions, 2 amnions, 2 placentas

~25-30% of MZ twins

✓ Lowest risk

Mono-Di (Days 4-8)

Division at blastocyst stage

1 chorion, 2 amnions, 1 placenta

~70% of MZ twins (most common)

⚠ TTTS risk

Mono-Mono (Days 8-13)

Division after amnion forms

1 chorion, 1 amnion, 1 placenta

~1% of MZ twins

⚠ HIGH RISK - cord entanglement

Conjoined (Day 13+)

Incomplete division

Twins physically connected

Very rare (~1:50,000)

⚠ Variable viability

Timing Mnemonic:
"Di-Di by Day 3, Mono-Di by Day 8, Mono-Mono beyond 8, Conjoined at 13+"

⚠️ Complications

Twin-Twin Transfusion Syndrome (TTTS)

Only in MONOCHORIONIC twins

Mechanism: Unbalanced arteriovenous anastomoses in shared placenta → one twin (donor) gives blood to other (recipient)

Donor twin: Anemia, growth restriction, oligohydramnios ("stuck twin")

Recipient twin: Polycythemia, volume overload, polyhydramnios, hydrops

Treatment: Laser ablation of communicating vessels, amnioreduction

Twin Anemia-Polycythemia Sequence (TAPS)

Chronic form of unbalanced transfusion without fluid imbalance. One twin anemic, one polycythemic, but amniotic fluid volumes normal.

Monoamniotic Twin Risks

Cord Entanglement

Both twins in same sac → cords can knot and entangle → acute fetal death

Management: Intensive monitoring, early delivery (~32-34 weeks)

Placental Abnormalities

Placenta Previa

Placenta covers internal cervical os. Painless vaginal bleeding. Risk factors: prior C-section, prior previa, multiparity.

Placenta Accreta Spectrum

  • Accreta: Attaches to myometrium (no decidua basalis)
  • Increta: Invades into myometrium
  • Percreta: Penetrates through myometrium (may invade bladder)

Risk: prior C-section + previa. Massive hemorrhage risk at delivery.

Placental Abruption

Premature separation of placenta from uterine wall. Painful vaginal bleeding, uterine tenderness, fetal distress. Risk factors: hypertension, trauma, cocaine.

🎯 Board Pearl: Painless bleeding = previa. Painful bleeding = abruption. Both are emergencies requiring rapid assessment.

🎯 Test Your Knowledge

Question 1

Monozygotic twins that split on day 5 will have which membrane configuration?

A. Dichorionic-diamniotic
B. Monochorionic-diamniotic
C. Monochorionic-monoamniotic
D. Conjoined
Answer: B. Division on days 4-8 (after inner cell mass forms but before amnion) results in monochorionic-diamniotic twins - one shared placenta/chorion but two separate amniotic sacs. This is the most common type of identical twins (~70%).

Question 2

Twin-twin transfusion syndrome can ONLY occur in which twin type?

A. Dizygotic twins
B. Dichorionic-diamniotic
C. Monochorionic twins
D. Any twin pregnancy
Answer: C. TTTS requires a shared placenta with vascular anastomoses, which only occurs in MONOCHORIONIC twins. Dichorionic twins have separate placentas and cannot develop TTTS even if the placentas are fused.

Question 3

The fetal portion of the placenta develops from which structure?

A. Chorion frondosum
B. Decidua basalis
C. Amnion
D. Yolk sac
Answer: A. The chorion frondosum (the part of chorion with villi that persists) forms the fetal portion of the placenta. The decidua basalis (maternal endometrium under the embryo) forms the maternal portion.

Question 4

A pregnant woman has painless vaginal bleeding at 32 weeks. Ultrasound shows the placenta covering the internal os. What is the diagnosis?

A. Placenta previa
B. Placental abruption
C. Vasa previa
D. Uterine rupture
Answer: A. Placenta previa presents with PAINLESS vaginal bleeding, typically in third trimester. The placenta covers the cervical os. Abruption causes PAINFUL bleeding with uterine tenderness.

Question 5

The normal umbilical cord contains:

A. 1 artery, 2 veins
B. 2 arteries, 1 vein
C. 2 arteries, 2 veins
D. 1 artery, 1 vein
Answer: B. Normal umbilical cord has 2 arteries and 1 vein (AVA). The arteries carry deoxygenated blood FROM fetus to placenta; the vein carries oxygenated blood TO fetus. Single umbilical artery is associated with anomalies.
