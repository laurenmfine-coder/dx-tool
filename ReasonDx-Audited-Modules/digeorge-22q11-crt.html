💡 3rd & 4th Pharyngeal Pouch Derivatives

  • 3rd pouch (dorsal): Inferior parathyroids
  • 3rd pouch (ventral): Thymus
  • 4th pouch (dorsal): Superior parathyroids

Abnormal development of the 3rd/4th pouches explains the combination of thymic aplasia (T-cell deficiency) and parathyroid hypoplasia (hypocalcemia).

💡 Conotruncal Defects

The cardiac neural crest contributes to the aorticopulmonary septum and outflow tract. 22q11 deletion disrupts neural crest migration → conotruncal heart defects.

💊 Management Considerations

💡 Key Management Points

  • Hypocalcemia: IV calcium gluconate acutely; oral calcium + vitamin D long-term
  • Cardiac: Surgical repair of interrupted aortic arch + VSD
  • Immunodeficiency: Avoid live vaccines until T-cell function assessed; may need irradiated blood products
  • Development: Early intervention, speech therapy, monitor for learning disabilities

🔑 Key Learning Points

  • CATCH-22: Cardiac, Abnormal facies, Thymic aplasia, Cleft palate, Hypocalcemia, 22q11
  • Embryology: 3rd/4th pharyngeal pouch and cardiac neural crest affected
  • Parathyroids: 3rd pouch (inferior) and 4th pouch (superior) → hypocalcemia
  • Thymus: 3rd pouch (ventral) → T-cell deficiency
  • Heart: Conotruncal defects (interrupted arch, TOF, truncus)
  • Diagnosis: FISH or chromosomal microarray (standard karyotype may miss it)
  • Inheritance: Most cases are de novo; AD if inherited (50% risk)

📚 Spectrum of 22q11 Deletion

Previously called DiGeorge syndrome, velocardiofacial syndrome (VCFS), and Shprintzen syndrome - now recognized as the same genetic condition with variable expression. Most common microdeletion syndrome (~1:4000 births).
