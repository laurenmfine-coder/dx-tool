Contrast Phases

Non-contrast

  • Baseline density
  • Detect stones, calcifications, acute hemorrhage

Arterial Phase (25-35 sec)

  • Aorta, arteries enhance brightly
  • Hypervascular lesions (HCC, renal cell CA)
  • Active bleeding

Portal Venous Phase (60-70 sec)

  • Standard "with contrast" phase
  • Best for most abdominal pathology
  • Liver parenchyma enhances maximally

Delayed Phase (3-5 min)

  • Ureters, bladder opacify
  • Cholangiocarcinoma (delayed enhancement)
  • Urothelial pathology

Cholecystitis

  • GB wall thickening >3mm
  • Pericholecystic fluid/fat stranding
  • Gallstones (may not be visible)
  • GB distension >4cm transverse
  • Gangrenous: Lack of wall enhancement, intramural gas

Biliary Dilatation

  • CBD >6mm (or >10mm post-cholecystectomy)
  • Intrahepatic ducts: "parallel channel sign" (dilated duct parallels portal vein)
  • Look for level of obstruction and cause

🔄 Bowel Pathology

Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)

  • Dilated proximal loops >3cm
  • Decompressed distal bowel
  • "Small bowel feces sign" - particulate matter in lumen
  • Transition point: Where dilated meets decompressed
  • Causes: Adhesions (#1), hernia, tumor, stricture

Signs of Strangulation/Ischemia

  • Bowel wall thickening or thinning (paper-thin)
  • Decreased or absent wall enhancement
  • Mesenteric haziness/fluid
  • Closed loop obstruction (U or C-shaped)
  • Pneumatosis intestinalis (air in bowel wall)
  • Portal venous gas

Appendicitis

  • Appendix diameter >6mm
  • Appendiceal wall thickening/enhancement
  • Periappendiceal fat stranding
  • Appendicolith (present in 25%)
  • Perforation: Abscess, extraluminal air, phlegmon

Diverticulitis

  • Colonic wall thickening (typically sigmoid)
  • Pericolonic fat stranding
  • Diverticula
  • Complicated: Abscess, perforation, fistula

🚨 Emergency Findings

Free Air (Pneumoperitoneum)

  • Air outside bowel lumen
  • Most sensitive: Under anterior abdominal wall on supine CT
  • Causes: Perforation (ulcer, diverticulitis, trauma), post-procedure
  • Look for site of perforation

Active Hemorrhage

  • "Contrast blush" - extravasated contrast on arterial phase
  • Increases on delayed phase (pooling)
  • Requires urgent intervention (surgery/IR)

Aortic Pathology

AAA

  • Diameter >3cm
  • >5.5cm or rapidly expanding = repair
  • Rupture: Retroperitoneal hematoma, draped aorta sign

Aortic Dissection

  • Intimal flap separating true and false lumen
  • Stanford A: Involves ascending aorta (surgical emergency)
  • Stanford B: Descending only (medical management)

Mesenteric Ischemia

  • SMA/SMV thrombus or occlusion
  • Bowel wall thickening or paper-thin
  • Decreased/absent bowel enhancement
  • Pneumatosis, portal venous gas (late findings)
  • High mortality - requires urgent surgery

📝 Quiz

Q1: The classic enhancement pattern for HCC is:

A. Arterial enhancement with portal venous washout
B. Peripheral nodular enhancement filling in
C. No enhancement
HCC shows arterial hyperenhancement with washout on portal venous phase - diagnostic in cirrhotic liver.

Q2: The most common cause of small bowel obstruction is:

A. Adhesions
B. Hernia
C. Tumor
Adhesions from prior surgery are the #1 cause of SBO in developed countries.

Q3: Portal venous gas on CT suggests:

A. Bowel ischemia/necrosis
B. Biliary obstruction
C. Hepatic abscess
Portal venous gas is an ominous finding suggesting bowel ischemia/necrosis or severe infection.

Q4: Stanford Type A dissection:

A. Involves ascending aorta - surgical emergency
B. Involves descending only - medical management
C. Involves abdominal aorta only
Stanford A = ascending aorta involved = surgical emergency due to risk of rupture, tamponade, coronary occlusion.
