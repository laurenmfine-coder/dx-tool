🚨 Red Flag Symptoms

SNOOP4 - When to Worry:
  • Systemic symptoms (fever, weight loss, cancer, HIV)
  • Neurologic signs (confusion, focal deficits, papilledema)
  • Onset sudden ("thunderclap" - worst headache of life)
  • Older age (new headache >50 years)
  • Position-related (worse lying down = ↑ICP; better lying = low pressure)
  • Precipitated by Valsalva, cough, exertion
  • Progressive or different from prior headaches
  • Pregnancy or postpartum

Thunderclap Headache - Emergency Workup

Definition: Maximal intensity within seconds to 1 minute

Workup:
  1. Non-contrast CT head (rule out SAH - 95%+ sensitive within 6 hours)
  2. If CT negative → LP (xanthochromia)
  3. If LP negative → Consider CTA/MRA (reversible cerebral vasoconstriction syndrome, dissection)

💆 Primary Headache Disorders

Migraine

Diagnostic Criteria (need A-D):
  • A: ≥5 attacks
  • B: Duration 4-72 hours (untreated)
  • C: ≥2 of: unilateral, pulsating, moderate-severe, aggravated by activity
  • D: ≥1 of: nausea/vomiting, photophobia AND phonophobia
TypeFeatures
Without auraMost common (~75%)
With auraVisual (scintillating scotoma), sensory, or speech; precedes headache by 5-60 min
Chronic migraine≥15 headache days/month for >3 months, ≥8 with migraine features

Tension-Type Headache

Most common primary headache
  • Bilateral, pressing/tightening (non-pulsating)
  • Mild-moderate intensity
  • NOT aggravated by activity
  • No nausea; may have photophobia OR phonophobia (not both)

Cluster Headache

"Suicide headache" - most severe pain
LocationStrictly unilateral, orbital/supraorbital/temporal
Duration15-180 minutes
Frequency1-8 attacks per day, in "clusters" lasting weeks-months
Autonomic featuresIpsilateral: lacrimation, conjunctival injection, nasal congestion, ptosis, miosis
BehaviorRestless, pacing (vs migraine patients prefer dark/quiet)
DemographicsMale predominance (3:1), often smokers

Giant Cell Arteritis Emergency:
If suspected (age >50, new headache, elevated ESR/CRP, visual symptoms), start high-dose steroids IMMEDIATELY (prednisone 60mg or IV methylpred). Do NOT wait for biopsy - vision loss can be permanent!

💊 Treatment

Migraine - Acute Treatment

SeverityTreatment
Mild-moderateNSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen), acetaminophen, aspirin + caffeine
Moderate-severeTriptans (sumatriptan); take early in attack
Refractory/EDIV metoclopramide + diphenhydramine, ketorolac, DHE
Triptan contraindications: CAD, uncontrolled HTN, stroke history, hemiplegic/basilar migraine, pregnancy, MAOIs

Migraine - Preventive Treatment

Indications: ≥4 headache days/month, significant disability, medication overuse

Options:
  • Beta-blockers: Propranolol, metoprolol
  • Antiepileptics: Topiramate, valproate
  • Antidepressants: Amitriptyline, venlafaxine
  • CGRP inhibitors: Erenumab, fremanezumab (newer, injectable)
  • Botox: For chronic migraine

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A 55-year-old woman has new-onset headaches with jaw pain when chewing and scalp tenderness. ESR is 85. What's the next step?

A. MRI brain
B. Start high-dose steroids immediately
C. Wait for temporal artery biopsy results
Correct: B. This is giant cell arteritis. Start steroids IMMEDIATELY to prevent blindness. Biopsy can be done within 1-2 weeks while on steroids.

Q2: A patient has severe unilateral orbital headaches lasting 45 minutes with ipsilateral tearing and nasal congestion. He paces during attacks. Diagnosis?

A. Migraine
B. Cluster headache
C. Tension headache
Correct: B. Cluster headache: severe unilateral orbital pain, 15-180 min, autonomic features (lacrimation, nasal congestion), and restlessness (vs quiet/dark for migraine).

Q3: What is the acute treatment of choice for cluster headache?

A. High-flow oxygen
B. Oral sumatriptan
C. Indomethacin
Correct: A. High-flow O2 (12-15 L/min) is first-line acute treatment for cluster. SC sumatriptan is also effective. Indomethacin is for paroxysmal hemicrania.

Q4: "Thunderclap headache" with negative CT should prompt what next step?

A. Lumbar puncture
B. Reassurance and discharge
C. Start migraine prophylaxis
Correct: A. CT misses ~5% of SAH. LP looking for xanthochromia (RBC breakdown) is required if CT negative but clinical suspicion high.

Q5: Which headache disorder responds specifically to indomethacin?

A. Cluster headache
B. Paroxysmal hemicrania
C. Migraine
Correct: B. Paroxysmal hemicrania and hemicrania continua have an "absolute response" to indomethacin - it's diagnostic! Cluster uses verapamil/O2.
