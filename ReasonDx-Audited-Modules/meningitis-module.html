🩺 Clinical Presentation

Classic Triad

Fever + Headache + Neck Stiffness
(Present in only ~45% of bacterial meningitis)
Additional features:
  • Altered mental status
  • Photophobia
  • Nausea/vomiting
  • Seizures
  • Petechial/purpuric rash (N. meningitidis)

Physical Exam Signs

SignDescriptionSensitivity
Nuchal rigidityResistance to passive neck flexion~30%
Kernig signPain with knee extension when hip flexed~5%
Brudzinski signHip/knee flexion with passive neck flexion~5%

When to Get Imaging Before LP?

CT head before LP if ANY of:
  • Immunocompromised
  • History of CNS disease (mass, stroke, infection)
  • New-onset seizure
  • Papilledema
  • Altered consciousness
  • Focal neurologic deficit
Do NOT delay antibiotics for imaging! Give empiric treatment, then image, then LP.

🦠 Bacterial Meningitis

Medical emergency! Mortality 15-25% even with treatment
Give antibiotics within 1 hour of presentation

Empiric Antibiotic Therapy

NeonatesAmpicillin + cefotaxime (or gentamicin)
Children/AdultsVancomycin + ceftriaxone (or cefotaxime)
Age >50 or immunocompromisedVancomycin + ceftriaxone + ampicillin (for Listeria)
Post-neurosurgeryVancomycin + cefepime (or meropenem)

Adjunctive Dexamethasone

Give dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg q6h × 4 days
  • Give with or before first dose of antibiotics
  • Reduces mortality and neurologic sequelae in S. pneumoniae meningitis
  • Continue if Gram stain shows gram-positive diplococci or pneumococcus confirmed
  • Consider stopping if another pathogen identified

Chemoprophylaxis

N. meningitidis: Close contacts within 7 days
Rifampin, ciprofloxacin, or ceftriaxone (single dose)

H. influenzae type b: Household contacts if unvaccinated child <4 years
Rifampin × 4 days

🧬 Viral & Other Meningitis

Viral (Aseptic) Meningitis

Most common cause of meningitis overall

Pathogens:
  • Enteroviruses (most common) - summer/fall
  • HSV-2 (primary genital herpes)
  • HIV (acute retroviral syndrome)
  • Arboviruses (West Nile)
  • Mumps, VZV, EBV
Treatment: Supportive; self-limited (1-2 weeks)

HSV Encephalitis

HSV-1 encephalitis is a medical emergency!

Features:
  • Fever, altered mental status, focal neurologic signs
  • Temporal lobe predilection (personality changes, seizures)
  • CSF: lymphocytic pleocytosis, elevated protein, may have RBCs
  • MRI: temporal lobe signal changes
Treatment: IV acyclovir 10 mg/kg q8h × 14-21 days
Start empirically if suspected - don't wait for PCR!

TB Meningitis

Features:
  • Subacute/chronic course (weeks)
  • Basilar meningitis → cranial nerve palsies
  • CSF: lymphocytic, very low glucose, very high protein
  • AFB smear often negative; PCR helpful
Treatment: 4-drug therapy (RIPE) + steroids

Autoimmune/Inflammatory

  • Drug-induced (NSAIDs, IVIG, TMP-SMX)
  • Lupus cerebritis
  • Sarcoidosis (neurosarcoid)
  • Behçet's disease

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: CSF shows WBC 2500 (90% neutrophils), glucose 25, protein 250. Most likely diagnosis?

A. Bacterial meningitis
B. Viral meningitis
C. Normal CSF
Correct: A. High WBC with neutrophil predominance, LOW glucose, and high protein is classic for bacterial meningitis.

Q2: Which antibiotic must be added for meningitis in patients >50 years old?

A. Ampicillin (for Listeria coverage)
B. Metronidazole
C. Azithromycin
Correct: A. Ampicillin covers Listeria monocytogenes, which is more common in elderly, neonates, and immunocompromised patients.

Q3: When should dexamethasone be given in bacterial meningitis?

A. With or before first dose of antibiotics
B. 24 hours after antibiotics
C. Only if patient deteriorates
Correct: A. Dexamethasone should be given with or before the first antibiotic dose to reduce inflammation from bacterial lysis.

Q4: HSV encephalitis primarily affects which brain region?

A. Temporal lobes
B. Frontal lobes
C. Cerebellum
Correct: A. HSV encephalitis has a predilection for temporal lobes, causing personality changes, memory deficits, and seizures.

Q5: Most common cause of viral meningitis?

A. Enteroviruses
B. HSV-1
C. West Nile virus
Correct: A. Enteroviruses are the most common cause of viral meningitis, typically occurring in summer and fall.
