🎯 Stroke Overview

"Time is Brain" - 1.9 million neurons die per minute during stroke
Door-to-CT: <25 minutes | Door-to-needle (tPA): <60 minutes
BE-FAST for Stroke Recognition:
Balance (sudden loss)
Eyes (vision loss)
Face (drooping)
Arm (weakness)
Speech (slurred or confused)
Time (call 911, note onset time)

Stroke Types

Type%Cause
Ischemic~87%Thrombotic, embolic, lacunar
Hemorrhagic - ICH~10%Hypertensive, amyloid angiopathy
Hemorrhagic - SAH~3%Aneurysm rupture, AVM

Immediate Workup

Don't delay for these:
  • Non-contrast CT head - rule out hemorrhage (MUST before tPA)
  • Glucose - hypoglycemia mimics stroke
  • O2 saturation
Do NOT delay tPA for these:
  • CBC, BMP, coags (unless known coagulopathy)
  • Chest X-ray
  • Lumbar puncture

🔴 Ischemic Stroke

Mechanisms

TypeMechanismFeatures
Large vessel (thrombotic) Atherosclerosis of carotid/vertebral/intracranial Stuttering onset, prior TIAs, carotid bruit
Cardioembolic AFib, valve disease, MI with mural thrombus Sudden maximal deficit, multiple territories
Lacunar Small vessel lipohyalinosis (HTN, DM) Pure motor/sensory, no cortical signs
Other Dissection, hypercoagulable, vasculitis Young patients, neck pain (dissection)

Workup for Etiology

Complete stroke workup includes:
  • Cardiac: ECG (AFib), telemetry, TTE ± TEE
  • Vascular: Carotid ultrasound or CTA/MRA head & neck
  • Labs: Lipids, HbA1c, homocysteine
  • Hypercoagulable workup if young/cryptogenic

NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS)

CategoryItems Assessed
Level of consciousnessAlertness, orientation, commands
VisionVisual fields, gaze
MotorFacial palsy, arm/leg drift
SensationPinprick
LanguageNaming, reading, repetition
OtherDysarthria, extinction/inattention, ataxia

Score interpretation: 0 = no stroke symptoms; 1-4 = minor; 5-15 = moderate; 16-20 = moderate-severe; 21-42 = severe

Acute Management

  • Airway/breathing - maintain O2 sat >94%
  • Blood pressure: Do NOT lower unless >220/120 (or >185/110 if giving tPA)
  • Glucose: Treat hypoglycemia; avoid hyperglycemia
  • Aspirin 325mg within 24-48h (if not getting tPA immediately)
  • DVT prophylaxis (pneumatic compression)
  • Swallow evaluation before oral intake

💥 Hemorrhagic Stroke

Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH)

CauseTypical Location
Hypertension (most common)Basal ganglia (putamen), thalamus, pons, cerebellum
Amyloid angiopathyLobar (elderly)
AnticoagulationAny
AVMAny (younger patients)
TumorLobar
ICH Management:
  • Reverse anticoagulation: Vitamin K, FFP, PCC for warfarin; idarucizumab for dabigatran; andexanet alfa for Xa inhibitors
  • BP control: Target SBP 140 mmHg (if presenting SBP 150-220)
  • ICP management: Elevate head of bed, mannitol/hypertonic saline, consider EVD
  • Seizure prophylaxis: Consider if lobar hemorrhage
  • Surgery: Consider for cerebellar hemorrhage >3cm or causing hydrocephalus

Subarachnoid Hemorrhage (SAH)

"Thunderclap headache" = worst headache of life, maximal at onset
FeatureDetails
Cause~85% ruptured aneurysm (berry), 10% perimesencephalic, 5% other
PresentationThunderclap headache, neck stiffness, photophobia, N/V, altered LOC
Hunt-Hess Grade1 = asymptomatic to 5 = coma (predicts outcome)

SAH Workup & Management

Diagnosis:
  1. Non-contrast CT (95%+ sensitive within 6 hours)
  2. If CT negative but high suspicion → LP (xanthochromia)
  3. CTA or angiogram to find aneurysm
Management:
  • Secure aneurysm: coiling (endovascular) or clipping (surgical)
  • Nimodipine × 21 days (prevents vasospasm)
  • Euvolemia, avoid hypotension
  • Monitor for vasospasm (days 4-14), hydrocephalus, rebleeding

💉 tPA & Thrombectomy

IV Alteplase (tPA) Criteria

Time window: Within 4.5 hours of symptom onset (3 hours for some criteria)

Absolute Contraindications

  • Hemorrhage on CT
  • History of ICH
  • Ischemic stroke or head trauma within 3 months
  • Intracranial neoplasm, AVM, or aneurysm
  • Recent intracranial or spinal surgery
  • Active internal bleeding
  • Suspected aortic dissection
  • BP >185/110 despite treatment
  • Platelets <100,000 (if known)
  • INR >1.7 or PTT >40 (if on anticoagulation)
  • Current use of direct thrombin inhibitors or factor Xa inhibitors with elevated labs

Additional Exclusions for 3-4.5 Hour Window

  • Age >80 years (relative)
  • Severe stroke (NIHSS >25)
  • History of both diabetes AND prior stroke
  • Taking any oral anticoagulant

Relative Contraindications

  • Minor/rapidly improving symptoms
  • Pregnancy
  • Seizure at onset
  • Recent major surgery or trauma (14 days)
  • Recent GI/GU bleeding (21 days)
  • Recent arterial puncture at non-compressible site

BP Goals

ScenarioBP Target
Before tPA eligible<185/110 mmHg
During/after tPA (24 hours)<180/105 mmHg
Not receiving tPAPermissive HTN unless >220/120

Mechanical Thrombectomy

Indications:
  • Large vessel occlusion (ICA, M1, basilar)
  • Within 24 hours with favorable imaging (perfusion mismatch)
  • NIHSS ≥6
  • Pre-stroke mRS 0-1
  • Can be done WITH or AFTER tPA

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: What is the first imaging study to obtain in suspected stroke?

A. Non-contrast CT head
B. MRI brain with diffusion
C. CTA head and neck
D. Carotid ultrasound
Correct: A. Non-contrast CT head is the first study to rule out hemorrhage before thrombolytic therapy. It must be done before tPA can be given.

Q2: What is the time window for IV tPA in ischemic stroke?

A. 2 hours
B. 3 hours
C. 4.5 hours
D. 6 hours
Correct: C. IV tPA can be given up to 4.5 hours from symptom onset (with some additional exclusions for the 3-4.5 hour window). Mechanical thrombectomy may extend to 24 hours with favorable imaging.

Q3: What BP must be achieved BEFORE giving tPA?

A. <185/110
B. <160/90
C. <220/120
D. <140/90
Correct: A. BP must be <185/110 before giving tPA and maintained <180/105 for 24 hours after. If not receiving tPA, permissive hypertension is allowed unless >220/120.

Q4: A patient has contralateral face and arm weakness greater than leg, with expressive aphasia. Which artery is affected?

A. Middle cerebral artery (MCA)
B. Anterior cerebral artery (ACA)
C. Posterior cerebral artery (PCA)
D. Basilar artery
Correct: A. MCA stroke causes face/arm > leg weakness (homunculus representation), plus aphasia if dominant hemisphere. ACA would cause leg > arm weakness.

Q5: A patient with SAH should receive which medication to prevent vasospasm?

A. Aspirin
B. Nimodipine
C. Heparin
D. Mannitol
Correct: B. Nimodipine (a calcium channel blocker) is given for 21 days after SAH to prevent cerebral vasospasm, which typically occurs days 4-14 after hemorrhage.
