🔬 Normal Abdominal Wall Development

Timeline:
  • Week 6: Rapid midgut growth → physiologic herniation into umbilical cord
  • Week 10: Midgut returns to abdomen with 270° counterclockwise rotation
  • Week 12: Abdominal wall closure complete

Lateral Body Fold Development

The ventral body wall forms from 4 embryonic folds that fuse at the umbilicus:

  • Cephalic fold (head)
  • Caudal fold (tail)
  • Two lateral folds (sides)

Omphalocele - Embryologic Basis

Mechanism: Failure of lateral body folds to fuse → midline defect
Result: Abdominal contents remain in umbilical cord base, covered by peritoneum and amnion (membrane/sac)
Timing: Defect occurs during weeks 3-4 of development

Gastroschisis - Embryologic Basis

Mechanism: Vascular accident - disruption of omphalomesenteric artery (right) OR involution of right umbilical vein
Result: Paraumbilical defect (usually RIGHT of umbilicus), bowel herniates into amniotic cavity with NO membrane covering
Timing: Occurs after body wall closure (later than omphalocele)

⚖️ Omphalocele vs Gastroschisis

"GASTRO-schisis = Goes to the RIGHT, no sac, Gut matted"
"OMPHALO-cele = O = central (at umbilicus), sac present"
FeatureOmphaloceleGastroschisis
Location Central (AT umbilicus) Paraumbilical (RIGHT of umbilicus)
Covering SAC present (peritoneum + amnion) NO SAC - exposed bowel
Umbilical cord Inserts INTO the sac Normal insertion (defect is lateral)
Bowel appearance Normal (protected by sac) Matted, thickened, edematous (chemical peritonitis from amniotic fluid)
Contents Bowel ± liver, spleen Usually bowel only (small defect)
Defect size Variable (can be large) Usually small (2-4 cm)
Associated anomalies 50-70% - cardiac, chromosomal (trisomy 13, 18, 21), Beckwith-Wiedemann 10-15% - mostly GI (atresia, stenosis)
Chromosomal abnormalities HIGH (30-40%) LOW (<1%)
Maternal age Older mothers Young mothers (<20 years)
Risk factors Advanced maternal age Young age, smoking, vasoactive drugs
Prognosis Depends on associated anomalies Generally good (90%+ survival)
Key Distinction: Omphalocele has HIGH rate of chromosomal/cardiac anomalies. Gastroschisis is usually isolated with excellent prognosis. Always get echo and karyotype for omphalocele!

🏥 Clinical Management

Prenatal Diagnosis

Ultrasound findings:
  • Omphalocele: Central defect with sac, cord insertion into sac, may see liver
  • Gastroschisis: Right paraumbilical defect, free-floating bowel loops, no sac
Additional workup for omphalocele:
  • Fetal echocardiogram (50% cardiac defects)
  • Amniocentesis for karyotype
  • Detailed anomaly scan

Delivery Planning

AspectOmphaloceleGastroschisis
Delivery modeC-section if giant/liver-containingVaginal usually safe
TimingTerm if isolatedOften 37-38 weeks (monitor bowel)
LocationTertiary center with pediatric surgeryTertiary center with pediatric surgery

Immediate Postnatal Care

Both defects:
  • Keep bowel moist and warm (saline-soaked gauze, bowel bag)
  • Position infant on side to prevent kinking of mesentery
  • NG decompression (NPO)
  • IV fluids (increased losses - may need 150-200 mL/kg/day)
  • Temperature regulation
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics

Surgical Options

OptionIndicationDescription
Primary closure Small defect, adequate abdominal domain Single-stage repair; reduce contents, close fascia
Staged closure (silo) Large defect, viscero-abdominal disproportion Spring-loaded silo; gradual reduction over 5-7 days
Paint and wait Giant omphalocele with liver Apply silver sulfadiazine; allow epithelialization; delayed repair

Complications

  • Abdominal compartment syndrome - from tight closure
  • Intestinal atresia/stenosis - especially gastroschisis
  • Necrotizing enterocolitis
  • Prolonged ileus - weeks in gastroschisis
  • Short bowel syndrome - if bowel loss
  • Ventral hernia - long-term

🎯 Clinical Reasoning Trainer

Case: Prenatal Ultrasound Finding

A 19-year-old G1P0 at 20 weeks presents for anatomy scan. Ultrasound shows free-floating bowel loops in the amniotic fluid to the right of the umbilical cord insertion. There is no visible membrane covering the bowel. The bowel appears slightly thickened and matted.

What is the most likely diagnosis?

Omphalocele
Gastroschisis
Umbilical hernia
Bladder exstrophy

What additional workup is MOST important for this condition?

Fetal echocardiogram and amniocentesis
Serial growth ultrasounds and bowel monitoring
MRI of fetal brain
Maternal glucose tolerance test

What is the expected prognosis?

Poor due to high rate of chromosomal abnormalities
Generally excellent (>90% survival) as this is usually an isolated defect
Depends entirely on liver involvement
Summary: This is gastroschisis - right paraumbilical defect, no sac, matted bowel, young mother. Unlike omphalocele, gastroschisis has LOW rate of chromosomal abnormalities (<1%) and is usually isolated. Main concerns are bowel complications (atresia, prolonged ileus). Prognosis is excellent with >90% survival. Monitor with serial ultrasounds for bowel dilation and plan delivery at tertiary center.

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: Which feature distinguishes omphalocele from gastroschisis?

A. Omphalocele has a peritoneal sac covering
B. Omphalocele is located to the right of the umbilicus
C. Omphalocele has lower risk of chromosomal abnormalities
D. Omphalocele bowel appears matted and inflamed
Correct: A. Omphalocele has a sac (peritoneum + amnion) covering the herniated contents. Gastroschisis has NO covering - the bowel is exposed to amniotic fluid.

Q2: A prenatal ultrasound shows a central abdominal wall defect with liver herniation into a membrane-covered sac. What additional testing is essential?

A. No additional testing needed
B. Fetal echocardiogram and karyotype
C. Maternal serum AFP only
D. Fetal MRI
Correct: B. This is omphalocele. 50-70% have associated anomalies, especially cardiac (50%) and chromosomal (30-40%). Fetal echo and karyotype are essential.

Q3: Which maternal demographic is classically associated with gastroschisis?

A. Young mothers (<20 years)
B. Advanced maternal age (>35 years)
C. Diabetic mothers
D. Mothers with prior chromosomal abnormality
Correct: A. Gastroschisis is associated with young maternal age (<20), smoking, and vasoactive drug use. Omphalocele is associated with advanced maternal age.

Q4: What is the embryologic mechanism of gastroschisis?

A. Failure of lateral body folds to fuse
B. Vascular accident (omphalomesenteric artery or right umbilical vein)
C. Persistence of physiologic herniation
D. Failure of midgut rotation
Correct: B. Gastroschisis results from a vascular accident - disruption of the omphalomesenteric artery or involution of the right umbilical vein, causing a paraumbilical defect (usually right-sided).

Q5: What is the "paint and wait" technique used for?

A. All gastroschisis cases
B. Giant omphalocele with liver involvement
C. Ruptured omphalocele sac
D. Small omphalocele without anomalies
Correct: B. "Paint and wait" involves applying silver sulfadiazine to the omphalocele sac, allowing epithelialization, with delayed repair later. It's used for giant omphaloceles where primary closure would cause abdominal compartment syndrome.
