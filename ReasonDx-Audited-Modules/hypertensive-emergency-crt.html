🎯 Definitions

Hypertensive EMERGENCY = Severe HTN (usually >180/120) WITH acute end-organ damage

Hypertensive URGENCY = Severe HTN WITHOUT acute end-organ damage
EmergencyUrgency
BPUsually >180/120Usually >180/120
End-organ damageYES (acute)NO
Treatment settingICU, IV medsCan treat outpatient/ED
BP reduction goalGradual (see below)Hours to days
The NUMBER doesn't define emergency - the END-ORGAN DAMAGE does!
A patient with BP 220/120 but no symptoms may be urgency.
A patient with BP 180/110 with encephalopathy IS an emergency.

End-Organ Damage Manifestations

End-Organ Damage: "HEAR"
Heart (ACS, acute HF/pulmonary edema)
Eyes (retinopathy, papilledema)
Artery (aortic dissection)
Renal (AKI) + CNS (encephalopathy, stroke, hemorrhage)

Workup

Assess for end-organ damage:
  • BMP (renal function)
  • Urinalysis (proteinuria, hematuria)
  • Troponin (cardiac)
  • CBC, peripheral smear (microangiopathic hemolytic anemia)
  • ECG
  • Chest X-ray (pulmonary edema, aortic widening)
  • Fundoscopic exam
  • CT head if neurologic symptoms
  • CT angiography if aortic dissection suspected

💊 Treatment

BP Goals

General principle: Lower BP gradually to prevent cerebral hypoperfusion!

Standard goal: Reduce MAP by ~25% in first hour, then to 160/100-110 over next 2-6 hours

Exceptions:
  • Aortic dissection: Rapid reduction to SBP <120 in 20 minutes
  • Ischemic stroke (no tPA): Only treat if >220/120
  • Ischemic stroke (receiving tPA): Goal <185/110 before, <180/105 after
  • ICH: Target SBP <140 if safe

🎯 Clinical Reasoning Trainer

Case: 58-year-old with Severe Chest Pain

A 58-year-old man presents with sudden onset severe tearing chest pain radiating to his back. BP is 210/120 in the right arm and 170/100 in the left arm. He is diaphoretic and in distress. HR 110.

What is the most likely diagnosis?

STEMI
Aortic dissection
Hypertensive urgency
Pulmonary embolism

What is the BP goal and timeframe?

Reduce MAP by 25% in first hour
SBP <120 within 20 minutes
SBP <180 over 24 hours
No BP treatment needed

What is the first-line treatment?

Nitroprusside alone
IV beta-blocker (esmolol) THEN add vasodilator
Hydralazine
Aspirin and heparin
Summary: This is aortic dissection - tearing pain radiating to back, BP differential between arms, hypertension. This is the ONE emergency where you need RAPID BP reduction (SBP <120 in 20 min). Start beta-blocker FIRST to reduce HR and prevent reflex tachycardia before adding vasodilator. Get CTA chest. Avoid anticoagulation until diagnosis confirmed.

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: What distinguishes hypertensive emergency from hypertensive urgency?

A. BP >180/120
B. Presence of acute end-organ damage
C. Symptoms of headache
D. Need for hospitalization
Correct: B. The defining feature is acute end-organ damage (brain, heart, kidney, eye, aorta). The BP number alone does not determine emergency vs urgency.

Q2: In aortic dissection, why must you give beta-blocker BEFORE vasodilator?

A. Beta-blockers work faster
B. To prevent reflex tachycardia which worsens aortic shear stress
C. Vasodilators are contraindicated
D. To reduce afterload first
Correct: B. Vasodilators cause reflex tachycardia, which increases aortic wall shear stress and worsens dissection. Give beta-blocker first to control HR, then add vasodilator to lower BP.

Q3: Which agent should be AVOIDED in cocaine-induced hypertensive emergency?

A. Benzodiazepines
B. Beta-blockers
C. Phentolamine
D. Nicardipine
Correct: B. Beta-blockers cause unopposed alpha-adrenergic stimulation in cocaine toxicity, worsening vasoconstriction and hypertension. Use benzodiazepines first, then phentolamine or nicardipine if needed.

Q4: What is the general BP reduction goal in most hypertensive emergencies?

A. Reduce MAP by ~25% in first hour
B. Normalize BP within 1 hour
C. SBP <120 immediately
D. Reduce BP over 1 week
Correct: A. Reduce MAP by ~25% in the first hour, then toward 160/100-110 over next 2-6 hours. Rapid reduction can cause cerebral hypoperfusion. Exception: aortic dissection requires rapid reduction to SBP <120.

Q5: A pregnant woman at 34 weeks with BP 180/110 and seizure. What is the treatment priority?

A. Immediate cesarean section
B. Magnesium sulfate and labetalol/hydralazine
C. Nitroprusside
D. ACE inhibitor
Correct: B. This is eclampsia. Magnesium sulfate is first-line for seizure prevention/treatment. Safe antihypertensives in pregnancy include labetalol and hydralazine. Nitroprusside can cause cyanide toxicity in fetus. ACE inhibitors are contraindicated in pregnancy.
