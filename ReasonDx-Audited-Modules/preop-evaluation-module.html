When to Get Cardiac Testing

Stepwise Approach:
  1. Is it emergency surgery? → Proceed, optimize perioperatively
  2. Does patient have ACS? → Delay elective surgery, treat ACS
  3. Low-risk surgery? → Proceed without testing
  4. Can patient do ≥4 METs? → Proceed without testing
  5. If poor/unknown functional capacity + elevated risk → Consider stress testing

Functional Capacity (METs)

  • 1-4 METs: Eating, dressing, walking indoors
  • 4-10 METs: Climbing stairs, walking 4 mph, heavy housework
  • >10 METs: Strenuous sports

≥4 METs = Good functional capacity

Risk Reduction Strategies

  • Smoking cessation: Ideally ≥8 weeks preop (even 24h helps)
  • COPD optimization: Continue bronchodilators, steroids if needed
  • OSA: Bring CPAP, consider monitored bed postop
  • Lung expansion: Incentive spirometry, early mobilization
⚠️ PFTs are NOT routine! Only indicated for: unexplained dyspnea, uncharacterized COPD, lung resection surgery.

Anticoagulation Management

Warfarin

  • Hold 5 days before surgery (INR <1.5)
  • Bridge with LMWH if: Mechanical mitral valve, recent VTE (<3 mo), high-risk AF
  • Most patients do NOT need bridging

DOACs

  • Low bleeding risk: Hold 24-48 hours
  • High bleeding risk: Hold 48-72 hours
  • Adjust for renal function (especially dabigatran)

Aspirin

  • Recent stent (<6 wks BMS, <6 mo DES): Continue
  • Primary prevention: Hold 7 days
  • Secondary prevention: Usually continue

🧪 Pre-Operative Testing

Routine testing is NOT recommended. Order tests based on patient conditions and surgical risk.

When to Order

TestIndications
CBCExpected blood loss, anemia symptoms, hematologic disease
BMP/CrRenal disease, DM, HTN, heart failure, diuretics, ACEi/ARB
CoagulationAnticoagulant use, liver disease, bleeding history
ECGKnown CAD, arrhythmia, structural heart disease, vascular surgery
CXRNew/unstable cardiopulmonary symptoms, thoracic surgery
Pregnancy testWomen of childbearing potential
⚠️ Avoid Routine Testing:
  • Routine CXR in healthy patients
  • Routine PFTs (unless lung resection)
  • Routine ECG in low-risk patients
  • Routine coagulation studies without indication

📝 Quiz

Q1: A patient can climb 2 flights of stairs without symptoms. This suggests:

A. Functional capacity ≥4 METs - can proceed without stress testing
B. Low functional capacity - needs stress testing
C. Should get echocardiogram
Climbing 2 flights of stairs = ≥4 METs = good functional capacity. Can proceed without cardiac testing even if elevated RCRI.

Q2: Beta-blockers in a patient already on them preoperatively should be:

A. Continued perioperatively
B. Held the day of surgery
C. Held 1 week before surgery
Continue beta-blockers if already on them - abrupt withdrawal increases MI risk. Do NOT start new beta-blockers day of surgery.

Q3: Metformin should be held perioperatively primarily due to risk of:

A. Hypoglycemia
B. Lactic acidosis (if renal function impaired)
C. Hyperglycemia
Metformin is held due to risk of lactic acidosis, especially if contrast used or renal function impaired. Resume 48h post if Cr stable.

Q4: Most patients on warfarin for atrial fibrillation:

A. Do NOT need bridging anticoagulation
B. Always need bridging with LMWH
C. Should not undergo elective surgery
BRIDGE trial showed most AF patients do NOT need bridging - bridging increases bleeding without reducing thromboembolism. Bridge only high-risk (mechanical mitral valve, recent VTE).
