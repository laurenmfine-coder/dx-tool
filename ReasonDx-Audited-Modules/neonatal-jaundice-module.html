📚 Overview

Neonatal jaundice affects ~60% of term and ~80% of preterm newborns. Most is benign physiologic jaundice, but severe hyperbilirubinemia can cause kernicterus - preventable bilirubin-induced brain damage.

Why Newborns Get Jaundiced

  • ↑ RBC turnover: Shorter lifespan (70-90 vs 120 days), higher Hct
  • Immature conjugation: Low UGT1A1 enzyme activity
  • ↑ Enterohepatic circulation: More β-glucuronidase, less gut bacteria
  • ↓ Albumin binding: Lower levels, competing substances

Key Definitions

TermDefinition
Unconjugated (indirect)Lipid-soluble, albumin-bound, crosses BBB when free
Conjugated (direct)Water-soluble, excreted in bile, ALWAYS pathologic if ↑
PhysiologicNormal, peaks day 3-5, resolves by 2 weeks
Pathologic<24h onset, rapid rise, or >2 weeks duration

🔬 Bilirubin Metabolism

RBC breakdown → Heme → Biliverdin → UNCONJUGATED BILIRUBIN (heme oxygenase) (biliverdin reductase) ↓ Binds ALBUMIN (lipid-soluble, can't excrete) ↓ LIVER: Conjugation with glucuronic acid (UGT1A1) ↓ CONJUGATED BILIRUBIN (water-soluble) ↓ BILE → Intestine → Urobilinogen (gut bacteria) ↓ Stercobilin (stool=brown) + Urobilin (urine)

🎯 Why Unconjugated is Dangerous: Lipid-soluble. When FREE (unbound to albumin), crosses BBB → deposits in basal ganglia → kernicterus.

↑ Free Unconjugated Bilirubin

  • Low albumin (prematurity, sepsis)
  • Acidosis (↓ albumin binding)
  • Displacing drugs: sulfonamides, ceftriaxone
  • Free fatty acids (IV lipids, stress)

⚖️ Physiologic vs Pathologic

✅ PHYSIOLOGIC

  • Appears >24 hours
  • Peaks day 3-5 (term)
  • Resolves 1-2 weeks
  • Rise <5 mg/dL/day
  • Peak <15 mg/dL
  • Unconjugated only
  • Baby well

❌ PATHOLOGIC

  • Appears <24 hours ⚠️
  • Rise >5 mg/dL/day
  • Peak >15 mg/dL
  • Persists >2 weeks
  • Direct >1 or >20%
  • Signs of illness
  • Needs workup!

🧪 Workup

Initial Labs - All Jaundiced Newborns

  • Total and direct bilirubin
  • Blood type and Coombs (mother and baby)
  • CBC with smear, reticulocyte count

If Hemolysis Suspected

  • Direct Coombs (DAT) - positive in isoimmune hemolysis
  • Peripheral smear - spherocytes, fragments
  • Reticulocyte count - elevated
  • G6PD level
  • Haptoglobin, LDH

If Conjugated Hyperbilirubinemia

Urgent workup - biliary atresia requires surgery <60 days!
  • Liver ultrasound (gallbladder, triangular cord sign)
  • HIDA scan
  • TORCH titers
  • Urine culture, metabolic screen
  • α1-antitrypsin level
  • Sweat chloride (CF)
Bili >95th percentile for age = plot on Bhutani nomogram

💡 Treatment

Phototherapy

Mechanism: Blue light (460-490nm) converts unconjugated bili to water-soluble photoisomers (lumirubin) excreted WITHOUT conjugation.

When to Start

  • Use Bhutani nomogram (hour-specific levels)
  • Lower threshold if: preterm, hemolysis, sepsis, acidosis

Phototherapy Checklist

  • Maximize skin exposure (diaper only)
  • Eye shields (retinal protection)
  • ↑ fluids (insensible losses)
  • Monitor temp (hyperthermia risk)
  • Check bili q4-12h

Exchange Transfusion

Indication: Bili approaching kernicterus levels (~25 mg/dL term, lower preterm) OR severe hemolysis with rapid rise despite phototherapy.
  • Double-volume exchange (160-200 mL/kg)
  • Removes bili AND antibody-coated RBCs
  • Risks: electrolytes, infection, NEC

🧠 Kernicterus

Definition: Bilirubin-induced neurologic dysfunction (BIND) from deposition in basal ganglia, hippocampus, brainstem nuclei.

Acute Bilirubin Encephalopathy

PhaseFeatures
Early (days 1-2)Lethargy, hypotonia, poor suck
IntermediateIrritability, hypertonia, fever, high-pitched cry
AdvancedOpisthotonus, seizures, apnea, coma

Chronic Bilirubin Encephalopathy (Kernicterus)

Classic Tetrad:
1. Choreoathetoid CP
2. Sensorineural hearing loss
3. Gaze palsy (upward)
4. Dental enamel dysplasia

Risk Factors for Kernicterus

  • Prematurity (immature BBB)
  • Hemolysis
  • Sepsis, acidosis
  • Hypoalbuminemia
  • G6PD deficiency
  • Asphyxia
Prevention is key! Universal bilirubin screening, risk assessment, appropriate follow-up, parent education on jaundice warning signs.

🎯 Quiz

Q1: Jaundice appearing in the first 24 hours suggests:

A. Physiologic jaundice
B. Breast milk jaundice
C. Hemolytic disease
D. Gilbert syndrome
C. Jaundice <24h is NEVER physiologic. Think hemolysis (ABO/Rh incompatibility, G6PD) or infection.

Q2: Which is water-soluble and cannot cross the BBB?

A. Unconjugated bilirubin
B. Conjugated bilirubin
C. Free bilirubin
D. Biliverdin
B. Conjugated (direct) bilirubin is water-soluble and cannot cross the BBB. Unconjugated is lipid-soluble and dangerous when free.

Q3: Direct bilirubin >1 mg/dL in a 2-week-old with pale stools suggests:

A. Breast milk jaundice
B. Physiologic jaundice
C. Biliary atresia
D. G6PD deficiency
C. Conjugated hyperbili + pale stools = biliary obstruction until proven otherwise. Biliary atresia needs surgery <60 days!

Q4: Phototherapy works by:

A. Inducing hepatic UGT1A1
B. Converting bili to water-soluble isomers
C. Increasing albumin binding
D. Destroying bilirubin molecules
B. Blue light converts unconjugated bili to lumirubin (water-soluble photoisomer) that can be excreted without conjugation.

Q5: The classic tetrad of chronic kernicterus includes all EXCEPT:

A. Choreoathetoid cerebral palsy
B. Sensorineural hearing loss
C. Spastic diplegia
D. Dental enamel dysplasia
C. Kernicterus causes CHOREOATHETOID (not spastic) CP. Tetrad: choreoathetosis, hearing loss, upward gaze palsy, enamel dysplasia.
