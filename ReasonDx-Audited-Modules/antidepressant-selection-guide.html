🎯 Antidepressant Classes

First-line agents: SSRIs and SNRIs
Response time: 2-4 weeks for initial effect; 6-8 weeks for full effect
Adequate trial: Therapeutic dose for ≥6-8 weeks before declaring failure
ClassMechanismExamples
SSRIsSelective serotonin reuptake inhibitionSertraline, fluoxetine, escitalopram, paroxetine, citalopram

💙 SSRIs - First Line

DrugUnique FeaturesConsiderations
Sertraline (Zoloft) Safest in cardiac disease; mild dopamine effect Good first choice; can cause more GI upset initially
Escitalopram (Lexapro) Most selective SSRI; fewest drug interactions QTc prolongation at higher doses; citalopram has more QT risk
Fluoxetine (Prozac) Long half-life (no taper needed); most activating Strong CYP2D6 inhibitor (many drug interactions)
Paroxetine (Paxil) Most anticholinergic SSRI; most sedating Worst withdrawal; avoid in pregnancy (cardiac defects); weight gain
Citalopram (Celexa) Clean pharmacology QTc prolongation; max 40mg (20mg in elderly, hepatic impairment)
Fluvoxamine (Luvox) Primarily for OCD Many CYP interactions

SSRI Class Side Effects

Side EffectManagement
Sexual dysfunctionSwitch agents, add bupropion, sildenafil, reduce dose, drug holiday
GI upsetTake with food; usually improves in 1-2 weeks
Insomnia/activationTake in morning; may need short-term sleep aid
Weight gainSwitch to more weight-neutral agent (bupropion)
Bleeding riskCaution with anticoagulants, NSAIDs
HyponatremiaCheck Na+ if elderly, diuretic use, symptoms
Discontinuation Syndrome: Flu-like symptoms, dizziness, paresthesias ("brain zaps"), irritability, insomnia. Worst with paroxetine (shortest half-life). Prevent by tapering over 2-4 weeks. Fluoxetine rarely causes this (long half-life).

TCAs (Tricyclic Antidepressants)

Examples: Amitriptyline, nortriptyline, imipramine, desipramine

Side effects: Anticholinergic (dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, confusion), orthostatic hypotension, cardiac conduction abnormalities, sedation, weight gain

TCAs are lethal in overdose! Cardiac arrhythmias, seizures. Avoid in patients with suicide risk. Treatment: sodium bicarbonate for QRS widening.

Current uses: Neuropathic pain, migraine prophylaxis, insomnia, treatment-resistant depression

MAOIs

Examples: Phenelzine, tranylcypromine, selegiline patch

Dietary restrictions: Must avoid tyramine-containing foods (aged cheese, cured meats, tap beer, soy sauce) → Hypertensive crisis
Drug interactions: Serotonergic drugs → Serotonin syndrome; sympathomimetics → Hypertensive crisis
Washout periods required when switching from/to other antidepressants

Current uses: Treatment-resistant depression, atypical depression

After First Agent Fails

Options after inadequate response:
  1. Optimize: Ensure adequate dose and duration
  2. Switch: To different SSRI or different class
  3. Augment: Add second agent (aripiprazole, lithium, bupropion, thyroid, atypical antipsychotic)
  4. Combine: Add second antidepressant (e.g., SSRI + bupropion or mirtazapine)

⚠️ Safety Concerns

Serotonin Syndrome
Cause: Excess serotonergic activity (usually from drug combinations)
Triad: Mental status changes + Autonomic instability + Neuromuscular abnormalities
Features: Agitation, hyperthermia, diaphoresis, tachycardia, hypertension, tremor, hyperreflexia, clonus, rigidity
Treatment: Stop offending agents, supportive care, cyproheptadine, benzodiazepines for agitation

Black Box Warning

All antidepressants carry FDA black box warning:
Increased risk of suicidal thinking and behavior in children, adolescents, and young adults (≤24 years) during initial treatment.

Monitor closely: Weekly for first 4 weeks, then biweekly for next 4 weeks, then at 12 weeks.

Drug Interactions

CombinationRisk
SSRI/SNRI + MAOISerotonin syndrome (contraindicated)
SSRI + triptansSerotonin syndrome (use cautiously)
SSRI + tramadolSerotonin syndrome; seizures
SSRI + anticoagulants/NSAIDsIncreased bleeding risk
Fluoxetine/paroxetine + CYP2D6 substratesIncreased drug levels (strong inhibitors)
Bupropion + stimulantsIncreased seizure risk

Pregnancy Considerations

Safest options: Sertraline (most data)
Avoid: Paroxetine (cardiac defects)
Third trimester exposure: Risk of neonatal adaptation syndrome (jitteriness, irritability, respiratory issues)

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: Which antidepressant is BEST for a patient with depression, chronic pain, and diabetic neuropathy?

A. Bupropion
B. Duloxetine
C. Fluoxetine
D. Mirtazapine
Correct: B. Duloxetine (SNRI) is FDA-approved for depression, diabetic neuropathy, fibromyalgia, and chronic pain. The noradrenergic component helps with pain conditions.

Q2: A patient on an SSRI develops sexual dysfunction. Which switch would most likely resolve this?

A. Bupropion
B. Paroxetine
C. Venlafaxine
D. Citalopram
Correct: A. Bupropion has the lowest rate of sexual dysfunction among antidepressants (it's not serotonergic). It works via norepinephrine and dopamine. Paroxetine has the highest rate of sexual side effects.

Q3: Which antidepressant should be AVOIDED in a patient with an eating disorder and purging behavior?

A. Bupropion
B. Sertraline
C. Escitalopram
D. Mirtazapine
Correct: A. Bupropion lowers the seizure threshold and is contraindicated in patients with bulimia/purging behaviors due to increased seizure risk from electrolyte abnormalities.

Q4: A patient on phenelzine (MAOI) eats aged cheese and develops severe hypertension, headache, and diaphoresis. What happened?

A. Serotonin syndrome
B. Tyramine reaction (hypertensive crisis)
C. Allergic reaction
D. Drug overdose
Correct: B. MAOIs prevent tyramine breakdown. Tyramine in aged foods causes norepinephrine release → severe hypertension. This is why strict dietary restrictions are required with MAOIs.

Q5: Which SSRI has the longest half-life and typically does NOT require tapering?

A. Fluoxetine
B. Paroxetine
C. Sertraline
D. Escitalopram
Correct: A. Fluoxetine has a half-life of 4-6 days (active metabolite even longer). This means it self-tapers and rarely causes discontinuation syndrome. Paroxetine has the shortest half-life and worst discontinuation syndrome.
