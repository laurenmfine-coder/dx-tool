OR

Empiric Treatment - ICU

β-lactam + macrolide (ceftriaxone + azithromycin)
OR
β-lactam + respiratory FQ

Add MRSA coverage (vancomycin or linezolid) if risk factors
Add Pseudomonas coverage if risk factors (piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, meropenem)

Duration

  • Minimum 5 days
  • Should be afebrile 48-72 hours and clinically stable before stopping
  • Longer for severe disease, complications, certain pathogens

🏥 Hospital-Acquired & Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

HAP: Pneumonia occurring ≥48 hours after admission
VAP: Pneumonia occurring >48 hours after intubation

Risk Factors for MDR Pathogens

  • IV antibiotics within prior 90 days
  • Hospitalization ≥5 days
  • Septic shock at time of VAP
  • ARDS preceding VAP
  • Acute renal replacement therapy prior to VAP
  • High local MDR prevalence (>10-20%)

Empiric Treatment - HAP (not high MDR risk)

Option
Piperacillin-tazobactam
Cefepime
Levofloxacin
Meropenem (if local ESBL concern)

Empiric Treatment - VAP or High MDR Risk

Two anti-pseudomonal agents from different classes:
  • Anti-pseudomonal β-lactam (pip-tazo, cefepime, ceftazidime, meropenem)
  • PLUS FQ or aminoglycoside
PLUS MRSA coverage: Vancomycin or linezolid

De-escalation

  • Obtain cultures before antibiotics
  • Narrow based on culture results
  • Duration: 7 days (shorter if good clinical response)
  • Procalcitonin can guide de-escalation

Legionella - Special Considerations

Clues: GI symptoms (diarrhea), hyponatremia, elevated LFTs, CNS changes
Exposure: Water systems, cooling towers
Diagnosis: Urine antigen (only detects serogroup 1)
Treatment: Fluoroquinolone or azithromycin

HAP/VAP Pathogens

  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • MRSA
  • Acinetobacter
  • ESBL-producing Enterobacteriaceae
  • Klebsiella, E. coli

Aspiration Pneumonia

Risk factors: Altered consciousness, dysphagia, GERD, NG tube
Location: Right lower lobe (most common), posterior segments
Pathogens: Oral anaerobes, Strep, GNRs
Treatment: Ampicillin-sulbactam, or clindamycin + FQ, or moxifloxacin alone

Immunocompromised Hosts

PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii)

Presentation: Subacute dyspnea, dry cough, hypoxia out of proportion to CXR
CXR: Bilateral interstitial infiltrates
Diagnosis: Induced sputum or BAL with methenamine silver or DFA
Treatment: TMP-SMX × 21 days; add steroids if PaO2 <70 or A-a gradient >35

Influenza Pneumonia

  • Test with PCR (rapid tests less sensitive)
  • Treat with oseltamivir within 48 hours (can still help later in severe disease)
  • Watch for secondary bacterial pneumonia (especially Staph aureus)

COVID-19 Pneumonia

  • Bilateral ground-glass opacities on CT
  • Treat with dexamethasone if requiring supplemental O2
  • Consider remdesivir in hospitalized patients
  • Add empiric antibiotics if bacterial superinfection suspected

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: CURB-65 score of 3-5 indicates:

A. Outpatient treatment
B. Hospitalization, consider ICU
C. Short observation only
Correct: B. CURB-65 of 3-5 indicates high mortality risk (15-40%) requiring hospitalization with ICU consideration for scores of 4-5.

Q2: First-line empiric treatment for outpatient CAP in a healthy adult?

A. Amoxicillin
B. Piperacillin-tazobactam
C. Vancomycin
Correct: A. Amoxicillin, doxycycline, or macrolide (if local resistance <25%) for healthy outpatients without comorbidities.

Q3: A patient with CAP has diarrhea and hyponatremia. What pathogen should you suspect?

A. Legionella
B. Strep pneumoniae
C. Mycoplasma
Correct: A. Legionella classically presents with GI symptoms (diarrhea), hyponatremia, and elevated LFTs along with pneumonia.

Q4: What is the minimum duration of antibiotics for CAP?

A. 5 days
B. 10 days
C. 14 days
Correct: A. Minimum 5 days; patient should be afebrile 48-72 hours and clinically stable before stopping.

Q5: What coverage should be added for severe CAP in ICU if MRSA is suspected?

A. Azithromycin
B. Vancomycin or linezolid
C. Metronidazole
Correct: B. Add vancomycin or linezolid for MRSA coverage in severe CAP with risk factors (prior MRSA, post-influenza, cavitation).
