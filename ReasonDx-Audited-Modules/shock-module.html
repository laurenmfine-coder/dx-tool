📊 Types of Shock

Shock = Inadequate tissue perfusion resulting in cellular hypoxia. Defined by end-organ dysfunction, NOT just low BP.

🩸 Hypovolemic Shock

  • Cause: Volume loss (hemorrhage, dehydration, burns)
  • Mechanism: ↓ Preload → ↓ CO
  • Signs: Tachycardia, flat neck veins, cool extremities

💔 Cardiogenic Shock

  • Cause: Pump failure (MI, cardiomyopathy, arrhythmia, valve failure)
  • Mechanism: ↓ Contractility → ↓ CO
  • Signs: JVD, pulmonary edema, cool extremities

🫁 Obstructive Shock

  • Cause: Mechanical obstruction (PE, tamponade, tension PTX)
  • Mechanism: ↓ Venous return or ↓ Outflow
  • Signs: JVD, pulsus paradoxus (tamponade), hypoxia (PE)

🦠 Distributive Shock

  • Cause: Vasodilation (sepsis, anaphylaxis, neurogenic, adrenal crisis)
  • Mechanism: ↓ SVR → Relative hypovolemia
  • Signs: Warm extremities (early sepsis), flash cap refill, wide pulse pressure

Key Distinction:
Cold shock (hypovolemic, cardiogenic) = High SVR, cool extremities
Warm shock (distributive) = Low SVR, warm extremities, wide pulse pressure

Lactate

  • Marker of tissue hypoperfusion
  • Normal: <2 mmol/L
  • >4 mmol/L = severe; associated with high mortality
  • Lactate clearance >10%/2h = good prognostic sign

⚠️ Key Points:
  • Norepinephrine = first-line for most shock (except cardiogenic)
  • Dobutamine = add for low CO; causes ↓ BP alone (use with pressor)
  • Vasopressin = catecholamine-sparing; don't titrate (fixed dose)
  • Phenylephrine = pure vasoconstriction; use with adequate CO

⚕️ Treatment by Shock Type

Hypovolemic Shock

  1. IV access (2 large-bore)
  2. Crystalloid resuscitation (30 mL/kg bolus)
  3. Blood products if hemorrhagic (MTP: 1:1:1)
  4. Source control (surgery, GI intervention)
  5. Vasopressors only after volume repleted

Cardiogenic Shock

  1. Caution with fluids (may worsen pulmonary edema)
  2. Vasopressor (norepinephrine) if hypotensive
  3. Inotrope (dobutamine) if low CO
  4. Revascularization if STEMI (PCI/CABG)
  5. Consider mechanical support (IABP, Impella, ECMO)
  6. Treat underlying cause (arrhythmia, valve)

Obstructive Shock

  • Tension PTX: Needle decompression → Chest tube
  • Tamponade: Pericardiocentesis → OR
  • Massive PE: Anticoagulation ± thrombolytics ± thrombectomy

Septic Shock (Hour-1 Bundle)

  1. Measure lactate
  2. Blood cultures before antibiotics
  3. Broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour
  4. Crystalloid 30 mL/kg for hypotension or lactate ≥4
  5. Vasopressors (norepinephrine) if MAP <65 after fluids

Anaphylactic Shock

  1. Epinephrine IM 0.3-0.5 mg (first and most important)
  2. IV fluids (massive vasodilation)
  3. Airway management
  4. Antihistamines (H1 + H2 blockers)
  5. Steroids (prevent biphasic reaction)

📝 Quiz

Q1: A patient in shock has JVD, hypotension, and clear lungs. Most likely type?

A. Obstructive (tamponade, PE, tension PTX)
B. Hypovolemic
C. Cardiogenic
JVD + hypotension + clear lungs = obstructive shock. Cardiogenic would have pulmonary edema. Hypovolemic has flat neck veins.

Q2: First-line vasopressor for septic shock:

A. Norepinephrine
B. Dopamine
C. Phenylephrine
Norepinephrine is first-line for septic shock (and most shock states). Dopamine is associated with more arrhythmias.

Q3: In cardiogenic shock, which agent improves contractility?

A. Phenylephrine
B. Dobutamine
C. Vasopressin
Dobutamine (β1 agonist) increases contractility. Phenylephrine is pure vasoconstrictor. Vasopressin only increases SVR.

Q4: First treatment for anaphylactic shock:

A. Epinephrine IM
B. IV fluids
C. Diphenhydramine
Epinephrine IM is the FIRST and most important treatment for anaphylaxis. Delay kills. Antihistamines and fluids are adjuncts.
