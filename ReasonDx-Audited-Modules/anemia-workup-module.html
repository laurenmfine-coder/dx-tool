🎯 Systematic Approach

Step 1: Confirm anemia
Hgb <13 g/dL (men) or <12 g/dL (women)

Step 2: Look at MCV
  • MCV <80: Microcytic
  • MCV 80-100: Normocytic
  • MCV >100: Macrocytic

Reticulocyte Count

Reticulocyte Index (RI):
RI = Retic % × (Patient Hct / Normal Hct) × (1/Maturation factor)

Interpretation:
  • RI > 2%: Appropriate response (hemolysis or blood loss)
  • RI < 2%: Inadequate response (production problem)

Iron Deficiency Anemia

Most common cause of anemia worldwide
Causes:
  • Blood loss (GI most common in adults - must rule out malignancy!)
  • Menstrual loss (premenopausal women)
  • Decreased absorption (celiac, gastric bypass)
  • Increased demand (pregnancy, growth)
  • Poor dietary intake
Treatment: Oral iron (ferrous sulfate 325mg TID) × 3-6 months; address underlying cause

Thalassemia vs Iron Deficiency

Mentzer Index = MCV / RBC count
  • > 13 suggests iron deficiency
  • < 13 suggests thalassemia
RDW: Elevated in iron deficiency, normal in thalassemia
Hemoglobin electrophoresis: Confirms thalassemia (↑HbA2 in beta-thal trait)

⭕ Normocytic Anemia (MCV 80-100)

First question: What's the reticulocyte count?
  • High retics → Hemolysis or acute blood loss
  • Low retics → Production problem

B12 Deficiency

Causes:
  • Pernicious anemia (most common) - autoimmune gastritis
  • Gastrectomy, gastric bypass
  • Terminal ileum disease (Crohn's)
  • Strict vegan diet (rare)
  • Metformin, PPIs (mild)
Neurologic findings: Subacute combined degeneration (dorsal columns + corticospinal)
Paresthesias, ataxia, dementia - MAY BE IRREVERSIBLE

Treatment: IM B12 (cyanocobalamin) or high-dose oral

Folate Deficiency

Causes:
  • Poor dietary intake (alcoholics, elderly)
  • Malabsorption (celiac, tropical sprue)
  • Increased demand (pregnancy, hemolysis)
  • Drugs (methotrexate, phenytoin, trimethoprim)
NO neurologic findings (distinguishes from B12)
Treatment: Oral folic acid 1-5mg daily
Warning: If B12 deficiency, do NOT give folate alone - it can worsen neurologic damage!
Always check B12 before treating with folate.

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A patient has microcytic anemia with low iron, high TIBC, and low ferritin. What's the diagnosis?

A. Iron deficiency anemia
B. Anemia of chronic disease
C. Thalassemia
D. Sideroblastic anemia
Correct: A. Iron deficiency shows ↓iron, ↑TIBC, ↓ferritin. ACD shows ↓iron, ↓TIBC, ↑ferritin. Thalassemia has normal iron studies.

Q2: What peripheral smear finding indicates microangiopathic hemolytic anemia?

A. Spherocytes
B. Schistocytes
C. Target cells
D. Rouleaux
Correct: B. Schistocytes (fragmented RBCs) indicate MAHA - seen in TTP, HUS, DIC, and mechanical heart valves.

Q3: Which deficiency causes neurologic symptoms (subacute combined degeneration)?

A. B12
B. Folate
C. Iron
D. B6
Correct: A. B12 deficiency causes subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord. Folate deficiency does NOT cause neurologic symptoms - this helps distinguish them.

Q4: An alcoholic patient has macrocytic anemia. What lab would suggest megaloblastic cause?

A. Low reticulocyte count
B. Hypersegmented neutrophils
C. Target cells
D. Elevated LDH
Correct: B. Hypersegmented neutrophils (≥5 lobes) indicate megaloblastic anemia (B12 or folate deficiency), not just alcohol-related macrocytosis.

Q5: A patient has normocytic anemia with elevated reticulocytes, elevated LDH, and low haptoglobin. What's happening?

A. Hemolysis
B. Iron deficiency
C. B12 deficiency
D. Anemia of chronic disease
Correct: A. High retics + high LDH + low haptoglobin = hemolysis. The reticulocyte response and hemolysis markers are classic.
