🎯 Approach to AMS

Altered Mental Status = Any change from baseline cognitive function, including confusion, decreased consciousness, agitation, or personality change.
AMS Differential: "AEIOU TIPS"
Alcohol • Epilepsy/electrolytes/encephalopathy • Infection/insulin
Overdose/oxygen • Uremia
Trauma/temperature • Infection • Psychiatric/poison • Stroke/shock

Delirium is a medical emergency! It indicates underlying illness and carries high mortality if untreated. Always look for the cause.

🔬 AMS Workup

Immediate Assessment

Check immediately:
  • Fingerstick glucose (rule out hypoglycemia)
  • Vitals including temperature and O2 sat
  • Airway, breathing, circulation
  • Pupils (miosis, mydriasis, asymmetry)
  • Signs of trauma

History (from family/caregivers)

  • Baseline cognitive function
  • Timeline of changes
  • Recent illness, fever, falls
  • Medications (new, stopped, overdose potential)
  • Substance use
  • Recent surgery/procedures

Initial Labs

Standard workup:
  • Glucose (already checked)
  • CBC
  • BMP (electrolytes, BUN/Cr, glucose)
  • Urinalysis
  • LFTs, ammonia (if hepatic encephalopathy suspected)
  • TSH
  • Urine drug screen
  • Blood alcohol level
  • ABG/VBG if respiratory concerns

🔥 Delirium Management

CAM Criteria (Confusion Assessment Method)

Delirium diagnosis requires:
1. Acute onset AND fluctuating course
2. Inattention
AND EITHER:
3. Disorganized thinking OR
4. Altered level of consciousness

Delirium Subtypes

TypeFeaturesCauses
HyperactiveAgitation, restlessness, hallucinationsAlcohol withdrawal, stimulants, anticholinergics
HypoactiveLethargy, decreased activity, quiet confusionMetabolic, organ failure, sedatives (often missed!)
MixedFluctuates between hyper and hypoMost common

Non-Pharmacologic Management

First-line interventions:
  • Treat underlying cause
  • Reorientation (clock, calendar, family photos)
  • Normalize sleep-wake cycle (lights on during day)
  • Minimize tethers (Foley, IVs, restraints)
  • Ensure glasses/hearing aids available
  • Early mobilization
  • Avoid anticholinergic medications
  • Avoid benzodiazepines (except alcohol/benzo withdrawal)
  • Pain control
  • Prevent constipation/urinary retention

Pharmacologic Management (if needed)

For severe agitation when non-pharmacologic measures fail:
  • Antipsychotics: Haloperidol 0.5-1mg, quetiapine, olanzapine
  • Use lowest effective dose, shortest duration
  • Monitor QTc with haloperidol
  • Avoid in Parkinson's/DLB (use quetiapine if needed)
For alcohol/benzo withdrawal: Benzodiazepines (symptom-triggered)

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: What is the hallmark distinguishing feature of delirium from dementia?

A. Impaired attention
B. Memory loss
C. Hallucinations
D. Progressive course
Correct: A. Impaired attention is the hallmark of delirium. Patients cannot focus or sustain attention. In dementia, attention is relatively preserved early. Delirium also has acute onset and fluctuating course.

Q2: An 80-year-old nursing home patient is "just not acting right" per staff. What should you check FIRST?

A. Fingerstick glucose
B. CT head
C. Lumbar puncture
D. EEG
Correct: A. Hypoglycemia is immediately life-threatening and easily treatable. Always check fingerstick glucose first in any patient with altered mental status.

Q3: Visual hallucinations in an altered patient suggest:

A. Schizophrenia
B. Delirium or organic cause
C. Major depression
D. Anxiety disorder
Correct: B. Visual hallucinations suggest an organic/medical cause such as delirium, substance intoxication/withdrawal, or neurological disease. Primary psychiatric conditions like schizophrenia more commonly cause auditory hallucinations.

Q4: What is the FIRST-LINE treatment for delirium?

A. Haloperidol
B. Lorazepam
C. Treat underlying cause + non-pharmacologic measures
D. Physical restraints
Correct: C. The cornerstone of delirium treatment is identifying and treating the underlying cause, plus non-pharmacologic interventions (reorientation, sleep hygiene, early mobilization). Medications are reserved for severe agitation when needed for safety.

Q5: Which finding suggests hepatic encephalopathy?

A. Asterixis
B. Pinpoint pupils
C. Neck stiffness
D. Unilateral weakness
Correct: A. Asterixis (flapping tremor) is characteristic of metabolic encephalopathies, especially hepatic and uremic encephalopathy. It's elicited by having the patient hold their arms outstretched with wrists dorsiflexed.
