📚 Principles of Teratology

A teratogen is any agent that can cause abnormal development. The effects depend on: timing of exposure (critical period), dose, duration, and genetic susceptibility of the fetus.

Wilson's Six Principles of Teratology

  1. Susceptibility depends on genotype - both maternal and fetal genetics affect response
  2. Susceptibility varies with developmental stage - critical periods exist for each organ system
  3. Teratogenic agents act in specific ways - mechanisms include cell death, altered growth, disrupted differentiation
  4. Final manifestations are death, malformation, growth restriction, or functional deficit
  5. Access of agent to developing tissue depends on placental transfer
  6. Dose-response relationship exists - threshold below which no effect, increasing severity with dose

Timing of Exposure - The Key Concept

Pre-implantation (Weeks 1-2): "All or none" period
  • Exposure either kills the embryo (spontaneous abortion) or has no effect
  • Surviving cells can compensate - no malformations
Embryonic Period (Weeks 3-8): MAXIMUM SENSITIVITY
  • Organogenesis - all major organs forming
  • Exposure causes STRUCTURAL malformations
  • Most critical time - often before pregnancy is recognized!
Fetal Period (Week 9 - Birth): Continued vulnerability
  • Major structures formed, but growth and maturation continue
  • Brain, eyes, teeth, genitalia still developing
  • Exposure causes FUNCTIONAL deficits and growth restriction
🎯 Clinical Pearl: The most vulnerable period is weeks 3-8, when organogenesis occurs. Most women don't know they're pregnant until 4-6 weeks. This is why pre-conception counseling and avoiding teratogens when trying to conceive is crucial.

🦠 TORCH Infections

Toxoplasmosis, Other (Syphilis, VZV, Parvovirus), Rubella, CMV, Herpes/HIV

🐱 Toxoplasmosis

Source: Cat feces, undercooked meat

Classic Triad: Chorioretinitis, hydrocephalus, intracranial calcifications (diffuse)

Other features: Seizures, hepatosplenomegaly, jaundice

Prevention: Avoid cat litter, cook meat thoroughly

Memory tip: Toxo = "Trio" = 3 findings. Calcifications are DIFFUSE (scattered throughout brain).

🌀 Syphilis (Treponema pallidum)

Early congenital: Hepatosplenomegaly, snuffles (bloody rhinitis), rash, osteochondritis

Late congenital: Hutchinson teeth, mulberry molars, saddle nose, saber shins, deafness

Treatment: Penicillin (safe in pregnancy)

Hutchinson's triad: Notched incisors + interstitial keratitis + CN VIII deafness

💉 Rubella (German Measles) ⭐

Highest risk: First trimester exposure (up to 85% affected if infected <11 weeks)

Classic Triad: Cataracts, Cardiac defects (PDA, PS), Cochlear deafness

Other: "Blueberry muffin" rash (extramedullary hematopoiesis), microcephaly, intellectual disability

Prevention: MMR vaccine BEFORE pregnancy (live vaccine - contraindicated in pregnancy)

HIGH-YIELD: The 3 C's of rubella - Cataracts, Cardiac, Cochlear deafness

🧬 CMV (Cytomegalovirus) ⭐

Most common congenital infection (~1% of newborns)

Classic features: Periventricular calcifications, microcephaly, chorioretinitis, hearing loss

Other: Hepatosplenomegaly, thrombocytopenia, "blueberry muffin" rash, ventriculomegaly

Outcome: #1 infectious cause of congenital hearing loss and intellectual disability

CMV vs Toxo calcifications: CMV = PERIVENTRICULAR | Toxo = DIFFUSE/scattered

🔥 Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV)

Transmission: Usually during delivery (passing through infected birth canal)

Features: Skin vesicles, encephalitis, disseminated disease

Prevention: C-section if active genital lesions at time of labor

Note: Rarely causes congenital malformations; mainly neonatal infection

🩸 Parvovirus B19 (Fifth Disease)

Risk: Fetal anemia → hydrops fetalis (2nd trimester highest risk)

Mechanism: Infects erythroid precursors → aplastic crisis in fetus

Management: Monitor with serial ultrasounds, intrauterine transfusion if severe

💧 Varicella Zoster (Chickenpox)

Congenital varicella syndrome: Limb hypoplasia, cutaneous scars, eye abnormalities

Risk period: First 20 weeks (rare after this)

Prevention: Varicella vaccine before pregnancy (live vaccine)

💊 Medication Teratogens

Always weigh benefits vs risks. Some conditions (epilepsy, bipolar disorder) require treatment during pregnancy, and uncontrolled disease may be more harmful than medication. Use lowest effective dose.

✅ Generally Safe Medications

  • Acetaminophen - analgesic of choice
  • Penicillins, Cephalosporins, Erythromycin - antibiotics of choice
  • Insulin - does not cross placenta
  • Heparin/LMWH - does not cross placenta (unlike warfarin)
  • Levothyroxine - essential for fetal brain development
  • Prenatal vitamins with folic acid - protective!

🍺 Substances & Environmental Teratogens

🍷 Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorder (FASD) ⭐⭐

#1 preventable cause of intellectual disability

Features of Fetal Alcohol Syndrome (FAS):

  • Facial: Smooth philtrum, thin vermilion border, short palpebral fissures
  • Growth: Pre- and postnatal growth restriction
  • CNS: Microcephaly, intellectual disability, behavioral problems
  • Cardiac: VSD, ASD

Safe amount of alcohol in pregnancy: NONE. No safe threshold established.

FAS Face: "Smooth Thin Short"
Smooth philtrum, Thin upper lip, Short palpebral fissures

🚬 Tobacco

Effects:

  • Intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR)
  • Preterm birth
  • Placental abruption, placenta previa
  • SIDS risk increased
  • Orofacial clefts (modest association)

Mechanism: Nicotine causes vasoconstriction; CO reduces oxygen delivery

💉 Cocaine

Effects:

  • Placental abruption (vasoconstriction)
  • IUGR, preterm birth
  • Limb reduction defects (vascular disruption)
  • GU abnormalities
  • Neurobehavioral effects

☢️ Radiation

Threshold: >10 rad (100 mGy) associated with teratogenicity

Most sensitive: Weeks 8-15 (CNS development)

Effects: Microcephaly, intellectual disability, growth restriction

Diagnostic imaging: Chest X-ray, CT of extremities = low risk. Avoid CT abdomen/pelvis if possible.

♨️ Hyperthermia

Source: Fever, hot tubs, saunas

Effects: Neural tube defects (especially weeks 3-4)

Prevention: Treat fevers with acetaminophen; avoid prolonged hot tub use

🎯 Test Your Knowledge

Question 1

A pregnant woman is found to have periventricular calcifications on fetal ultrasound. Which TORCH infection is most likely?

A. Toxoplasmosis
B. CMV
C. Rubella
D. Herpes simplex
Answer: B. CMV causes PERIVENTRICULAR calcifications. Toxoplasmosis causes DIFFUSE/scattered intracranial calcifications. This is a classic board distinction!

Question 2

A newborn has smooth philtrum, thin upper lip, and short palpebral fissures. What is the diagnosis?

A. Fetal alcohol syndrome
B. Fetal hydantoin syndrome
C. Congenital rubella
D. Congenital CMV
Answer: A. The classic FAS facial features are: smooth philtrum, thin vermilion border (upper lip), and short palpebral fissures. FAS is the #1 preventable cause of intellectual disability.

Question 3

Which antiepileptic drug has the HIGHEST risk of neural tube defects?

A. Lamotrigine
B. Valproic acid
C. Levetiracetam
D. Carbamazepine
Answer: B. Valproic acid has the highest risk of neural tube defects (1-2%) among AEDs. It should be avoided in pregnancy if possible, especially in the first trimester. High-dose folic acid (4mg) is recommended for all women on AEDs.

Question 4

The "classic triad" of congenital rubella syndrome includes all EXCEPT:

A. Cataracts
B. Sensorineural deafness
C. Periventricular calcifications
D. Cardiac defects (PDA)
Answer: C. The rubella triad is Cataracts, Cardiac defects (PDA, pulmonary stenosis), and Cochlear/sensorineural deafness (the 3 C's). PERIVENTRICULAR calcifications are associated with CMV, not rubella.

Question 5

During which period is a teratogen exposure most likely to cause major structural malformations?

A. Pre-implantation (weeks 1-2)
B. Embryonic period (weeks 3-8)
C. Early fetal period (weeks 9-16)
D. Late fetal period (weeks 17-38)
Answer: B. The embryonic period (weeks 3-8) is when organogenesis occurs and is the time of MAXIMUM sensitivity for structural malformations. Pre-implantation is "all or none" (death or no effect). Fetal period exposures cause functional deficits and growth restriction.
