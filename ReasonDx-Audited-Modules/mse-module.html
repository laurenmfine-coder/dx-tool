🎯 MSE Fundamentals

The MSE is the psychiatric equivalent of the physical exam - a structured, point-in-time assessment of psychological functioning.
MSE Components: "ASEPTIC"
Appearance/Behavior • Speech • Emotion (mood/affect)
Perception • Thought (content/process) • Insight/Judgment • Cognition

Key Principles

  • Observation begins when patient enters the room
  • Separate observed (affect) from reported (mood)
  • Document specific examples, not just labels
  • Note what is ABSENT as well as present
  • Compare to patient's baseline when possible

📋 MSE Components

1. Appearance

Observe: Age (stated vs apparent), grooming, hygiene, dress, body habitus, distinguishing features, eye contact

"45-year-old man appearing older than stated age, disheveled with unkempt hair, wearing soiled clothing, poor eye contact"
FindingConsider
Poor hygiene/self-neglectDepression, psychosis, dementia, substance use
Bright/flamboyant dressMania, histrionic personality

4. Mood & Affect

Mood = Patient's SUBJECTIVE emotional state (ask: "How have you been feeling?")
Affect = OBJECTIVE observation of emotional expression

Affect Descriptors

DimensionOptions
QualityEuthymic, dysphoric, euphoric, anxious, irritable, angry
RangeFull, restricted, blunted, flat
CongruenceCongruent or incongruent with mood/content
ReactivityReactive (changes with topic) or non-reactive
StabilityStable or labile
Incongruent affect (e.g., laughing while describing trauma) may indicate psychosis, dissociation, or la belle indifférence.

🧩 Cognitive Assessment

Cognition includes: Alertness, orientation, attention, memory, language, visuospatial, executive function

Screening Tools

ToolScore RangeTimeBest For
Mini-Mental State Exam (MMSE)0-30 (≤24 abnormal)10 minDementia screening (copyrighted)
Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA)0-30 (≤25 abnormal)10 minMild cognitive impairment; more sensitive
Mini-Cog0-5 (≤2 abnormal)3 minQuick dementia screen (3-word recall + clock)

Domains to Assess

Orientation

Person → Name, age, DOB
Place → Building, city, state
Time → Date, day, month, year, season
Situation → Why are you here?

Orientation is lost in order: Time → Place → Person (remembered as "Time flies, Place crawls, Person stays")

Attention & Concentration

  • Serial 7s: Subtract 7 from 100 repeatedly (93, 86, 79...)
  • Spell WORLD backward: D-L-R-O-W
  • Digit span: Repeat numbers forward (normal ≥5) and backward (normal ≥3)
  • Months backward: December, November, October...

Memory

Immediate: Repeat 3 words back immediately
Short-term (working): Recall 3 words after 5 minutes
Long-term: Historical facts, personal history

Delirium: Impaired attention + memory
Dementia: Memory impaired, attention relatively preserved initially
Depression ("pseudodementia"): Poor effort, "I don't know" answers

Language

  • Naming: Point to objects (pen, watch)
  • Repetition: "No ifs, ands, or buts"
  • Comprehension: Follow commands
  • Fluency: Name animals in 1 minute (normal >12)

Executive Function

  • Clock drawing: Draw clock showing 11:10
  • Similarities: How are apple and orange alike?
  • Proverb interpretation: Concrete vs abstract
  • Go/No-Go: "Tap once when I tap once, don't tap when I tap twice"

Clock Drawing Test

Scoring (multiple systems, simplified):
  • Circle drawn correctly (1 point)
  • Numbers in correct position (1 point)
  • All 12 numbers present (1 point)
  • Hands pointing to correct time (1 point)
  • Hour hand shorter than minute hand (1 point)
Tests visuospatial skills, executive function, comprehension

Red Flags

Psychiatric emergencies requiring immediate action:
  • Active suicidal ideation with plan
  • Homicidal ideation with specific target
  • Command hallucinations to harm self/others
  • Catatonia (can be life-threatening)
  • Acute psychosis with agitation
  • Delirium (indicates underlying medical issue)

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A patient describes feeling "on top of the world" but you observe them crying during the interview. This is an example of:

A. Labile affect
B. Blunted affect
C. Incongruent affect
D. Flat affect
Correct: C. Incongruent affect means the observed emotional expression doesn't match the stated mood or thought content. The patient reports euphoria but displays sadness (crying).

Q2: A patient's speech goes from topic to topic with tenuous connections, and you can barely follow along. They speak rapidly and can't be interrupted. This is:

A. Flight of ideas
B. Loose associations
C. Tangentiality
D. Circumstantiality
Correct: A. Flight of ideas involves rapid speech with topic shifts that have tenuous but followable connections - classic for mania. Loose associations have NO logical connection between ideas (schizophrenia).

Q3: Visual hallucinations are most suggestive of:

A. Schizophrenia
B. Major depression
C. Delirium or organic cause
D. Panic disorder
Correct: C. Visual hallucinations suggest an organic/medical cause such as delirium, substance intoxication/withdrawal, or dementia. Primary psychiatric disorders (schizophrenia) more commonly cause auditory hallucinations.

Q4: Which cognitive domain is most impaired in delirium?

A. Attention
B. Long-term memory
C. Language
D. Praxis
Correct: A. Impaired attention is the hallmark of delirium. Patients cannot maintain focus, are easily distracted, and have fluctuating awareness. This distinguishes it from dementia where attention is relatively preserved early.

Q5: A patient states "The news anchor was sending me secret messages through the TV." This is an example of:

A. Auditory hallucination
B. Ideas of reference
C. Thought broadcasting
D. Illusion
Correct: B. Ideas of reference are beliefs that external events, objects, or people have special personal significance - like believing TV messages are directed at oneself. This is different from thought broadcasting (believing others can hear your thoughts).

Q6: A patient with depression performs poorly on cognitive testing, answering "I don't know" to most questions without trying. This pattern is called:

A. Dementia
B. Pseudodementia
C. Delirium
D. Malingering
Correct: B. Pseudodementia refers to cognitive impairment due to depression. Unlike true dementia, patients give up easily ("I don't know"), have relatively abrupt onset, and improve with depression treatment.
