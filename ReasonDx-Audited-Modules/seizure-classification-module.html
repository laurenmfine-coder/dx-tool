📋 ILAE 2017 Classification

Three Key Questions:
  1. Where does the seizure BEGIN? (Focal vs Generalized vs Unknown)
  2. What is the AWARENESS level? (For focal seizures)
  3. What are the MOTOR features? (Motor vs Non-motor)

🔍 First Seizure Workup

Immediate Evaluation

Labs:
  • Glucose (hypoglycemia)
  • Electrolytes - Na, Ca, Mg
  • BUN/Cr (uremia)
  • Toxicology screen
  • Prolactin (elevated 10-20 min post-seizure; helps distinguish from pseudoseizure)

Neuroimaging

Who needs imaging?
  • ALL adults with first unprovoked seizure → MRI preferred
  • Focal seizure or focal findings on exam
  • Prolonged postictal state
  • Immunocompromised
  • Head trauma
  • Fever (rule out CNS infection)
CT if emergent (rule out hemorrhage, mass); MRI for definitive evaluation

EEG

  • Should be done within 24-48 hours if possible (higher yield)
  • Can help classify seizure type and guide treatment
  • Normal EEG does NOT rule out epilepsy

Provoked vs Unprovoked

Provoked (Acute Symptomatic)Unprovoked
Within 7 days of: stroke, TBI, CNS infection, metabolic derangement
Drug/alcohol withdrawal
Medication toxicity		 No identifiable acute cause
Remote symptomatic (old stroke, TBI)
Usually no long-term AED neededMay need AED; ~40% recurrence risk

To Treat or Not After First Seizure?

Consider starting AED if:
  • Abnormal EEG (epileptiform discharges)
  • Structural abnormality on MRI
  • Nocturnal seizure
  • Prior brain injury
  • Patient preference (driving, occupation)
Recurrence risk: ~40% without treatment; ~50% reduction with AED

Drugs that can WORSEN certain seizures:
  • Carbamazepine/Phenytoin: Can worsen absence, myoclonic (JME)
  • Gabapentin/Pregabalin: Can worsen myoclonic seizures

Special Populations

Pregnancy:
  • Lamotrigine or Levetiracetam preferred
  • AVOID: Valproate (neural tube defects), Topiramate (cleft lip)
  • High-dose folic acid (4mg daily)
Elderly:
  • Lamotrigine, Levetiracetam, Gabapentin preferred
  • Avoid enzyme inducers (interactions)
  • Start low, go slow

🚨 Status Epilepticus

Definition: Seizure lasting >5 minutes OR ≥2 seizures without return to baseline
Emergency! Mortality 20%; neuronal injury begins at 30 min

Treatment Protocol (Time-Based)

TimeStageTreatment
0-5 min Stabilization ABCs, O2, IV access, glucose, vitals
Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4mg) OR Midazolam IM
5-20 min First-line Benzodiazepines:
Lorazepam 4mg IV (repeat ×1)
OR Midazolam 10mg IM if no IV
20-40 min Second-line Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg IV
OR Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV
OR Valproate 40 mg/kg IV
>40 min Refractory ICU, intubation, continuous EEG
Anesthetic infusions: Propofol, Midazolam drip, Pentobarbital

Non-Convulsive Status Epilepticus

Suspect if: Prolonged altered mental status, subtle eye deviation, automatisms
Diagnosis: Continuous EEG monitoring
Treatment: Same as convulsive SE

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: A 7-year-old has brief staring spells multiple times daily, triggered by hyperventilation. EEG shows 3 Hz spike-wave. Diagnosis?

A. Childhood absence epilepsy
B. Juvenile myoclonic epilepsy
C. Focal seizures with impaired awareness
Correct: A. Childhood absence has classic 3 Hz spike-wave, brief staring (5-10 sec), hyperventilation trigger, no postictal confusion.

Q2: First-line drug for childhood absence epilepsy?

A. Carbamazepine
B. Ethosuximide
C. Phenytoin
Correct: B. Ethosuximide is first-line for absence seizures. Carbamazepine and phenytoin can WORSEN absence seizures.

Q3: What defines status epilepticus?

A. Seizure >5 minutes or ≥2 seizures without return to baseline
B. Any seizure lasting >1 minute
C. Seizure with postictal confusion
Correct: A. Status epilepticus is seizure >5 minutes OR ≥2 seizures without return to baseline. It's a medical emergency.

Q4: First-line treatment for status epilepticus?

A. Lorazepam IV
B. Phenytoin IV
C. Propofol
Correct: A. Benzodiazepines (lorazepam, midazolam) are first-line. Phenytoin/fosphenytoin is second-line. Propofol is for refractory cases.

Q5: Which AED is safest in pregnancy?

A. Valproate
B. Lamotrigine
C. Topiramate
Correct: B. Lamotrigine and levetiracetam are preferred in pregnancy. Valproate causes neural tube defects; topiramate causes cleft lip/palate.
