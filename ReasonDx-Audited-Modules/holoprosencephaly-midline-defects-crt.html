🔬 Prosencephalon Development

Primary Brain Vesicles (Week 4):
  1. Prosencephalon (forebrain)
  2. Mesencephalon (midbrain)
  3. Rhombencephalon (hindbrain)
Secondary Brain Vesicles (Week 5):
  • Prosencephalon → Telencephalon (cerebral hemispheres) + Diencephalon (thalamus, hypothalamus)
  • Mesencephalon → Mesencephalon (midbrain)
  • Rhombencephalon → Metencephalon (pons, cerebellum) + Myelencephalon (medulla)

Forebrain Cleavage

Normal development: The prosencephalon divides (cleaves) into two cerebral hemispheres during weeks 5-6

Holoprosencephaly: Failure of prosencephalon to cleave into two hemispheres

Critical signaling: Sonic Hedgehog (SHH) pathway is essential for ventral patterning and midline cleavage
"The face predicts the brain"
Facial development and forebrain development are closely linked - midline facial defects often accompany brain defects

📊 Holoprosencephaly Spectrum

Holoprosencephaly (HPE) = spectrum of forebrain malformations from incomplete cleavage
Incidence: 1:250 conceptuses (most miscarry); 1:10,000-20,000 live births
Most common cause of forebrain malformation
TypeSeverityFeatures
Alobar HPE Most severe Single ventricle, no interhemispheric fissure, fused thalami, absent corpus callosum, cyclopia common
Semilobar HPE Intermediate Partial separation posteriorly, rudimentary interhemispheric fissure, partial fusion of thalami
Lobar HPE Least severe Most separation present, some frontal fusion, may have fused frontal horns, septum pellucidum absent
Middle Interhemispheric Variant (MIH) Variant Fusion of posterior frontal and parietal lobes, with separation frontally and occipitally

🔗 Related Midline Defects

Agenesis of Corpus Callosum (ACC)

Embryology: Corpus callosum develops weeks 12-20 (anterior to posterior)
ACC: Partial or complete absence of corpus callosum
Imaging: "Racing car" sign (parallel lateral ventricles), colpocephaly (dilated occipital horns), absent cavum septi pellucidi
May be: Isolated (often normal development) or syndromic (Aicardi, FAS)

Dandy-Walker Malformation

Triad:
  1. Cystic dilation of 4th ventricle
  2. Complete or partial agenesis of cerebellar vermis
  3. Enlarged posterior fossa with elevation of tentorium
Variants: Dandy-Walker variant (partial vermian hypoplasia), mega cisterna magna
Associated with: Hydrocephalus, other CNS anomalies, cardiac defects

Chiari Malformations

TypeFeaturesAssociation
Chiari I Cerebellar tonsils herniate >5mm below foramen magnum Usually acquired/developmental; headaches, syringomyelia
Chiari II Cerebellar vermis + tonsils herniate; elongated 4th ventricle Always associated with myelomeningocele
Chiari III Herniation into high cervical/occipital encephalocele Rare, severe
Chiari II = "2" things always present:
Cerebellar herniation + Myelomeningocele (spina bifida)

Septo-Optic Dysplasia (de Morsier Syndrome)

Classic triad (need 2 of 3):
  1. Optic nerve hypoplasia
  2. Midline brain defects (absent septum pellucidum, ACC)
  3. Pituitary/hypothalamic dysfunction
Associated: HESX1 gene mutations, young maternal age
Presentation: Visual impairment, nystagmus, hypopituitarism (growth failure, hypothyroidism)

🩺 Clinical Approach

Prenatal Diagnosis

First trimester: Absent "butterfly" sign of choroid plexuses (alobar HPE)
Second trimester findings:
  • Single ventricle or fused ventricles
  • Absent midline structures (cavum septi pellucidi, corpus callosum)
  • Facial anomalies (hypotelorism, proboscis, cleft)
  • Thalamic fusion
Workup: Detailed anatomy scan, fetal MRI, amniocentesis/CVS for karyotype, consider microarray

Postnatal Features

SystemFindings
NeurologicIntellectual disability (severity varies), seizures, motor dysfunction, spasticity
EndocrineHypothalamic-pituitary dysfunction (diabetes insipidus, growth hormone deficiency, hypocortisolism)
FeedingOromotor dysfunction, aspiration, failure to thrive
TemperaturePoikilothermia (can't regulate temperature)
SleepDisordered sleep-wake cycles

Prognosis

Alobar HPE: Usually lethal (most die in utero or in infancy)
Semilobar HPE: Variable; many have severe disabilities
Lobar HPE: Best prognosis; some have near-normal intelligence

Key factors: Brain malformation severity, associated anomalies, endocrine function

Management Considerations

  • Genetic counseling (recurrence risk depends on etiology)
  • Endocrine evaluation and hormone replacement
  • Seizure management
  • Feeding support (may need G-tube)
  • Physical/occupational therapy
  • Palliative care discussions for severe cases

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: What signaling pathway is most critical for prosencephalon cleavage, and its disruption causes holoprosencephaly?

A. Sonic Hedgehog (SHH)
B. Wnt
C. Notch
D. FGF
Correct: A. The Sonic Hedgehog (SHH) pathway is essential for ventral patterning and midline cleavage. SHH mutations are one of the most common causes of familial HPE.

Q2: What is the most common chromosomal abnormality associated with holoprosencephaly?

A. Trisomy 21
B. Trisomy 13
C. Trisomy 18
D. Turner syndrome
Correct: B. Trisomy 13 (Patau syndrome) is the most common chromosomal cause of HPE. About 70% of trisomy 13 fetuses have some degree of HPE.

Q3: A fetus has a single midline eye (cyclopia). What type of holoprosencephaly does this suggest?

A. Alobar HPE
B. Semilobar HPE
C. Lobar HPE
D. Middle interhemispheric variant
Correct: A. "The face predicts the brain." Cyclopia is the most severe facial manifestation and is associated with alobar HPE, the most severe form. This is incompatible with postnatal survival.

Q4: Chiari II malformation is ALWAYS associated with which condition?

A. Holoprosencephaly
B. Myelomeningocele (spina bifida)
C. Agenesis of corpus callosum
D. Dandy-Walker malformation
Correct: B. Chiari II is always associated with myelomeningocele. The neural tube defect causes CSF to leak, reducing intracranial pressure and leading to cerebellar herniation.

Q5: What is the classic triad of septo-optic dysplasia (de Morsier syndrome)?

A. Cyclopia, proboscis, single ventricle
B. Optic nerve hypoplasia, absent septum pellucidum, pituitary dysfunction
C. Hydrocephalus, vermian agenesis, cystic 4th ventricle
D. Cerebellar herniation, syringomyelia, myelomeningocele
Correct: B. Septo-optic dysplasia triad: optic nerve hypoplasia + midline brain defects (absent septum pellucidum, ACC) + hypothalamic-pituitary dysfunction. Diagnosis requires 2 of 3 features.
