Key distinction:
DKA = Ketoacidosis (insulin deficiency → lipolysis → ketones)
HHS = Hyperosmolar state (enough insulin to prevent ketosis, not enough to prevent hyperglycemia)

💧 Fluid Management

Fluids are the FIRST priority!
Start IV fluids immediately - don't wait for labs

Initial Resuscitation

Step 1: 0.9% NS bolus 1-1.5 L in first hour
Then assess: Corrected Na and volume status

Subsequent Fluids (250-500 mL/hr)

Corrected NaFluid Choice
Low or normal0.9% NS
High (>145)0.45% NS
Corrected Na = Measured Na + 1.6 × [(Glucose - 100)/100]
(Add 1.6 mEq/L to Na for every 100 mg/dL glucose above 100)

When Glucose reaches 200-250 (DKA) or 300 (HHS)

Switch to D5 + 0.45% NS to prevent hypoglycemia while continuing insulin

Fluid Deficit Estimates

ConditionTypical Deficit
DKA3-6 liters (~100 mL/kg)
HHS8-10 liters (~100-200 mL/kg)

Replace 50% in first 8-12 hours, remainder over 24 hours

💉 Insulin & Potassium

Insulin Protocol

Regular insulin IV infusion:
Bolus: 0.1 units/kg IV
Drip: 0.1 units/kg/hr (or 0.14 units/kg/hr without bolus)

Goal: Glucose decrease 50-70 mg/dL per hour
Glucose DropAction
<50 mg/dL/hrDouble insulin rate
50-70 mg/dL/hrContinue same rate
>70 mg/dL/hrDecrease rate by half

Potassium Management

CHECK K+ BEFORE INSULIN!
Insulin drives K+ into cells → can cause fatal hypokalemia
Serum K+Action
<3.3 mEq/LHOLD insulin, give 20-40 mEq K+/hr until K+ >3.3
3.3-5.3 mEq/LGive insulin + 20-40 mEq K+ per liter of fluid
>5.3 mEq/LGive insulin, hold K+, recheck q2h
Remember: Initial K+ may be normal or high despite total body K+ depletion (acidosis shifts K+ out of cells)

Bicarbonate

Only give if pH <6.9
100 mEq NaHCO3 in 400 mL water over 2 hours
Routine bicarb is NOT recommended (may worsen outcomes)

✅ Resolution Criteria

DKA Resolution (need ALL)

  • Glucose <200 mg/dL
  • pH >7.3
  • Bicarbonate ≥15 mEq/L
  • Anion gap ≤12

HHS Resolution

  • Osmolality <315 mOsm/kg
  • Patient alert and oriented
  • Glucose <300 mg/dL

Transition to Subcutaneous Insulin

When resolved and eating:
  1. Give subcutaneous basal insulin
  2. Continue IV insulin for 1-2 hours AFTER subQ dose (overlap)
  3. Then discontinue IV drip
Dose: ~0.5-0.8 units/kg/day total; 50% basal, 50% bolus

Complications to Monitor

ComplicationPrevention/Management
HypokalemiaReplace K+ aggressively; monitor q2-4h
HypoglycemiaAdd D5 when glucose 200-250
Cerebral edemaAvoid rapid osmolality changes (more common in children)
Pulmonary edemaMonitor for fluid overload, especially in elderly/HF
Hyperchloremic acidosisFrom NS; may persist after gap closes - monitor

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: Patient has glucose 850, pH 7.35, HCO3 22, minimal ketones. Diagnosis?

A. DKA
B. HHS
C. Alcoholic ketoacidosis
Correct: B. HHS has very high glucose (>600), normal pH (>7.3), normal bicarb (>18), and minimal ketones. DKA would have acidosis and significant ketones.

Q2: Initial K+ is 2.9 mEq/L in DKA. What should you do?

A. Start insulin immediately
B. Hold insulin, give K+ until >3.3
C. Give insulin and K+ together
Correct: B. If K+ <3.3, HOLD insulin and replace potassium first. Insulin will drive K+ into cells and can cause fatal hypokalemia.

Q3: When should you add dextrose to IV fluids in DKA?

A. Immediately
B. When glucose reaches 200-250 mg/dL
C. When DKA resolves
Correct: B. Add D5 when glucose reaches 200-250 to prevent hypoglycemia while continuing insulin to resolve ketoacidosis.

Q4: What is the target rate of glucose decline in DKA?

A. 50-70 mg/dL per hour
B. 100-150 mg/dL per hour
C. <50 mg/dL per hour
Correct: A. Goal is 50-70 mg/dL/hr. If dropping <50, increase insulin; if >70, decrease insulin.

Q5: When transitioning from IV to subQ insulin, what's critical?

A. Stop IV insulin immediately when giving subQ
B. Overlap IV and subQ insulin for 1-2 hours
C. Give subQ insulin only at mealtimes
Correct: B. Continue IV insulin for 1-2 hours AFTER giving subQ basal insulin to allow overlap. Stopping IV too soon causes rebound DKA.
