🎯 Defining Psychosis

Psychosis = Loss of contact with reality, characterized by:
• Delusions - Fixed false beliefs
• Hallucinations - Perceptions without external stimuli
• Disorganized thinking/speech
• Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior

Hallucination Types

Rule of thumb:
Auditory hallucinations → Think PRIMARY psychiatric (schizophrenia, severe mood disorders)
Visual hallucinations → Think SECONDARY/organic (delirium, substances, dementia, seizures)

Secondary (Organic) Causes

ALWAYS rule out medical causes, especially in:
  • First episode psychosis
  • Age >40 at onset (late-onset)
  • Visual hallucinations predominate
  • Acute onset (hours to days)
  • Fluctuating course
  • Abnormal vital signs
  • No psychiatric history
CategoryExamples
SubstancesIntoxication: stimulants, cannabis, PCP, hallucinogens
Withdrawal: alcohol, benzos, barbiturates
MedicationsSteroids, anticholinergics, dopamine agonists, anticonvulsants
NeurologicalDementia (esp DLB), Parkinson's, Huntington's, seizures, stroke, MS, brain tumor
InfectiousEncephalitis (HSV, HIV), neurosyphilis, prion disease, UTI (elderly)
AutoimmuneAnti-NMDA receptor encephalitis, SLE cerebritis
MetabolicHepatic encephalopathy, uremia, electrolyte disturbances, hypoglycemia
EndocrineThyroid disease, Cushing's, Addison's, hyperparathyroidism
NutritionalB12 deficiency, thiamine (Wernicke's), niacin (pellagra)
Anti-NMDA Receptor Encephalitis - Young woman with psychiatric symptoms + movement disorder + autonomic instability + seizures. Associated with ovarian teratoma. Check anti-NMDA antibodies!

🔬 First Episode Psychosis Workup

Goals:
  1. Rule out medical/organic causes
  2. Ensure patient safety
  3. Establish baseline before treatment
  4. Begin appropriate treatment

Pre-Antipsychotic Baseline

Before starting antipsychotics, document:
  • Weight, BMI, waist circumference
  • Fasting glucose or HbA1c
  • Fasting lipid panel
  • Blood pressure, heart rate
  • ECG (QTc) for certain medications
  • Prolactin (optional baseline)
  • AIMS exam (baseline for tardive dyskinesia)

💊 Treatment Approach

Acute Management

Safety First:
  • Assess for danger to self/others
  • Consider 1:1 observation
  • Remove dangerous items from environment
  • May need involuntary hold if danger present

Pharmacotherapy

AgentKey Points
Second-generation (atypical)First-line for most patients; less EPS risk
Examples: risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole, ziprasidone
First-generation (typical)Effective but more EPS; haloperidol useful acutely (IM available)
ClozapineReserved for treatment-resistant schizophrenia; risk of agranulocytosis (requires monitoring)
Long-acting injectables (LAI)For adherence issues; monthly or longer dosing

Acute Agitation

First try: Verbal de-escalation, calm environment, oral medications

If needed:
  • Oral: Olanzapine 5-10mg, risperidone 2mg, haloperidol 5mg + lorazepam 2mg
  • IM: Haloperidol 5mg + lorazepam 2mg + diphenhydramine 50mg ("B52")
    OR olanzapine 10mg IM (don't combine with IM benzos - respiratory depression)
  • Avoid: IM olanzapine + IM benzodiazepines together

Treatment-Resistant Schizophrenia

Definition: Failed adequate trials of ≥2 different antipsychotics (including at least one atypical) at adequate dose for adequate duration (≥6 weeks each)

Treatment: Clozapine - most effective antipsychotic
Monitoring required: Weekly CBC × 6 months, then biweekly × 6 months, then monthly (agranulocytosis risk)

🧠 Test Your Knowledge

Q1: Visual hallucinations are most suggestive of which etiology?

A. Schizophrenia
B. Organic/medical cause (delirium, substances)
C. Major depression
D. Delusional disorder
Correct: B. Visual hallucinations suggest an organic cause such as delirium, substance intoxication/withdrawal, or neurological disease. Primary psychiatric disorders like schizophrenia more commonly cause auditory hallucinations.

Q2: What duration of symptoms distinguishes schizophreniform disorder from schizophrenia?

A. 2 weeks vs 1 month
B. 1-6 months vs >6 months
C. 1 month vs 3 months
D. There is no time distinction
Correct: B. Schizophreniform: 1-6 months total duration. Schizophrenia: >6 months total duration (with at least 1 month of active-phase symptoms).

Q3: A young woman presents with acute psychosis, seizures, movement abnormalities, and autonomic instability. What should you test for?

A. Urine drug screen only
B. Anti-NMDA receptor antibodies
C. Thyroid function only
D. HIV only
Correct: B. This presentation (psychiatric symptoms + movement disorder + seizures + autonomic instability in a young woman) is classic for anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis. Check for antibodies and image ovaries for teratoma.

Q4: Which antipsychotic is reserved for treatment-resistant schizophrenia and requires regular blood monitoring?

A. Risperidone
B. Haloperidol
C. Clozapine
D. Quetiapine
Correct: C. Clozapine is the most effective antipsychotic but reserved for treatment-resistant cases due to risk of agranulocytosis. Requires weekly CBC monitoring initially.

Q5: Which combination should be AVOIDED when treating acute agitation?

A. IM haloperidol + IM lorazepam
B. IM olanzapine + IM lorazepam
C. Oral risperidone + oral lorazepam
D. IM haloperidol + IM diphenhydramine
Correct: B. IM olanzapine + IM benzodiazepines can cause severe respiratory depression and hypotension. If olanzapine IM is given, wait at least 1 hour before giving IM benzos.
