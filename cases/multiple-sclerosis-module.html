MECHANISM
Pathophysiology

Multiple sclerosis (MS): autoimmune demyelination of the CNS (brain and spinal cord). T-cell mediated attack on oligodendrocytes → plaques of demyelination disseminated in TIME and SPACE.

FeatureDetails
DemographicsYoung adults (20-40), female predominance (2-3:1), higher prevalence at higher latitudes (vitamin D connection), Northern European descent
Lesion locationPeriventricular white matter (most common), corpus callosum ("Dawson fingers"), optic nerves, brainstem, cerebellum, spinal cord
Key conceptDissemination in SPACE (multiple CNS locations) + TIME (attacks separated temporally) = MS diagnosis

Disease Subtypes

TypeFeatures
Relapsing-remitting (RRMS)85% at diagnosis. Discrete attacks with full/partial recovery. Most common initial presentation
Secondary progressive (SPMS)RRMS transitions to gradual decline (most RRMS converts over 10-20 years)
Primary progressive (PPMS)10-15%. Gradual decline from onset without relapses. Older age, equal sex ratio, worse prognosis
CLINICAL
Presentation & Diagnosis

Classic Presentations

SyndromeFeatures
Optic neuritisMost common initial presentation. Unilateral vision loss + pain with eye movement. APD (Marcus Gunn pupil). May be first sign of MS
Internuclear ophthalmoplegia (INO)MLF lesion. Impaired adduction of ipsilateral eye + nystagmus of contralateral abducting eye. Bilateral INO in young adult = MS until proven otherwise
Transverse myelitisSpinal cord inflammation → weakness, sensory level, bowel/bladder dysfunction. MS lesions typically PARTIAL (asymmetric), spanning <2 vertebral segments
Lhermitte signElectric shock sensation down spine with neck flexion (posterior column demyelination)
Uhthoff phenomenonSymptoms worsen with heat (heat slows conduction in demyelinated nerves). Classic: vision worsens with hot bath/exercise
🔑 Diagnosis (McDonald Criteria): MRI brain/spine: T2 hyperintense lesions disseminated in space and time. Gadolinium-enhancing lesions = active inflammation. CSF: oligoclonal bands (present in >95% MS) + elevated IgG index. MRI is the most important diagnostic tool.
TREATMENT
Management

Acute Relapse

IV methylprednisolone 1g/day × 3-5 days. Shortens relapse duration but does NOT change long-term outcome. Plasmapheresis for steroid-refractory relapses.

Disease-Modifying Therapies (DMTs)

CategoryExamplesKey Notes
InjectableInterferon-beta, glatiramer acetateTraditional first-line. Moderate efficacy. Flu-like symptoms (IFN-β)
OralDimethyl fumarate, fingolimod, teriflunomide, siponimodFingolimod: S1P modulator, first-dose bradycardia monitoring. Flushing with DMF
High-efficacyNatalizumab, ocrelizumab, alemtuzumab, ofatumumabNatalizumab: α4-integrin antibody, risk of PML (JC virus). Ocrelizumab: anti-CD20, also approved for PPMS
⚠️ PML (Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy): JC virus reactivation in immunosuppressed patients. Risk with natalizumab (especially if JCV antibody +, >2 years treatment, prior immunosuppression). Fatal demyelinating infection. Check JCV antibody before starting natalizumab.

Symptom Management

Fatigue: amantadine, modafinil. Spasticity: baclofen, tizanidine. Neuropathic pain: gabapentin, pregabalin, duloxetine. Bladder: oxybutynin, intermittent catheterization. Depression: SSRIs.
