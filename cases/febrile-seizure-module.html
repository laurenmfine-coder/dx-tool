Febrile Seizures

Classification, workup, management, and parental counseling for simple and complex febrile seizures.

📚 2 Sections ❓ 4 MCQs ⏱ ~20 min

📊 Classification & Workup

Simple vs Complex Febrile Seizures

FeatureSimpleComplex
Duration<15 minutes≥15 minutes or status febrilis
TypeGeneralized tonic-clonicFocal onset or focal features
RecurrenceSingle in 24 hoursMultiple in 24 hours
Post-ictalBrief, full recoveryProlonged, possible Todd paralysis
Proportion~70-75% of febrile seizures~25-30%
Epilepsy riskNo increase over baseline (1-2%)~2-5% risk

Key Features

  • Age: 6 months to 5 years (peak 12-18 months)
  • Temperature: Usually >38°C (100.4°F); often rising rapidly
  • Incidence: 2-5% of children; most common seizure type in childhood
  • Recurrence: ~30% overall; higher if: age <18 months, lower fever at seizure, family history, short duration of fever before seizure
  • Genetics: Strong familial tendency; ~25-40% have positive family history

Workup Guidelines (AAP)

  • Simple febrile seizure, ≥12 months, immunizations current: NO routine LP, imaging, labs, or EEG. Focus on fever source.
  • Consider LP if: Age <12 months, incomplete Hib/pneumococcal vaccination, pretreated with antibiotics (may mask meningitis), meningeal signs
  • Complex febrile seizure: Consider LP, neuroimaging if focal features, and EEG (especially if prolonged or focal)
  • EEG: NOT recommended after simple febrile seizure (does not predict epilepsy)
  • Neuroimaging: NOT routine; only if focal features, abnormal neuro exam, or suspicion of structural cause

💊 Management & Counseling

Acute Management

  • Most self-resolve within 1-2 minutes. Protect child from injury, position on side.
  • Prolonged seizure (>5 min): Benzodiazepines — rectal diazepam, intranasal midazolam, or IV lorazepam
  • Febrile status epilepticus (>30 min): Treat aggressively per status epilepticus protocol
  • Antipyretics: Acetaminophen or ibuprofen for comfort. DO NOT prevent febrile seizures but treat underlying illness.

💎 No AED Prophylaxis

Neither continuous AEDs (valproate, phenobarbital) nor intermittent benzodiazepines are recommended for simple febrile seizures. The risks of medication side effects outweigh benefits given the benign nature and low epilepsy risk. AAP strongly recommends AGAINST prophylactic treatment.

Parental Counseling Points

  • Prognosis is excellent: Simple febrile seizures do NOT cause brain damage, intellectual disability, or death
  • Risk of epilepsy: Not increased after simple febrile seizure (same as general population ~1-2%)
  • Recurrence: ~30% chance of another febrile seizure, but this is still benign
  • What to do if recurs: Stay calm, place child on side, do not put anything in mouth, time the seizure, call 911 if >5 minutes
  • Antipyretics do NOT prevent febrile seizures — important to set expectations
